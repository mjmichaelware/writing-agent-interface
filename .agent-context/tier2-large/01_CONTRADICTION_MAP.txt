  > Father Aviel had been a scholar, sharp as fresh-knapped flint, sought and summoned by those whose questions twisted beyond their own unraveling. When Mother Zuna passed — nameless there forevermore — something stopped within him: not death, which would have been the cleaner mercy, but the stillness of a cart whose axle has been shattered so completely that no carpenter in the province can name the right repair. The gods he had gathered from the western plains — brittle bronze forms of forgotten flame, of fading fortune, of fertility that promised and did not deliver — could not fill what had gone hollow in him. So he swept them away and kept only one: the lord of limitless dust and earthly plenty, who whispered that hoarded objects might hold back time himself, just to spite whatever kept making new things. That one stayed. That one, as you will discover, was paying close attention all along.
  > On a night when the moon rode full on its third day, the son moved through the monument his father's madness had made. They had called him Dan once, when names still mattered in this dwelling that hardly stood. He was sixteen, with kind eyes that held more sorrow than a boy should carry and unruly black hair that wrestled with his simple commandments. He had started at the hearth: crouching low, striking flint against pyrite twice before the sparks scattered in a brief gold cascade and the pitch-soaked rag caught, guttering once in protest before it committed. Now the frail flame trembled not from wind but from the staccato percussion against his ribs, casting suspicious shadows across the narrow corridor. Each barefoot step through the refuse was a wager against sound: one shard of pottery, one brittle bone breaking beneath his weight, and the house would know. He was moving toward his father's room, and tonight he would enter the dreamscape to accomplish what no daylight conversation had been able to.
  > The corridor choked with debris that had long since stopped being merely debris. Broken pottery lay scattered like the shards of failed offerings — Dan had counted eighteen visible pieces one morning before stopping, unable to push past that number. He would not have been able to say why eighteen stopped him. He simply stopped. Fragments of parchment and brittle wax tablets, once bearing records of lineage and covenant, lay stripped clean by mold that had developed what could only be called ambition — spreading in patterns too deliberate for accident, following lines that suggested a map of something no one had commissioned and no one sane would consult. Ruined brass astrolabes gathered in the shadowed corners, their rings seized into arrangements no craftsman had designed, pointing with insistent consistency toward a direction that corresponded to no coordinate in heaven or on earth. Desiccated fruits clung to their seeds in the manner of things that insist they are still alive while offering nothing by way of evidence. From the darkest pocket of the corridor — the corner the torch's light did not quite reach — something made no sound, but in a manner that was different from ordinary silence. The distinction was slight. Dan noticed it the way you notice that a room has someone in it before you find the someone.
  > The smell came in layers. Ammonia sharp enough to sear the passages and raise involuntary water to the eyes. Decay: sweet and oppressive as overripe fruit three days past its reckoning. Dusty herbs that had surrendered their virtue and become merely powder with the memory of virtue. Maggots worked the rotted cloth in the near dark — pale as mushroom flesh, fat as thumbs — the only living things in this corridor that had found their purpose and intended to keep it, as purposeful as priests at a rite no one had thought to cancel. Flies rose and fell in low, patient clouds; their droning was the liturgy of the house, as established and particular as any psalm. Beneath everything, the mineral reek of disturbed earth, of things buried being pulled back into light where they had no business being. Dan had stopped noticing any of this six years ago. The fact that he noticed it again tonight told him something about the weight of what he was about to attempt.
  > Here is what the record seldom clarifies, and what you deserve to understand before we go further: some few children come into the world able to remain awake inside the country where all dreamers travel. Not awake the way the body is awake — no muscle moves, no eyelid shifts — but present with the full attention of a watcher who cannot be deceived about what is actually happening. Dan's mother had been such a traveler, and she had begun teaching him when he was still young enough to learn without fully understanding what was being handed to him. To enter another dreamer's country required proximity — the closer the watcher, the cleaner the passage, and this was why Dan moved toward his father's door rather than attempting the work from his own bed. The TECHNIQUE was simple enough to describe and requires more discipline than most adults possess: release yesterday, letting it dissolve the way salt dissolves in deep water; release tomorrow, unhooking from its obligations; then release now — this is the difficult one — until the body achieves the stillness of deep water and what is true may surface in it. He had practiced this for five years in secret, in this house. Tonight the practice had a destination.
  > Dan arrived at his father's door and stopped. He pressed his back to the wall beside it and went still — not the still of patience of serpents in a garden, but the particular stillness of someone listening through stone for a sound they need to hear. From the other side came the sound of a man trying to outrun his own breath: that ragged, straining inhalation belonging to the body running drills for a crisis that has not yet arrived or has long since passed. Dan waited through one long stretch of this, then another. The breath gradually deepened, steadied, lost its urgency. Then, finally — the slow and heavy pull of a man gone fully under. His father was down. He was not running anymore.
  > The dark that arrived was not absence but presence — the darkness that waits behind every flame for exactly this invitation, moving in with the confidence of something that knows the space and has been here before. Dan pressed his palm flat to the doorframe — cold, slightly rough, exactly as solid as stone had always been regardless of what was asked of it — and, barely above a breath, said something to himself. He whispered at the doorframe, “Not ready. Yet here, I am.” Then he closed his eyes.
  > The dreamscape received him with the patient depth of a country that has been waiting longer than waiting has a name. The formless dark churned below and the void was total above, as things must have been before the first word of the first morning was spoken into the dark that had not yet known it was waiting. Dan oriented quickly — he knew this country the way a man knows the house he grew up in, could move through it without a lamp — and turned toward his father's presence. He found it immediately: a wall thick and pulsing with old bruise-light, sick and humid, beating with the slow regularity of a man trying not to know something he already knows.
  > Something bled through that wall that was not Dan's. A child's scream cut short. Blood on white stone. A voice calling one name and stopping mid-syllable. The deep, bottomless shame of a man who cannot locate the moment he should have acted differently. Dan pressed his palm against the wall. Then he pressed his whole intention.
