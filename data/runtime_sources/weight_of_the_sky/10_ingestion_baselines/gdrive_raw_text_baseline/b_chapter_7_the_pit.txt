Chapter 7: The Pit 

The last rays of the sun, the color of old blood and bruised fruit, were staining the sky behind him as Dan approached the gates of Megiddo. The city rose before him, not a gentle hill like Bethlehem or a proud peak like Jebus, but a tell – a layered mound of history, built upon itself over forgotten ages. A place where countless lives had been lived, and countless more had ended, their dust mingling in the very soil of the place. The prior encounter with the aerial sovereign's seeker had left a psychic gelidity that had nothing to do with the setting sun. Her frantic energy, her warped faith, had cast a long shadow that seemed to stretch and deepen as he neared the ancient stones of the city walls. He tightened his grasp on the strap of his waterskin, stood up straight with his shoulders back, and walked towards the gate, the heavy leather feeling oddly cold against his palm. The air itself seemed to grow heavy, thick with the scent of ancient earth and something else, something that whispered of things long buried and best left undisturbed, a faint, almost imperceptible hum that vibrated just beneath the threshold of hearing.
He passed through the thick stone archway, the transition feeling less like entering a city and more like stepping into a different kind of viscosity. The air didn't change temperature, but it felt... old. A stagnant weight, like standing at the bottom of a deep well where the air hasn't moved in years, thick with the breath of things long buried. The stones underfoot felt older here, worn smooth not just by the passage of feet, but by the relentless grind of Time itself, a force that felt coiled and watching, sensing his presence, a silent, ancient sentinel.
The people in the street seemed peculiar too. Not the easy smiles of Shechem or the open faces of Bethel or even the wary sorrow of Shiloh. Their eyes, as they looked at him, held something guarded, something... hard. A coldness that seemed to seep out from beneath their skin, a mirror reflecting a soul that had seen too much and liked too little. Dan, with his uncommon ability to discern the deeper currents of the soul, saw it clearly: a profound weariness, a resignation to the heavy, downward pull of this place. They didn't call out welcomes or offer lodging right away, as was the custom in many towns. They simply stopped what they were doing – haggling over goods, mending nets, chatting by doorways – and watched him pass, their stares following him like invisible threads pulling at his clothes, his pack, his very being. It was the deep, unsettling silence of a crowd that chooses not to acknowledge you, but simply to observe with a silent, palpable disapproval.
A group of children playing with pebbles in a dusty square stopped their game abruptly as he drew near. Their laughter, a sound that should have been light and joyful, cut off like a snapped string. Their young faces, usually innocent and curious, turned towards him, and their gazes were sharp, judging, giving him looks that spoke of a deep-seated dislike he hadn't earned, a dislike that felt ancient, taught. Even the children seemed to sense he didn’t belong, that something about him was fundamentally unwelcome here. One small boy, no older than five, threw a stone that bounced harmlessly near Dan's feet, his eyes wide with a hostility that felt too large for his small body. He was clearly not accustomed to the hospitality of his people. And the boy with messy black hair, and kind eyes which had seen too much picked his way through the refuse once more.
He walked deeper into the city, the streets winding upwards onto the tell, the incline steady and demanding on his legs. The ground under his feet felt strangely resistant, not just from the slope. Sometimes it gave a strange, soft give, like stepping onto thick moss, but it was only packed earth and stone. At other moments, it felt solid, yet seemed to vibrate with a low hum he felt more in his bones than heard with his ears, a subtle, unsettling thrumming that spoke of something vast and alive beneath the surface. This low, persistent vibration, a quiet buzzing, seemed to grow stronger with every step, a subtle warning that tightened the muscles in his back. He passed a public well, its stone lip worn smooth by centuries of ropes and hands. As he neared it, meaning to check if its water looked clean, a ripple, violent and sudden, burst across the surface of the still water, not from wind, but from some unseen disturbance beneath, as if the water itself recoiled from him, spitting a fine spray of droplets onto the dusty ground. A shiver traced a cold path down his spine. The earth under his feet felt sluggish, clinging to his sandals, making each step require more effort than it should. It wasn't just tiredness from the journey; it was a profound lethargy, a feeling of being pulled down, anchored to the heavy, layered, resistant soil of Megiddo. The city itself seemed to sigh with a low, unhappy sound that wound through the narrow streets as he passed, a feeling that resonated with the ache that had settled in his bones, pulling him towards the ground.
A city built on layers of battle and worship, dedicated to powers who valued roots and earth and the strength found in the low places, could become a very uncomfortable spot for someone whose very essence was aimed at the stars. Megiddo, perched between valley and sky, a place of crossroads and conflict, seemed determined to show Dan exactly which side it was on: the side that preferred things rooted, heavy, part of the long, undeniable history of the earth.
He needed food, water. His stomach rumbled, a hollow echo in the oppressive air, a basic need that felt somehow wrong in this place of spiritual rejection. He saw a vendor selling dried fruit and nuts near what looked like a small marketplace, a cluster of stalls huddled together like wary sheep. He approached, holding out a few small silver pieces, hoping for a simple transaction, a brief moment of normalcy. The vendor, a man with a face like cracked leather and eyes that seemed to hold the coldness of old stones, eyed him up and down, his gaze lingering on Dan's simple traveler's clothes, on something unseen that marked him as an outsider, unwanted.
"What give for a handful of figs?" Dan asked, his voice feeling thin and dry in the heavy air.
The vendor scoffed, a dry, unpleasant sound that scraped like stone on stone. "For you, traveler? Double that of anyone else. Perhaps triple. My goods don't care for your... Ways." He gestured vaguely towards the sky, towards the space above Dan's head, where the stars would soon appear. He made it sound like an insult, an impurity, a stain upon his very presence. Dan's hand, still outstretched with the silver, began to tremble slightly. The vendor's cold, dismissive gaze made the silver feel like a burden, a heavy, useless weight. He turned away, hunger a sharp pang in his belly, a physical manifestation of the city's rejection.
He saw another vendor, this one with large clay jars of water, beads of condensation suggesting coolness within. His throat felt raw, parched by the dusty road and the strange, thick air of the city. He approached, holding out a large shiny piece, the simple need for water overriding his growing unease. "Water, please."
This vendor, a woman with tight lips and colder eyes than the first, eyes that seemed to hold the grayness of storm clouds, took the piece without a word. She poured water into a simple clay cup. Dan lifted it to his lips, eager for the cool, clear liquid, picturing the relief it would bring. The moment it touched his tongue, a wave of sickening disgust washed over him. The water tasted foul, stagnant, like liquid decay mingled with ash and bitter herbs, the flavor of something that should have been pure, utterly corrupted. It burned his tongue, felt thick and wrong, like drinking mud and sorrow. He gagged, spitting it out onto the dusty ground near the stall, the taste lingering, vile and pervasive, coating his mouth with bitterness.
The vendor watched him, a thin, cruel smile touching her lips, a smile that didn't reach her cold eyes. "Doesn't suit your palate, traveler? Our waters are blessed by the Lords who hold up the Earth. Perhaps you prefer the thin, tasteless water of the high places, where nothing truly grows." She snatched the coin from the counter where he had placed it, pocketing it with a speed that belied her casual cruelty, the silver disappearing into her robes as if it had never been offered.
Dan wiped his mouth with the back of his hand, the rotten taste refusing to leave, a constant reminder of the city's rejection. A cold anger began to simmer within him, a slow burn against the injustice. The water, meant to sustain, had become a weapon, a physical manifestation of the city's spiritual hostility. It was Baal, he knew, the storm-claimers, the water-bringers who also controlled the depths of the earth. Their power flowed through the very water he needed to survive, and they had tainted it, turning it to poison. They were angry. He felt their anger, a palpable pressure in the air, a silent roar against his very presence, against his daring to aim higher than their domain. He wouldn't be able to buy food or water here if this was the reception at every turn.
The physical toll of the city's hostility was beginning to weigh heavily. His stomach cramped with hunger, his throat burned with thirst and the taste of corrupted water. He pushed on, needing to find shelter before night fell completely, before his strength failed entirely. The feeling of lethargy grew stronger with every step, as if the very stones of the tell were trying to absorb his energy, to make him part of their ancient, layered history. He walked through the maze of streets, past homes made of sun-baked mud brick and stone, past silent, watchful children who stopped their play to stare with unsettling intensity, past adults whose eyes narrowed as he passed, some even turning their backs pointedly as he approached. The ground beneath his sandals felt uneven, sometimes giving a strange, soft give, as if the earth itself were rippling, shifting in discomfort or anger at his presence, like skin crawling over bone. He felt a growing pressure, a crushing weight that seemed to push down from the very air above him, a silent, powerful opposition that mirrored the downward pull of the earth below. The subtle buzzing, which had begun as a faint hum, now felt like a low vibration in his bones, a constant, unsettling thrumming beneath the city's surface.
He needed lodging. Urgently. He approached what looked like a larger home set slightly apart from the others, built of sturdy stone, the kind of place known for taking in travelers where they shared a common space. The owner, a large man with arms like tree trunks and a face carved from weathered rock, came to the door, his expression wary, assessing.
"Lodging?" Dan asked, his voice raspy from thirst and the strange taste, trying to keep the desperation out of it.
The man looked him over, his eyes sharp, missing nothing. He named a weight, easily twice what Dan had paid in Shechem or even Dothan for a simple corner.
It was a bribe, pure and simple. The price of passage through a place that wished him gone. It was his father's five shekels. The reserve meant for trouble, for just such moments when the world demanded more than it offered. He reached into the hidden pouch, his fingers closing around a few heavy pieces from his “bribe stash,” feeling their solid, undeniable presence. He pulled them out and placed them in the man's outstretched hand. They felt cool and heavy, a tangible surrender to the demands of this place, a physical payment for spiritual passage.
The man's eyes widened slightly as he weighed the pieces in his hand. "Aye," he grunted. "That will suffice. Come in, traveler. Find a space by the hearth. The fire is warm, and no one here will bother you tonight. Your payment has been noted."
Dan stepped inside the traveler's house, leaving the hostile streets of Megiddo behind. Exhausted, hungry, and deeply unsettled, he found a space by the fading embers of the hearth in the common room, where other travelers were already settled, their forms quiet in the dim light. He unrolled his thin mat, placed his pack beneath his head, and lay down. The fire's warmth did little to chase the chill that the city had pressed into his bones.
The Terminal Tension
He needed rest. Desperately. He needed to escape the oppressive weight of Megiddo, to find the upward path in the Dreamscape, to cleanse his spirit of the city's clinging hostility, to seek the clarity that lay far above this place of decay and demands. He closed his eyes, focusing his will, yearning for the familiar lift, the journey towards the stars. He needed to fly, to remind himself that the heights still existed, that the downward pull was not the only force in the world.
But this time, the familiar ascent was denied. The dream-currents, usually responsive to his will, felt thick and resistant, like trying to swim through mud. A cold, invisible hand seemed to press down on his chest, pinning him to the earth of his dream, denying him the sky. His spirit strained, pushed, but the upward path remained closed, sealed by an unseen force.
The Dreamscape became real around him not as a field or a sea, but as a vast, empty place filled with a heavy, gray dread. He felt their purpose before he saw them. A presence. Or rather, two. They took shape from the grayness, beings of immense power. Lord El, ancient and terrible in his quiet strength, stood beside a Lady goddess, glowing with a beauty that promised both bliss and total destruction. El’s form was like a mountain of polished obsidian, his skin reflecting the dim gray light, his eyes like twin voids that seemed to absorb all light and warmth. The Lady, a shifting, shimmering form of captivating allure, her skin like moonlight on water, her hair a cascade of dark, living shadows, promised everything and gave nothing. Her eyes, when they met his, were like huge, dark storms, swirling with ancient fury and cold, calculating intent.
They didn't speak. Their message hit him like a physical blow – cold, final, and filled with old, angry power. He had listened where he shouldn't have listened. This was the judgment of the Lord of Anger, a breaking of sacred rules, a violation of holy boundaries.
Then, they struck. Not with a hand or a weapon, but with pure will, a command sent right into his spirit. It hit him in the chest, a shockwave that stole his breath and broke his focus. The feeling of wanting to go up vanished, completely wiped out. Instead, there was a sickening lurch, a terrifying flip. His dream, meant for climbing, was violated. His spirit's core was warped into a tool for his collapse.
He was falling.
The Inverted Axis of Existence
It started slow, a dizzying plunge, but sped up with terrifying speed. The grayness above him rushed away, replaced by a deeper and deeper blackness below. This wasn't a thrilling flight into the stars; this was a dive into something dark and huge and hungry. The air grew thick, heavy, pressing in, making it hard to breathe. It smelled of wet dirt, then of rot, then of something much worse – the sick, foul stench of decomposition, of things that had lain dead and breaking down for ages, a choking smell of filth and awful decay, thick with unclean promises. A low, constant buzzing, like distant swarms, began to fill the air, growing steadily louder.
He tumbled head over heels, disoriented, the very concepts of Up and Down ceasing to exist, only the sickening feeling of falling remaining. The speed was impossible now, the blackness a blur. This was the Pit made [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE]: the inverted axis of existence, the chasm that smelled faintly of salt and iron—the ghosts of dried tears and ancient blood.
The light of Wisdom was not merely blocked; it was un-thought, leaving behind an aching blackness. His corporeal form dissolved into a freefall of pure, screaming consciousness, a horrifying silence where the only sound was the shriek of his own unraveling, abstract thoughts. This descent was his Sacrifice of Judgment—a terrifying, irreversible psychological and spiritual decomposition.
The fall was a rapid-fire tour through the collapsing universal hierarchy, an Edgar Allan Poe nightmare of spiritual decay:
I. The Layer of Apathy (Sloth and Separation)
He passed a stratum of clinging, gelid gray ether, the color of exhausted bone. This was not the horror of struggle, but the chilling, uncanny peace of Apathy, the mid-point where the light of Wisdom is just a phantom echo and the roots of Anger are still too far to feel. Here, the abstract concepts he had sought—Truth, Courage, even Loyal Love—hung limp and meaningless, like tattered flags in a vacuum. The gravity of hope failed, and the will to ascend simply evaporated. His only horror was the sudden, awful understanding that the universe did not care about his striving, rendering it pointless, a chilling echo of the city's rejection. He felt his spirit yearn to stop, to simply float in the lukewarm indifference, but the command of the low gods propelled him downward, through the veil of neutral nothingness.
II. The Layer of Greed (Avarice and Hoarding)
The gray dissolved into a sickly, pulsing torrent of yellow-brown light—the layer of Greed. This was the lust not for material gold, but for the possession of the source itself. Phantom hands, the spectral echoes of his father's hoarding, clawed at his spirit, not to harm him, but to cling to him, trying to arrest his fall by burdening him with the weight of every unspent emotion, every un-surrendered artifact. He felt the terrifying, crushing pressure of unnecessary weight, an anchor of material guilt. The stench of stale metal and unmoving possessions became unbearable, a suffocating perfume of avarice and the terrifying sense of being trapped by the very things meant to set him free. Every single possession he had ever owned, every silver coin he hoarded, every memory he clung to, manifested here as a solid, choking weight, attempting to halt his necessary plunge.
III. The Layer of Wrath (Anger and Anti-Motion)
The yellow-brown was ripped apart by a tearing, deafening chaos of crimson—the layer of Wrath. This was not simple heat, but anti-motion itself, the kinetic expression of pure, destructive friction. The air screamed, vibrating with the noise of every word of judgment, every act of violence that had ever been committed. The force was not pushing him down; it was tearing him apart laterally, a sheer, destructive tension that threatened to atomize his soul into the dust he had so recently mastered. The sound was the collective, unending roar of all disappointed ego, all broken covenant, all terminal anger—a sound so violent it should have annihilated him instantly. The roots of the terrible tree in the center of the dreamscape whipped past him like lashes, their bark scorched by the sheer, unquenchable fury of the layer they passed through.
IV. The Bedrock of Pride
And then, the sudden, absolute cold.
The chaos ceased. He struck the bedrock of Pride. It was not fire or chaos, but a smooth, polished, mirrored surface—the chilling plane of the Ultimate Ego, reflecting only himself, forever separate, forever isolated. The final horror was not in the death of self, but the eternal, cold preservation of the solipsistic self. The Pit wasn't the devil; it was the realization of I AM—the absolute loneliness of separation from the Source. The fall had been his Sacrifice of Judgment, and the pit had accepted the offering.
The Consequence
He found himself lying on a woven mat in a simple, mudbrick room, the walls whispering of earth. A stream of strange vowels and consonants, soundless to his mind but violent on his throat, erupted uncontrollably—a foreign, shattered tongue spilling from his mouth. He was awake, but he was not whole.
Close by, he saw a small altar made of rough, uncut stones piled together, a simple, unadorned structure. This was not the gilded idolatry of the low gods, but a token of austere faith.
Figures were nearby, their faces indistinct at the periphery of his field of vision, observing him with confused and scared looks. He saw hands reaching toward him, heard voices raised in alarm, their words a collection he couldn't disaggregate, lost in the mix of noises erupting from his own broken instrument. He felt a cool palm touch his cranium, heard a gentle utterance he couldn't identify. These were not the antagonistic faces of Megiddo's streets. These were visages etched with solicitude, with something that felt like... kindness.
He saw, even through his indistinct sight, simple symbols woven into their clothes, carved into a wooden beam overhead—symbols that spoke of The Source of All, of a faith that looked upwards, not down.
The Pit had left its mark—a chilling, absolute echo that remained not just in his psyche, but on his very tongue, completing his Sacrifice of Judgment. He had gone down, and the fall had broken him.
What does a traveler do when his mind is clear, his destination fixed, but the voice that connects him to the world of men is forever shattered?
