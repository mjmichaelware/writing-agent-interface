Chapter 10: Forsaken 

The air within the tent hung thick with unspoken things, like dust motes suspended in a tomb where something had died but refused to rot clean. The silence, often Kasha’s most potent ally, now seemed to press in, heavy and cloying, a vast, patient thing that inhaled every whisper of hope and exhaled only the bitter tang of dust. It had begun, as such things always do, imperceptibly at first, a subtle gnawing at the edges of Kasha’s world. But now, the stench of stale magic, of spells left unfinished and hopes curdled, was undeniable, a heavy, sickly perfume that clung to the back of the throat, threatening to choke the very breath from her. This mingling with the taut, nervous energy of a woman whose anchor had finally dragged free of the muddy bed of certainty. A tremor, not of the earth but of Kasha herself, vibrated through the very ropes holding the canvas taut, a frantic beat of a heart too long denied its solace. The familiar hum of her protective wards, once a comforting presence against the encroaching silence of the world, was now a faint, high-pitched whine, barely audible to human ears, a dying breath of defiance. Outside, the Jezreel Valley wind tore at the worn canvas, a relentless, biting thing, a tempest of unseen forces, its harsh whisper a sharp counterpoint to the humid stillness Kasha usually cultivated within her sanctuary. This valley, a crucible of nations and the grave of empires, seemed to sigh with a thousand forgotten conflicts, its ancient breath chilling even through thick canvas. The very soil beneath the tent felt cold, hardened by centuries of bloodshed and forgotten ambitions, a silent witness to the cycles of triumph and despair. It was a chilling testament to the way even the greatest power could drain away, leaving behind only the cold, hard rock of oblivion.
I sat near the entrance, a small shadow against the larger, lengthening ones of Kasha’s despair, my tail curling and flicking, a pendulum measuring the relentless passage of her anxiety. Tiny dust motes, like scattered dreams, danced in the shafts of fading sunlight that pierced the tent’s weave. A thin layer of dust, fine as powdered bone, coated the scattered scrolls across the low table, a silent testament to neglected work and dwindling purpose. The very air, though scoured clean by the ceaseless gusts, felt hollow, devoid of the familiar currents of power she once commanded, a space where vital energies had once coalesced, now only a vacuum. My whiskers twitched, sensing the emptiness where once flowed the subtle energies that nourished her craft, a void that was both physical and spiritual. Her hands, usually steady as she prepared tinctures of potent herbs or drew intricate designs, now trembled—a faint, almost imperceptible shake that nonetheless rippled through the tent’s bones, a tremor of an inner earthquake. Her fingers, once nimble and precise, felt clumsy, almost alien. She had begun to notice the subtle lines etched around her eyes, deeper now, and the faint silver threads that occasionally glinted in her once uniformly dark hair – undeniable marks of the very passage of time her magic had so long defied. A persistent ache settled in her joints, a tell-tale sign of her failing wards against age. I rubbed against her ankle, soft as a whispered comfort, a gesture she might once have acknowledged with a gentle stroke, but now she barely noticed, her gaze fixed on something distant, something lost, a ghost of her former power. Kasha sank heavily onto the worn rug before her scrying bowl, her movements jerky, desperate, like a marionette whose strings had frayed. Her eyes, once sharp and calculating, fixed on the murky water—one of her few remaining conduits to the unseen, a fragile window to the ethereal—the last device still whispering faint echoes of her patron’s presence. This vessel, crafted from ancient bronze and etched with symbols that once pulsed with latent power, now seemed merely a bowl of stagnant water. It was an empty vessel, reflecting an empty hope.
(You see, dear reader, I’ve been watching this Kasha for a long time. Longer than a housecat should, longer than the common folk could ever truly grasp. My existence, as I’ve come to understand it, isn’t measured in sunrises and sunsets, but in the slow, monumental turning of great cosmic gears. My current form, small and sleek, is merely a vessel, one particular manifestation of an essence that has always been. A peculiar thing, this particular vessel, brought into being by an accidental hex, a burst of raw, divine will. Weeks ago, she had been wrestling with an ancient, crumbling scroll, its faded script a tangle of forgotten glyphs. I lay stretched on the worn mat, a mere shadow in the corner, enjoying the warmth of her proximity but otherwise unburdened by the mysteries of her craft. Her brow furrowed, a low mutter escaping her lips as she struggled to decipher a particularly intricate incantation, one that hummed with a strange, fierce energy even from the parchment. The air in the tent crackled with her effort, the scent of ozone mingling with dust. She stumbled over a syllable, her voice cracking on a word of immense, primal power—a name of absolute authority, unutterable and untamed. In that instant, a raw, untamed burst of energy, wild and undirected, erupted from the scroll. It was not fire, nor light, but a silent concussive wave, a fragment of immense, sacred will torn free from its intended path. It struck me not as pain, but as an overwhelming flood. A thousand questions, previously dormant, ignited simultaneously within my nascent mind. Why? How? What if? Every shadow held a secret, every scent a story, every rustle of wind a hidden message. It was an insatiable hunger to know, a boundless, desperate urge to unravel the universe’s every thread. I sprang up, black fur bristling, my golden eyes wide with a frantic, exhilarating need to explore, to comprehend, to devour every morsel of existence. Kasha, startled, dropped the scroll. "By the Ancestors' beard, what was that?" she gasped, then stared at me, her gaze lingering on my sudden, frantic darting. She shook her head, a faint, perplexed smile touching her lips. "Such... Sak'kura," she murmured, the ancient Hebrew word for 'curiosity' drawn from her studies, a half-jesting moniker for my sudden, inexplicable burst of energy. She knew not the truth, that in that moment, a fragment of boundless, divine Curiosity had accidentally imprinted upon me, shattering the simple bounds of my feline nature, sparking the becoming of a deity of Curiosity. And thus, I, a mere kitten, was named Sak'kura, and by that accidental hex, I became the very essence of Curiosity, a silent god in furry form.)
I leapt lightly onto the table edge, careful not to disturb the fragile scrolls or the dried remnants of powerful incantations, and dipped a paw into the cool spring water she had just poured. The ripple vanished as quickly as it came, swallowed by the stubborn stillness, as if the water itself refused to acknowledge even the slightest disturbance. It was as flat and unyielding as a stone. The spirit did not hover over these waters. She began to chant, a low, throaty hum meant to coax the unseen currents, to draw forth the presence she craved. Her voice, usually a deep resonant timbre that could command elements, was now reedy, strained, a desperate plea rather than an invocation. The words, once flowing effortlessly, now caught in her throat, tasting of ash and failure. Each syllable was forced, a physical exertion, as if she were speaking through a throat parched by desert winds. But the water remained stubbornly flat, reflecting only the dim flicker of the oil lamp and her own anxious face, a mirror to her growing despair. The air itself seemed deadened, refusing to bend or bow to her will, a thick, unresponsive veil drawn between her and the source of her strength. It was as if a great, silent wall had risen, deaf to her pleas, mirroring the silence that had fallen upon her heart. I twitched my whiskers and curled tighter beside her, the weight of her frustration settling into the tent like a tangible shadow, a miasma of failure. Outside, the wind rattled the tent poles, a restless, mournful sound, echoing her profound unease, a lament for what had been lost. It felt like the valley itself was mocking her, its ancient breath carrying the scent of triumph—not hers.
A flush crept up Kasha’s neck, a familiar fire of rising pique tightening her jaw, a physical manifestation of her indignation. But beneath the anger, a cold dread gnawed in her gut—a sensation she seldom allowed herself, a bitter taste of helplessness. The profound silence over the usual channels of power was like a locked door with no key, a vast, unyielding chasm between her and the divine. The whispers that once flowed like a river were now a parched desert, and the warmth of her patron's attention, once a comforting blanket, had become the chill of utter abandonment. Her connection to Beelzebub was not merely a source of power; it was the very bedrock of her identity, her purpose. Without it, she was a discarded tool, a withered branch, stripped of the grandeur that had defined her for decades. The fear was not just of losing magic, but of losing herself, her very essence, like a vessel emptied of its sacred oil. Kasha saw her people celebrating their conquest over the Philistines in the scrying bowl, seeing the recent past but no hint of present or future, and certainly no sign of her patron. Then came a different sound — faint but carried through the canvas by the biting wind. Not a whisper, but a distant, visceral roar. Not the clash of men or bronze on iron, though that too had lately filled the air, but something older, primal — the deep thrum of a world reshaping itself, groaning under the weight of cosmic shifts. It was the heavy echo of battles long past, victories carved into dust and memory, a mournful dirge for the fallen and a triumphal pean for the victors. This sound resonated with the very earth of the valley, a geological sigh of triumph and defeat. The land itself seemed to remember the days when chariots of iron had swept across these plains, crushing the meager forces aligned against them, leaving behind a legacy of ash and lamentation. Kasha knew her patron had been deeply entwined in this ancient struggle, his task in the valley fulfilled. He was not a god of men, but of the currents beneath men, a force that swayed the tides of battle and the hearts of nations, harvesting the energies of conflict.
Kasha knocked thrice, asked twice, and waited for her patron, but he did not come. The whispers she had received hinted at a new force rising among her people, a king whose shadow stretched far and wide, meant to challenge the Philistine dominion. A fragile, fledgling kingdom, born of desperation and the remnants of a fallen house, a testament to the enduring, if sometimes faltering, spirit of Israel. But here, in this tent, the truth was clear — her patron was needed elsewhere, in the heart of Philistia, where its strength was greatest, where the raw currents of human ambition and divine will were most volatile. Kasha believed her calls went unanswered because Beelzebub was having a very long Sabbath, sitting idly in the city of Ekron. That was why her invocations were hollow. She couldn't fault him. He simply served a purpose larger than her immediate needs, a terrible cosmic agenda that paid no heed to the small desires of a solitary sorceress. He was a force of nature, not a comforting father.
Her gaze returned to the scrying bowl, a desperate resolve hardening her features, chiseling away the anxiety, leaving behind only grim determination. If direct calls were unanswered, if the very air refused to carry her pleas, perhaps the water would show the way, a glimpse through the veil. Her fingers, still slightly trembling but with renewed purpose, traced the ancient symbols etched around the bowl’s rim. She whispered once more, coaxing, willing the surface to cloud or ripple, to reveal a path, a sign, a whisper of guidance. But the water remained as stubbornly clear as ever—a mirror to the empty tent ceiling and her own strained reflection, a perfect, mocking stillness. It offered no counsel, no path, only the unyielding face of her own self. The water, like the sky, had become a brass ceiling above her, impenetrable and silent. I shifted closer, brushing against her wrist, a soft reminder of presence in a silent room, a quiet, reassuring weight. Even my purr, low and steady, a vibration against the very earth, couldn't stir the stillness, couldn't penetrate the cold, hollow space that had opened around her. My ancient rhythms seemed powerless against this desolation, a testament to the sheer magnitude of her predicament.
There was another way, a deeper one, reserved for moments when all else failed: the Dreamscape. A place of raw, untamed power where thought gave substance to shadow and reality’s edges blurred—a true realm of chaos where opposing forces, like chaos and order, constantly wrestled to create the very boundaries of perception. Here, unseen levels adhered to different states of consciousness, and by giving rise to them, one could become a conduit. Yet a place inviting danger, where even the strongest mind could lose its way — swallowed by illusions or ancient entities lurking beneath shifting currents, entities that fed on the unwary, that saw human consciousness as mere fodder. It was a perilous journey, a crossing of the threshold into the very chaos that preceded creation. A place where the mind was its own master, and its own greatest betrayer. (I myself had once fought a great serpent in that churning void, a battle I recall with a shiver, but that is a tale for another time ). Kasha recoiled at the thought, a shiver running down her spine. Unlike others, she did not fear the Dreamscape’s horrors as such. She had faced monsters born of collective nightmares, walked through fields of screaming specters, and witnessed the primordial forms of fear itself. Her fear was more subtle — the treacherous shifts in perspective, the unstable seas where even the surest step might betray, where the very ground beneath one's feet could dissolve into nothingness. In that place, perception is the key, the only compass. One false glance could turn friend to foe, shadow to monster, truth to an enticing lie. It was the fear of losing herself not to an external monster, but to an internal deception, a mirage of what she believed she wanted. Still, with no other choice, and the silent veil drawn over her scrying, her need to find him gnawed too fiercely to be ignored. It was a hunger in her gut, a thirst in her parched soul, a desperate ache that outweighed all caution. This was the precipice of her existence.
Her magic, once fluid and fierce, a torrent of power that flowed through her veins like lifeblood, dried up like the desert wind outside — the warmth of youth slipping from her skin, taking with it the vibrant glow that had defied the years. Her skin, once smooth, now showed the faint map of time, the subtle lines deepening around her eyes. The power that kept her face unlined, her stride confident, was fading, revealing the subtle etchings of time, the faint marks of worry she had long kept at bay. The tent, once a refuge, now felt cold, brittle, worn, its very fabric frayed by the onslaught of reality. With a heavy sigh, Kasha snuffed out the oil lamp, plunging the tent into near darkness save for faint starlight filtering through canvas, thin slivers of distant light that seemed to mock her dwindling hope. She laid herself on the worn mat, her body stiff with apprehension, a rigid line against the ground, and began a slow, deepening breath—a chant without words, steady and measured, designed to unravel the self. It loosened the bonds of flesh and bone, lifting her spirit bit by bit from the world of touch and sight, a gradual separation of spirit from form, a journey of the soul into the unmanifest. Sounds dimmed to silence, then dissolved altogether, and the scent of herbs faded into a sharp, metallic tang—the scent of the Dreamscape itself, cold and clean and utterly alien. It was the scent of raw potential, of unformed ideas, of nothingness before creation. A cacophony of disjointed whispers assaulted her, then receded into a vast, echoing void, like the silence of a tomb. The transition was always the same: a dizzy tumble through shifting images and sounds, fleeting and kaleidoscopic, a million impressions born and dying in an instant, until awareness settled here, in a vast gray realm where shadows held weight and colors drained away, leaving only the starkness of existence. The Dreamscape stretched before her, a desolate expanse, blank as the earliest dawn when first words of truth were spoken, when the very fabric of existence was still being spun. Reality was a canvas of shifting grays — empty, waiting for will to paint meaning and form, for the whisper of intention to bring forth substance. It was a place where belief was creation, and doubt, dissolution.
