Chapter 9: The Ascent

The air, already thin and sharp at the base of Hermon, bit at Daniel’s lungs with the first step. A faint, earthy scent of damp rock and ancient snow filled his nostrils, a prelude to the climb. The ground, still damp from Rafa’s cleansing tears, felt cold beneath his bare feet, a stark contrast to the warmth of the giant's parting words. He looked up, his single eye taking in the colossal, snow-capped sentinel that pierced the sky like a spear. Its upper reaches were shrouded in a permanent, icy shroud, even in the warmth of the season below, a stark reminder of the heights he sought. "Where the veil is thinnest," his father had whispered. The words echoed, a new mantra, silencing the lingering ache of separation from Rafa, the last true kindness he had known. His walking stick, worn smooth by countless miles, became an extension of his will, his only other possession. He had nothing else. No silver, no sandals, no water container. Only the clothes on his back, the stick, and the resolute purpose in his heart. He was a man stripped bare, like Job, losing not just his possessions, but the very comforts and certainties that tethered others to the earth. He was an empty cup, ready to be filled, with nothing left to lose but the very essence of his striving.
The path was steep, winding like a serpent coiling around the mountain’s flank. Each step was a deliberate act of will, a push against the unseen forces that seemed to cling to the very rock. The air grew colder, thinner, stealing the warmth from his bones. The biting chill of the raw stone seeped into his exposed soles, a relentless ache that climbed his shins, stripping away the last illusion of earthly comfort. He had not gone far when the sky, which had been a clear, indifferent blue, began to bruise. Dark, churning clouds, the color of old lead and bruised fruit, boiled up from the horizon, swallowing the sun whole. A low, guttural growbled in the distance, growing swiftly into a deafening roar that shook the very ground. The air itself felt like a clenched fist, tightening around him, a heavy, suffocating pressure that seemed to squeeze the breath from his lungs. Then, the sky ripped open. Not with rain, but with a torrent of raw, unbridled fury. The first drops struck him like flung pebbles, cold and sharp, quickly escalating into a torrential downpour that seemed to seek to drown him where he stood. The wind, a furious, invisible hand, tore at his cloak, whipping his messy black hair across his face, blinding his single eye. Lightning, jagged as broken bones, ripped across the sky, momentarily illuminating the mountain in blinding flashes, each one followed by a thunderclap that vibrated through his very teeth, rattling his skull. This was no mere storm; the mountain itself shuddered with the untamed roar of Lord Baal, expressing a primal displeasure at Daniel's upward aim, seeking to cast him down with the might of a tempest.
As the storm raged, a suffocating heat, thick and cloying, began to rise from the very stones, clinging to his skin, making each breath a struggle. The air grew heavy, like the oppressive weight of a grave, pressing down on him, stealing his strength, making his limbs feel like lead. A profound lethargy seeped into his bones, making every movement an act of monumental effort. He stumbled, his walking stick slipping on the slick rock, and a wave of nausea washed over him, leaving him clammy and weak. His skin prickled with a phantom fever, chills running through him despite the oppressive heat, and his throat felt raw, as if he had swallowed dust and ashes. The cold of the raw stone against his bare feet intensified, a merciless bite, a constant reminder of his stripped vulnerability. The earth itself seemed to sigh, a deep, ancient exhalation of profound weariness, the very breath of Lord Mot, drawing him into its endless, quiet slumber.
Then came the whispers, slithering from the shadows of the rocks, unseen, unheard by the ear, but felt in the deepest parts of his mind. "Turn back, little one. This path is not for you. This strength you cling to… it is a fragile thing."

"It will break. You are alone. Always alone. What is the point of striving when all will be lost?". A sudden, inexplicable fear bloomed in his chest, cold and sharp, urging him to flee, to abandon the climb, to seek the safety of the lowlands, where other humans huddled for warmth. His Courage, a tight knot in his gut, felt as though it were unraveling, thread by agonizing thread, leaving him exposed and trembling. The very air seemed to thin around his resolve, leaving him vulnerable, a warrior without armor. The ground beneath his bare soles became treacherous. It shifted beneath his weight, sometimes giving a strange, soft give, like stepping onto thick moss, but then turning slick and unstable, as if the very rock had dissolved into a watery mire. A disorienting mist, thick and cold, swirled up from unseen crevices, clinging to his face, making him feel as though he were drowning in air, each breath a struggle against the suffocating dampness. The path ahead seemed to twist and contort, impossibly complex, a labyrinth designed to confuse and deter. His mind, usually sharp and clear, felt clouded, his thoughts scattering like dust in the wind. "This path… it makes no sense. It is ill-designed. You cannot navigate this."

"You are a creature of the earth, like all others, bound to its currents." The mountain itself seemed to writhe beneath him, a living, chaotic entity seeking to throw him from its back, as if the very essence of Lord Yam, the Prince of the Sea, churned the rock to mire.
Through the blurring haze of rain and fatigue, he saw them. Shimmering, ethereal figures, beautiful beyond mortal comprehension, their forms sculpted from moonlight and ripe fields, appeared at the edges of his vision. They danced, their movements fluid and inviting, beckoning him towards soft beds and warm hearths, whispering promises of companionship, of gentle hands and soft breaths. A profound, aching Loneliness bloomed in his chest, a hunger for connection so intense it brought tears to his single eye, mingling with the rain. This, he knew, was the deceptive allure of Lord Astarte, seeking to distract him with the siren call of companionship, twisting his natural need for connection into a torment of absence. Memories of his mother, of the family in Dothan, of the simple warmth of shared bread, flashed before him, twisting into a cruel reminder of the solace he had abandoned. The very air around him seemed to whisper of missed embraces, of empty spaces in his heart. His stomach, a hollow drum, began to gnaw at itself, a sharp, insistent Hunger that intensified with every step. He saw visions of succulent roasted lamb, of fresh bread steaming from the oven, of ripe, bursting figs. The physical craving was overwhelming, a desperate, primal urge to simply stop, to gorge himself, to fill the vast emptiness within, to be rooted to the earth by the simple, undeniable needs of the flesh. The very scent of the barren rock seemed to mock his empty belly, a constant reminder of what the earth withheld.
Then came the fire, not from the sky, but from within, a searing heat that consumed, not stifled. It crawled up his legs, an invisible blaze that scorched his skin, leaving phantom blisters in its wake. He felt a deep, piercing throb in the scar of his missing eye, and a dull, constant ache in his joints, old wounds flaring with new, inexplicable pain. A sense of malevolent eyes, unseen, watchful, seemed to press from the dark recesses of the rocks, judging his every weakness, his every faltering breath. His very flesh seemed to turn against him, a betrayal from within, as if Lord Resheph, the god of plague, sought to consume him from the inside out. Each step was a battle, not just against the mountain, but against unseen hands that sought to pull him down, to break his spirit, to root him firmly in the earth. His strength faltered, his legs trembling, his will a flickering candle flame in a gale. Doubt, cold and sharp, pricked at the edges of his resolve. "Why this suffering? Why this endless striving for a height that offers only cold indifference? Turn back. Find comfort, find ease, find warmth." And from the very rock beneath his bare feet, a deep, resonant hum, a pervasive, crushing weight, seemed to rise, trying to pin him, to absorb him into the ancient, immutable stone. It was the vast, silent authority of the mountain itself, the very power of Lord El, the High God of the Canaanites, demanding he submit, that he cease his upward striving, that he remain bound to their dominion. Above it all, the sun, when it broke through the clouds, offered no solace, only a cold, indifferent glare, a vast, unfeeling eye that seemed to judge his every faltering step, a reminder of the unchanging, unyielding order of the cosmos. He was being tested, like Job, stripped of everything, even the capacity for negative states, burned away by the fires of his trials. The principle of those who have nothing, from whom even more will be taken, was laid bare in his torment, a painful shedding of old flesh, a rebirth in fire.
But then, a pinpoint of light, impossibly pure and unwavering, pierced through the churning darkness above him. It was not the sun, nor a star, but something else, something that called to the deepest part of him, a silent, insistent beckoning. "Aim up," his spirit cried, echoing his mother’s voice from a time long past. "Always up". The dagger in his hand felt warm, a tiny ember of defiance against the overwhelming cold. Its inner light, once a steady hum, now pulsed erratically, a frantic beat against his palm, as if struggling to keep pace with his own diminishing strength, yet still offering a faint, defiant glow. With each agonizing step, the dagger's light dimmed further, its warmth draining away, as if its ancient power was being drawn into his own core, transforming from an external aid to an internal, burning will. He gritted his teeth, digging his bare toes into the treacherous rock, finding purchase where there was none, refusing to yield. His single eye, though blurred by rain and tears, fixed on that distant, unwavering light. He was a small, defiant figure against the colossal wrath of the sky, a fragile vessel battling a cosmic storm. And yet, he held. He was shedding skin, burning away the old, the weak, the fearful, just as the Phoenix rises from ash. Each torment was a cleansing fire, each pain a lesson in the necessity of release.
The ascent became a blur of agony and stubborn refusal. The cold bit into his bare feet, the phantom fevers burned, the loneliness gnawed, the hunger clawed. He stumbled, fell, scraped his knees on the unforgiving rock, but always, always, he pushed himself up. He was a creature of pure, indomitable will, a single thread of defiance against the vast tapestry of opposition. The mountain seemed to stretch endlessly, its summit a cruel, distant promise. He felt a palpable pressure in the air, a silent roar against his very presence, against his daring to aim higher than their domain. He would not break. He would not yield. He was a cup, now empty of all earthly desire, ready to be filled with something boundless, a foundation built not on possessions, but on pure, upward intention. His very existence was becoming a testament to the upward aim, a living prayer against the downward pull.
The Summit
Finally, after what felt like an eternity, the slope began to ease. The wind, though still biting, lost its furious edge. The rain lessened, becoming a fine, cold mist. The oppressive heat lifted, replaced by the crisp, clean air of the heights. He pushed through the last few meters, his legs screaming, his lungs burning, and then, he stood. He stood at the very top of Mount Hermon. The air was thin, almost painfully so, but exhilarating. The ground beneath his feet, now bare rock, was cold, but the chill no longer bit. Snow, ancient and undisturbed, lay in patches in sheltered crevices, a testament to the mountain's enduring height, even in the warmth of the season below. It was feasible, barely, for a human to reach this summit, but only one driven by an unearthly will. The sky above was no longer a tumultuous storm, but a vast, inky canvas, studded with a million indifferent stars, their light sharp and cold. The air was thin, pure, and utterly silent, save for the faint whisper of the wind.
He looked around, and saw them. Not monuments, not altars, but two hundred holes in the ground, perfectly circular, as if something immense had fallen from the heavens and punched through the very rock. They were ancient, scarred, their edges worn smooth by millennia, like the mouths of forgotten wells leading to unimaginable depths. These were the marks of things fallen long ago, beings that had chosen a downward path, leaving these silent, gaping wounds in the earth. The air around them felt heavy, a lingering echo of immense power and profound regret, a testament to the duality of existence, the necessary darkness that defines the light. For even the Source of All, in its infinite goodness, had cast away parts of itself, a painful but necessary shedding, creating the very contrasts that allowed for ascent.
Daniel, his body utterly spent, the giant’s dagger still clutched in his hand, its inner light now a faint, almost imperceptible pulse, stumbled forward. The weight of the world, the battle with the unseen, the sheer, unrelenting effort of the climb, finally claimed him. He fell face down onto the cold, hard rock of the summit, the rod and staff slipping from his grasp to lie beside him, a final, sacrificial act. His breath caught, a ragged gasp that tore at his throat, then hitched, and finally, stilled. His body, once a vessel of fierce will and endurance, lay broken and utterly still, its battered form finally yielding to the profound, irreversible exhaustion. This was not sleep. This was the raw, cold quiet of an ending, a finality that echoed the silent, vast indifference of the stars above. His earthly vessel, its purpose brutally served, was shed, not gracefully, but in a wrenching, agonizing separation, a painful but necessary surrender. The essence that had lived, breathed, and suffered within that fragile form was tearing free, a silent, desperate cry for release from the mortal coil. His right eye, the one that had been injured, remained as a lingering imprint on his ascending consciousness, a reminder of the physical trials he had endured.
The Dreamscape and Divine Becoming
Yet, even as the last warmth fled his fingertips and the chill of the rock claimed him, something else began. His mind, no longer bound by flesh, no longer tethered by pain or breath, was already soaring. It was the Dreamscape, yes, but unlike any he had known, a realm of pure, unadulterated consciousness. There was no effort, no struggle, only an unbearable lightness, a poignant, almost painful freedom. He simply was moving. Up. He shot past the moon, a colossal pearl now, not cold and indifferent, but impossibly luminous, a gentle, knowing presence. He laughed, a soundless, joyful burst echoing through the void, a sound so pure it brought a fresh wave of tears to an unseen eye, tears for the life he was leaving, and for the boundless existence he was entering. He surged past the stars, not distant pinpricks, but vast, welcoming suns, their light warm and inviting, their ancient eyes now smiling at him too, even as their silent vastness spoke of the infinite separation from all that was human. He raced past groups of stars, then past groups of groups of stars, and then past those groups of groups of groups – galaxies, clusters of galaxies, superclusters – leaving the very universe behind, a tiny, receding speck of light in the boundless expanse. He was moving beyond the physical, beyond the known, his essence expanding, becoming one with the infinite, leaving behind every memory, every touch, every human connection, even the pain, in a silent, irreversible farewell.
And as he ascended, something shifted within him. The emptiness left by the pit, the stripping away of his capacity for negative states (cast away out of fear, like a painful shedding of old flesh), had made him a vessel. Now, that vessel was filling, not with anything tangible, but with realms of being, each more real, more vibrant than the last, leading him further up and further in, pulling him away from all he had known. He rose.
Where the sticky miasma of Lust had promised false warmth and delivered emptiness, a crystalline Purity now permeated his very essence. It was a cool, clear current, washing away all stain, leaving behind a profound Fulfillment that sang through his being, needing no outward object, only the quiet knowing of perfect completeness. His essence swelled into currents of Heavenly Abundance, a boundless fount for all, a wellspring of pure, generative energy. This realm shimmered with a soft, inner luminescence, like moonlight filtered through clean water, its air tasting of dew and nascent life, the very breath of a reality more solid and joyous than any he had known, yet also the taste of a world from which he could never return.
Higher still. The grasping void of Gluttony dissolved, and the scorching heat of Anger faded into cool air. An absolute Serenity settled deep within his core, a Cooling Presence washing over him like balm, leaving behind only the most gentle calm, a profound Healing Essence that resonated through him, mending every unseen wound. Then, the thorny thicket of Resentment gave way to universal Forbearance, unfurling a seamless Connection to all things that bound him in a silken embrace, a web of gentle understanding. His spirit flowed with a Smoothness that knew no friction, no sharp edge. This plane was a tapestry of interwoven light, where every thread, every being, pulsed with an undeniable, gentle harmony, leading him deeper into the heart of things, away from the discord he had known.
He passed the ragged edges of Despair and the heavy sorrow, those buried stones of Sadness. Luminous Hope pulsed within him, an unyielding current, a vibrant, living flame that could not be extinguished. His will, once fractured, was utterly Resilient, a core of Wholeness that sang with an undiminished vibrance, unbreakable and eternal. An effervescent Joy now danced through him, making him weightless, a lightness that bordered on pure exhilaration, a silent, internal peal of laughter. His spirit soared freely, Unburdened, a laughter echoing through the silent expanse. This realm hummed with a quiet, vibrant energy, like a thousand tiny bells chiming in perfect unison, each sound a testament to endless possibility, a boundless, sun-drenched meadow of pure light where unseen currents of delight lifted him higher and higher, always further in, leaving behind the weight of all earthly tears.

The bitter cloud of Envy dissolved, and the gaping voids left by failed Courage filled with a true, unwavering light. Pure Admiration for the beauty of all creation filled him, a deep Contentment resonating through his boundless spirit, a Shared Joy expanding his presence infinitely. Unwavering Courage pulsed within him, an infinite wellspring of Boundless Strength, a fearless embrace of the unknown, for fear had no place in this truer world. This realm was a symphony of light and color, where every hue and every form sang a silent song of interconnected wonder, a vast, open sky where winds of pure resolve propelled him onward, and distant, towering peaks of light beckoned, drawing him ever more deeply into the heart of reality, a reality where human frailty was but a forgotten whisper.
The yearning of the things below him, the cries for help, the silent prayers – they began to resonate within him, not as external sounds, but as the very voice of his own mind. All he heard now was a vast, incessant buzzing, a shimmering, distorted haze that accompanied the cacophony of longing and sorrow, the myriad voices of others, cleaning out the heavens. These were the "Flies," the lingering echoes of earthly torment, which he now transcended, not by force, but by a simple, profound shift in his perception, their clamor receding as he ascended, unable to cling to his rising essence.
The Vision of Pride and Potential
At his feet, directly beneath him, he saw the true form of Pride. It was not a grand, imposing entity, but a vast, consuming nothingness. Not the absence of something, but a nothing that believed it was the only thing, the sum total of existence. It was like a black canvas, a void that was not truly empty, but a space that had been so consumed by self-worship that it had devoured everything else, becoming the sole reality. It was the terrifying belief that one was the only thing that truly existed in the universe, the only thing that was real. This great nothingness, which believed it was everything and deserved everything, was tugging downwards, seeking to devour him, to pull him back into its self-centered oblivion.
Yet, above him, in the space between him and the ultimate light, he saw a mirror. This was not a mirror of his past self, of Daniel, the boy from Hebron, but a reflection of his potential. It showed him not what he was, but what he could be, a boundless manifestation of all his unfulfilled possibilities, now illuminated and brought into being by his ascent. He gazed into it, a sense of immense inspiration washing over him, a profound recognition of the infinite paths stretching before him. He reached out, not with a physical hand, but with the expanding tendrils of his consciousness, to embrace this vision of his boundless self, and in that embrace, he felt a deep, resonant peace, a profound sense of coming home to a self he had always been meant to be. A silent, internal chime echoed through his being as his potential shimmered and merged with his consciousness. The light above him was not a distant star, but a vast, luminous presence that began to narrow. It drew in, its edges sharpening, until it resolved into an immense, vertical slit of pure, piercing light. It was an eye, ancient and infinitely curious, its gaze both neutral and profound, like the pupil of a colossal feline, observing his becoming. Daniel felt no judgment from this gaze, only an immense, quiet understanding, a cosmic eye that saw not just his present, but the entirety of his boundless future, acknowledging the path he had walked and the being he was destined to become. It was the eye of the cosmos, a silent witness to all becoming, and in its depths, he saw a reflection of the ultimate purpose, not his own, but the grand design itself, a design that hummed with a silent, profound wisdom that settled into his very core, a knowing beyond thought, a comforting truth that resonated with the deepest parts of his being.
He felt an immense, overwhelming love bloom in his heart, a love for everything beneath him, even the dead courage and tainted hope, the gnawing envy and burning anger, the bitter resentment that had once clung to him. He understood them now, having met them, having been born of flesh and subject to their pull. He understood the burdens, the failures, the desperate clinging to the earth. Not because he knew every intricate story, for human hearts were too complex for full comprehension – indeed, who could ever truly know another's story, or even their own, in all its tangled complexity? – but because he knew the essence of their struggle, having walked through his own hell. And in that understanding, in that boundless, all-encompassing love, he began to become. It was a silent, internal blossoming, a shift in the very fabric of his being, as if countless tiny threads of light, woven from compassion and understanding, began to knit together, forming something vast and new. And then, with a profound, resonant click that echoed through the boundless void, a sense of perfect alignment and instantaneous transformation, he was.
The Deity of Love
He became a deity of love. And so, Daniel, the boy from Hebron, whose life had been measured in burdens and entanglements, whose journey had been a relentless climb against the very forces of the earth, soared beyond the stars. He was no longer a seeker, but the sought. He was no longer a judge, but the embodiment of understanding. His physical form, broken and abandoned on the cold, hard summit of Hermon, was simply taken, dissolving into the thin mountain air, leaving behind only the profound silence of absence, just as some ancient texts whispered of those who had ascended before him. No one ever saw him again—not his mother, not Rafa, not any soul who had touched his fleeting, earthly life. And the dagger, left behind on the cold, hard summit of Hermon, now utterly devoid of its inner light, lay gleaming faintly in the indifferent starlight, a silent, hollow testament to a journey that had ended not in rest, but in a profound, eternal becoming. Its essence, once contained, had been fully absorbed into Daniel, leaving it a mere shell. It was a becoming bought with the last gasp of his mortal breath, an ascension that shattered the very vessel that had borne him. For his soul, a being not bound by flesh or stone, began to settle. Not into a chair of earth, but into a throne forged of the very fabric of existence: the whisper of the beyond, the ancient thrum of formless essence, the gentle current of pure consciousness, the flickering dance of divine light, the boundless expanse of true void – a void not of emptiness, but of infinite potential, the primordial space from which all creation springs, much like the Source of All that had cast away parts of itself to create existence. This was his seat, a throne where a soul truly sat, not physically, but as the essence of creation, encompassing a love so vast it encompassed every broken thing below, a love born of the ultimate sacrifice, of an ending that was also an agonizing, beautiful, eternal beginning. He felt its substance beneath him, not hard or soft, but a vibrant resonance, a hum of pure being, a constant, gentle vibration that was the very pulse of the cosmos.
In this moment of becoming, Daniel realized he was not the God, but a god, one among many who had ascended. He now understood that even in this divine state, everyone must still look up and seek guidance. The two hundred holes on Hermon's summit, the marks of those who had fallen, echoed in his mind – a stark reminder that even gods, if they ceased their upward gaze, if they believed themselves the sole source of truth, could falter, adrift in their own power, consumed by the very nothingness of Pride, their light dimming into a cold, indifferent void, their once vibrant essence twisting into a torment of isolation, much like the torments he had faced on the mountain, but eternal.
