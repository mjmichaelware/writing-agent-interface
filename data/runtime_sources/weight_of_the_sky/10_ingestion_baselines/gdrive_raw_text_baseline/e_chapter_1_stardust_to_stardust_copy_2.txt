Chapter 1: Stardust to Stardust

The trembling torch clutched in the boy's hand scattered dull copper light across the corridor. The frail flame fought against the invading dark. It cast suspicious, jittering shadows across the narrow hallway. The small fire shivered, not from wind, but from the beat of his own muffled heart: a rough, offbeat thrum against the overwhelming mass of a condemned dwelling.

Each calculated, barefoot step on the unforgiving limestone was a wager of his own mass against the increasing weight of the house. The slightest change in pressure risked betrayal: a metallic sound, a dry scrape, any clatter that might announce his forbidden errand. This was not mere silence. The atmosphere felt like the still patience of serpents in a garden. The suffocating emptiness smelled of sulfur and expectant soil. His father's closed-off room held the precise, dead air of an opened tomb: the stagnant, sharp scent of a destiny that had been rigorously refused. This was no home. It was the heavy, unmoving lair of a man committed to the earth's sheer pull. Thus, the boy's every disciplined movement toward his father's room was an act of necessary heresy.

The night carried sounds. Crickets pulsed their urgent chirping in the darkness. The wind sighed through brittle grass like a thousand whispers. A distant dog barked, cutting through the stillness. Every sound threatened to expose him. The smaller the sound, the louder it seemed when one tried to be quiet. A twig snapping, a loose stone rolling—they could seem louder than thunder in the stillness, a cruel trick of the ear.

The four-room house had once been warmed by the voice of a mother's songs. It had since ceased to be a simple structure. It had spread into ruin, a physical testament to Father Aviel's total, deliberate withdrawal of will. The dissolution began the moment of Mother Zuna's disappearance. Aviel had fled Philistia seven years past, bringing his gods from the Western plains to Hebron. After Zuna's death, Dagon became his only anchor. The man created a vile, intricate shrine built of forgotten things. It was consecrated by a scholar who willingly surrendered his life's momentum to the earth's claim. This divided house was cold, but with the specific, internal chill of a place the sun had never reached. The air itself was a heavy, saturated cloak. Every inhalation was an exhausting chore. The entire structure pressed inward, a manifestation of the collected mass of his self-pity. It sought to crush its inhabitants back into formless dust. The ceiling beams sagged not from age, but from the sheer, internal volume of the man's detachment.

Navigating its turns was not simple walking. One was forced to pick a deliberate path through the snare of matter. He felt its terrible, rooting pull with every step, like wading through sweet honey. Piles of broken pottery shards and failed instruments loomed like small, silent monuments to futures that died stillborn. Ruined brass astrolabes lay scattered. Cracked wooden compasses gathered dust. Rotting textiles—Mother Zuna's sacred threads left mid-shuttle—had unraveled into mildew and waste. The decaying fabrics sloughed off the looms, draping over discarded, brittle animal bones. They made a horrifyingly dry rustle: the sound of all life surrendering to the material. Rotting textiles teemed with maggots, their pale bodies writhing in the fetid cloth. The stench—desiccated herbs mixed with excrement—stung his nostrils. Swarms of flies rose from mounds of decaying food. Their buzzing was a morbid symphony, a living cloud over the corruption.

The failed records lay everywhere. Fragments of parchment and crumbling wax tablets, once holding records of birth, debt, and lineage, were scattered beneath the debris. They had been stripped clean of meaning by unseen, hungry mold. Withered sandals and rusted bronze tools lay scattered like the cast-off implements of a defeated messenger. Desiccated fruits, once heavy with the promise of life, now clung to their seeds like mummified burdens. They were stark testament to a harvest perpetually withheld. The water skins were brittle and cracked. They smelled of stale leather and the sharp mineral tang of absolute dryness, mocking the very concept of living clarity. Even the well, the house's source, had forgotten how to hold water. It was a hollow stone throat perpetually dry.

Dan, the boy with kind eyes and dark hair, picked his way through the refuse. He was sixteen. For seven years, he had watched helplessly as Father Aviel lost himself. A bone-deep chill seized his spine, born not just of the filth, but from the corrupt heart of a man who saw treasure in rot and comfort in self-pity. Father Aviel had been a scholar whose mind was sharp as flint. He had traded that bright, clean currency for the slow drag of decay and the sticky joy of letting go. He had ceased the daily rites of cleaning and maintenance: the small, repetitive acts that hold the chaos of the world at bay. This deliberate neglect of order, this surrender to the downward claim, was the true horror. Dan had to leave this place of reckoning before the decay claimed him, too.

"Before it swallows me whole," Dan muttered under his breath, his voice inaudible.

He paused by the hearth in the main room. The glowing coals pulsed like an exhausted red heart in the blackness. His anchor, the Zuna stone, was cool and perfect against his ribs—the only simple thing he owned in a house devoted to the horrifying, beautiful density of the messy. He thumbed the smooth surface, a silent reminder of Hope, a single good tethering left behind when the worst of the world had escaped its confines. The stone's absolute coolness, a ghost of Zuna's hand, carried the faint, sharp metallic tang that marked the source—the unmanifest itself. It was a fragment of the high, white cold fire, a frozen echo of Mother's songs.

Dan crept to Aviel's doorframe and listened. A low, ragged snoring issued from within. The sound was of a man trying to outrun his own breath. The boy held his breath, eliminating the friction of his own presence. With a quiet snuff, he deliberately extinguished the torch. He plunged the area into deep, drowning darkness. He relied only on the sharp memory of light and the faint moonlight filtering through a narrow window slit in the hallway wall. The pungent smell of burnt wick lingered: a bitter tang of parting, the first small expenditure of the night. The ache in Dan's chest was a fixed pressure. He remembered his father's final, damning dismissal from three nights past, a statement of total surrender to matter:

"If you're still searching for your source, boy," Aviel had hissed, his voice heavy with the sluggish drag of his own self-deception, "then pick up a stone and give it a name! Only a fool seeks answers from a power he cannot touch."

The words were bitter acid. Dan refused the stone, and the heavy earth it represented. A question burned in Dan's mind. If gods could be made of rocks, formed by the sheer weight of a man's attention and despair, then where should a searching heart like his truly turn? Dan's eyes often searched upward, as if answers resided there, in the vast, swirling tapestry above. His dreams had always taken him to the high places: not fleeting images, but exercises of will where he flew or climbed endless stairs, a constant preparation for the ascent.

"Mother said we're from the stars," he whispered to the darkness, a cool stone at his chest. "If that's a lie, then let me be a fool."

But this night's work wasn't for his own dreams. Though he was entering the Dreamscape—the sleeping mind of the world, a boundless place where thought was substance. It was the means he needed to access Aviel's mind. Dan knelt by his father's cot. The thin, crystalline cold of the floor immediately seized his bare knees. He slowed his breath, silencing the frantic voices in his mind as his Zuna had taught him. She had been a dreamwalker too, both blessed and cursed, for some feared her kind and whispered of witches who consorted with cats and commanded elements.

He remembered her hands: "Forget yesterday, forget tomorrow, forget now, just be. Empty the vessel," she would say, her voice echoing in his memory, "before you can fill it with the light." 

He forced his mind to empty. He chased the last fragments of earthly attachment away. He let go of the phantom scent of Zuna's memory and the comforting shadow of Aviel's history. The pressure of leaving the familiar was a sharp agony, a deep shedding. The stone beneath his knees dissolved. He waited until the final, cold friction of matter was gone. In that terrifying, perfect stillness—that necessary, terrible emptiness—he stepped sideways and into the Dreamscape.

The immediate change was the profound, aching silence. He stood at the shimmering border of his father's sleep, a wall dense with years of self-deception that Dan could only absorb and pass through. Drawing a breath of pure intent, his fixed resolve pushed against the barrier. He hit the solid wall of shattered memories and black regret. For a terrifying, suspended breath, he was crushed under a sudden volume of pure sound: a child screaming, a hand slipping, a city's final death rattle. This rush of paralyzing grief—heavy with the stench of rot—was his father's internal debt. He channeled his will, demanding passage. The boundary gave way with a painful, inward implosion of pressure. He was released into a featureless field of raw material.

Aviel stood alone in the center, waiting to dream. He was a solitary figure. Dust swirled around him like a shroud. Not the coarse dust of the earth, but something finer, eternal—the raw material of creation itself. His formless void teemed with potential, as things were before Time when the first words of truth were spoken. At Dan's will, a surge of power flowed through him. It was primal and thrilling as a lightning strike. It woke forgotten currents in his blood. It made his spirit hum with profound, almost terrifying energy. Dan exhaled, a soundless act of command, a precise manipulation of the unseen ether:

"Let it rain."

The creation was instantaneous and corrupt, an accelerated genesis toward a state of total collapse. Glittering motes rained down. They knit together, hardening, forming smooth pebbles, then the heavy, crushing mass of boulders. It was the unnatural accumulation of matter, a devastating creation built upon the lie of rest. The dust, stirred by an unseen hand, began to dance. It coiled like living smoke, thick as morning mist, a swirling vortex of creation. It coalesced, thickening, darkening, taking on substance with a soft, ethereal sigh of transformation. By Dan's will and word, Aviel saw only treasure. His hands grasped at gold, copper, and fine silver. The noise of his hidden greed was a soundless scream in the dream. He shoved them into his pockets and then beneath his clothing, whispering, "Mine," hoarding his own delusion. He gathered the ever-burgeoning accumulation until his very form was distended, crushed. He became a lonesome pillar of stone in a barren field, the mass of the precious metal squeezing the breath from his lungs. The father's eyes glazed over. His mouth opened in a soundless gasp. The suffocating density of his possessions silenced him, permanently locking his hands in the posture of hoarding.

Then, with a final, deliberate breath that was both a sigh and a judgment, Dan summoned a wind. It was a cold, scouring current of pure intent. The shaping left him hollow, leaving a sharp ache behind his ribs. A sudden, cold draft rushed into his own lungs to replace the borrowed space. The wind swept across the field, gathering the grit and its crushing creations, carrying everything back to the void. In that brief moment of unburdening, the father looked up. He saw a single star pierce the gray, impossibly bright. But even there, in the star's radiant core, a colder, absolute light struggled to be born: a subtle, divine resentment prefiguring the final, eternal measure. Dan poured his final, potent intent into that fleeting vision: "I must depart this house. You must prepare your heart." He held the image of the star, imprinting it upon his father's mind, a non-material exertion of vast effort. Then, gently, with a deep breath, he released his father's consciousness. He let him drift in the gentle rhythm of sleep. Aviel's mind would remain dreamless for the rest of the night, a silent respite from the chaos.

The stench hit him first—rot, waste, surrender's sweet perfume—before the cold floor reregistered beneath his feet. Drained but with renewed sense of purpose, a purpose that was now a raw wound, Dan left his father's room. He sat up, gasping for breath as a physical ache settled in his bones. The blackness of the hallway was a sudden, heavy, oppressive presence. He felt smaller now. His resolve was spent in the collapse of his posture. He knelt by the glowing coals in the hearth. He extended an unsteady hand. He coaxed a flame from the embers with a small piece of kindling. The flame caught with a quiet hiss. He lit his torch. Its light was a familiar, hopeful presence in the gloom. With new, resolute purpose, he left the main room.

"It's done," he whispered at a doorframe as he passed. His legs were heavy as stone.

Back in his room, the door closed behind him. Dan's small room was a meticulously tidy corner of perfect order, a hard-won proof that he had put his own house in order before daring to criticize the world. This was his sanctuary, his final act of self-possession. His body was a dull, aching mass after the dream walk. He crumpled onto the cot. He allowed the simple linen and the welcoming darkness to pull him toward oblivion. Yet, even as the darkness claimed him and the torch light's final flicker, a chilling dread coiled around his fading awareness. Dan's sleep was no more rest than Aviel's.

He found himself standing on a shore of glittering, black sand. The water was unnaturally calm, a sheet of polished glass that promised horizontal peace. He disposed of a small boat at the shoreline with his will. He stepped out onto the water. It held him. He savored the impossible ease of his stride. He was halfway across the cove when the glass shattered with a deafening crack. It pulled the false foundation out from under him. The peaceful blue instantly bled into an angry, bruised purple. The sea didn't merely roar. It was a terrifying churn of water and raw earth, a maelstrom of pure, liquid rock and bitter brine. Suddenly, he was clinging to a single, inadequate plank of wood. It was his only security against the colossal forces. Massive, dark waves crashed over him. The cold stench of the deep blue clung to white foam. The sky, a suffocating, churning blanket of obsidian blackness, offered no escape. It offered only the inverse reflection of the water's depths. His vision fractured into a kaleidoscope of blue, black, and white swirling endlessly, threatening to consume him. Then, from the very heart of the storm, from the impossible depths, it rose.
