Here is a short, bullet-point rundown of my book including the first chapter:
Genre: Mythological/Biblical Fantasy with allegorical and philosophical elements.
Narrator: Sak, a timeless cat deity (transformed in Chapter 7 by a curse in Yahweh's name) who narrates out of curiosity not just to help the reader understand, but with hopes of receiving feedback so that he can understand better.
Part 1 (Ch 1-6): Focuses on Dan, a young man in ancient Hebron, escaping his father's decay and worship of Dagon. Dan seeks a higher path ("aiming up"), eventually dying and ascending to the Dreamscape as a deity of love (Ch 6).
Part 2 (Ch 7-12): Introduces Kasha, a witch from Jezreel Valley, who eventually aligns with Beelzebub (associated with "Time"), becoming his follower ("going down").
Part 3 (Ch 13-19): Features Isabel, the daughter of Beelzebub and Kasha, born from Beelzebub's desire for control. Isabel represents "hell" but yearns to "go up." Chapters 13-14 detail her struggles and attempts to escape, including subtle references to eating disorders.
Climax/Resolution Setup (Ch 14-15): Isabel forgives her father (Ch 14), leading to her death but allowing her spirit to depart and ascend to the Dreamscape (Ch 15) as a deity of forgiveness.
Meeting in Dreamscape (Ch 15): Dan (deity of love) and Isabel (deity of forgiveness) meet and fall in love in the Dreamscape after their deaths/ascensions.
Themes: Decay vs. Ascent ("aiming up" vs. "going down"), Idolatry, Spiritual Struggle, Nature of Abundance, Hell and Control, Forgiveness, Love, Dreams and the Spiritual Realm, Boundaries between Realms.
References: Layered biblical and ancient Near Eastern mythological figures and concepts (Hebron, Dagon, El, Yahweh, Samson, Beelzebub, dust to dust, light/darkness, sea as chaos, etc.).
Length: Chapter 1 is ~3835 words. Total book (20 chapters) estimated at ~76,700 words (~307 pages).

Chapter 1. Dust to Dust
A torchlight clenched in the boy's hand flickered, casting suspicious shadows around the four-room house as he tiptoed down the dimly lit hallway. The flame danced nervously, a tiny, unreliable beacon in the oppressive gloom. Each step on the cool sunbaked stone conspired against him, for any clank or clatter could undo his desperate mission. A mere whisper of sound, the scrape of a sandal or the shift of a loose stone, and all would be lost. His heart hammered against his ribs, a frantic drumbeat in the suffocating stillness. He had to reach his father. He had to try, just one more time, before the point of no return. And I, having seen countless such moments across the ages, knew the weight of this one.
Now, you might think a house is just a house, made of stone and wood, but sometimes, reader, a house can become something else entirely. It can become a cage, or worse, a tomb for the living. I've seen it happen more times than one can count on the stars, and it's always a sad sight. This hallway, for instance, was a labyrinth of his father's making. Since Mother's death, Father had retreated into a world of worthless possessions, a hoarder worshipping broken memories as if they could fill the void. Each discarded item was a monument to a moment lost, a desperate attempt to hold onto something that had already slipped away. He was building his own prison, brick by dusty brick. Piles of pottery shards, cracked and discolored by years of neglect, crusted with dried filth and the ghostly remnants of meals loomed like ancient cairns. They rose in uneven mounds, treacherous underfoot, whispering of forgotten feasts and shattered lives. Rotting textiles, alive with the squirming of maggots, draped over discarded animal bones, their marrow sucked dry and polished by time and neglect. The air itself felt thick with the dust of decay, clinging to the back of his throat. The stench of desiccated herbs mixed with excrement stung the boy's nostrils, a vile perfume that spoke of decay. It was a smell that coated everything, a constant reminder of the rot that had taken root here. Swarms of flies, their buzzing a morbid symphony, rose from mounds of decaying food. They swirled and buzzed, a living cloud over the corruption, their tiny bodies adding to the general sense of foulness. In the corners, a slick of unidentifiable matter glistened in the torchlight, reflecting the horror of this self-made tomb. This wasn't grief; it was a desecration. It was, if you can imagine it, a slow surrender to the opposite of life. A deliberate turning away from the light and towards the shadow. A downward spiral, if ever I saw one, and Ive seen plenty.
The boy with messy hair and kind eyes picked his way through the refuse, his bare feet touching a fresh altar. It was a small, deliberate construction amidst the chaos, a focal point for his father's twisted de isvotion. A place built for the worship of decay, a sad irony. He saw the stones and pebbles, gathered with such care, the bones arranged like an abandoned offering, the clay tablets whispering forgotten stories. Each element seemed placed with a perverse reverence, a mockery of true worship. He was building something, yes, but building it from ruin. A shiver, sharp as a shard of pottery, pierced him. It wasn't just the filth; it was the wrongness of it all. His father, once a respected scholar, now saw treasure in rot, abundance in decay. He had traded wisdom for waste, knowledge for putrefaction. A cold anger began to simmer beneath the fear. "We're not living," he thought, his breath catching in his throat. "We're dying, slowly, from the inside out." The house itself was a corpse, and they were trapped within its decaying embrace. The name Dagon almost came from him unbidden, a bitter taste on the back of his tongue. That damned god had stolen his father's soul and Time, that insatiable devourer, whom he served bored the blunt of guilt. Time, Dan felt, was not just the passage of moments, but something else entirely, a force he would later understand had a name whispered in shadows, a name like Beelzebub. He knew, he would meet them both if he continued on this path. The path of stagnation, the path of decay. But Dan, even then, had a different path calling to him.
Outside, stars, ancient and indifferent, and a full moon on its third day watched over Hebron. The city, nestled in the Judean Hills, was a fortress of walls and vineyards. From a distance, it looked peaceful, a haven of order against the wild hills. Though echoes from the marketplace and busy fields had ceased long ago, the night was not silent. The crickets' urgent chirping was a lively song, a constant, pulsing rhythm in the darkness, and the wind's mournful sigh through the brittle grass mimicked a thousand whispers. It carried the scent of dry earth and distant dust. Every sound threatened to expose the boy, to drag him back into the quicksand of his father's madness. It is often the small sounds, you see, that carry the greatest danger when you are trying to be quiet. A twig snapping, a loose stone rolling – they can seem louder than thunder in the stillness of the night. Especially when you are trying to escape something that doesn't want to be left behind.
The boy was called Dan, though you wouldn’t understand why until later in the story. Sometimes names have meanings that unfold over time or change entirely. Dan's is one of them. He had been waiting for this moment—not because the message he was about to send was an easy thing to say, but because telling it would be the first of many burdens to lift. A weight he had carried for too long, pressing down on his spirit. This time, however, was not so much of his choosing. The last straw had fallen. It had been a small thing, a final act of surrender from his father, but it had broken something vital within Dan. Dan had watched helplessly as Father Aviel lost himself, while his only son had 17 years and worked to find himself. Seventeen years of navigating the growing darkness, of trying to hold onto the light within. It was time to leave this infected home before he, too, was consumed. A difficult decision for any young person, especially when it involves leaving behind the only parent you have left. But sometimes, staying is more dangerous than leaving. Sometimes, the greatest act of love is to seek your own survival.
He paused outside Aviel's room to ensure his father was asleep. He held his breath, listening intently. Indeed, deep sleep filled the room with the soft, rhythmic symphony of slow, whistling breaths and the occasional, low rumble of a snore. Sounds of a body at rest, but a mind lost in its own wilderness. The ache in Dan's chest was not new, but it had grown heavier in recent days. A dull, persistent throb of sorrow and frustration. He remembered what his father had once told him during a heated disagreement about gods and idols: “My son, if you are still looking for a god, then pick up a rock and make it so!”
That thought followed Dan. If gods could be made of rocks simply by sacrificing attention and care, then where should that attention be directed? Dan's eyes often searched upward as if to find an answer there. He wasnt just looking at the sky; he was looking through it. He looked past the stars, knowing long before the rest that humans were made from them, but from where did the stars come? This is a question that has occupied many minds, both mortal and otherwise, over the millennia. Dreams had often pulled him upward—climbing stairs, leaping, floating, sometimes flying. Each time, he woke with a strange longing. A yearning for altitude, for perspective, for a place where the air was clear and the decay could not reach. If from dust humans came and to dust they return, then perhaps from stardust they came, and to the stars they must return (or wherever it was they came from). He looked up at them now, through a narrow slit in the hallway wall, and they looked back, waiting patiently. That was where he would direct his attention. That was where he would go. It is a strange thing, isn't it, this pull towards something unseen, something higher than ourselves? A call to the infinite, when the finite world feels suffocating. A desire to aim up, towards the highest possible good, even if that good is not yet fully understood.
You, gentle reader, would do well to heed your own dreams, especially those that carry you upward. They are not mere phantoms of the night, but echoes of a deeper truth, a call to ascend. Dan knew this. Its why he often stared up into the sky. He saw it not just as a ceiling, but as a floor to another realm. He was seeking the highest point, the ultimate peak, even if he didn't have a name for it yet.
Dan knelt, becoming one with the cold stone. He felt the ancient chill seep into his bare knees, grounding him. He slowed his breath, silencing the frantic voices in his mind just as his mother and nurturer had taught him. “Forget yesterday, forget tomorrow, forget now, just be. Empty the vessel,” she would say, “before you can fill it with the light.” His mother Zuna’s words, whispered from a place beyond the veil, guided him. A dreamwalker, she had been both blessed and cursed, for some feared her kind and sought to prosecute, whispering of witches who consorted with cats and commanded elements, words Dan dismissed as fearful ignorance. Witches and cats, a curious pairing, wouldn't you agree? Especially when the cat might have a few secrets of his own. But we will get to that later. 
Zuna had also spoken of the heavens, of journeys beyond the mortal coil. Dan would find her talking to the clouds. "Find a still place," she’d say, "be like the wind, ever moving yet rooted to the earth." And in that stillness, he found the still place. He slipped into the Dreamscape, where the spirit reigned, and the flesh held no dominion. This is a skill few possess, and a dangerous one too, as you will soon see. For the Dreamscape has its own inhabitants, and not all of them are welcoming. It is a realm where intentions shape reality, for better or worse.
Inside the dreamscape, he found his father. A pale light revealed an empty field, stretching to the horizon in shades of gray and silver. It was a desolate landscape, devoid of life, a reflection of the emptiness within Aviel. It was a blank canvas, desolate but blooming with potential, as things must have been before Time. Aviel stood alone, dust swirling around him like a shroud. Not the dust of the earth, but something finer, eternal, the raw material of creation. The very stuff of possibility, yet trapped in his father's grasp. He was surrounded by the potential for everything, yet chose to create nothing. At Dan's will, the dust coalesced, and grew into larger things. First motes, then grains, then tiny, recognizable shapes – perhaps fragments of memories, distorted and broken. Sand rained down, then pebbles, then rocks. The weight of them grew, accumulating around Aviel. A sane man would have fled in terror, but Aviel, lost in his delusion, clutched at them, filling them into his hands and into his garments, desperate for possession. He gathered them greedily, mistaking mass for meaning. He was aiming down, you see, towards the accumulation of the meaningless. Only when the weight of his treasures choked him, when he could no longer breathe, did their insignificance become clear. It is a sad thing to see someone weighed down by things that have no true value. A value measured not in worth, but in the burden they impose. A heavy price to pay for worshipping dust.
Then, Dan summoned a wind, a sigh from the void. It swept across the field, carrying the dust and its creations back to nothingness. A gentle, cleansing force, seeking to restore the blank canvas. A breath of the infinite, clearing away the finite clutter. Aviel looked up, and for a fleeting moment, saw a single star pierce the oppressive gray. It pulsed, a faint, insistent heartbeat. A tiny point of light, a reminder of the vastness beyond his self-made prison. A hint of the upward path, even in his darkness.
Dan poured his intent into that vision: The son must depart on his journey, and the father must prepare his heart. A silent message, sent from spirit to spirit. A necessary separation for growth requires space. Then, he released his father's mind, letting him drift in the gentle rhythm of sleep. Aviel's mind would remain dreamless for the rest of the night though the beat of his slumber now made it's way out into the hallway and filled the whole house. The silence in his father's sleeping mind was a fragile peace, hard-won. A temporary reprieve from the weight of his choices.
Weak and drained, Dan retraced his steps through the defiled hallway. The torchlight guttered, casting his slouched shadow in a grotesque parody of his former self. He felt smaller now, the energy of the Dreamscape fading. He moved on tiptoe still, his borrowed confidence spent. Each creak of the floorboards was a jolt. Back in his room, the door closed with a soft click. The small cot offered no comfort, the walls seemed to whisper of decay. Even in his own small, immaculate space, the house's sickness permeated. But sleep came quickly. A desperate, weary sleep.
So did the nightmare. And sometimes, dear reader, the nightmares are more real than the waking world. Sometimes, they are visitations. And this one was a visitor of considerable power.
The sea roared, a maelstrom of chaos. The sound filled his ears, a deafening symphony of destruction. Dan's small boat, splintered and fragile, was tossed like a toy. He was utterly at the mercy of the waves. Waves like mountains crashed around him, a symphony of destruction. The sky, a suffocating blanket of black, offered no escape. Not a single star or moonbeam could pierce the oppressive darkness above. His vision fractured into a kaleidoscope of blue, black, and white—water, darkness, foam. A dizzying, disorienting swirl of chaos. Then, from the heart of the storm, it rose.
At the southern edge of the world, a colossal head emerged from the waves. The face, carved from drowned stone, was ancient and impassive. Barnacles and strange, phosphorescent growths clung to its immense surface. Eyes like the deepest trenches stared out from the stone. Its unblinking eyes, wide as an abyss, locked onto Dans. They held the cold, indifferent gaze of the deep sea. Seaweed draped its chin like a rotting beard, trailing in the churning water, and barnacles clung to its cheeks like grotesque jewels. Dan knew, with a certainty that chilled him to the bone, the purpose of this visitation. For he had just judged a God. And gods, even those bound by ancient laws, do not take kindly to judgment. Especially when that judgment comes from a mere mortal.
In the far north, where the sea turned to ink, a monstrous tail breached the surface. Scaled and shining, it lashed back and forth, each movement a cataclysmic ripple. The sheer power of it sent tremors through the water and through Dan's tiny boat. A force capable of reshaping coastlines with a flick. The body, vast and unknowable, remained submerged, connecting the human head and the serpentine tail into a single, terrifying entity. A creature of the deep, indeed. A being of two worlds, yet fully belonging to neither the land nor the sky Dan yearned for. A deity of the downward pull, of the depths where light does not reach.
The boat tilted, threatening to capsize. The water screamed, a chorus of the damned. It was the sound of a thousand drowning souls, a horrifying lament. And Dan, who had once commanded his dreams, was utterly helpless. It is a fearsome thing to lose control in your own dream, where you thought you were master. To be at the mercy of something far older and more powerful. Something that embodies the very decay he was trying to escape.
The waves towered, blotting out the last vestiges of light. Dan clung to the splintered wood, his heart a trapped bird. Beating wildly against his ribs, desperate for escape. There was nowhere to run. No sky, no land, only the endless, hungry sea and the monstrous god that ruled it. Then, the voice, ancient and vast, echoed not from the monstrous mouth, but from the depths of Dan's own skull. It resonated in his very bones, a sound that was felt as much as heard. A voice that claimed dominion over all that sinks and settles.
“I am Lord Dagon,” the figure boomed, his voice a coiling, inescapable tide. “The god your father has chosen. Lord of abundance. For this I was made, and I have come.”
Dan's fists clenched, the splinters biting into his skin. Pain, a welcome distraction from the terror. “But I did not call you!”
“You have always called, and are always calling. Your people did. Your blood does. But you have sinned this night, daring to undo my work in your father's house.” A cruel smile twisted the stone lips. It was a horrifying sight, stone cracking into something like amusement. “Come, beg for my forgiveness, embrace my abundance, and I shall spare you the inevitable.” He offered a different kind of abundance than the stars promised.
“Abundance of things?” Dan spat, his voice trembling but defiant. “What use are they in the face of death? My people say your temple fell before Samson. Your statue crumbled before Yahweh. Why should I choose you over El, who dwells in the heavens? You are nothing!”
Dagon leaned closer, his shadow swallowing the remnants of the dream. The air grew cold, heavy with his presence. “My temple did not fall because I am weak, boy. My statue did not crumble because I am powerless. There are boundaries, lines that should not be crossed. The realm of the living cannot trespass too far into the realm of the dead. We are bound by different laws. My presence near holiness cracks the illusion of your world. Do not mistake restraint for weakness.” A chilling thought, isn't it, that even gods might be bound by rules we do not fully understand? Rules that govern the very fabric of reality, keeping different realms apart. Rules I myself understand, having seen the consequences of breaking them.
Dan's voice shook, but his gaze did not waver. “So you admit it. You are a mere deity of death. A gatekeeper.” A collector of lost souls and forgotten things. A being who thrives on the downward pull.
“Look around you,” Dagon whispered, his voice a seductive caress. “Look at the war within you. Look at the wars outside. Death is not a distant shore, boy. It is the sea you swim in. I offer you a raft, a haven. Choose me, and I will protect you from drowning.” He gestured to the churning water around the boat, the endless, dark expanse. He offered a different kind of salvation, one that involved sinking rather than soaring.
“I'd rather drown standing than float in your shadow.” Dan's voice was stronger now, fueled by a desperate resolve. He chose the difficult upward path, even in the face of the overwhelming downward pull.
A flicker of something like disappointment crossed Dagon's face but with a hint of amusement. “You might. And everyone you love might drown with you. Defy me, and I will crush them all.” His voice dropped, becoming a low, menacing growl that vibrated in Dan's skull. A threat that reached beyond the dream, into the waking world.
“Then you will know that I stood,” Dan snarled, pushing against the crushing weight of the nightmare. He pushed with all his might, straining against the spiritual pressure. A defiance born of desperation, but defiance nonetheless.
Dagon's form surged forward, a tidal wave of darkness. The dream began to fracture, lines of light cracking the suffocating blackness. The pressure intensified, threatening to tear Dan's mind apart. The encounter was ending, but the ripples would remain.
“If you ever change your mind,” Dagon hissed as the dream shattered, his voice a promise and a threat, “just knock three times, ask twice, then wait. I will come.” The words echoed even as the vision dissolved, lingering like a foul taste. A standing invitation to the depths.
Dan awoke with a strangled cry, his body slick with sweat. The cot was still. His room was his again. But the sea still roared in his ears. The sound was fainter now, but the echo remained, a reminder of the power he had faced. He sat up, the air cold and heavy. Dawn was almost upon him. A sliver of pale light was just beginning to touch the horizon. The world was waking, and so was Dan.
He looked toward the narrow slit in the wall, where a sliver of the lightening sky was visible, and whispered, “From stardust we came…” A promise to himself, a reaffirmation of his true origin and destination. An affirmation of the upward journey.
Then Dan lay back down, his eyes open. A new journey awaited him, one that would take him far from his kindred and farther still from his people and their lands. But it was not distance that mattered now. It was truth. And Dan knew he would find it for the sea inside him was not calm but he had learned its tides. And he had learned how to flood. He had faced the darkness within and without, and found the strength to stand against it. He had chosen his direction, and it was upward.
And so, dear reader, our young hero prepares to step out into a world far larger and more dangerous than the decaying house he leaves behind. A world full of both wonder and peril, where the lines between the physical and the spiritual are often blurred. A world I have observed for a very long time, and whose stories I am curious to share with you at least to hear your perspective. For curiosity is my name. 

(End Chapter 1)
