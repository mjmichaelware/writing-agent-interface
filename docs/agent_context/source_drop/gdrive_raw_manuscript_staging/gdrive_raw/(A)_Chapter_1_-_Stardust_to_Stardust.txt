Chapter 1: Stardust to Stardust

The torchlight flickered in the boy's hand, casting suspicious shadows across the narrow hallway. The flame danced nervously—a tiny, unreliable beacon in the gloom. His heart hammered against his ribs, a frantic drumbeat in the suffocating stillness. Each step on the cool sunbaked stone threatened to betray him—any clank or clatter could undo his desperate mission. He had to reach his father. *He had to try, just one more time, before the point of no return.*.
The hallway had become a labyrinth, a testament to his father's unraveling. Piles of pottery shards loomed like ancient cairns, their stained edges whispering of dinners long forgotten, whispering of forgotten feasts and shattered lives. Rotting textiles, teeming with maggots, draped over discarded animal bones. The air itself felt thick, not merely with dust, but with the cloying scent of stagnation, clinging to the back of his throat. Withered leather sandals lay scattered, their straps brittle and cracked; rusted bronze tools, long abandoned, added to the metallic tang of decay; and desiccated, shriveled fruits, once vibrant, now clung to their seeds like mummified offerings. The stench—desiccated herbs mixed with excrement—stung Dan's nostrils, a vile perfume that spoke of surrender, of a life slowly consuming itself from the inside out. Swarms of flies, their buzzing a morbid symphony, rose from mounds of decaying food, swirling in living clouds over the corruption. This was a place where everything good had gone to die, and the remnants were left to fester.
The boy, with messy hair and kind eyes, picked his way through the refuse. His bare feet, usually calloused and firm, now felt as though they touched a fresh grave with every silent step. A shiver pierced him. It wasn't just the filth; it was the wrongness of it all, a palpable sense of something deeply disturbed. His father, Aviel, once a respected scholar whose wisdom had been sought by many, now saw treasure in rot, abundance in decay. He had traded insight for squalor, a mind once bright now clouded by the accumulating detritus.
Dan's breath caught in his throat, a sharp, metallic tang on his tongue. This house was a slow drowning. They were not living here; they were sinking, slowly, from the inside out.
His bare feet found a small, deliberate construction amidst the chaos—a fresh altar, a focal point for his father's twisted devotion. It was a sad irony that mocked the very concept of plenty. Aviel had fled from Philistia, but he brought their gods with him to Hebron, and after his mother's death, Dagon became his only source of hope. Around this crude shrine, Dan saw stones and pebbles, gathered with obsessive care; bones arranged like abandoned offerings; clay tablets whispering forgotten stories, their cuneiform script half-erased by time and neglect. Each element placed with perverse reverence, a mockery of true worship. His father was building something, yes, but building it from ruin.
 

The name Dagon almost came from him unbidden, a bitter taste on the back of his tongue, like stale ash. That damned god had stolen his father's soul. And Time—that insatiable, devouring force, a silent, relentless predator—bore the brunt of guilt. Time was not just the passage of moments but something else entirely, a force Dan would later understand had a name whispered in shadows. He knew, with the certainty of youth and the wisdom of suffering, that he would meet them both if he continued on this path—the path of stagnation, the path of decay. But this boy had a different path calling to him, a path whispered by the wind and traced by the stars.
Outside, stars—ancient and indifferent—and a full moon on its third day watched over Hebron. The city, nestled in the Judean Hills, was a fortress of walls and vineyards. The air hummed with the scent of sun-baked earth and distant woodsmoke, while the faint shimmer of oil lamps dotted the darkness like fallen stars. A whisper of cool breeze carried the ghost of grapevines and the promise of dawn, stirring the leaves in the surrounding olive groves. King David reigned here and all of Judah, his power rising quickly throughout the lands, a new order blooming. And new orders, you see, rarely arrive without a certain amount of… shuffling. A shifting of old pieces, a breaking of old molds.
Though echoes from the marketplace had ceased long ago, the night wasn't silent. The crickets' urgent chirping pulsed in the darkness, a constant, pulsing rhythm, almost like the quick beat of a frightened heart. The wind sighed through brittle grass like a thousand whispers, carrying the scent of dry earth, and the distant bark of a dog cut through the stillness. Every sound threatened to expose Dan, to drag him back into the quicksand of his father's madness. The smaller the sound, the louder it seems when one is trying to be quiet. A twig snapping, a loose stone rolling – they could seem louder than thunder in the stillness of the night, a cruel trick of the ear.
The boy was called Dan, though a name whose full significance he wouldn't understand until later. He had been waiting for this moment—not because the message he carried was easy to deliver, but because telling it would lift the first of many burdens. A weight had settled on his spirit, heavy as the dust that coated his father's possessions, and he yearned to cast it off.
For seven years, Dan had watched helplessly as Father Aviel lost himself. The house, once filled with the warmth of his mother's presence, had slowly become a tomb, each passing season adding another layer of refuse, another shadow to Aviel's eyes. For seven years, he had navigated the growing darkness, trying to hold onto the light within, trying to remember the man his father used to be. For a boy of sixteen, the decision to leave this infected home was perhaps the hardest he had ever faced, a tearing at the roots of his being. He had to leave before he, too, was consumed. Sometimes, the greatest act of love was to seek your own survival, even if it meant leaving a piece of your heart behind.
Dan paused outside Father Aviel's room, the torch still clutched in his hand, its small flame casting frantic shadows against the doorframe. With a quiet snuff, he extinguished the torch, plunging the immediate area into deeper darkness, relying now only on the faint moonlight filtering through the slit in the hallway wall and the memory of light. The smell of burnt wick lingered, a bitter tang in the stagnant air. He confirmed his father was asleep; deep sleep filled the room, a soft, rhythmic symphony of slow, whistling breaths and the occasional, low rumble of a snore. The ache in Dan's chest was not new, but it had grown heavier in recent days, a cold, dull throb behind his ribs. He remembered his father's words from a heated disagreement about gods and idols: “My son, if you are still looking for a god, then pick up a rock and make it so!”
The question burned in Dan's mind. If gods could be made of rocks, formed by the sheer weight of a man's attention and despair, then where should a searching heart like his truly turn? Dan's eyes often searched upward, as if answers resided there, in the vast, swirling tapestry above. He looked past the stars, harboring a suspicion long before the rest that humans were made from them. From dust to dust, from stardust to stardust, he mused. Dreams had often pulled him upward—climbing impossible stairs, leaping across vast chasms, floating among the constellations as if gravity itself had ceased to exist. Each time, he awoke with a strange longing, a sense of having been home. He looked up now, through the narrow slit, and the stars gazed back, patient and knowing, their ancient light a silent beckoning. That was where he would direct his attention. That was where he would go.
Dan knelt by his Father's cot, becoming one with the cold stone. He felt the ancient chill seep into his bare knees, grounding him, rooting him to the very bedrock of the world. He slowed his breath, silencing the frantic voices in his mind as his mother and nurturer, Zuna, had taught him. "Forget yesterday, forget tomorrow, forget now, just be. Empty the vessel," she would say, her voice echoing in his memory, "before you can fill it with the light."
His mother Zuna's words, whispered from beyond the veil, often guided him. She had been a dreamwalker, both blessed and cursed, for some feared her kind and whispered of witches who consorted with cats and commanded elements. (A curious pairing, those witches and cats, wouldn't you agree? Especially when the cat might have a few secrets of his own, and a tale to tell that stretches longer than a tangled ball of yarn. But that, as they say, is another story). Zuna had spoken of journeys beyond the mortal coil, of realms hidden just behind the waking world, waiting for those with the stillness of spirit to find them. "Find a still place," she'd say, "be like the wind, ever moving yet rooted to the earth."
In that stillness, that profound absence of self, he found the still place. He slipped into the Dreamscape, a realm where spirit reigned and flesh held no dominion. Inside the dreamscape, he found his father in an empty field lit by a shy ethereal light stretching to the horizon in shades of gray and silver. It was a blank canvas, desolate but blooming with potential, as things must have been before Time, before the first truth was spoken.
Aviel stood alone in the center, a solitary figure, dust swirling around him like a shroud, not the coarse dust of the earth, but something finer, eternal, the raw material of creation itself. At Dan's will, a surge of power, primal and thrilling as a lightning strike, flowed through him, waking forgotten currents in his blood, making his spirit hum with a profound, almost terrifying energy. The dust, stirred by an unseen hand, began to dance, then to coil like living smoke, thick as morning mist, a swirling vortex of creation. It coalesced, thickening, darkening, taking on substance with a soft, ethereal sigh of transformation.
First, glittering motes of sand rained down, countless crystalline grains forming from the boundless nothingness, whispering softly as they settled upon the desolate field. Then, with a slow, deliberate groan that seemed to echo from the very heart of the dreamscape, these grains knit together, drawing close, hardening, forming smooth, cool pebbles that clicked softly as they accumulated. In turn, these pebbles fractured and grew, erupting into rough, jagged rocks that hailed down from the blank sky—each one a solid, undeniable testament to his burgeoning will, a tangible manifestation of pure thought made terrifyingly real. A sane man, even in the depths of slumber, would have recoiled from such an unnatural genesis, raining down on him. But Aviel, lost in the deep, treacherous currents of his delusion, and directed by Dan's will, perceived only treasure. He scrabbled at the growing piles, his fingers frantic, his hands shaking, stuffing his pockets and then his garments, his eyes burning with a feverish, desperate greed that distorted his face into a grotesque mask of rapacity.
He crammed the ever-burgeoning weight into his pockets, his robes, until his very form was distended, deformed, a monstrous caricature of a man, burdened by the very wealth he so slavishly craved. The stones piled higher, crushing him, pressing against his chest, squeezing the very breath from his lungs. Aviel's feverish eyes glazed over, his mouth open in a soundless gasp, his desperate grasping fingers finally falling limp. The sheer, suffocating weight silenced him, not with a roar but with a profound, internal crack.
Then, Dan summoned a wind, a sigh from the void that rippled through the formless expanse. It swept across the field, gathering the dust and its creations, swirling them into a vortex of dissolution, carrying everything back to nothingness, to the serene oblivion from which it came. Aviel looked up, and for a fleeting moment, saw a single star pierce the oppressive gray, impossibly bright. It pulsed, a faint, insistent heartbeat against the vast canvas of his dream, a promise of something beyond the crushing weight of his hoard.
Dan poured his intent into that vision, shaping it with every fiber of his being: The son must depart on his journey, and the father must prepare his heart. He held the image of the star, a beacon of hope and change, imprinting it upon his father's sleeping mind. Then, gently, with a deep breath, he released his father's consciousness, letting him drift in the gentle rhythm of sleep. Aviel's mind would remain dreamless for the rest of the night, a silent respite from the chaos.
Weak and drained, Dan rekindled his light with flint and pyrite upon exiting his father's room, then retraced his steps through the defiled hallway. The torchlight guttered, casting his slouched shadow in a grotesque parody. He felt smaller now, the energy of the Dreamscape fading, and borrowed confidence spent, leaving him vulnerable once more to the oppressive reality of the house. Each shadow seemed to watch as he gingerly made his way through the maze on the now freezing stone floor.
Back in his room, the door closed with a soft clack. In stark contrast to the decaying labyrinth outside, Dan's small room, stone-walled with a simple cot and a few carefully arranged possessions, was meticulously tidy, a small corner of order he had managed to carve out from the house’s disarray. One might say it was much like putting one's own house in order before attempting to change the world. He sought oblivion on the cot, a temporary reprieve from the dust and the sorrow. Yet, even as darkness claimed him, the stillness of his room began to ripple, and a chilling sense of dread, cold as the deepest ocean, coiled around his fading awareness. The nightmare, it seemed, had already found its way in.
The sea roared, a maelstrom of chaos, a violent, churning abyss of black and white. Dan's small boat, splintered and fragile, was tossed like a toy, helpless against the colossal fury. Waves like mountains crashed around him, a deafening symphony of destruction that vibrated through his very bones. The sky, a suffocating blanket of black, offered no escape, no glimpse of light or hope. His vision fractured into a kaleidoscope of blue, black, and white—water, darkness, foam, swirling endlessly, threatening to consume him. Then, from the very heart of the storm, from the impossible depths, it rose.
At the Southern edge of the world, where the horizon bled into an endless, turbulent gray, a colossal head emerged from the waves. The face, carved from drowned stone, was ancient and impassive, radiating an inhuman cold. Its unblinking eyes, wide as abysses, blacker than the deepest trench, locked onto Dan's, a gaze that stripped away all pretense and left him naked in his terror. Seaweed, thick and rotting, draped its chin like a hideous beard, and barnacles, sharp and encrusted, clung to its cheeks like jewels of corruption. Dan knew, with a certainty that chilled him to the bone, the purpose of this visitation. He had angered a god.
In the far north, where the sea turned to ink, a monstrous tail breached the surface, a colossal fin that dwarfed his shattered boat. Scaled and shining with the slickness of ancient brine, it lashed back and forth, each movement a cataclysmic ripple that sent new waves crashing towards him. The body, vast and unknowable, remained submerged, an unseen horror connecting the human head and the serpentine tail into a single, terrifying entity that spanned the very world.
The boat tilted precariously, groaning, threatening to capsize and drag him into the frigid depths. The water screamed, a chorus of the damned, of souls lost to the hungry ocean. And Dan, who had grown used to commanding his dreams, who had woven reality from dust, could not calm the storm.
The waves towered, blotting out the last vestiges of light, crushing the very air from his lungs. Dan clung to the splintered wood, his heart a trapped bird, beating a desperate rhythm against his ribs. There was nowhere to run. No sky, no land, only the endless, hungry sea and the monstrous god that ruled it. Then, the voice, ancient and vast, echoed not from the monstrous mouth, but from the depths of Dan's own skull, a resonant thrum that vibrated through his very being.
