Chapter 1: Stardust to Stardust

The copper light clutched in the boy's hand was a frantic, frail scattering of color. It fought the sheer mass of the dark, casting suspicious, jittering shadows across the dim, dreadful hallway. The small flame shivered, not from wind, but from the beat of his muffled heart: an offbeat percussion against the overwhelming density of a condemned dwelling. The air itself felt heavy, as if the entire structure had swallowed all light.
Each calculated, barefoot step on the unforgiving limestone was a profound, private wager against the increasing mass of the house. The slightest change in pressure risked a betrayal: a metallic sound, a dry scrape, or any clatter that might announce his forbidden errand. This was not mere silence. The atmosphere felt like the still patience of serpents in a garden. The suffocating emptiness smelled of sulfur and expectant soil, but his father’s closed-off room held the precise, dead air of an opened tomb: the stagnant, chemical scent of potential that had been rigorously refused. This was no home. It was the heavy, unmoving lair of a man committed to the earth's sheer gravitational claim. Thus, the boy's every disciplined movement toward his father’s room was an act of necessary heresy. “This house is a liar,” he whispered to the stone floor. “But a lie needs the listener’s will to stand.”
The four-room house had once been warmed by the voice of a mother's songs. It had since ceased to be a simple structure. It had spread into a ruin, a physical testament to Father Aviel's systemic collapse. The dissolution began the moment of Mother's disappearance. The man had brought his own gods from the Western plains to fill the void, creating a vile, intricate shrine built of forgotten things. It was consecrated by a scholar who willingly surrendered his life's momentum to the gravitational pull of the earth. The house was cold, but with the specific, internal chill of a place the sun had never reached. The air itself was a heavy, saturated cloak, making every inhalation an exhausting chore. The entire structure pressed inward, a manifestation of the collective mass of his self-pity, seeking to crush its inhabitants back into formless dust. The ceiling beams sagged not from age, but from the sheer, internal volume of the man’s detachment. The air clung to the boy; he was constantly struggling to pay the loss-pressure debt with every breath.
Navigating its turns was a deliberation, a forced choice through the snare of matter. One felt its terrible, rooting pull with every step, like wading through sweet, slow honey. Piles of broken pottery shards and failed instruments—ruined brass astrolabes and cracked wooden compasses—loomed like small, silent monuments to futures that died stillborn. Rotting textiles, Mother Zuna's sacred threads left mid-shuttle, had unraveled into mildew and waste: a promise broken not by time, but by overwhelming, internal density. The decaying fabrics sloughed off the looms, making a horrifyingly dry rustle: the sound of all vitality surrendering to the crushing density of the material. The failed records lay everywhere: fragments of parchment and crumbling wax tablets, meticulously stripped clean of meaning by unseen, hungry mold. Desiccated fruits, once heavy with the promise of life, now clung to their seeds like mummified burdens: a stark testament to a harvest perpetually withheld. The water skins were brittle and cracked, smelling of stale leather and the sharp mineral tang of absolute dryness, mocking the very concept of living clarity.
Dan, the boy with kind eyes and dark hair, picked his way through the refuse. A bone-deep chill seized his spine, born not just of the filth, but from the corrupt heart of a man who saw treasure in rot. His throat tightened, dry and tight, at the thought of the shame this house carried. Father Aviel had been a scholar whose mind was sharp as flint. He had traded that bright, clean currency for the slow drag of decay and the sticky joy of letting go. He had ceased the daily rites of cleaning and maintenance: the small, repetitive acts that hold the chaos of the world at bay. This deliberate neglect of order, this surrender to systemic collapse, was the true horror. Dan had to leave this place of reckoning before the decay claimed him, too.
He paused by the hearth in the main room. The glowing coals pulsed like an exhausted red heart in the blackness. His anchor, the Zuna stone, was cool and perfect against his ribs—the only simple thing he owned in a house devoted to the horrifying, beautiful complexity of the messy. He thumbed the smooth surface, a silent reminder of hope, a single good tethering left behind when the worst of the world had escaped its confines. The stone's absolute coolness, a ghost of Zuna's hand, carried the faint, sharp metallic tang that marked the source—the unmanifest itself. It was a fragment of the high, white cold fire, a frozen echo of Mother’s songs.
Dan crept to Aviel's doorframe and listened. A low, ragged snoring, the sound of a man trying to outrun his own breath, issued from within. The boy held his breath, eliminating the friction of his own presence, and with a quiet snuff, deliberately extinguished the torch. He plunged the area into a deep, drowning darkness, relying only on the sharp memory of light and the faint moonlight filtering through a narrow window slit. The pungent smell of burnt wick lingered: a bitter tang of parting, the first small expenditure of the night. The ache in Dan's chest was a fixed pressure. He remembered his father's final, damning dismissal from three nights past, a statement of total surrender to matter:
"If you're still searching for your source, boy," Aviel had hissed, his voice heavy with the sluggish drag of his own self-deception, "then pick up a stone and make it so! Only a fool seeks answers from a power he cannot touch."
The words were bitter acid. "Lies," Dan murmured to the doorframe. "Lies coated in dust and honey. But your weight won't buy you peace, Father." Dan refused the stone, and the heavy earth it represented, his gaze instinctively seeking the open sky, searching the vast, starry canvas above. He knew the true answers lay not in the heavy ground, but in the luminous heights, for his mother, Zuna, had taught him the ancient spiritual law: that all things were but borrowed light, destined to return to the cold, unwavering truth of the source of the stars. His dreams had always taken him to the high places: not fleeting images, but exercises of will where he flew or climbed endless stairs, a constant preparation for the ascent.
But this night's work wasn't for his own dreams. He was entering the Dreamscape—the sleeping mind of the world, a boundless place where thought was substance. This mastery was the perilous art of commanding raw elements. It was the means he needed to access Aviel's mind. Dan knelt by his father's cot. The thin, crystalline cold fire of the floor immediately seized his bare knees. He slowed his breath, silencing the frantic voices in his mind as Zuna had taught him before her passing. "The last scrubbing is the one that matters, Dan. Don't fear the emptiness. Make the vessel empty before you try to use it, or your message will contain the very dirt you hold."
He forced his mind empty, chasing the last fragments of earthly tethering away, letting go of the phantom scent of Zuna's memory. The pressure of leaving the familiar was a sharp agony, a deep shedding. The stone beneath his knees dissolved, not instantly, but with a horrifying, slow certainty, as if the floor itself was sinking into the quicksand of non-being. He waited until the final, cold friction of matter was gone before the sensation of matter completely vanished. In that terrifying, perfect stillness—that necessary, terrible emptiness—he stepped sideways and into the Dreamscape.
The immediate change was the profound, aching silence. He found himself standing at a shimmering border: the veil of his father's sleep, a wall dense with years of self-deception. He couldn't fight it; he could only absorb and pass through. Dan drew a breath of pure intent, and a primal surge of focus flowed through him. The cold, fixed pressure of his resolve pushed against the obstacle. The barrier, a solid wall of shattered, disorganized memories and the heavy, black mass of regret, did not dissolve with grace. Dan hit it like a wall of cold, congealed resistance. For a terrifying, suspended breath, his jaw locked and his throat tightened under a sensory onslaught: a flash of a child’s face screaming in the smoke, a woman’s hand slipping from a grasp, and the hollow sound of a city’s final death rattle. This rush of profound, paralyzing grief wasn't his own; it was heavy with the stench of self-pity and rot. He heard the soundless crash of his father's guilt. He channeled his will forward, a single, concise thought demanding passage. The boundary gave way, not with a burst, but a painful, inward implosion of pressure. Then, silence and release: he was inside, witnessing a featureless field of raw, indistinguishable material.
Aviel stood alone in the gray, waiting to dream. His formless void teemed with potential, as things were before Time when the first words of truth were spoken. Dan exhaled, a soundless act of command, a precise manipulation of the non-material element: “Let it rain.”
The creation was instantaneous and corrupt, an accelerated genesis toward a state of systemic collapse. Glittering motes rained down, then knit together, hardening, forming smooth pebbles, and then the heavy, crushing mass of boulders. It was the unnatural accumulation of matter, a devastating creation built upon the lie of rest. By Dan's will and word, Aviel saw only treasure. He shoved them into his pockets and then beneath his clothing, whispering, "Mine," hoarding his own delusion. He gathered the ever-burgeoning accumulation until his very form was distended, crushed. He became a lonesome pillar of stone in a barren field, the mass of the precious metal squeezing the breath from his lungs. The father's eyes glazed over, his mouth open in a soundless gasp. The suffocating density of his possessions silenced him, permanently locking his hands in the posture of hoarding.
Then, with a final, deliberate breath that was both a sigh and a judgment, Dan summoned a wind, a cold, scouring current of pure intent. The shaping left him hollow, leaving a sharp ache behind his ribs; the loss-pressure now physically registered. The wind swept across the field, gathering the grit and its crushing creations, carrying everything back to the void. In that brief moment of unburdening, the father looked up and saw a single star pierce the gray, impossibly bright. “I must depart this house,” Dan projected into the light. “You must prepare your heart for the reckoning.” He held the image of the star, imprinting it upon his father's mind, a non-material exertion of vast effort. Then, gently, he released his awareness, pulling the cold mass of his own body back to the threshold.
The stench hit him first—rot, waste, surrender's sweet perfume—before the cold floor reregistered beneath his feet. His legs trembled, drained of strength, but with renewed sense of purpose, a purpose that was now a raw wound, Dan left his father's room. He sat up, gasping for breath. The coldness was a physical ache that settled in his bones. The blackness of the hallway was a sudden, heavy, oppressive presence. “The cost is higher here,” he whispered, pushing his palms flat against the cold stone. “Always higher.” He knelt by the glowing coals in the hearth. He extended an unsteady hand, coaxing a flame from the embers with a small piece of kindling. The flame caught with a quiet hiss. He lit his torch, its light a familiar, hopeful presence in the gloom, and with new, resolute purpose, left the main room.
Back in his room, the door closed behind him. Dan's small room was a meticulously tidy corner of perfect order, a hard-won proof that he had put his own house in order before daring to criticize the world. This was his sanctuary, his final act of self-possession. His body was a dull, aching mass after the non-material exertion. He crumpled onto the cot, allowing the simple linen and the welcoming darkness to pull him toward oblivion. Yet, even as the darkness claimed him, a chilling dread coiled around his fading awareness. But sleep wasn't rest.
He found himself standing on a shore of glittering, black sand. The water was unnaturally calm, a sheet of polished glass that promised horizontal peace. He disposed of a small boat at the shoreline with his will. The memory of his easy mastery was already fading; the cost of the first journey demanded repayment. He stepped out onto the water. It held him, savoring the impossible ease of his stride. He was halfway across the cove when the glass shattered with a deafening crack, pulling the false foundation out from under him. The peaceful blue instantly bled into an angry, bruised purple. The sea didn't merely roar. It was a terrifying churn of water and raw earth, a maelstrom of pure, liquid rock and bitter brine. Promptly, he was clinging on to a single, inadequate plank of wood, his only tethering against the colossal forces.

His vision blurred and his jaw locked as he recognized the Leviathan, the deep power of the earth's mass.
