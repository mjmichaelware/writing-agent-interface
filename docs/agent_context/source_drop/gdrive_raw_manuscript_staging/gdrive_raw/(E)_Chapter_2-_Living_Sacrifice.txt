Chapter 2: Living Sacrifice
“Dan!”
The scream tore through the pre-dawn stillness of Hebron, ragged and desperate. It was the sound of a man wrestling not with sleep, but with the things sleep had shown him, a man whose carefully constructed world had just been rattled to its rotten foundations.
Dan’s eyes snapped open. Sleep, thin and fleeting, vanished instantly, leaving him suspended in that disorienting space between dreaming and waking. He knew that sound. It was Aviel. His father. Stories like this, one might tell you, often begin with a cry in the night, a moment when the mundane unravels to reveal something far older, far stranger. And sometimes, those voices echo from deeper places than mere memory can fathom.
He scrambled off his cot, bare soles meeting the cool, familiar stone of his meticulously tidy room – a small island of order in the rising tide of chaos his father had allowed. He didn't hesitate. He knew the path, even through the dim light, though it wound through his father's self-made labyrinth. He needed to understand what the vision had achieved, what strange fruit his carefully planted seed of intent had borne. Or perhaps he just needed to stop the shouting, that immediate, primal need to silence the noise, for noise, you see, often draws far more attention than one desires.
He moved quickly but cautiously, bare feet stepping over unseen mounds of decaying fabric, dodging brittle stacks of pottery shards that scraped like dry bones. A muffled clink here, a rustle there. Each sound reminded him of the filth he was trying to escape, the physical manifestation of his father's spiritual decay. His heart hammered, a frantic echo of the drumbeat from before, a frantic bird trapped in a cage of stone and refuse.
He reached the doorway of his father’s room, the air thick with the stale scent of sickness and dust. His father sat bolt upright on his cot, eyes wide and wild in the gloom, chest heaving.
Dan stepped into the room, his voice quiet but firm. “Yes, Father. Here I am!”
Aviel blinked, his gaze focusing slowly. Relief warred with terror in his eyes. “Dan! You are here. Thank the... thank the lord.” He swallowed hard, his throat working. “I… I had a vision, son.”
Dan moved closer, sitting carefully on the edge of the cot beside his father. The simple straw mattress offered little comfort, but it was a place to anchor himself in the face of his father’s turmoil. “I am yours to listen, Father,” Dan said, keeping his tone neutral, though a knot of anticipation had tightened in his stomach. What had the message wrought? Had the truth, however unwelcome, finally pierced the veil of delusion? Such things, as the old ones say, are often hoped for, but rarely seen.
Aviel recounted the vision, though it was a distorted, fragmented version of the one Dan had crafted. He spoke of the gray, empty field, of dust swirling, then of pebbles and rocks falling from the sky, accumulating around him, weighing him down until he couldn’t breathe. He described the wind that swept it away, the brief glimpse of a single, distant star. He recounted the feeling more than the images – the oppressive weight, the struggle, the sudden lightness, the glimpse of something far off. He had felt the pressure, the cleansing wind, the hint of ascent… yet his mind, trapped in its familiar patterns, had twisted the map to a mountain peak into a comfortable ditch. Oh, the peculiar ways in which a man’s heart can deceive him, even when the truth is laid bare!
Aviel finished, a strange, unsettling excitement dawning in his eyes – an excitement Dan hadn't seen directed at anything real or productive in years. It was the feverish energy of a man who believes he's found a treasure map, even if the map leads nowhere good. "It is a sign, Dan! A clear sign! The weight... the accumulation... it was showing me this place! This house! It is time, son of my flesh, it is time for me to cast off this burden! The vision tells me I must leave! I must go and find my path, away from here!"
He began to scramble off the cot, fumbling for the worn sack he kept by his bed, the one containing his few precious, worthless possessions. It was as if he planned to walk out into the dark morning right now, leaving everything behind as easily as shedding a cloak.
Dan stared, aghast. His father, leaving? Abandoning everything, including him, based on that interpretation? It made no sense! Aviel was the market man, the scholar, the one with responsibilities, however neglected they might be now. He was tied to the very fabric of Hebron, however frayed. His departure, Dan knew, would unravel more than just their household. It would affect the very threads of the city itself, though the townsfolk would only mutter of it later, or perhaps never. One can never be certain of what whispers on the wind when the great pieces begin to move.
"Father!" Dan reached out, having the nerve to grab Aviel by the shoulders, shaking him gently but firmly. "Father, have you gone senile?! Have you finally lost it all?! This is my journey! The vision was for me! It showed my path, clearing away the things holding me down! Look at me! I am able-bodied! Send me!"
Aviel froze, his hands still fumbling with the sack. The excitement drained from his face, replaced first by a familiar sadness, deep and bone-weary. To lose his son now, after losing Zuna, after the emptiness that consumed him… it was too much. But then, as Dan’s words sank in – the mention of the vision being his, the implicit rejection of Aviel’s current path, the audacity of the boy claiming a journey the father had misinterpreted for himself – the sadness curdled. It turned into something sharper, colder. Recognition flickered in Aviel's eyes, then hardened into understanding and, finally, anger. He saw the truth: Dan wasn't just leaving the house; he was leaving the world his father had chosen, a house of Dagon.
"Your journey?" Aviel's voice dropped, losing its frantic edge and gaining a dangerous steel. "Is this about... chasing after some god? You dare send me visions in my sleep, disrupting the peace, simply because you scorn Lord Dagon?!" He spat the words, laced with the old bitterness. "If you are still looking for a god," he quoted himself, the words heavy with accusation, "then pick up a rock and make it so!"
The air in the small room grew heavy, not with dust, but with the weight of years unsaid. Accusations, sharp as flint, flew from Aviel's lips, each one landing like a physical blow. "Still wrestling with your gods, are we?" Aviel sneered, his voice edged with bitter amusement. "Or is it just the one, now? The mighty Dagon, perhaps? Did you think I wouldn't know?" His father's face, already drawn by fear, hardened, a muscle jumping in his jaw, and his eyes, once wild, now held a chilling, wounded glint as they raked over Dan, as if the boy's very posture was a silent, damning indictment. Dan watched, a cold knot tightening in his gut, as Aviel's trembling fingers tightened on the tattered sack by his bed, a desperate grip on the very things that chained him. The truth, starkly revealed in a dream, seemed to shatter against a mind fiercely barricaded, leaving no impression. The room pulsed with the unspoken, two opposing currents in the same river: one sinking deeper into the suffocating comfort of rot, the other straining, every sinew taut, to break free and climb towards the vast, terrifying silence of the unknown.
Finally, Aviel’s face set in grim resolve. The grief was buried again under layers of anger and stubborn pride. "Then so be it!" he declared, his voice shaking with suppressed fury. "If this house, if I, are such a burden to you, if you must chase after gods in the sky like a fool... then yes! You must go! Pack your things! Leave this house! Leave Hebron! Leave immediately!" He leaned forward, his voice dropping to a harsh whisper, laced with a vindictive satisfaction that chilled Dan to the bone. "And may whatever god you seek protect you from the consequences of spurning Lord Dagon this night! For even a god of the depths has a reach you cannot fathom!"
Stunned but strangely resolute, Dan stood. There was nothing more to say, nothing more to do here. His desperate mission to shake his father awake had failed, spectacularly. Instead, it had propelled him toward the inevitable, like a stone finally dislodged from a cliff face.
He turned and walked back down the dreadful hallway, the silence that followed Aviel's outburst even heavier than the previous gloom. He made his way to his small room, the haven of order, and began to gather his few necessary possessions. From a neatly folded pile, he took a sturdy tunic and breeches, rolling them tight. He then secured his thick wool cloak against the night chill, and a simple, tightly woven blanket that would serve as his sleeping mat if called for. He tied these into a compact roll, preparing his basic necessities for the road.
Next, he moved to his small, worn leather satchel. Into it went a small pouch of dried figs or cured olives, a precious pinch of salt, and the flint and pyrite for fire. (The exact contents of that pouch? A detail lost to the mists of time, for now. But rest assured, it will matter.) His mother's small, smooth carving stone – a tactile link to the stillness she had taught him – nestled safely within. He took his small leather pouch, adding the twenty shekels he had earned working in the vineyards with his father and convincing Aviel to part with things they could actually sell rather than hoard – hard-earned and harder saved.
As he prepared to leave the house, standing at the threshold, his father appeared in the hallway, looking slightly less wild, the anger now mixed with a deep, visible sadness. The brief storm had passed, leaving behind the familiar landscape of his grief.
"Where do you go, son?" Aviel asked, his voice rough, eyes still searching, as if for the last echoes of his dream. "The vision... it spoke of stars, did it not? Of burdens shed, of rising." He gestured vaguely north, his hand sweeping across the unseen landscape beyond their walls. "Always up. From these southern hills, through the lowlands, past the salty waters, and then ascend to the fresh waters of the Sea of Galilee. From there, to Dan, at the base of Mount Hermon, and then to its very tops, where the veil is thinnest." His voice grew quiet, almost reflective, as if reciting an ancient map of the soul, a path laid out by generations of travelers and whispered legends. "From the high places, through the valleys, to the highest peaks. Where the boundary between the world of men and the places unseen is most fragile.”
Dan's breath caught. He stared at his father, a strange, almost knowing look on his face. The words resonated, a sudden, undeniable clarity. Dan. His namesake city. The path, once vague, now etched itself in his mind with the certainty of prophecy. He held his father's gaze. "Yes, Father," he said, his voice firm, a new resolve settling in his chest. "Up. To Dan! My namesake city!"
Aviel reached into his own simple pouch and pulled out five additional shekels, pressing them into Dan's hand. "For reserve," he said gruffly. "For bribe, should you need it. The world outside... it is not kind to fools chasing heights." He also pressed a heavy, aged bottle of wine into his other hand. "From a colleague at the market. A fine aged wine from the hills. Good stuff. Don't squander it." A dreadful vintage, truth be told. It tasted like fermented dust and regret. The mysteries of human tastes remain quite profound. And finally, a familiar leather water pouch – the kind used in these lands for centuries, a simple, necessary vessel. "Take this. You will need water."
Dan accepted the gifts, the weight of them heavy in his hands, heavier still with the complex emotions swirling between them. This was a farewell, perhaps a final one. A father, lost in his own descent, now, through these very gifts, propelled his son towards an ascent Aviel could only watch from below, a painful severing of ties for a greater, unknown purpose. It was a paradox, a final, twisted act of love and resignation.
"Go now," Aviel said, stepping back. "Before I change my mind."

He checked the small, heavy dagger tucked into his simple belt, the one his father had taught him to use when he was younger. A good memory, that. Aviel, before the dust claimed him, patient and steady, showing a much smaller Dan how to hold the blade, how to carve simple shapes from wood, how to hone an edge. It made Dan's chest ache now, remembering that time, that different man.

He secured his leather water container and the heavy, aged bottle of wine to his side, then picked up the sturdy walking staff leaning against the wall, and finally, a length of strong, thin rope coiled neatly at its base. He pushed the ache down and continued onward.

Further on, as he neared the edge of the city, he saw a small procession. A noble, perhaps, or a wealthy merchant, walking with an air of self-importance, surrounded by a small retinue of servants, and yes, musicians playing flutes and drums, and dancers, their movements fluid even in the soft morning light. They moved with a kind of cushioned ease, a bubble of comfort in the harsh world. Dan watched them pass. Could a person like that go up? He wondered. Could they shed the weight of their possessions, their status, their easy lives? He looked at the noble's face, impassive in the growing light, and understood, with a pang of sorrow, that he could not read the heart behind the eyes. He did not know their burdens, or their dreams.
As he made his way towards the northern gate, the city walls loomed ahead, a fortress of ancient stone against the wild, awakening hills. He stopped briefly at the homes of his closest friends, the ones whose laughter still echoed in his memory, whispering quick, tearful goodbyes through closed doors. He promised to return someday, knowing even as he spoke the words that the boy who left tonight would not be the same one who might come back. He was leaving behind more than just a house; he was leaving a life, a childhood, and the very air he had breathed since birth.

He stepped through the final arch of the northern gate, the cool, worn stone brushing his shoulder like a final, silent goodbye. The massive timbers of the gate groaned softly as he passed, a guttural sigh of farewell. The city's sounds, once a comforting hum, now seemed to recede instantly, swallowed by the vast, open expanse of the Judean wilderness. He paused, just outside the threshold, turning for one last glance at the familiar silhouette of Hebron's walls, stark against the lightening sky. A lone goat bleated from a distant hillside, its cry echoing in the new stillness, a stark contrast to the human bustle he had just left. The air here was cleaner, sharper, carrying the wild scent of dry grass and ancient rock, tasting of freedom and the unknown.
His feet, accustomed to the city's paved paths, now met the rougher, uneven earth of the ancient road. The path stretched before him, disappearing into the pre-dawn gloom, beckoning him onward. He took a deep breath, filling his lungs with the crisp, cool air, and then, without looking back, began to walk.

The ancient road north from Hebron, a ribbon of worn stone and packed earth, wound through vineyards and olive groves, their leaves shimmering silver in the nascent light. Beyond these cultivated lands, it opened onto rockier, more unforgiving terrain, the path itself a testament to countless footsteps over millennia. He walked through the long hours of the day, the sun climbing higher, transforming from a gentle eye to a blazing, furious orb, a molten disc of pure rage, beating down on the Earth with its relentless, unforgiving heat. The landscape blurred into a shimmering haze of heat and dust, the distant hills rippling like water.

He passed workers heading to fields and shepherds leading flocks into Tekoa, a city just off his path. As the sun began its slow descent, painting the sky in hues of orange and purple, Dan passed near a valley of springs and caverns, a place whispered about by locals, who spoke of a prophecy that one day "a king of peace" would carve great pools there to bring life to the desert.
Sweat beaded on his brow and trickled down his back, a constant, irritating stream. His throat grew dry, a persistent dryness that promised worse to come. The water pouch, once reassuringly full, now felt lighter with every step, its contents dwindling with alarming speed. The dust, fine and insidious, coated his tongue. His muscles began to ache with the steady, unyielding rhythm of the walk, a dull throb that spread from his calves to his hips.

With each rhythmic thud of his sturdy walking staff against the earth, Dan felt the rhythm of the journey settle into his bones. Up, his father had said, to Dan, the base of Mount Hermon, and then to its very tops, where the veil is thinnest. The words echoed, a strange, compelling prophecy. He thought of the squalor he'd left, the suffocating weight of his father's house, the dust that seemed to cling to everything and everyone. This road, though harsh, felt clean. Every ache, every bead of sweat, felt like a cleansing, a shedding of the old skin. He was not just walking away from Hebron; he was walking into something, stepping into the vast, unknown expanse his father had only hinted at.

Such journeys, dear reader, are not merely measured by the miles beneath one's feet. Oh no, not at all. They are, rather, about the turning of the soul, the shedding of the past, and the brave, foolish hope for what lies beyond the next horizon. This is a truth often observed by those who watch such tales unfold. He kept a steady pace, the wine bottle and water pouch bouncing softly against his side, a counterpoint to the thrumming anticipation in his chest. He was on his way. On his way up.

Bethlehem was near, and what appeared to be a well was right ahead next to a cluster of olive trees and moving figures. His thirst was a burning ache now, a constant reminder of his empty vessel. As he drew closer, one of the figures, this one a woman, detached herself from the others. She wore bright clothes and an even brighter smile. Her clothing was scanty, revealing more of herself than was considered decent among his people, or indeed, most people of that time. Well-kept hair flowed down to her waist and danced around her like a mist. She approached him just before he reached that savior of a well, the stone worn smooth by countless hands and ropes over centuries.

"Well, now," she purred, her voice like honey poured over warm stones, her gaze lingering on his dusty face. "A young man, clearly on a grand mission. And so terribly thirsty, aren't you, my dear? Don't you worry your head about it. Let me just fetch you a drink." She smiled, a slow, knowing curl of her lips, and her eyes, bright as fresh coins, were utterly alluring, pulling him in. He noticed she was not one of his people; her features, her accent, marked her as a foreigner. She told him she had traveled west from Edom, a place where, as he recalled, warnings had been whispered about "muddying" with certain women.
But when thirst envelops the mind, one loses direction. They went to the well together. She lowered the bucket, the rope creaking, and drew up cool water. She offered him a drink, her hand brushing his as he took the cup, a touch that lingered, light as a butterfly wing. He drank deeply, splashing some cool water on his face, the blessed cold startling him.

"Ah, that is much better, is it not, sweet pilgrim?" she purred, drawing near, and the very air around him thickened, growing heavy with her scent—jasmine and something else, something primal and rich, like warm earth after rain. Her fingers, light as a moth's wing, brushed his shoulder, and a jolt, quite electric, seemed to pass from her skin to his. "The road is long and hard for one so young, precious. There are so many easier paths. Paths with comfort, with joy, with... delightful companionship." She leaned closer, her voice a conspiratorial murmur, her eyes, bright as freshly minted coins, holding his gaze, promising unspoken wonders. She spun a silken web of words: of rest, of soft beds, of forgetting the dust of the road in surroundings far, far softer, of pleasures that would make the journey seem like a distant, unpleasant dream.
And oh, how the very air around her shimmered, promising all manner of sweet forgetting. Dan felt a dizzying warmth bloom in his chest, a pull originating deep in his bones, a current dragging at his thoughts, clouding them as surely as a desert wind clouds the sky. His vision blurred at the edges, his very senses conspiring against him, drawn downwards by an unseen, insistent force, leaving his resolve a wavering candle flame in a sudden gust. It was a terrible, beautiful thing, the way her presence seemed to resonate with a fundamental part of him, a forgotten melody, a deep, ancient call. To deny it felt like tearing a piece from his very soul, a most unnatural thing, you understand, for who would turn from such a feast? Yet, even as the heat burned, a chill certainty began to spread through him. This was no mere woman, no simple roadside encounter. Oh no. This was the very essence of the things he fled, a shimmering, living embodiment of easy pleasure, of entanglement, of distraction from the upward path. He knew, with a certainty that settled cold and sharp in his gut, that this was a crucible, a test, perhaps, sent by powers who disliked his chosen direction.
He stopped, met her gaze with calm respect, and spoke words that surprised even himself with their firmness, words that felt borrowed from a place of deeper strength. "Peace to you, woman from Edom," he said. "But my path lies elsewhere, and I cannot stop. May you find what you seek." He gave her a small, polite bow, and turned back to the well. The woman from Edom watched him, her smile fading, replaced by a look of cold, sharp surprise. Without another word, she turned and walked back to the group she had come from, melting back into their midst.
Dan felt the residual heat of her presence slowly recede, leaving behind a faint chill. He was still thirsty, bone-dry and weary, but the air around him now seemed sharper, purer. He had said no. He had pushed back against a subtle, insidious pull, and in that refusal, he felt a spark of something new and fierce ignite within him. A small victory, yes, but a victory nonetheless, on a road that promised many more battles unseen. He needed more water, desperately, but more than that, he needed clarity. He needed to wash away the last lingering traces of her intoxicating presence. He began to lower the bucket. As he looked down into the dark mouth of the well, seeking his own dusty, weary reflection, something happened.
A ripple.
It spread across the surface, though nothing had disturbed the water. The ripple grew into small waves, swirling faster and faster. And then, instead of his face, the water showed… something else…
