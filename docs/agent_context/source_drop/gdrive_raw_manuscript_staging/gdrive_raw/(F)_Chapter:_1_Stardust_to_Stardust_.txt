CHAPTER 1: STARDUST TO STARDUST (Rewritten)
The torch in the boy's hand was a nervous, fluttering thing, casting a sick, copper light across the corridor. The small flame did not so much burn as it was squeezed by the terrible dark, which seemed to press in from the walls like a huge, black sponge. The jittering shadows it cast were suspicious and thin, like long-legged thieves debating an ugly deed. Dan’s heart thumped a rough, hurried rhythm, and the tiny flame shivered with every beat.
The house itself had settled into the earth like a dull, unhappy toad. Every calculated, barefoot step Dan took on the unforgiving limestone was a terrible gamble—his small body against the house's increasing grumbling, downward pull. The air was not silent; it smelled of sulfur and expectant soil, like the stale, waiting patience of snakes in a coil. His father’s closed-off room held a sharp, dead-tomb scent—the smell of a life that had been rigorously and utterly refused. It was an evil, heavy lair, and the boy's simple walk toward that door was an act of magnificent naughtiness.
Outside, the night was a noisy beast. Crickets chirped their urgent, needle-sharp song. The wind dragged through the brittle grass like a thousand sighs. Even a distant dog’s bark was a ragged tear in the stillness. It seemed that the smaller the sound, the louder the danger; a loose stone would clatter like a gong hit by a giant.
Since his mother, Zuna, had gone silent, the four-room house had bled its warmth away. Its decay was a deliberate testament to Aviel’s total and frightening refusal to carry on. Seven years ago, Aviel had dragged his Western gods from Philistia to Hebron, but after Zuna’s disappearance, he kept only the low god, Dagon, for an anchor. He built a vile, intricate shrine of forgotten things. The house became cold with a specific, internal chill, the kind of place the sun had given up trying to reach. The air was a heavy, saturated blanket that made every breath an exhausting, burning chore. The ceiling beams sagged, not because they were old, but because they could not bear the sheer, internal volume of his father’s misery. The whole structure was trying to crush them back into the mud they came from.
Moving was not simple walking. It was wading through a snare of matter. He felt the earth’s terrible, rooting drag with every step, like sinking through sweet, viscous honey. Piles of shattered pottery and broken instruments lay about like silent monuments to dreams that died as mere notions. Ruined brass astrolabes and cracked wooden compasses were left to gather silt-fine dust.
Mother Zuna’s sacred threads, left mid-shuttle, had simply given up. They had unraveled into mildew and waste, draping over brittle animal bones with a horrible, dry rustle—the sound of all life surrendering to mud. Maggots, pale as soft, wriggling candy, teemed on the rotting cloth. The stench was sharp as ammonia: dried herbs mixed with something foul and human. Swarms of flies rose from the mounds of decaying food, their buzzing a morbid, living cloud over the corruption.
Fragments of parchment and crumbling wax tablets lay scattered like lost shoes, stripped clean of meaning by hungry mold. Dry, mummified fruits clung to their seeds—a harvest perpetually and stubbornly withheld. The leather water skins were brittle and cracked, smelling of stale leather and the mineral tang of absolute thirst. The house’s well, the source of life, was just a hollow stone throat, perpetually dry, as though it had forgotten how to drink.
Dan, who was sixteen, felt a chill deeper than the filth itself. It came from the corrupt heart of his father, a man who had decided that rot was treasure and self-pity was a cozy blanket. Aviel had been a scholar, his mind as sharp as new flint, but he had traded that clean edge for the slow, sticky joy of letting go. He had stopped the cleaning. He had stopped the daily, repetitive work of maintenance, which is the only thing that truly holds the chaos of the world at bay. Dan knew he had to leave this place of reckoning before the rot claimed him.
"Before it swallows me whole," he sighed, the words a single expelled breath in the suffocating air.
He paused by the hearth. The glowing coals pulsed like a tired red heart in the blackness. Against his ribs lay his only treasure: the Zuna stone, cool and perfect. It was the only simple thing in a house that worshipped the confusing mess of matter. He touched the smooth surface, and the perfect coolness, a ghost of his mother's hand, carried a faint, sharp metallic tang—a frozen echo of her songs from the high, white cold.
Dan crept to Aviel’s doorframe and listened. From inside came a low, ragged snoring—the sound of a man running a frantic, terrible race from his own breath. The boy snuffed out his torch. The area plunged into a deep, drowning darkness, but Dan relied on the memory of light and the faint moon-dust filtering through a narrow slit. The bitter tang of burnt wick lingered, the first small expenditure of the night.
The fixed pressure in Dan's chest reminded him of his father's damning words from three nights before, a statement of total surrender to the dull, clinging world:
"If you're still searching for your source, boy," Aviel had hissed, his voice thick with the sluggish, sticky drag of self-deception, "then pick up a stone and give it a name! Only a fool seeks answers from a power he cannot touch."
Those words were bitter acid. Dan refused the stone and all the heavy earth it represented. He felt a question pull upward in his heart: If gods could be made of mere rocks, then where should a truly searching heart turn? Dan often searched the ceiling and the darkness above, for his dreams had always been of high places—not fleeting images, but earnest, constant preparations for the ascent.
"Mother said we're from the stars," he whispered to the darkness, the cool stone at his chest. "If that's a lie, then let me be a fool."
But his work this night was not for his own climb. He was entering the Dreamscape, which is the sleeping mind of the world, a boundless place where a mere thought can be as solid as a boulder. He needed to get inside his father’s mind.
Dan knelt by the cot. The crystalline cold of the floor bit instantly into his bare knees. He slowed his breath, silencing the frantic, panicked voices within, just as Zuna had taught him. She had been a dreamwalker, too, a blessed and cursed sort of person whom some feared, whispering of witches and commanded elements.
He remembered her voice: "Forget yesterday, forget tomorrow, forget now, just be. Empty the vessel before you can fill it with the light."
He emptied his mind. He chased away the last fragments of earthly friction—the familiar, comforting scent of memory, the last shadow of history. The stone beneath his knees dissolved. He waited until the final, cold grittiness of matter was gone entirely. In that terrifying, perfect stillness, he did not walk forward or turn back. He simply stepped sideways, and into the Dreamscape.
The immediate change was a profound, aching silence. He stood at the shimmering border of his father's sleep, a thick wall of years of self-deception that Dan had to absorb. He pushed his fixed resolve against the barrier, and for a terrifying second, he was crushed under a sudden volume of pure sound: a child screaming, a hand slipping, a city's final death rattle. This terrible rush of grief—heavy with the stench of old waste—was his father’s internal debt. He channeled his will. The wall gave way with a painful, inward implosion, and Dan was released into a featureless field of raw creation.
Aviel stood alone in the center, waiting for his dreams to begin. He was a solitary figure in a void of creation's raw material—a fine, eternal dust swirling around him like a gray shroud. At Dan's will, a thrill of power, primal and sharp as lightning, flowed through him. It made his spirit hum with a quiet, terrifying energy. Dan exhaled, a soundless act of command, a precise manipulation of the unseen air:
"Let it rain."
The creation was instant and deeply corrupt, an accelerated genesis toward a total collapse. Glittering motes rained down. They knit together, hardening into smooth pebbles, and then the crushing mass of heavy boulders. It was a devastating, unnatural accumulation of matter built upon the very lie of rest. The dust began to dance like living smoke, swirling and darkening, taking on substance with a soft, ethereal sigh.
By Dan’s word, Aviel saw only treasure. His hands, with a soundless scream of hidden greed, grasped at gold and copper and fine silver. He shoved the treasure into his pockets and beneath his clothes, whispering, "Mine," hoarding his delusion until his very form was distended and crushed. He became a lonely pillar of stone in a barren field, the sheer thickness of the precious metal squeezing the breath from his lungs. Aviel’s mouth opened in a silent gasp, silenced by the suffocating drag of his possessions, his hands permanently locked in the posture of hoarding.
Then, with a final, deliberate breath, Dan summoned a wind—a cold, scouring current of pure intent. The shaping left him hollow, a sharp ache behind his ribs as a cold draft rushed into his own lungs to replace the borrowed space. The wind swept across the field, gathering the grit and its crushing creations, carrying everything back to the void.
In that brief moment of unburdening, his father looked up. He saw a single star pierce the gray, impossibly bright. Dan poured his final, potent message into that vision: "I must depart this house. You must prepare your heart." He held the image of the star, imprinting it upon his father's mind with vast effort. Then, gently, he released Aviel’s consciousness, allowing him to drift into a dreamless, silent sleep.
The stench hit him first—rot, waste, and surrender’s sweet perfume—before the cold, solid floor returned beneath his feet. He was empty, but a purpose that felt like a raw, exposed nerve drove him. He scrambled from the room, gasping for air. His limbs betrayed him, feeling as if they were suddenly filled with poured lead. A cold pain hammered behind his eyes.
Dawn was just beginning to etch a cold, gray line through the narrow window slit. The cost of his defiance instantly registered as a rigid, painful knot in his muscles. His jaw felt tight. His breathing was a frantic, high-pitched whistle. He lifted a trembling hand. His fingers were swollen, sluggish, and strange. He tried to speak the simplest word: Mother. His mouth moved, but the syllables were caught, a cold, dense knot in his throat. A wave of crushing paralysis lasted only a terrifying second, leaving his tongue heavy and numb. The flesh of his lips felt like old, dry leather—the first, terrible wound of the curse.
"Mother. Moth—" The word caught, a knot of failure. He rasped, his hand at his throat, fighting for sound. What cost?
He moved to the window slit. The cool air touched his face as he looked up. The sky was turning from black to a bruised, hopeful purple. He closed his eyes and whispered, recognizing the fundamental geometry of the world. His voice, thin and hoarse, was still there:
"Stardust to stardust…"
The small, ragged sound was instantly swallowed by the infinite black space he had just flown through.
He lay back down on the cot. He accepted the tremor in his throat as the price of the vision, and the numb sensation in his mouth as the new reality. He reached for his stylus, a piece of sharpened flint, and tried to grip it. His fingers, heavy and slick, refused. Clatter. The sound was immense. He forced his hand to move, trying to scratch 'MOTHER' into the limestone floor, but the stylus dragged, leaving only a faint, worthless scratch. His instrument was already shattered.
Below his room, the bedrock groaned—a low, grinding claim. Dagon, compacting his titanic mass beneath the house. But at his chamber door, something else stirred. A soft sound—not a rap, but a gentle, purring insistence. A wave of heat washed over his skin, a sweet, cloying sensation that promised ease. His entire body was a cold, aching refusal. Two presences now: one below, ancient and crushing. One at the entrance, watchful and strange.
He pulled the thin blanket up to his chin. He could not move. He could not speak. He could not write. The words No witness echoed as the last light failed.
