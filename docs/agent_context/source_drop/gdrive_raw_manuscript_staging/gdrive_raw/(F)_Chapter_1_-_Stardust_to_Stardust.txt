CHAPTER 1: STARDUST TO STARDUST

The trembling torch shuddered, a sick, copper light scattered across the corridor. The frail flame did not merely fight; it was throttled by the invading dark. Suspicious, jittering shadows danced across the narrow hallway. The small fire shivered, not from wind, but from the brutal beat of his own muffled heart: a rough, offbeat thrum fighting the crushing mass of a condemned dwelling.
Each calculated, barefoot step on the unforgiving limestone was a constant wager: his meager mass against the house's increasing gravimetric claim. The slightest pressure risked betrayal: a metallic sound, a dry scrape, a single clatter that could announce his forbidden errand. This was not silence. The air felt like the stale, waiting patience of serpents. The suffocating emptiness smelled of sulfur and expectant soil. His father's closed-off room held the precise, dead air of an opened tomb: the stagnant, sharp scent of a destiny that had been rigorously refused. The house was no dwelling; it was the heavy, unmoving lair of a man committed to the earth's sheer pull. Thus, the boy's every disciplined movement toward that door was an act of necessary heresy.
The night carried sounds. Crickets pulsed their urgent, needle-sharp chirping in the darkness. The wind dragged itself through brittle grass like a thousand whispers. A distant dog barked, a ragged tear through the stillness. Every sound threatened to expose him. The smaller the sound, the louder the threat; a loose stone rolling would feel louder than thunder, a cruel trick of the ear against the silence.
The four-room house had bled its warmth since the mother's songs ceased. It had since spread into ruin, a physical testament to Father Aviel's total, deliberate withdrawal of volition. The dissolution began the moment Mother Zuna disappeared. Aviel had fled Philistia seven years past, bringing his gods from the Western plains to Hebron. After Zuna's death, the low god Dagon became his only anchor. The man created a vile, intricate shrine built of forgotten things. It was consecrated by a scholar who willingly surrendered his life's momentum to the earth's claim. This divided house was cold, but with the specific, internal chill of a place the sun had never reached. The air itself was a heavy, saturated cloak. Every inhalation was an exhausting, burning chore. The entire structure pressed inward, the physical weight of his self-pity. It sought to crush its inhabitants back into formless dust. The ceiling beams sagged, not from age, but from the sheer, internal volume of the man's detachment.
Navigating its turns was not simple walking. One was forced to pick a deliberate path through the snare of matter. He felt its terrible, rooting pull with every step, like wading through sweet, viscous honey. Piles of broken pottery shards and failed instruments loomed like small, silent monuments to futures that died stillborn. Ruined brass astrolabes lay scattered. Cracked wooden compasses gathered dust. Rotting textiles—Mother Zuna's sacred threads left mid-shuttle—had unraveled into mildew and waste. The decaying fabrics sloughed off the looms, draping over discarded, brittle animal bones. They made a horrifyingly dry rustle: the sound of all life surrendering to the material. Rotting textiles teemed with maggots, their pale bodies a writhing, soft landscape on the fetid cloth. The stench—desiccated herbs mixed with excrement—stung his nostrils, sharp as ammonia. Swarms of flies rose from mounds of decaying food. Their buzzing was a morbid, living cloud over the corruption.
The failed records lay everywhere. Fragments of parchment and crumbling wax tablets, once holding records of birth, debt, and lineage, were scattered beneath the debris. They had been stripped clean of meaning by unseen, hungry mold. Withered sandals and rusted bronze tools lay scattered like the cast-off implements of a defeated messenger. Desiccated fruits, mummified burdens clinging to their seeds, were a stark testament to a harvest perpetually withheld. The water skins were brittle and cracked. They smelled of stale leather and the sharp mineral tang of absolute dryness, mocking the very concept of living clarity. Even the well, the house's source, had forgotten how to hold water. It was a hollow stone throat perpetually dry.
Dan, sixteen, watched helplessness curl into a bone-deep chill that seized his spine. It was born not just of the filth, but from the corrupt heart of a man who saw treasure in rot and comfort in self-pity. Father Aviel had been a scholar whose mind was sharp as flint. He had traded that bright, clean currency for the slow, agonizing drag of decay and the sticky joy of letting go. He had stopped the cleaning. He had ceased the daily, repetitive acts of maintenance. This neglect was the true terror. Maintenance held the chaos of the world at bay. He surrendered to the downward claim. Dan had to leave this place of reckoning before the decay claimed him, too.
"Before it swallows me whole," Dan did not mutter; the phrase was a single, expelled breath in the suffocating air.
He paused by the hearth in the main room. The glowing coals pulsed like an exhausted red heart in the blackness. His anchor, the Zuna stone, was cool and perfect against his ribs—the only simple thing he owned in a house devoted to the horrifying, beautiful density of the messy. He thumbed the smooth surface, a silent reminder of the tethering that remained when the worst of the world had escaped its confines. The stone's absolute coolness, a ghost of Zuna's hand, carried the faint, sharp metallic tang that marked the source—the unmanifest itself. It was a fragment of the high, white cold fire, a frozen echo of his mother's songs.
Dan crept to Aviel's doorframe and listened. A low, ragged snoring issued from within. The sound was of a man trying to outrun his own breath. The boy held his breath, eliminating the friction of his own presence. With a quiet snuff, he deliberately extinguished the torch. The area plunged into deep, drowning darkness. He relied only on the sharp memory of light and the faint moonlight filtering through a narrow window slit in the hallway wall. The pungent smell of burnt wick lingered: a bitter tang of parting, the first small expenditure of the night. The ache in Dan's chest was a fixed pressure. He remembered his father's final, damning dismissal from three nights past, a statement of total surrender to matter:
"If you're still searching for your source, boy," Aviel had hissed, his voice heavy with the sluggish drag of self-deception, "then pick up a stone and give it a name! Only a fool seeks answers from a power he cannot touch."
The words were bitter acid on Dan's tongue. Dan refused the stone, and the heavy earth it represented. He felt a question pull upward in his chest: If gods could be made of rocks, formed by the sheer weight of a man's attention and despair, then where should a searching heart truly turn? Dan’s eyes often searched upward, as if answers resided there, in the vast, swirling tapestry above. His dreams had always taken him to the high places: not fleeting images, but exercises of volition where he flew or climbed endless stairs, a constant preparation for the ascent.
"Mother said we're from the stars," he whispered to the darkness, a cool stone at his chest. "If that's a lie, then let me be a fool."
But this night's work wasn't for his own dreams. Though he was entering the Dreamscape—the sleeping mind of the world, a boundless place where thought was substance—it was the means he needed to access Aviel's mind. Dan knelt by his father's cot. The thin, crystalline cold of the floor immediately seized his bare knees. He slowed his breath, silencing the frantic, panicked voices in his mind as his Zuna had taught him. She had been a dreamwalker too, both blessed and cursed, for some feared her kind and whispered of witches who consorted with cats and commanded elements.
He remembered her hands: "Forget yesterday, forget tomorrow, forget now, just be. Empty the vessel," she would say, her voice echoing in his memory, "before you can fill it with the light."
He forced his mind empty. He chased the last fragments of earthly tethering away. He let go of the phantom scent of Zuna’s memory and the comforting shadow of Aviel’s history. The pressure of leaving the familiar was a sharp agony, a deep shedding. The stone beneath his knees dissolved. He waited until the final, cold friction of matter was gone. In that terrifying, perfect stillness—that necessary, terrible emptiness—he stepped sideways, and into the Dreamscape.
The immediate change was the profound, aching silence. He stood at the shimmering border of his father's sleep, a wall dense with years of self-deception that Dan could only absorb and pass through. Drawing a breath of pure intent, his fixed resolve pushed against the barrier. He hit the solid wall of shattered memories and black regret. For a terrifying, suspended breath, he was crushed under a sudden volume of pure sound: a child screaming, a hand slipping, a city's final death rattle. This rush of paralyzing grief—heavy with the stench of rot—was his father's internal debt. He channeled his will, demanding passage. The boundary gave way with a painful, inward implosion of pressure. He was released into a featureless field of raw creation.
Aviel stood alone in the center, waiting to dream. He was a solitary figure. Dust swirled around him like a shroud. Not the coarse dust of the earth, but something finer, eternal—the raw material of creation itself. His formless void teemed with potential, as things were before Time when the first words of truth were spoken. At Dan's volition, a surge of power flowed through him, primal and thrilling as a lightning strike. It woke forgotten currents in his blood. It made his spirit hum with profound, terrifying energy. Dan exhaled, a soundless act of command, a precise manipulation of the unseen ether:
"Let it rain."
The creation was instantaneous and corrupt, an accelerated genesis toward a state of total collapse. Glittering motes rained down. They knit together, hardening, forming smooth pebbles, then the heavy, crushing mass of boulders. It was the unnatural accumulation of matter, a devastating creation built upon the lie of rest. The dust, stirred by an unseen hand, began to dance. It coiled like living smoke, thick as morning mist, a swirling vortex of creation. It coalesced, thickening, darkening, taking on substance with a soft, ethereal sigh of transformation. By Dan's will and word, Aviel saw only treasure. His hands grasped at gold, copper, and fine silver. The noise of his hidden greed was a soundless scream in the dream. He shoved them into his pockets and then beneath his clothing, whispering, "Mine," hoarding his own delusion until his very form was distended, crushed. He became a lonesome pillar of stone in a barren field, the mass of the precious metal squeezing the breath from his lungs. The father's eyes glazed over. His mouth opened in a soundless gasp. The suffocating density of his possessions silenced him, permanently locking his hands in the posture of hoarding.
Then, with a final, deliberate breath that was both a sigh and a judgment, Dan summoned a wind. It was a cold, scouring current of pure intent. The shaping left him hollow, leaving a sharp ache behind his ribs. A sudden, cold draft rushed into his own lungs to replace the borrowed space. The wind swept across the field, gathering the grit and its crushing creations, carrying everything back to the void. In that brief moment of unburdening, the father looked up. He saw a single star pierce the gray, impossibly bright. But even there, in the star's radiant core, a colder, absolute light struggled to be born: a subtle, divine resentment prefiguring the final, eternal measure. Dan poured his final, potent intent into that fleeting vision: "I must depart this house. You must prepare your heart." He held the image of the star, imprinting it upon his father's mind, a non-material exertion of vast effort. Then, gently, with a deep breath, he released his father's consciousness. He let him drift in the gentle rhythm of sleep. Aviel's mind would remain dreamless for the rest of the night, a silent respite from the chaos.
The stench hit him first—rot, waste, surrender's sweet perfume—before the cold floor reregistered beneath his feet. He was drained, but a purpose that felt like a raw wound drove him. Dan left his father's room. He sat up, gasping for breath. The weight of his limbs was a betrayal: lead poured into the bones of his arms. A fissure of cold pain hammered behind his eyes. The familiar stench of the house returned with sickening clarity. Dawn was just beginning to etch a cold, gray line through the narrow window slit. The cost of his defiance instantly registered as a rigid, painful knot in his muscles. His jaw felt tight. His breathing was shallow, a frantic, high-pitched whistle. He lifted a trembling hand to his face. The fingers felt swollen, sluggish. He tried to speak the simplest word: Mother. His mouth moved, but the syllables were caught, a cold, dense knot in his throat. He felt a wave of crushing paralysis that lasted only a terrifying second. It left his tongue heavy and numb. The flesh of his lips felt like old leather—the first physical wound of the curse.
"Mother. Moth—" The word caught, a dense knot of failure on his numb tongue. He rasped, his hand at his throat, fighting for any sound. What cost?
Moving to the slit in the wall, the cool air brushing his face, he looked up. The sky was turning from black to a bruised, hopeful purple. He closed his eyes and whispered, recognizing the fundamental geometry of the world. His voice, thin and hoarse, was still there:
"Stardust to stardust…"
The small, ragged sound was instantly swallowed by the infinite, black geometry of the void he had once flown through.
He lay back down on the cot. His gaze fixed on the ceiling. The tremor in his throat was the price of the vision. The numb sensation in his mouth was the new reality. He reached for a stylus—a small, sharpened piece of flint used for wax tablets—and tried to grip it. His fingers, heavy and slick, refused. He dropped it. Clatter. The sound felt immense. He tried to scratch the word 'MOTHER' into the limestone floor. He forced his hand to move. The stylus dragged, leaving only a faint, broken line—a single, worthless scratch. The truth was simple: his instrument was already shattered.
Below his room, the bedrock groaned—a low, grinding claim. Dagon, compacting his titanic weight beneath the house. But at his chamber door, something else stirred. A soft sound—not quite tapping, not quite rapping—a gentle insistence at the threshold. A wave of heat washed over his exposed skin, a sweet, cloying sensation. It was a rhythmic vibration, felt first not in his ears, but deep in his sternum, beneath the Zuna stone, a soundless hum that promised ease. His entire body was a cold, aching restraint. Two presences now: one below, ancient and crushing. One at the entrance, a watchman of agonizing beauty.
He pressed his eyes shut. He pulled the thin blanket to his chin, a final, futile barrier against the terror. He could not move. He could not speak. He could not write. The words No witness echoed as the last light failed.
