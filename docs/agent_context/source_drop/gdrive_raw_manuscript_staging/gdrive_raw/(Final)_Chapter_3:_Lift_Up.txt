Chapter 3: Lift Up

The air, which had been dry and light, now grew thick and heavy with a promise of paradise; cloyingly sweet with the narcotic scent of jasmine and the heavy, earthy aroma of ripe pomegranates. The well water, which had initially promised the sterile, life-giving clarity of the desert's heart, had instantly and violently betrayed itself, twisting into a mirror for a presence utterly alien to the simple thirst of flesh. It was a phenomenon so jarringly otherworldly, so potent a manifestation of the unseen realm, that Dan's weary mind, already thinned by the day's relentless psychological stripping, struggled to hold its functional shape against the mental assault.

The vision that had coalesced was not a mere, passive reflection; it was an active, shimmering, impossible entity, rising with deliberate, hypnotic speed from the dark, motionless depths of the well. A being of impossible, almost agonizing beauty emerged, a form not fixed, but fluid, instantly and perfectly wrought to embody the utmost, innermost desire of the consciousness that beheld it.

A dizzying, profound emptiness, a hunger for the absolute end of all struggle and of gravity that pulled inward toward peace, settled deep in the pit of his stomach. It was the desire to simply lay down his burden and reap what he had not sown.

They were adorned not with mere metal and stone, but with the very essence of them: rubies that pulsed with the primal, undeniable warmth of a lover’s heart, emeralds that gleamed with the earth’s promise of green pastures and endless harvest, and sapphires that held the cool, deep mystery of a night not yet born. Symbols of fertility and abundance, carved from ancient bone and polished stone, seemed to orbit them like silent satellites, humming with a vibrant, potent energy that sang a promise of solace, softness, and the comforting, irresistible gravity of the soil. Their eyes, luminous and impossibly ancient, were pools of shadowed desire and cold starlight, promising the culmination of every deeply buried hunger for enough. This was not a creature of simple, breakable flesh. This was a goddess woven from the fabric of life's deepest, most animal needs, a being whose power lay in her utter command over the beautiful, terrible reality of existence.

“I am Lady Astarte,” the figure boomed, their voice a coiling, inescapable tide, an ancient, familiar murmur dragging Dan into its fertile, intoxicating depths. “I am the lord of fertility and the lady of tribute. For this purpose I wrought in the low places, and for this purpose I rose. Their presence pulsed from the well water, a silent, powerful energy, a force that resonated not with the light in his mind, but with the very lifeblood in his veins. It struck a primal, insistent rhythm deep in the hollows of his bones, demanding to be fed. And they spoke, their voice a sound woven from moonlight, rustling silk, and the murmur of ancient rivers swollen with life, an intoxicating harmony that bypassed his ears entirely. It echoed directly in his deepest chambers, a melody both terrifying and irresistibly sweet, a perfect, sophisticated attack on the ongoing, grinding struggle between body and mind he fought. It was a siren song promising an end to the inner battle, a final, irresistible conclusion to his lifelong striving.

They smiled, a slow, devastating expression that promised every earthly delight, a surrender to the simple, comforting leaven of existence. The well water swirled faster, the reflection of Astarte growing clearer, almost agonizingly vivid, as if all the available light in the twilight sky was being sucked into her image. The thick, sweet scent of jasmine and rich earth, a dizzying perfume that promised oblivion and a fatness of existence, rose heavily from the well, mingling with the dry desert air. Dan stumbled back, his hands scraping the rough, abrasive stone curb of the well. His mind reeled, a knot of confusion and exhaustion. His body, already broken by the long day’s march, the sun’s relentless weight, and the gnawing, physical thirst, felt an abrupt, profound, and debilitating weakness—a vulnerability that craved nothing more than relief that overrode all logic. He was profoundly alone, and the solitude, his chosen price, now clawed with desperate fingers at the raw edges of the courage of his convictions. The desire to simply turn back, to find a warm place and dwell securely, was overwhelming.

"[PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] path is one of utter emptiness," Astarte continued, their voice a silken ribbon unspooling in his mind, drawing him in even as he tried, desperately, to recoil. "[PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] seekest cold, distant stars, Dan, when all joy is found here: in the full, unyielding currents of the soil, the vine, the fruitful womb—my holy domain. Why deny the simple density of a full belly and the warmth of companionship? That solitary quest rejects the sustainable power that flows from the earth itself. It lacks the true courage of convictions in this green earth's essential abundance; for life is found in connection, not alone in the dust you seek to refine. Give over your burdensome friction, and accept the absolute rest.”

Rest. A grounding image flashed in his mind, a cold, sharp blade of clarity: his father, suffocated in the squalor of his own choices, crushed by the very weight of "things" and the sorrow of his own creation. The stagnant air, the rotting piles, the festering psychological decay—the crushing, visible reality of a father's deterioration. He remembered the chill, pure air of the peaks from his dream-flights and the exhilarating feeling of shedding burdens, of finding the unburdened way. He remembered the quiet, agonizing shame of his house, the stench of decay that was worse than any physical illness. He knew, with a certainty that shook him, that Astarte's promise was merely a beautiful, polished version of the same lie of rest that had deceived his father. 

He lifted his head, wrenching his gaze away from the swirling depths with a physical effort that felt like tearing muscle from bone, fixing his eyes on the distant horizon, where the faint glimmer of the first stars would soon appear, tiny pinpricks of pure, indifferent light. "I'm not seeking a settled life, Lady Astarte," Dan said, his voice raw and trembling, but now infused with a nascent, unyielding defiance. "Your abundance… It feels heavy. It clings. It demands a terrible tribute that I refuse to pay—the tribute of my will's ascending clarity.”

Astarte's beautiful face in the water contorted violently, a flash of pure, cold fury passing through their luminous eyes. The seductive growl hardened, gaining an edge of ancient, terrible, and crushing power. "Heavy? Nay! it is the very tapestry of existence. [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] art but a frail thread, and [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] dost presume to sever thyself from the weave? Foolish boy! [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] pride shall be [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] fatal flaw. To ascend to the barren, to float among the cold, distant lights, is to know truly nothing but the bleakest of all solitudes, a loneliness that will devour your very functional Self."

The well water began to boil violently, sending up plumes of fragrant steam that coated Dan's face like a shroud, burning his skin, the cost of his sacrifice made physical and immediate. Astarte's terrible image expanded, filling his vision, twisting into something monstrous, its form shifting and blurring at the edges, a dizzying specter of desire. The air grew thick and heavy, each inhale a painful struggle against an unseen weight—the tangible form of the chaos given flesh. He felt an invisible, crushing pressure threatening to drive him back into the earth, amplified a thousandfold from his father’s nightmare. The very ground trembled with her wrath. "[PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] art a frail, trembling thing!" Astarte's voice boomed, no longer a purr or growl but a resonant gong striking against the inside of his skull, demanding his submission, threatening to break his very shibboleth.

"To turn from the warmth of the soil, the embrace of the living, for what? for empty air? for the cold, indifferent gaze of distant stars? I tell [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE], boy, the vast, silent cosmos cares not for [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] puny aspirations! [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] art born of the earth, and to the earth shalt [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] return! Your ascent is a fiction of a proud and foolish heart!" The pressure intensified, forcing him to his knees beside the well. His head swam, and black spots danced before his eyes. He felt a profound sense of loss, a vital, living cord severed, as though something essential were being ripped from him, piece by agonizing piece. This was the sharp, somatic cost of refusing such overwhelming power.

But even as the darkness threatened to consume him, a grounding image pierced through the terror: the memory of the single, distant star from his vision, the one his father had dismissed, now grew brighter. He heard Zuna’s voice, faint but clear, breaking through the din of the goddess’s rage: “Up, my little pigeon. always up.” He gritted his teeth, digging his fingers into the rough, unforgiving stone of the well-curb. His muscles screamed with the effort, his body convulsing against the force, but he refused to fall entirely. His head was bowed in pain, but his core determination was not broken. He whispered, his voice hoarse, barely audible above the booming wrath of Astarte, but utterly resolute. “No. I will not return. I seek the light. I seek the truth that is above, not the lie that is below. I will not be pulled down by the burdens of the flesh, nor the comforting lies of the earth’s tribute!”

As Dan uttered those words, a seismic, chilling shriek tore through the air from the well—the sound of ancient power denied, of cosmic rage unleashed, a sound that cracked the very stones and sent a shiver of primordial dread down his spine. The image of Astarte flared, their face dissolving into a vortex of swirling dark water. "If ever [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] shouldst need me, child, merely knock thrice, ask twice, and I shall come, for I am the certainty you seek to outrun—”
The scent of jasmine and earth curdled instantly into something acrid, like burnt offerings and decaying flowers. The well water roiled violently, surging over the edge, drenching Dan in a cold, muddy spray that smelled not of life, but of death and corruption. He gasped, choking, spitting out the foul water, tasting the metallic tang of old blood and regret.

When he looked up, gasping for breath, the well was placid once more, reflecting only the distant, innocent stars beginning to pierce the twilight sky. Astarte was gone. Dan stood, shaky but utterly resolute, his clothes plastered to his skin, shivering despite the lingering heat of the day. He had faced a goddess, or something terrifyingly close, and had not broken. The exhaustion was bone-deep, a heavy, aching weight in his limbs, but beneath it, a strange, exhilarating lightness began to hum, a strength forged in the crucible of divine temptation. He had refused the goddess of the earth. Without filling his container, Dan turned and continued north, his pace unsteady at first, then firming with every step he took away from the cursed well and its potent noose.

He walked until the last light faded, navigating the rough, unpaved track with only the faint starlight to guide him. The path, far from being a smooth road, was a constant succession of loose stones, ankle-twisting ruts, and dried, stubborn clumps of earth that reminded him with every jarring step that the ascent required a constant, physical vigilance. His feet were raw, the cuts on his soles stinging with every contact, a constant, low-grade reminder of the path he chose.

He reached Bethlehem as the light faded entirely and night settled, the low hills softening into shadows like sleeping giants. He found a shepherd's shelter willing to offer him a corner for the night, sharing a meager meal of hard bread and cheese with the family, paying with a single, small gerah—a final, tiny tether to the earth he was stripping away. The low murmur of voices from nearby homes and the distant bleating of sheep on the hillsides, sounds as comforting as an old lullaby, drew him outside the shelter for a moment.

Bethlehem, even as evening settled, felt older, tied to the quiet rhythm of flocks and fields, but also expectant, alive with whispers of the times. It felt like a place rooted in the soil, dreaming ancient dreams of a kingdom that would dwell securely. He saw a few townsfolk gathered near a lamp hanging by a doorway, their faces illuminated in the soft glow, looking like faces carved from warm wood as they spoke in hushed tones. Curiosity, that familiar companion, drew him closer. He lingered just within earshot, listening.

Dan listened, the talk of leaders and uncertain futures an echo against the battles he had just fought. He heard a man declare, "King David! A true warrior, that one! He'll bring us peace and finally secure the inheritance of our land!"

A woman's voice, softer but firm, added, "No more Philistines raiding our threshing floors, not with him on the throne! The high power is clearly with him, guiding his hand!"

Another chimed in, "Born right here, in Bethlehem soil, he was! He won't forget us!" The quiet talk wove through admiration for his deeds and a soft anticipation of what his continued leadership might mean.

"A brighter future, a fullness in the vineyard—that's what he promises," someone murmured, "A true, earthly king for all Israel!"

He stood there for a while longer, absorbing the quiet hope and underlying anticipation of the town, feeling the weight of their collective outlook, like carrying a basket full of wishes. He then returned inside the shelter, lay down on a rough cot, the conversations of the townsfolk fading into the background like distant music, soft and indistinct. The exhaustion was immense but the strange lightness, the exhilaration born of resistance and resolve, still shimmered beneath it. He had held his ground against the very forces that sought to pull him down or root him firmly in the earth, and that defiance felt like wings. Sleep claimed him instantly. It was a deep sleep, deeper than any he had known before, uncluttered by the anxieties of the house or the lingering presence of dark gods. He slipped effortlessly into the dreamscape, but this time, he was not navigating a desolate field or a stormy sea. He was already moving.

Up.

He rose with incredible acceleration, leaving the sleeping city of Bethlehem far below, a tiny cluster of lights on the back of the world. The ground fell away with dizzying speed, the familiar hills flattening. He shot through the heavens, the air growing impossibly thin and cold, the sounds of the world fading to an absolute, profound silence. He shot past clouds, vast and swirling like celestial oceans, painted in impossible colours by the sun he was rapidly approaching. The moon, a colossal pearl, grew rapidly in his vision, a pockmarked, silent world hanging in the blackness. He passed it in an instant, and he noticed, with undeniable certainty, that the moon smiled at him. He felt a pull, a strange, insistent tug, as if the moon were trying to draw him in, like a great stone in the sky pulling at the water below. But the very core of him, hardened by refusal and lightened by shedding, offered no purchase to its ancient grasp. He was able to go even higher.

The sun itself began to shrink, becoming just another bright star as he left the dominion of its light. As he soared past it, a flash of red light from its surface revealed, for just a single heartbeat, the fleeting illusion of a face, one so familiar yet twisted by a chilling, resentful fury. Then it was gone, swallowed by the silent vacuum of space, leaving behind a cold shadow. And then, the countless stars erupted around him. This was new, higher than he had ever reached before. They didn't appear one by one, scattered across a distant canvas. Instead, as his speed surged, the very air seemed to stretch and twist, pulling him forward. The stars ahead, towards which he was hurtling, converged, their light shifting into piercing, brilliant points that crowded together, forming a blinding, shimmering field—a waterfall of pure light. The stars to his sides, meanwhile, seemed to stretch and smear into long, thin streaks of light, like cosmic rain falling past him with impossible speed. He flew through their midst, sensing the chilling emptiness, yet completely bathed in their ancient, pure light. 

The silence was absolute, broken only by the silent rush of the light streaks as they blurred past. He saw celestial clouds not as distant smudges, but as vast, swirling cathedrals of gas and dust, painted in colours that had no names in the waking world – electric blues that hummed with unseen energy, fiery oranges that burned with silent heat, deep, velvety purples that swallowed light – structures so immense they dwarfed any mountain, any city, any world he had ever known. He was among the stars which had been waiting patiently, the ones his people had named and told stories about for generations. A tremor of pure excitement coursed through him, a wonder so vast it made his heart ache. He felt utterly insignificant, a tiny speck, yet perfectly at home in this cosmic ocean. He was a vessel, emptied and ready to be filled with this light, a son returning to his true source—the unmanifest. He felt no fear here, only awe and a profound sense of belonging. This was the threshold of a new life, a place of lightness and clarity, utterly beyond the suffocating density of his father's house. He had cast off those earthly entanglements, and his dreams, fueled by his newfound will, had carried him here. 

He had reached the stars.

He still didn't understand the full meaning of his journey, nor the ultimate destination of his ascent. He didn't know what lay ahead, or what other ancient beings might test his resolve. But he knew, with a certainty that resonated in his very center of gravity, that he was no longer just running from something. He was moving towards something. Something vast and true and utterly beyond the confines of earthly desires. He was moving towards the stars, and the stars, he now knew, were moving towards him.
