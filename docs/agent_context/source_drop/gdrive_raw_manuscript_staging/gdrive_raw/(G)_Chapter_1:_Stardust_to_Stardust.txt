Chapter 1: Stardust to Stardust
The torch light, clenched in Dan's hand, trembled, casting flickering shadows across the narrow hallway. The frail flame scattered light like cheap, copper coins. It was a nervous thing. It shivered not from wind, but from the erratic tempo of Dan's muffled heart, a frantic drummer in a condemned structure.
Each barefoot step on the cold, sun-baked stone was a profound, life-or-death gamble. The slightest misstep risked the betrayal of a clank, a dry scrape, or any sound that might announce his small, forbidden errand. This was no mere silence; it was a coiled, ancient emptiness that reeked of sulfur and dust that held its breath. It was the patient, waiting dread of the stone in the field.
This was no home, but the heavy, unmoving lair of a man devoted to the earth’s sheer, magnetic weight. Dan moved with careful steps toward the door. Each movement was an act of quiet, necessary treason.
The four-room house, which had once been a space warmed by Zuna’s songs, stopped being a simple structure. It had grown with a beautiful, terrible cancer not merely into a ruin, but a physical testament to Aviel’s catastrophic unraveling since Zuna’s disappearance. The house was ancient, built during the reign of King David, but Dan’s father, Aviel, had brought his own gods with him from the Western plains. The boy, Dan, sixteen years old, understood this decay not as accident, but as design. It was a vile, intricate graveyard of sacred failures, a temple built of forgotten things, dedicated by a scholar who chose to worship the memory of his own disaster, which is to say: a life willingly surrendered to gravity.
He had abandoned the small, repetitive acts that hold chaos at bay; he had failed to put his own house in order before judging the world outside. Navigating its turns was not simple walking; one was forced to pick a path through the trap of things, feeling its terrible, rooting gravity with every breath. The air itself held a heavy, unpayable debt. The entire place felt like a crushing pressure applied inward, seeking to crush its inhabitants back into the formless clay from which all flesh begins.
________________

The house was cold, but with the specific, internal chill of a place the sun had abandoned. Piles of broken pottery shards and broken tools—things that had betrayed their original purpose—loomed like small, silent monuments to futures that died stillborn.
Rotting textiles, Zuna’s sacred threads left mid-shuttle, had unraveled into mildew and waste, a promise broken by overwhelming grief. The decaying fabrics sloughed off the looms, draping over discarded, brittle animal bones, making a horrifyingly dry rustle—the sound of the spirit surrendering to the clay.
The bones were everywhere: small, sharp ribs and smooth, bleached vertebrae scattered beneath the refuse, meticulously stripped clean by unseen, hungry life. Clay tablets, once stacked neatly with Aviel’s luminous texts on the stars and the Word, were now tumbled and cracked, their wisdom turning to the most meaningless kind of silt. The weight of the powder itself was their only covenant now. Desiccated fruits, once heavy with the promise of life, now clung to their seeds like mummified burdens, a stark testament to a harvest withheld. The water skins were brittle and cracked, smelling of stale leather and profound thirst, mocking the very concept of living water. Even the well, the house’s source, had stopped remembering how to hold water. Thirst was the final state of everything here, a physical manifestation of a profound internal craving.
________________

The air was a thick, sugary bruise on the tongue, the kind of sickeningly-sweet stench that breeds in closed places and calls itself comfort. It was the heavy, sticky breathing of a place that devours its own air. Dan, whose eyes had witnessed too much raw, spiritual surrender, picked his way through the refuse. A bone-deep chill settled on his spine, born not just of the filth, but from the soul-deep mistake of a man who saw treasure in rot and comfort in pity. Aviel, his father, had been a scholar whose mind was sharp as flint. He had traded that bright, clean currency for the slow, sticky joy of letting go.
He had ceased the daily rites of cleaning and maintenance, the small, repetitive acts that hold the chaos of the world at bay. This deliberate cessation of order, this ancient surrender to decay, was the true horror. It was the choice to stop fighting gravity, a choice that makes the earth cling to you a little too much. The profound silence of the house was punctuated only by the low, organic sounds of decay: the drip of moisture, the scuttle of insects, the inevitable, soft collapse of neglected rooms. Silence was the final word.
Dan had to leave this place of reckoning before the material decay claimed him, too. Turning back was not an option—it was a history of failure written in the first book of all beginnings.
________________

He paused by the hearth, where the glowing coals pulsed like a small, exhausted red heart in the surrounding blackness. His anchor, clutched in his hand, was the Zuna stone: a river rock his Mother had rendered perfect and cool against his ribs. It was the one truly simple thing he owned in a house dedicated to the beautiful, horrifying complexity of the messy. He didn't know the full history of the stone, or the tale that was longer than the threads in a ball of yarn, but he knew his Mother’s hands were capable of a quiet, subtle magic that changed things forever.
As he reached his father's doorframe, he held his breath, and with a quiet snuff, deliberately extinguished the torch. He plunged the area into a deep, choking darkness, relying only on the sharp memory of light and the faint moonlight filtering through a narrow slit in the hallway wall. The pungent smell of burnt wick lingered, a bitter tang of parting.
His Father Aviel was deep asleep. This sleeper was something else—a being who had chosen to stop climbing. The ache in Dan's chest was a cold, dull throb—the physical cost for the path he was about to take.
He remembered his father’s final, damning dismissal:
"If you must have a god, boy, then pick up a stone and give it your attention. That is how the thing is done."

"Belief is attention, nothing more."
The words were a bitter acid, a justification for his father's descent into materialism, where only tangible things held power. If power could be forged by the crushing weight of misguided will, then where should an unburdened heart turn? Dan’s gaze instinctively sought the open sky, tilting his head toward the narrow slit of the window, searching the vast, indifferent tapestry above. He was already focusing his intent, preparing for the difficult work. Dreams had always taken him there—not fleeting images, but profound, deliberate exercises of will. He looked up now, and the stars gazed back, ancient and knowing, their silent light a beacon of promise. That was where he would direct his absolute attention.
________________

Dan knelt by his father's cot. The cold stone of the floor immediately seized his bare knees, the sensation sharp and immediate. He slowed his breath, silencing the frantic voices in his mind as his Mother, Zuna, had taught him.
He remembered her hands—rough, reddened from the lye, scrubbing their pots until they gleamed. Her knuckles would bleed sometimes, the skin raw from the ash and sand.
"The last scrubbing is the one that matters, Dan."

"Do not fear the emptiness. Make the vessel empty before you try to use it. Otherwise, the clarity will only reveal, and then burn, the dirt you hold."
He forced his mind empty, chasing the last fragments of earthly attachment away. He let go of the phantom scent of Zuna’s memory and the comforting shadow of his father's history. The pressure of leaving the familiar was a sharp agony, a physical shedding. The stone beneath his knees dissolved, not instantly, but with a horrifying, slow certainty, as if the floor itself was sinking into quicksand. He waited through a full breath before the sensation of matter completely vanished. In that terrifying, perfect stillness—that necessary, terrible emptiness—he stepped sideways and into the field of the dream.
The journey began not with a lift, but a powerful commitment.
He stood at the base of a braided rope of solidified starlight, a line of perfect, cold geometry stretching into the black overhead. The line was blood-warm, slick with something between sweat and oil, and it vibrated with the predatory hum of the emptiness it pierced. Each grip burned with tiny splinters that felt alive, burrowing into his palms. He climbed until the air around him was thin and cold, ringing with the silence of the void. He thought of his usual dreams: walking up stairs, jumping high, always seeking the top. Now the climb was real.
________________

The intensity of the upward struggle reached a breaking point. He had to exchange the constant, physical effort for the heavier, stranger work of his will. He looked at the rope, then at the vast, star-shot blackness, and understood the final, terrible demand: He had to shed not only his body's weight but the weight of the unpayable debt. He tore his hands free, a soundless ripping sensation in the deep marrow. The rope, rejected, simply unwound from his perception and vanished. He had unstitched himself from the climb, shedding the concept of time itself.
He fell, a terrifying, absolute surrender, plunging into a stagnant, empty field illuminated by an ethereal gray light. He had landed, with the soundless impact of stone dropped into velvet, deep within his father's sleeping mind.
The father stood alone. Dust began to swirl around him, the raw, indistinguishable material of creation. Dan drew a breath of pure intent, and a primal surge of focus flowed through him. The dust, stirred by his absolute intention, coiled into a vortex.
"Behold the dust."
The creation was instantaneous and corrupt. First, glittering motes of sand rained down, then knit together, hardening, forming smooth pebbles and then heavy stones that clicked softly as they accumulated. It was the unnatural, accelerated genesis of a devastating weight.
________________

The father saw gold. His hands moved through the stones, but his eyes saw talents, oil jars, linen—everything he’d ever wanted. He wept as he gathered them, his fingers frantic, the noise of his hidden greed a soundless scream in the dream. He shoved them into his pockets and then beneath his clothing.
"Mine,"
He whispered, hoarding his own delusion, collecting the ultimate, silent pillar of permanent regret.
He gathered the ever-burgeoning weight until his very form was distended, crushed. He became a silent column in a barren field, the accumulation squeezing the breath from his lungs. The father's eyes glazed over, his mouth open in a soundless gasp. The suffocating mass of his possessions silenced him, permanently locking his hands in the posture of hoarding.
Then, with a final, deliberate breath that was both a sigh and a judgment, Dan summoned a wind, a quiet, purifying current. The shaping left him hollow, leaving a sharp, internal ache behind his ribs. The wind swept across the field, gathering the dust and its crushing, stone creations, carrying everything back to the nothingness. In that brief moment of relief, the father looked up and saw a single star pierce the gray, impossibly bright. But even there, in the star's radiant core, a colder light struggled to be born, a subtle resentment prefiguring the debt collector.
Dan poured his final, potent intent into that fleeting vision:
"Go and tell the truth."
He held the image of the star, imprinting it upon his father's mind. Then, gently, he released his father's awareness.
________________

The stench hit him first—rot, waste, surrender's sweet perfume—before the cold stone of the floor registered beneath his feet. The coldness was a physical ache that settled in his bones. Drained but with renewed sense of purpose, Dan left his father's room. The darkness of the hallway was a sudden, heavy, oppressive presence. He felt smaller now, his confidence spent. He felt the cold stone beneath his bare feet, a shocking return to matter. The shadows seemed to watch as he carefully navigated the maze on the now freezing stone floor.
He knelt by the glowing coals. He extended a steady hand, coaxing a flame from the embers with a small piece of kindling. The flame caught with a quiet hiss. He lit his torch, its light a familiar, hopeful presence in the gloom, and with new, resolute purpose, left the main room.
Back in his room, the door closed behind him. Dan's small room was a meticulously tidy corner of perfect order—the hard-won proof that he had put his own house in order. This was his sanctuary, his final act of self-possession. He sought oblivion on the cot. Yet, even as the darkness claimed him, a chilling dread coiled around his fading awareness. Sleep was not rest but summons.
________________

He found himself standing on a shore of glittering, black sand. The water was unnaturally calm, a sheet of polished glass. He stepped out onto the water, and it held him. He was halfway across the cove when the glass shattered with a deafening crack, pulling the false foundation out from under him. The peaceful blue instantly bled into an angry, bruised purple. The dream was no longer his.
The sea did not merely roar; it was a maelstrom of pure, liquid stone and bitter brine. He found himself clinging to a single, inadequate plank of wood. Colossal, black waves crashed over him, the cold stench of the deep clinging to the foam. The sky, a suffocating, churning blanket of obsidian blackness, offered no escape.
Then, from the impossible, crushing depths, a vast, ancient presence rose, eclipsing the world. A colossal head emerged from the waves, a mountain of wet, salt-caked stone. The face was impassive, carved from drowned earth and time, radiating an inhuman, profound cold. His unblinking eyes, vast and black like the starless void, locked onto Dan's. The body, vast and unknowable, stretched beneath the waves, connecting the stone head to a monstrous, serpentine tail fin far to the north. The staggering scale of the entity defied light and reason, an entity of absolute material chaos.
The plank tilted perilously. The voice, ancient and vast, echoed not from the mouth, but from the depths of Dan's own skull, a resonant thrum that shook his teeth. The air choked with salt, the cold stench of the deep.
"I am Dagon. The god your father chose. Lord of the beautiful, terrible abundance. Ask and receive."
Dan choked out, his throat burning with brine.
"I did not call you!"

"You have ever called," Dagon rumbled. "Your very flesh does so. But this night have you sinned, daring to undo my work in your father's house. Do not mistake your petty will for a living oblation."
Dagon leaned closer, a movement that displaced mountains of water, causing a secondary wave to crash over Dan.
"Come, cast yourself down... I shall give unto you your mother's voice in the night—dreams wherein she speaks comfort. I shall bestow a wife whose hands need not be reddened by lye, and sons who shall never know the stench of their grandfather's shame. I offer you the life your mother desired for you—peaceful, whole, and forgotten by the terrible heights that demand all."

"Abundance of things?" Dan spat, choking on the salt water. "Why devote my life to things I cannot carry into death? Your statue kept on crumbling before the sacred chest. You are nothing! A false promise carved from stone! And your abundance is only a fancy word for rot and weight!"
Dagon's eyes flashed, his patience dissolving into profound, ancient offense.
"My temple fell not for my weakness, boy. My statue crumbled not for my lacking. Covenant is the sole bridge between realms. There exist ancient lines which must not be breached. I can only whisper. Mistake not my ancient restraint for weakness."
Dan snarled, the absolute rejection silencing the roar of the sea for a terrible second.
"I'd rather drown standing than float in your shadow!"
The colossal head surged back, delivering the sentence.
"The star you become shall burn alone. The one who pursues the high path becomes the monument—cold, absolute, isolated. Choose the heights if you must, but know this: the star you become shall burn alone."
Dagon’s voice dropped, becoming a crushing, personal judgment that bypassed his ears and lodged itself directly in his consciousness.
"You might live. But those whom you hold dear shall live too, and in their living find only hardship. When you speak, no man shall understand. Your tongue shall be made a heavy stone, speaking a truth that is only heard as noise—the worthless babble of the judged."
Dagon's form surged forward, a tidal wave of darkness. The dream began to fracture, lines of blinding, white-hot light cracking the suffocating blackness.
"If ever you should alter your score," Dagon hissed, his voice the sound of massive, dry stone grinding against the bedrock as the nightmare diminished, "then merely knock thrice, ask twice, then wait. I shall come, for I am the cadence you seek to outrun."

Dan awoke with a strangled, silent cry. His body was slick with sweat, but a deep, internal chill had taken hold. The sea's phantom roar still echoed in his ears, a static memory of brine and chaos. He sat up, gasping for breath, the familiar stench of the house returning with sickening clarity. Dawn was just beginning to etch a cold, gray line through the narrow slit in the wall.
He was not merely terrified; he was shattered. The cost of his defiance was immediately registered in his physical being: his muscles were clenched, his jaw was locked, and his breathing was shallow, a frantic, high-pitched whistle. He had poured his conscious will into shaping his father’s dream and then spent his reserve defying a cosmic god. The terror was not the physical threat, but the promise of utter, total solitude—to possess a truth so profound that it rendered him mute, a prophet whose voice was only ever heard as worthless babble—the language of the judged. This was the curse of the chaos he sought to escape: to be perpetually misunderstood, cast out from the shared language of mankind. This was the silence of a covenant broken, directed solely at him.
He pressed the Zuna stone hard against his chest, the cool, smooth surface a tiny anchor of reality against the chaos. He walked to the slit in the wall and looked up. The sky was turning from black to a bruised, hopeful purple. He closed his eyes and whispered, the words a weary, but absolute commitment, not to the world below:
"Stardust to stardust…"
He lay back down on the cot, his gaze fixed on the ceiling. He had to be still. He had to wait for the sun to rise completely, for the safety of the full day. The time for action was not yet. The time now was for absorbing the sentence, for letting the cost of his defiance become part of his marrow.
He closed his eyes, accepting the tremor in his throat as the price of the vision. The phantom ache in his tongue was already settling in—a heavy, numb sensation, as if the muscle was already beginning its slow petrification. In the stillness, he felt it: a faint, resonant thrum—the deep, rhythmic friction of massive stone on stone, settling into the bedrock beneath the house. It was Dagon, claiming the earth under his feet, making his claim absolute.
He smiled, a grim, exhausted expression of defiance, recognizing the horror of his self-imposed isolation and the futility of his choice, yet embracing it anyway.
He had chosen the cold fire of the star over the warm rot of the sea. The suffering was absolute. The struggle was eternal. The climb had begun.
He felt a tiny shift in the air, a presence at the threshold of his small room, a soft, vibrating hum that was not quite a sound. It was a low, steady purr, waiting for the day to begin.
