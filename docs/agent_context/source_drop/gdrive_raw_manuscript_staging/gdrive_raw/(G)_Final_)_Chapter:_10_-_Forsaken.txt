CHAPTER 10: FORSAKEN

The interior of the goat-hair sanctuary carried the particular weight of a room where words have been spoken at length and received in silence.

For a daughter of the Peleset—broad-boned, deep-voiced, the kind of woman whose craft pressed down into the dense, layered record of the valley floor rather than reaching upward at the cold and assessless dark above it—this goat-hair tent, sewn tight against the Jezreel wind, was not shelter. It was second skin. A dark womb built to keep the frantic, staccato percussion of the self from the cold, judgmental eye of the deep sky. A space where she could pretend that Ba'al-Zebub of Ekron still leaned in from the low places, still weighed the petition, still kept the long account she had been depositing into for thirty years—even as the silence in the tent's corners settled the way moth settles into neglected gold: consuming one patient layer at a time, until what had been solid certainty was mostly void. I should know. I was sitting in the corner when it happened.

It had begun, as all failures begin, imperceptibly. Her fingers—usually steady as she pressed marks into wet clay in the petition-form, or cast the smooth knucklebones by lamplight in the reading-cast her teacher had called the scatter of honest bones—now drifted. She would reach for the hyssop sprig and find her hand resting on a shard of basalt instead; set the basalt down; find her hand back on it. Her mind attended to everything simultaneously, which is another name for attending to nothing: the hyssop, the scroll's re-oiling, the midday casting she had not done, the fig jar empty again with no memory of the emptying. The morning had grown indistinguishable from the dusk—she would mark the sun's angle through the canvas weave and find herself genuinely surprised that the world had continued while she remained suspended in a thought of the boy. The figs were gone. She was not sure she had eaten them.

She had been watching the boy for the better part of a year through the bronze scrying vessel—not by intention, not at first. He had wandered into the edge of her reading when she was attending to something else: a small figure on the north road with kind eyes that held more sorrow than a boy should carry and unruly black hair that caught the light like a problem looking for somewhere to land. What drew her was not brightness—that quality she associated with the hill-people and their upward-reaching religion, which gave her teeth a specific ache. What drew her was the weight of his direction. His aim registered in the long bones of her legs the way a heavy stone registers in the palm: by its pressure, its refusal to pretend it weighs less than it does. She had intercepted his dreamwalk into his sleeping father's room—a frantic, rhythmic reach through the shifting gray, dust shaped into the weight of his meaning—and felt both awe and resentment in equal measure. She had watched him lose everything he started with: his voice, his sandals, his eye—each thing surrendered with the completeness of a person who has decided he is not carrying anything through the last door.

She had watched him reach the mountain's base. Watched the giant's face in the moment of receiving what Dan gave him. Watched the boy begin to climb. Then the water had gone black and still as the eye of a drowned man. The deep currents simply stopped. The bowl held the ceiling's reflection and nothing else. Her own face stared back: a stranger, silver-threaded and etched with thirty years, the silence of the one who was nameless there forevermore written plainly in the lines around her eyes.

Five weeks of silence from a patron is not, by itself, unusual—or so Kasha had been telling herself on the first day, the fifth, the twentieth, with the diminishing conviction of a woman who knows she is doing precisely that. She knocked three times on the bronze rim—once for acknowledgment, once for approach, once for petition—and asked twice and waited. She laid herself face-down in the full prostration her teacher had called the offering of the skull: forehead and both palms against the cold soil, the mineral smell of packed earth filling her lungs with what tasted, at least, like home.

"You will answer me," she said into the ground. Not a question. Kasha of the Peleset did not ask questions of the earth; she told the earth things and waited for the current's acknowledgment the way you press a seal into clay and wait for the impression to hold. "I have given thirty years into this soil. I have pressed my palms into this valley's floor while kings rose and fell above it. I have spoken downward when every tendon in my body said otherwise." Her jaw pressed against the mat's weave. "I am owed a response."

The ground received this with the patience of the ground—and there had always been warmth in that patience before, a vibration beneath the silence that meant the address had been registered. Tonight the soil was only soil: cold packed earth, dense mineral patience, a silence as complete and sealed as a brass ceiling from which no petition passes through.

Outside, the Peleset camp had become its daily symphony—dry rock grinding against bedrock, iron blades against Cretan whetstones, bronze greaves on packed earth, the guttural deep-chest chanting of men whose theology was the weight of what they could lift. Kasha felt it in the marrow of her long bones: the patient declaration of the world's basement, the iron's new cold law. She had been assuming Ba'al-Zebub was busy at the war's deep work, bending the valley's tides from beneath. She had been assuming this for five weeks. The comfort was wearing thin in the way assumed things wear: not breaking, only becoming transparent.

She tried the oil: the golden film spread flat, refused to mingle, mirrored the ceiling and nothing else. She tried the astragali: the four pale knucklebones fell in the pattern of a current thinned to thread—a wadi worn down to its bones in a dry season. She tried the libation of cedar oil on the bronze rim, the invocation in the chest register, forehead to mat, both palms flat, the full sequence: knock, ask, wait. Silence. The tent held the quiet of a well that has gone below the rope's reach.

Now—Ba'al-Zebub, Lord of what flies and swarms and multiplies in the low places, was in Ekron. He had been there since the last campaign's end, doing nothing with the practiced ease of a being with no immediate necessity. Broad-shouldered and dark, handsome in the manner of things that acquired beauty as a function. He lounged watching the flies on warm stone, rubbing his temple with two deliberate fingers every quarter-hour—an old gesture connected to a wrong turn he could not locate precisely but could feel the distance of continuously. The Peleset had taken the hill-people's Sacred Chest into the house of the fish-god; the fish-god had been found prostrate before it three mornings running; the burning that followed had moved through the coastal cities; the Chest had been returned on a cart drawn by lowing cows that walked straight when every instinct said turn. He had watched all of it and determined the Peleset were destroying themselves with iron they had not understood and pride they could not carry. He was not refusing Kasha's calls. He was simply absent from them the way a man absorbed in a fire does not hear his name called from the far end of a dark field. The call arrived. There was no one positioned to receive it.

Kasha did not know this.

She reached for the oldest scroll at the table's back—cedar-oiled linen wrapped around marks pressed rather than drawn, running center-outward in concentric rings, a script older than any writing system she had been taught. Her teacher had given it with the gravity of a person transmitting something dangerous: use this only when everything else is exhausted. Then her teacher had not mentioned it again, which is precisely how teachers indicate a thing is serious. She unwrapped the linen and found the passage for the third form of deep contact: the sound that addressed not the patron but the source from which the patron drew his authority—the root-form, older than the liturgical name, from the time before the name had been separated from what it named. The pitch she had to find without first tuning for it, the way you find a dropped thing in complete dark by trusting the memory of where your hands were when it left them. She found the first syllable, then the second. On the third her throat closed wrong—and the sound that came out was close, the way a fired vessel is close to the vessel intended when the kiln ran too hot: close, but split at the seam where the syllables should have fused.

"Yah—Vah," she whispered, the sound catching like flint on stone.

The scroll went hot in her hands. Then very hot. She dropped it. In the interval between releasing and landing—the gap that is not nothing, however brief—a fragment of something that predated the current arrangement of things tore free from the broken path and crossed the tent's interior and arrived in me.

Every question I had never known I was not asking became, simultaneously, necessary. The crease in the goat-hair where the canvas had been folded for transport: how many times, in what season, by whose hands? The basalt shard: what formation, what depth, did anything witness the moment of its making? The oldest scroll: who first pressed these marks, in what light, with what intention—and does what a thing was made to say remain inside it after the maker is gone? And the boy—the boy with the kind eyes and the unruly hair and the specific weight of direction—where was he now, and what had the mountain made of him, and had anyone thought to ask? I sprang upright, black fur at full extension, golden eyes gone very wide.

Kasha spun from the table.

"By the Ancestors' beard," she said, in the specific quiet of a woman whose composure is working very hard, "what was that." She stared. Her eyes found my frantic, open gaze and held there. Something behind her expression moved—slowly, the way a heavy stone moves in deep water. The first smile I had seen on her face in five weeks touched the corner of her mouth. "Such—Sak'ranut," she murmured, using the old Peleset word for the quality of relentless, consuming inquiry—the word you give a thing that has begun opening every cabinet simultaneously and cannot be stopped. She did not yet understand what she had named. A deity of Curiosity, even a small one, even one wearing a cat, does not get named by accident. I was still working through this question when she pointed at the mat.

"You are my beast," she said. Her finger was steady. "Not my master. You do not look at me that way. Sit."

I sat. I continued looking at her in precisely the manner she had objected to, because the quality of the look was not a decision I was making but the condition I had entered.

"What was in the third syllable," I said. "Because whatever was in it is in me now, and I find I have considerable views about what I am made of—most things want to know what they are made of, even the ones that end up not preferring the answer. Also: the current. I can feel it through the ground—thin as thread where it should be rope, but present, moving west-southwest toward the coast. Your patron is not absent from this world. He is absent from you specifically, which is a different category of problem." I paused. "The direction is Ekron. He has been there for five weeks. If he is findable at all tonight, he is findable in the gray."

She sat down on the mat and pressed both palms against the cold soil—not the petition posture, not the prostration, just contact.

"I have been knowing it for five weeks," she said into the ground. "I know the current has thinned. I know the bowl shows me nothing. I know what this means and I have been telling myself it does not mean what it means." Her palms pressed harder, the mat leaving its impression in both. "I will not be abandoned. I have given thirty years of faithful work into this soil and kept every agreement. The record exists. Someone below this floor has been keeping it."

The ground held it without reply. Except: a vibration beneath the silence—the barest acknowledgment that the address had been registered. Which was not nothing. In five weeks of reaching, it was the first thing that had not been nothing.

There was another way—deeper, older, reserved for the moment every other channel has gone dry: the gray wilderness, the shifting expanse where reality held itself in patient suspension before the first word of the first morning. Kasha's jaw tightened. She was not afraid of the gray's creatures—she had faced them and found them manageable. Her fear was subtler: it was the fear of a woman who has spent thirty years knowing with absolute certainty where the floor is, asked now to exist in a place where the floor is a decision you make rather than a fact you stand on. She was a woman of the dense, weighty world—pressure and packed earth and the patient accumulation of what presses down. In the gray wilderness, down was something you constructed.

She snuffed the oil lamp. This darkness Kasha moved through with the ease of a woman at home in the low and lightless places—the dark was not threat but the comfortable density of enclosed space, the warm compression of the world at its most itself. She laid herself on the worn mat with the deliberate straightness of a woman who is afraid and has decided the fear does not alter what needs doing: a rigid line against the ground, every joint set with intention, the body deployed like a tool for difficult work. She pressed her palms down one final time—low, direct, no ceremony left, only contact. "You will hold me," she said to the soil. "When I return. You have always held me. I am coming back." She held the contact until the cold of the ground came fully into her palms. Then she began.

The wordless chant built in the center of her chest—the deep vibration that extends outward until it finds the body's boundary and reverses, pressing inward toward the central knot where the self is bound to the flesh, working it loose the way a well-packed bundle comes apart when the right knot is found: each cord releasing in patient sequence, the body's grip loosening layer by layer. The tent poles lengthened in the low dark. The scent of dried herbs thinned to the sharp metallic edge of the threshold. The warm, dense shadows grew elongated, twisting into the shapes of things that live at the world's edges and wait for the boundary between what is and what is becoming to grow thin enough.

She crossed.

It did not arrive. That is the first thing to understand about the Night-Hag, the Crushing Shadow, the old weight-claim: it makes no entrance and sends no herald. One moment the body is a tool in full service to a will; the next moment there is a will and there is a body and they are no longer in the same arrangement. The weight on the sternum had been building since before the chant began, building the way stalactite builds: one cold deposit compounding on another, until the structure is suddenly, completely, irrevocably there. First the sternum—not the weight of stone but heavier, the specific density of absolute certainty pressing down with no interest in argument. Then the limbs, which did not grow heavy but held—caught between the beat and the silence like a pendulum arrested at the summit of its arc, suspended in the interval where will and flesh were being carefully separated with the calm precision of a craftsman who has performed this work before. Her throat sealed. Her eyes fixed on the dark weave of the tent above and would not move. Her chest continued the minimum: breath as technical necessity rather than living act. She was awake in her living awareness and a sealed jar in her flesh, a prisoner in a house of clay that had forgotten how to open its own doors.

The specific cruelty: the iron. Grinding against the whetstone outside—the patient, dense percussion of the valley's long declaration, the sound that had always come through the canvas like a familiar voice in a known room, the sound that meant the floor was still below everything and the ground held as it always had. She could hear it. She could not reach it.

I stepped onto the center of her chest. My small mass added to the weight already pressing—this was intentional—and I purred, the vibration moving through my ribs, through my paws, through her sealed chest, through the mat, into the cold ground below. The staccato percussion against her ribs was the only proof of the frantic furnace within—a rhythmic drumming no one outside could hear. The sound I made meant: I am here. The ground is here. The floor is still below you. The cord has not yet run out.

In the gray wilderness—that blank expanse before the first word of the first morning, where the deep had not yet known what form it would be asked to take—Kasha stood with feet pressed to the shifting floor, the memory of solid ground held in the long bones of her legs. Something moved in the gray's vast interior. Not approaching—the princes of that place do not approach. They become more present, the way a shape materializes from surrounding dark when the eye adjusts: not arriving but assembling, taking on weight with the calm craft of a thing that has performed this assembly many times and has long since achieved the ease of mastery. It gathered from the gray's own density—the face most calibrated to the wanting that had been pressed into the ground for thirty years, to the faithfulness deposited across three decades of downward-pressing, to the thing most wanted and most purely kept faith with. The first of the six princes assembled with the confidence of a thing that knows exactly which face it is reaching for and has used it before, for people very like this one.

And I sat at Kasha's side with my golden eyes open in the dark, pressing my small warm weight against her still arm, purring steady into the cold ground below.

Outside, iron ground against stone—patient, particular, indifferent. Inside the tent, the ground held everything placed upon it: the mat, the cooling scroll, the copper bowl, the cedar box, the lamp that had been extinguished. As the ground has always held. As it will hold when the iron and the tent and the valley's long argument with armies have all been pressed into it—as though every petition and every palm pressed faithfully downward had always been exactly where it was going: settling, layer on patient layer, into the only archive that has never needed burning.

The choice—as all the true ones are, in every age, in every tent, in every gray wilderness where a human soul has stood alone and felt the weight of the offered face beginning its turn—was entirely hers.
