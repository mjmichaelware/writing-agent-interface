CHAPTER 1: STARDUST TO STARDUST

Let me tell you about a boy named Dan.

He was sixteen, had kind eyes that had seen too much sorrow, and could walk through dreams the way you or I might walk through a doorway. One night he used this gift to break into his father's sleeping mind, hoping to save him from grief's slow rot. It went badly. It went spectacularly wrong in ways that would send Dan on a journey out of his father's house, far from his kindred, and further still from the lands of his peoples. But I'm getting ahead of myself.
The torch shook in Dan's hand. Copper light scattered down the corridor, throwing suspicious shadows across limestone walls. The flame didn't burn so much as it got squeezed by darkness pressing in from all sides—darkness that felt like serpents waiting in a garden.
Dan's heart hammered against his ribs. Every barefoot step on the cold stone was a wager against chaos. One wrong move and something would clatter—pottery shard, brittle bone, any fragment scattered like landmines across the floor.
This was his father's house. Or what remained of it.
Now I should tell you what this house had been, because the contrast matters. When Dan's mother Zuna still drew breath, these four rooms had held warmth. She sang while she worked her loom, and her voice carried through the stone like honey dissolving in water. People came seeking Dan's father's counsel, because Aviel had been the cleverest scholar in Hebron, his mind sharp as fresh-knapped flint.
But then Zuna died, and something in Aviel stopped. Not died—that would have been cleaner—it just stopped, like a cart with a broken axle. The part of him that moved forward simply ceased its motion and sat down in the dust to wait for nothing.
Here's what you need to understand about grief: some people cannot bear to keep anything that reminds them of who they lost. They burn it all, start fresh with empty hands. But others become convinced that if they just hold onto enough objects they might somehow hold onto time itself.
Aviel was one of those. After they buried Zuna, he kept only one of the gods he had brought from Philistia: Dagon, the god of accumulation, the lord of inexhaustible dust, the deity who whispered that death could lose its sting if you just held tight enough to earthly things. Aviel built a shrine in the back room, started letting objects pile up, stopped throwing anything away. What Dan moved through now was a house that had stopped being a house and become instead a monument to one man's refusal to accept that everything changes, everything passes, everything ends.
"I am coming, Father," Dan whispered to the watching darkness. "Even if you curse me for trying."
The corridor was choked with debris. Broken pottery lay scattered like shattered vessels of a thousand failed offerings. Ruined brass astrolabes gathered dust, instruments that once measured heaven now unable to find even north. Wooden compasses with frozen needles pointed nowhere because when you give up on destination, navigation becomes a joke the universe tells at your expense.
His mother's loom sat against one wall. The threads had rotted into mildew now, but you could still see what they had been: crimson like pomegranate seeds split open, saffron like desert flowers at dawn, emerald like new wheat pushing through soil, amber like honey held to sunlight, violet like the sky at dusk, rose like oleander petals after rain. Every color under heaven woven together in patterns that made people stop and stare. Every color, that is, except three—never blue, never purple, never scarlet. She had never explained why.
Now those beautiful threads draped over brittle animal bones with that horrible dry rustle that sounds like all life surrendering to death. Maggots worked the rotted cloth, pale as mushroom flesh, fat things the size of your thumb. The smell came in layers: ammonia sharp enough to burn, decay sweet as overripe fruit, dusty herbs that had lost all virtue, and underneath it all that sulfurous reek of disturbed earth.
Dan's hand went to his chest where the Zuna stone hung from a leather cord. Smooth as prayer, cool as spring water. His mother had pressed it into his palm when he was small enough to fit in her lap.
"It is from the stars, Danny," she had said. "All things are. Every piece of us. We are made of stardust that forgot it was stardust, that got so caught up in being mud it forgot its home. So when the world feels heavy, when the mud tries to claim you, you remember: your home is not here. It is up there, in the cold clean fire of heaven where things burn without being consumed."

"Mother said we are stardust," he whispered to the stone. "If that is a lie, then let me be the fool who believed it."
He reached his father's doorframe and stopped. From inside came ragged snoring, frantic and uneven, the gasping of someone running a race in their sleep. Dan's throat went tight. Three nights ago they had fought. Dan had tried to pull his father outside into the sun, to make him walk. Aviel had responded with words that still burned: "If you are still searching for your source, boy, then pick up a stone and give it a name. Only a fool seeks answers from a power he cannot touch."

"Up," Dan told the doorway, shaping the word carefully. "Even if it kills me. Even if I am ground to dust on the way. Up."
He pinched the torch flame out between thumb and forefinger. The small burn barely registered as darkness rushed in like water filling a bowl. But he did not need light for what came next.
Dan knelt on the cold stone beside his father's door. The limestone bit into his knees. He slowed his breathing—four counts drawing air in, hold for one beat, six counts releasing it.
Now I need to tell you about dreamwalking, because otherwise what happens next won't make any sense. Some people—very few, but some—are born with the ability to step sideways out of their own sleep and walk through the vast strange country where dreamers wander lost. Dan's mother had been one of these rare souls. She had been blessed and cursed both, for some feared her kind and whispered of the craft of witches who consorted with cats and commanded elements. An odd pairing, don't you think? Especially when the cat has a tail to tell longer than the threads in a ball of yarn. But that is another story entirely.
The TECHNIQUE is simple but not easy. You have to forget things. First you forget yesterday—you let memory dissolve like salt in water. Then you forget tomorrow—you release your grip on fear and hope both. Then, and this is the hardest part, you forget now. You let go of your body—the cold floor, the rot-smell, the distant cricket-song, the weight of your own flesh. All of it has to go. You have to become nothing, empty, a cup with all content poured out.
Dan focused on his breath. His breathing slowed further, deeper, until each breath took an eternity and the space between breaths became a country he could wander in. The darkness behind his closed eyes began to shift and thin at the edges, and cracks appeared where a light that was not light seeped through from somewhere else.
And then—like falling sideways through a door made of nothing—he was through. He was in.
The Dreamscape opened around him. It was not exactly a place—or rather it was every place and no place all at once. The substance here had the quality of fog mixed with starlight and given weight, dense but endlessly shifting, responding to thought the way water responds to wind.
It was formless and void, and darkness was upon the face of the deep, and the spirit of the dreamer moved upon the face of the waters. It was the way things must have been before Time, when the first words of truth were spoken and creation condensed out of infinite possibility into finite form.
Dan turned toward his father's dream, toward the barrier he knew he would find there. The wall rose before him—thick and dark and profoundly wrong, pulsing with sick light like a bruise that had achieved consciousness. It was made of memory compressed into something nearly solid, grief and regret and shame layered over and over.
He could feel the contents bleeding through: a child's scream cut short, a hand slipping from his grasp, blood on white stone, smoke choking lungs, a city's final scream, his wife's voice calling his name and then stopping.
This was his father's private ledger. Every failure, every loss, stacked and mortared together into a barrier designed to keep the world out and Aviel locked safely inside.
Dan placed his palm against the wall. "Please," he said aloud. "I am not here to judge you. I am here to help."
The wall shuddered. Sound exploded through Dan's awareness like a dam breaking—screaming and weeping and stone crashing and fire crackling and metal rending and a thousand voices crying out. Images flashed: faces twisted in agony, hands reaching for help that would never come, his mother's body being lowered into dark earth.
Dan gasped as crushing force squeezed him from all sides. His vision went white, then black, then both at once in a way that should not have been possible. For a moment he thought he would fail.
Then the wall broke. Not shattered—broke, like ice under too much weight, sudden and total. Dan stumbled forward through the gap and found himself inside his father's dream.
A wasteland stretched before him under a sky the color of ashes. This was a flat plain of gray dust extending to the horizon under a flat gray sky, featureless and infinite. No sun overhead, no stars in the dark, no landmark of any kind. Just gray meeting gray meeting gray forever.
In the exact center stood Aviel. He looked smaller here, diminished, shoulders hunched forward, arms wrapped around himself. He was waiting for dreams to begin, or for them to end and grant him mercy.
Dan felt love surge through him with such fierce intensity it hurt. It was the terrible kind of love that sees someone destroying themselves and cannot look away, cannot stop caring, cannot give up.
"I am sorry, Father," he said quietly. "But you need to see what you are doing to yourself."
He lifted his hand toward the empty sky and spoke a single word: "Rain."
The gray dust began to swirl upward like smoke gaining intention, darkening from ash-gray to deep brown to almost black. It gained density and weight, condensed from vapor to liquid to solid, and then it began to fall.
Small smooth pebbles struck the dust first, clicking and clattering. Aviel looked up, confusion clouding his features. One stone struck his shoulder, another hit his outstretched hand.
He picked it up, and in perfect dream-logic where thought becomes reality, the dull stone transformed in his grip. It became gold—heavy and pure, real gold that caught light where no light existed.
Aviel's eyes went wide with wonder and hunger and desperate need.
More stones fell, and each one he touched transformed into treasure beyond imagining. Copper coins thick as his thumb, silver rings that sang when they struck each other, gems that held impossible colors—rubies like frozen blood, sapphires like captured sky, emeralds like distilled envy.
Aviel began to gather the treasure with movements that grew increasingly frantic. "Mine," he whispered. "Mine, mine, mine."
He shoved treasure into his pockets, and when his pockets filled he stuffed coins into his belt, under his tunic, down his sandals. More fell in an endless stream. He tried to catch it all, his movements becoming wild and desperate.
The stones kept falling. They piled around his feet like drifts of metallic snow, rose past his ankles to his calves, past his knees to his thighs. Still he grabbed at them with hands that would not stop, could not stop, driven by a hunger that had no bottom.
The pile grew higher, reached his waist and then his chest. Now Aviel was trapped, buried up to his neck in wealth he could not release. His hands were frozen mid-grasp, fingers curled around coins that had fused to his palms. The weight of all that accumulated metal pressed down on his chest, squeezing the air from his lungs.
His face flushed red, then went purple as oxygen stopped flowing. His mouth opened in a silent scream that had no breath behind it.
Dan watched, and his heart broke clean through. This was the hardest thing he had ever done—to stand and watch his father suffer knowing the suffering was necessary.
Then Dan lifted his hand again and spoke a different word: "Wind."
A cold scouring current of pure intention swept across the gray plain. It was not gentle—it howled like a living thing denied its prey, tore at the accumulated treasure with invisible fingers. The wind gathered the coins and gems, swept them up into a spiral that climbed toward the gray sky, and then scattered them back into the dust from which Dan's will had formed them.
The treasure vanished like smoke in a strong breeze, leaving Aviel kneeling alone on the gray dust, his hands still frozen in the grasping position though there was nothing left to grasp.
For one perfect moment his eyes cleared completely, the frantic hunger draining away like poison drawn from a wound.
He looked up, past the space where treasure had buried him, past the gray emptiness of his own despair, and he saw the sky.
And then—exactly as Dan had planned—a single point of light appeared in that endless grayness overhead.
A star. Small as a pinprick, distant as childhood, cold as truth itself. But there. Undeniably there in the empty sky where nothing had been before.
Aviel stared at it with an expression Dan would remember for the rest of his life—wonder and terror and something like hope all fighting for space on his father's face. Something in that expression cracked, just enough to let in one thin sliver of light.
Dan poured every ounce of intention he had left into a single message, pressing it into his father's sleeping mind: You have to leave. I have to leave. We cannot stay here. Change is still possible.
Then, gently, because this was his father and he loved him, Dan released his hold on the dream. The star faded first. The gray plain dissolved like morning fog. Aviel's consciousness drifted back down toward dreamless sleep.
Dan let himself fall back into waking.
He gasped as cold stone met his knees, as the smell of rot hit his nostrils. The corridor was dark except for his own ragged breathing. From behind his father's door came only peaceful sleep, the first peaceful sleep Aviel had known in months.
Dan tried to stand and his legs buckled. He caught himself against the wall. A profound exhaustion settled over him like a heavy blanket soaked in cold water.
He felt hollowed out, emptied, as if something essential had been poured out and not yet replaced. His limbs were heavy as stone. There was a weight on his spirit that went beyond mere physical tiredness.
Dan forced himself to move, one hand trailing along the wall. Each step was an effort, a small battle won through sheer stubbornness. By the time he reached the hearth he was moving like an old man, hunched and careful.
The hearth held only embers now, dull red eyes winking in accumulated ash. Dan knelt and fumbled for his torch with hands that would not quite obey. His fingers felt thick and clumsy, swollen somehow. When he finally got the torch positioned over the coals his hand shook badly.
The pitch finally caught and flame bloomed orange and alive.
"That was harder than it should have been," he told the flames quietly, his voice rough and hoarse.
The fire offered no answer. Dan used the wall to lever himself back to standing and began the short journey to his own room.
"It's done," he whispered to the doorframe as he passed. His legs were heavy as stone.
Now I should tell you about Dan's room, because the contrast matters.
The house was chaos incarnate—rot and refuse and accumulated debris. But Dan's room was different. You could step through his doorway and immediately feel the change. Everything in its place, arranged with thought and care. The blanket on his cot folded with crisp corners. The floor swept clean. His few possessions arranged along one wall: a clay cup, a wooden comb, the flint stylus he used for writing.
The walls were free of mold. The small window was clean, positioned to catch the morning light. The air did not smell of rot—it smelled of dust and stone and faint desert wind.
One might say, and perhaps one would be right to say, that Dan was attempting to set his own small room in order before daring to criticize the chaos of the larger world. He understood that the fight against disorder had to begin somewhere, and the somewhere you could actually control was always the right place to start.
It was a small thing, maintaining this one ordered space. But small things matter.
Dan lowered himself onto his cot with a sigh that came from somewhere deep in his bones. The torch still guttered in his hand. He wedged it carefully into a crack in the wall, then lay back against the thin blanket and stared at the ceiling.
The stone above him was just stone, gray and unchanging and utterly indifferent to his existence. But somehow that indifference was comforting after the intensity of the Dreamscape where everything responded to will and intention.
He lay there listening to the house settle with its many small groans and creaks. The torch flame made shadows dance on the walls. Dan felt himself beginning to drift. The exhaustion was simply too profound to fight.
His eyes closed. His breathing slowed. The tension in his muscles began to release.
He fell asleep.
And then he began to dream.
Dan found himself standing on water. Not floating, not swimming, but standing—his bare feet resting on a surface that was somehow both liquid and solid at once. It stretched away in every direction, and the sky above was that particular shade of blue you only see in dreams.
He looked down and smiled, and took a step. The water held him up solid as stone because this was his dream and he decided how things worked here. Dan walked across the water, and each step sent small ripples spreading out in perfect circles.
In the distance he saw a boat sitting at what might have been a shoreline. Just a simple fishing boat with oars and a small mast. Dan looked at it and smiled, then dispersed it with nothing more than a thought. He did not need it. In dreams you only keep what you need.
He kept walking, feeling lighter with each step. As he walked the water began to change beneath his feet. It thickened somehow, solidified, became less like liquid and more like glass—clear as crystal but hard as stone.
Then he heard it. A small sound at first, a tiny crystalline snap like ice breaking on a winter pond.
The glass cracked.
The fracture lines raced out from where Dan stood in a geometric pattern, spreading and multiplying and deepening with each passing heartbeat. The sound grew from a whisper to a groan to a roar like continents splitting apart.
The glass shattered.Water exploded upward in a geyser that reached toward the impossible sky. From the depths beneath rose a presence so powerful it made the whole dream shudder and bend. The peaceful blue bled into an angry, bruised purple. The sea became a terrifying churn of water and raw earth, a maelstrom of pure liquid rock and bitter brine.
Suddenly Dan was clinging to a single inadequate plank of wood, his only security against colossal forces. Massive dark waves crashed over him. The cold stench of the deep clung to white foam. The sky became a suffocating churning blanket of obsidian blackness. His vision fractured into a kaleidoscope of blue and black and white swirling endlessly.
Then, from the very heart of the storm, from the impossible depths, it rose.
At the southern edge of the world, where the horizon bled into an endless turbulent gray, a colossal head emerged from the waves. The face, carved from drowned stone, was ancient and impassive. It radiated an inhuman cold. Its unblinking eyes, wide as abysses, blacker than the deepest trench, locked onto Dan's—a gaze that stripped away all pretense.
Seaweed, thick and rotting, draped its chin like a hideous beard. Barnacles, sharp and encrusted, clung to its cheeks like jewels of corruption.

Dan's exhausted eyes turned toward his doorway. The shadow there seemed to shift and hold shape, seemed to be something that was not quite present and not quite absent, dancing at the edge of perception. Below, the grinding continued its slow patient work. Ancient. Crushing. Certain as the downward pull that everything falls, everything returns, everything feeds the depths eventually.

Dan could not move. Could barely breathe. Felt paralyzed by exhaustion and the weight of what he had done.
He pulled the thin blanket to his chin. His entire body was a cold, aching restraint. Two presences now: one below, ancient and crushing. One at the entrance, watchful and strange.
And somewhere in that narrow space between sleep and waking, between crushing depth and patient warmth, between what he had been when the sun set and what he was becoming as it prepared to rise, Dan understood with terrible clarity that his journey had truly begun.
The price was already being extracted, and he had not even left his father's house yet. The cost would only grow steeper as he climbed, as he shed the weight of earth and reached for stars that might burn anyone foolish enough to get too close.
The late visitor at his chamber door—that gentle purring thing—would wait. It had all the time in the world, could afford to sit there purring soft as secrets in the dark for as long as it took.
What stars truly cost. What they have always cost since the first dreamers looked up at the night sky and wanted to go home.
Everything. Stars cost everything.
And the journey to reach them would take Dan far from his father's house, further still from his kindred, and further still from the lands of his peoples where the language he spoke was understood and the gods he refused to worship were at least familiar in their hostility.
But that journey would not begin tonight.
