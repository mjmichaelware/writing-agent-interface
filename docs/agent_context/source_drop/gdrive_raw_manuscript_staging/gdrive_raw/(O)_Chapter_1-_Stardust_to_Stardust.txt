CHAPTER 1: STARDUST TO STARDUST

A four-room house in Hebron had become the shape of one man's heart, divided against itself. You might ask why a home would come to resemble something so treacherous as a human heart—such a question posed while reading this means you and I will get along.

Father Aviel had been a scholar, sharp as fresh-knapped flint, sought and summoned by those whose questions twisted beyond their own unraveling. But when Mother Zuna passed—nameless there forevermore—something stopped within him. It did not die, which would have been cleaner and quicker, but simply ceased like a cart with a cruelly shattered axle. The gods he'd gathered from the Western plains—brittle bronze forms: forgotten gods of flame, of fertility, of fading fortune—could not fill the void. So he swept them away and kept only one: the lord of limitless dust and earthly plenty, who whispered that hoarded treasures might hold back time himself and defy the demands he made.

On a night when the moon was full on its third day, the son moved through the monument his father's madness had made. They had called him Dan once, when names still mattered in this dwelling that hardly stood. He was sixteen, with kind eyes that held more sorrow than a boy should carry and unruly black hair that wrestled with his simple commandments. The torch trembled in his hand—the frail flame flickered not from wind but from the rhythm of his own heart, casting suspicious shadows across the narrow corridor. Each barefoot step through the refuse was a wager against sound: one shard of pottery, one brittle bone breaking beneath his weight, and the house would know. He was moving toward his father's room, and tonight he would enter the dreamscape to show what could not be undone.

The corridor choked with debris. I will not spare you the catalogue. Broken pottery lay scattered like shattered vessels of failed offerings—Dan had counted eighteen visible shards one morning before stopping, unable to push beyond that threshold. Fragments of parchment and brittle wax tablets, once holding records of lineage and covenant, lay stripped clean by ravenous mold. Ruined brass astrolabes gathered in shadowed corners, instruments that once measured heaven now unable to find even north. Desiccated fruits clung to their seeds. Water skins lay cracked and hollow, reeking of stale leather and sharp mineral tang.

The smell came in layers. Ammonia sharp enough to sear the nose. Decay sweet as overripe fruit left too long in summer heat. Dusty herbs that had lost all virtue. Beneath it all, that sulfurous reek of disturbed earth, of things buried being dragged back into light. Maggots worked the rotted cloth, pale as mushroom flesh, fat as thumbs—the only living things that still found purpose here. Flies rose and fell in living clouds, their droning buzz a constant dirge.

Ah, you will recognize this particular brand of desolate exhaustion, I'm sure—the kind that comes not from one disaster but from small deaths accumulating until the whole structure groans. Some people are born with the ability to step sideways out of sleep and walk through that vast country where dreamers wander lost; Dan's mother had been one of these rare souls, and she'd taught him when he was small. The TECHNIQUE is simple but not easy—forget yesterday, let it dissolve like salt in water; forget tomorrow, release fear and hope both; then forget now, let go of your body until you become nothing. His mother had told him: you must become as still as deep water, and then the threshold opens.

Dan knelt beside his father's door; the limestone bit into his knees. He slowed his breathing—four counts in, hold one beat, six counts out—and pressed his palm against the doorframe. "Stone," he whispered to it, "you've stood here seven years watching him rot. Tonight you'll see me try something foolish. If I fail, at least you'll know someone tried." The darkness behind his closed eyes began to shift and thin; cracks appeared where light seeped through from somewhere sideways. Then—like falling through a door made of nothing—he was through.

The Dreamscape opened; formless dark churned beneath, void teaming with potential as things must have been before time when the first words of truth were spoken. Every small part here contained the pattern of the whole, mirroring itself down into depths that had no bottom. Dan turned toward his father's dream and found the barrier: a wall thick and pulsing with sick light like a bruise that had learned to hate. He felt what bled through—a child's scream cut short, blood on white stone, his wife's voice calling then stopping.

For a moment he almost turned back; but he'd come this far to test himself, to prove his power was real, and he needed to know what he could do.

"I'm not here to judge," he told the barrier. "I'm here to see."

Sound exploded—screaming, weeping, stone crashing. Images flashed: faces twisted in agony, his mother's shrouded body lowered into dark earth. Every step was a silent, difficult expenditure of will, not muscle. Crushing pressure squeezed from all sides; his vision went white, then black. The wall broke—sudden and total—and Dan stumbled through.

Gray wasteland stretched under ashen sky; in the center stood Aviel, shoulders hunched, arms wrapped around himself. Dan lifted his hand toward empty sky. "Rain."

Gray dust swirled upward, darkening, gaining weight; pebbles began to fall. Aviel looked up; one stone struck his shoulder and he picked it up. In dream-logic it transformed—became gold, heavy and pure. His eyes went wide; more fell, each transforming: copper coins, silver rings, gems holding impossible colors—rubies like frozen blood, sapphires like captured sky, emeralds like distilled envy.

Aviel began to gather frantically. "Mine," he whispered—half prayer, half curse—shoving treasure into pockets, belt, under his tunic. More fell in endless stream; the pile grew, rising past ankles, past knees, past waist. Still he grabbed with hands that would not stop because it was trying to fill a void that went all the way through. The pile reached his chest, then his neck; Aviel was buried, hands frozen mid-grasp, fingers curled around coins fused to his palms. His face flushed red, then purple. His mouth opened in silent scream.

Dan's hands trembled—this was harder than expected, watching someone you love suffer even when the suffering serves a purpose—but he held his ground because holding tight to what's already gone only crushes the life out of what remains. He lifted his hand again. "Wind."

Cold current swept across the plain; the wind gathered coins and gems, swept them into spiral, scattered them back into dust. They dissolved as they fell. The treasure vanished; Aviel knelt alone with hands still frozen. For one moment his eyes cleared; he looked up and saw the sky. A single point of light appeared: a star, small and distant as memory, cold as truth, but there.

Aviel stared at it with wonder and terror and something like hope. Something cracked—not broke entirely, but cracked enough to let in one thin sliver of light. Everything changes, everything passes, everything ends—refusing to accept that only makes the ending worse. Dan released his hold; the star faded, the plain dissolved, and Aviel drifted back toward dreamless sleep.

Dan gasped as cold stone met his knees; rot hit his nostrils. From behind his father's door came only peaceful sleep—the first Aviel had known in months. Dan tried to stand; his legs buckled. He caught himself against the wall. Exhaustion settled over him; he felt hollowed out, emptier now than before he'd entered.

He forced himself to move, one hand trailing along stone. By the time he reached the hearth he was moving like an old man. He fumbled for his torch; his hand shook rather badly. The pitch finally caught.

"Harder than I thought," he told the flames. "Darkness," he whispered as he passed through the corridor, "you've seen what I did. My mother taught me to reach for the heights, but tonight I learned the cost. If you're listening—if anything's listening—tell me it was worth it."

Now I must ask you to pause, if only for a few heartbeats, before reading what comes next—because Dan's room matters more than you might think. The house was chaos—rot and refuse and seven years of accumulated debris. But Dan's room was different: the blanket folded with crisp corners, floor swept clean, possessions arranged in careful order. A clay cup for water, a wooden comb his mother had carved, the flint stylus for scratching words into stone. One small window positioned to catch morning light, kept clean so that light could enter.

One might say that Dan was attempting to set his own small room in perfect order before daring to criticize the chaos of the larger world, and perhaps one would be right to say so. The fight against disorder had to begin somewhere, and the somewhere you could actually control was always the right place to start. Some burdens are too heavy for one person to carry, but refusing to carry what you can manage is simply cowardice dressed up as humility.

Dan lowered himself onto the cot with a sigh from deep in his bones; he wedged the torch into a crack in the wall, then lay back. The stone above was just stone, gray and unchanging. Somehow that indifference was comforting after the Dreamscape where everything responded to will. He lay listening to the house settle; the torch made shadows dance. Dan felt himself drifting; the exhaustion was too profound to fight. His eyes closed, his breathing slowed, and he fell asleep.

And then, he began to dream a dream never dared to dream before. Because sleep after such expenditure is never truly restful.

Dan found himself standing on water—not floating but standing, bare feet resting on surface both liquid and solid. The sky above held that particular blue you only see in dreams. He took a step; the water held him solid. He walked across it, each step sending perfect circles spreading outward. In the distance sat a boat, but Dan dispersed it with a thought.

The water began to change; it thickened, solidified, became glass—clear as crystal but hard as stone. Dan could see down through it into depths that went on forever; for a moment he thought he saw shapes moving far below.

Then he heard it. A tiny crystalline snap. The glass cracked; fracture lines raced out, spreading and deepening. The sound grew from whisper to roar. The glass shattered; water exploded upward, and a presence rose so powerful the whole dream shuddered. The peaceful blue bled into bruised purple; the sea became churning water and raw earth. Dan clung to a single plank; massive waves crashed over him. Cold stench of the deep clung to white foam; the sky became churning obsidian.

Then, from the heart of the storm, it rose.

At the southern edge of the world a colossal head emerged from the waves. The face was carved from drowned stone, ancient beyond reckoning and impassive. Its eyes, wide as abysses and blacker than the deepest trench, locked onto Dan's with a gaze that stripped away all pretense. Seaweed draped its chin; barnacles clung to its cheeks. Far south—so far Dan could not see but knew with dream-certainty—a massive tail broke the surface, stretching from one edge of the world to the other.

The voice echoed from depths of Dan's own skull, resonant hum that shook his teeth: "I am Dagon, the power your father has chosen to serve. Lord of Abundance [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE], Master of Accumulation Eternal. Ask and receive with certainty absolute, for my path demonstrates ease beyond mortal comprehension."

"I didn't call you!"

"Always have you called; ceaselessly does your flesh cry out, seeking its due rest appointed. But this night have you sinned most grievously, daring to undo my work established within your father's dwelling through seven years of patient cultivation. Do not mistake your petty will—transient as morning mist—for a living offering worthy of divine consideration."

"Abundance of things? Why devote my life to what I cannot carry through death?"

Dagon leaned closer, displacing mountains of water: "I offer unto you a raft most substantial, so that the friction of death shall lose its sting entirely. Come, follow me into the comfortable depths. Your mother's voice I shall restore to you in nocturnal visions—dreams wherein she speaks comfort unceasing. I shall bestow upon you a wife whose hands need not be reddened by caustic lye, and sons who shall never comprehend their grandfather's shame. I offer the sluggish, sweet warmth of a life forgotten entirely by the cruelties which the heights inflict without mercy."

Dan wavered; the vision was blinding light against terrible truth. His heart lurched toward it with a force that felt like being pulled overboard, dragged down by weights he'd forgotten he was carrying. But his hand found the stone against his chest.

"Your Temple fell because of a blinded man! You crumpled before the Sacred Chest!"

"My temple fell not for want of My weakness [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE], boy." Dagon's voice remained patient. "My statue crumbled not for absence of My power divine. There exist boundaries ancient which must not be transgressed beyond measure; for the realm of the living cannot trespass excessively into the realm of the dead without consequence dire. We are bound by different laws immutable. Mistake not My ancient restraint—discipline practiced across eons—for weakness; it is the patience of ages, not failure of a moment."

"So you admit it! You are a deity of death!"

"Look about you, boy. Look at the war within you. Look at the wars between men. Death is not a distant shore—it is the very sea wherein you swim ceaselessly."

"Why should I serve you and not El who dwells higher?"

Dagon's eyes flashed: "The one who pursues the upward path becomes the monument isolated—cold, absolute, separated entirely. Choose the heights if you must, but know this: the silence of the cosmos awaits you eternal. The star you become shall burn alone."

His voice dropped, becoming crushing judgment: "You might live, yes—survive where others have failed. But those you hold dear shall live too, and in their living find only hardship manifold. When you speak, no man shall comprehend; your instrument shall be shattered beyond repair. Your tongue shall be made a heavy thing, speaking foretold truth that is only heard as noise—the worthless babble of the judged, like chaff that the wind drives away. You will have no witness."

Dan shoved salt water from his eyes. "You offer me a raft for soft lies? I just saw what that bought my father! I'd rather drink the sea than live one day in your filth!"

Dagon laughed—a sound like grinding of mountains against bedrock: "A brave little mouse! You choose the high places? Very well. I am the very earth those mountains are rooted within; your struggle ascending is my foundation sustaining."

Dagon surged forward: "If ever you should alter your tone—merely knock thrice, ask twice, then wait. I shall come with certainty absolute, for I am the cadence you seek to outrun."

Black water filled Dan's mouth, his nose, his lungs. Everything went dark.

Dan woke gasping—chest heaving, heart hammering, sweat soaking through his tunic. For a terrible moment he could not tell if he was still drowning.

Then details came into focus: the clean swept floor, the arranged possessions, the window showing first gray hints of dawn. He was awake.

But everything felt different—drained, as if all color and meaning had been diluted. The world seemed remote. He looked at the clay cup beside his cot; it was just a cup now, and it meant nothing. The wooden comb his mother had carved—just shaped wood. The flint stylus—just a rock. All of it empty.

Dan stood slowly and walked to the window; the sky was turning from black to bruised purple. "Sky," he whispered, pressing his palm against the stone sill, "she told me we're made of stardust that forgot it was stardust. She said the mud tries to claim us, pull us down. But I can't feel the heights calling anymore. I can't feel anything at all."

He closed his eyes. "Stardust to stardust," he whispered. "Mother, tell me it means something. Tell me I didn't waste it."

He lay back down, pulling the thin blanket to his chin though he felt neither cold nor warm—just nothing. The exhaustion was bone-deep, but more than that: the exhaustion of caring. He no longer cared if his father woke healed or broken, if the house collapsed, if the sun rose or if this gray darkness continued forever. Everything felt like too much effort, and nothing mattered. Like chaff that the wind drives away, he thought.

Then he heard them from two directions. From below—deep below—came grinding: stone against stone, slow and patient. Dagon was making his weight known.

And from his door came something else: a soft sound, gentle and patient. Not quite tapping, not quite rapping at his chamber door, but gentle insistence.

A purring, warm and alive.

Dan's exhausted eyes turned toward his doorway; the shadow there seemed to shift and hold shape. He felt warmth wash over him—not oppressive heat but something else, something that felt like Hope might feel if Hope had fur and whiskers, entreating entrance at his window lattice.

Below, the grinding continued—ancient, crushing, certain as the downward pull. At the door, the warmth waited—small where the other was vast, gentle where the other was crushing.

Dan could not move. He pulled the blanket tighter. The late visitor entreating entrance would wait as long as it took, could afford to sit there purring soft as secrets while Dan decided whether he had the strength to care—whether stardust remembered it was stardust, or if it truly had become mud that forgot its home.

Our boy Dan simply closed his kind eyes, weak and weary, too empty to resist, and let the darkness take him—while the grinding continued below and the purring continued at his threshold and the torch burned low and finally guttered out, leaving him in darkness that was somehow less complete than it should have been, as if even the dark itself was holding its breath to see what would happen next.
