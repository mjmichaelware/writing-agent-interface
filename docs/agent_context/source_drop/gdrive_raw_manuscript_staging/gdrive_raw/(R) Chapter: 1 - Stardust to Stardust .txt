CHAPTER 1: STARDUST TO STARDUST
(Anthropic)


The four-room house in Hebron had become the shape of one man's heart, divided against itself. You might ask why a house would come to resemble something so treacherous as a human heart—and if you are asking that question, then you are paying the right kind of attention to what you are reading.


Father Aviel had been a scholar, sharp as fresh-knapped flint, sought and summoned by those with questions knotted beyond common counsel. But when Mother Zuna passed beyond recall—when they buried his bride beneath the earth—something stopped within him. Not died, which would have been cleaner and quicker, but simply ceased like a cart with a cruelly shattered axle. The gathered gods he'd carried from the Western plains—bronze and brittle forms of fortune, flame, and fertility—could not fill the void. So he swept them away and kept only one: the lord of limitless dust and earthly plenty, who whispered in the watches and the waiting of the night that hoarded treasures might hold back time itself and defy the demands it made.


You cannot save someone who refuses saving. I know this for a fact, having watched grief work on people for longer than you might imagine. Some people throw everything away when they lose the thing they love most—burn it all, scatter the ashes, start fresh with empty hands. Others keep every thread, every cracked vessel, every scrap of parchment with nothing written on it, and pull it tighter and tighter until there is no room left to breathe. Aviel was one of those. Which is relevant because it is exactly what his son was creeping through the dark to undo.


On a night when the moon was full on its third day, a boy with kind eyes moved through the monument his father's madness had made. His name was Dan. Sixteen, with messy black hair that tufted upward no matter how many times he patted it down or slicked it back. The torch trembled in his hand—the frail flame flickered not from wind but from the percussion against his ribs, casting suspicious shadows down the narrow hallway. Each barefoot step through the refuse was a wager against sound: one shard of pottery, one brittle bone breaking beneath his weight, and the house would know.


And the house, I should tell you, was listening. That was rather the problem.


The smell of the place was a terrible architecture—designed, it seemed, by someone who had thought very carefully about what it would take to make a person stop wanting to breathe. Sulfur and sweet rot and the patient mineral cold of turned earth. The still patience of serpents in a garden. Rotting textiles—Zuna's sacred threads left mid-shuttle—had unraveled into mildew and draped over discarded animal bones, making a dry rustling sound when disturbed that was the sound of all life giving up its last argument with death. The threads had once been extraordinary things. You could still see what they had been if you looked with the right kind of attention: crimson like pomegranate seeds split open, saffron like desert flowers at dawn, emerald like new wheat pushing through soil, amber like honey held to sunlight, violet like the sky at dusk, rose like oleander petals after rain. Every color under heaven woven together in patterns that had made people stop in the street and stare. Every color, that is, except the three she never touched—never blue, never purple, never scarlet—and she had never explained why, but Dan had understood in the wordless way children understand holy things that some colors were not for human hands.


Now those threads draped over brittle bones. Maggots worked the rotted cloth in the near dark, pale as mushroom flesh, fat as thumbs.


Dan had counted eighteen visible pottery shards one morning, and something about that number had made him stop counting there, as if eighteen marked the boundary between what still counted as life and what had crossed over entirely. The failed records lay everywhere—parchment and crumbling wax tablets, stripped clean by mold that had developed what could only be called ambition. Ruined astrolabes in the corners, their rings seized into arrangements pointing toward no coordinate in heaven or earth. Desiccated fruits clinging to their seeds. Water skins cracked and smelling of stale leather. Even the well had stopped remembering how to hold water.


Thirst was the final state of everything in that house.


He stopped by the hearth in the main room. The coals pulsed—crimson like the threads, exhausted and faint in the surrounding dark. Cool against his ribs hung the Zuna stone. He didn't reach for it consciously. His hand simply found it the way a hand finds familiar things in darkness.


He knew what this place was. A house that had chosen to destroy itself. Some old stories warn you not to look back at such a place when you leave it. The lesson is not about the city. He was not going to look back.


He crept to Father Aviel's doorframe and pressed his ear against the wood.


From within: the low, ragged sound of a man trying to outrun his own breath in sleep. Rough and uneven. Unconscious of itself.


Good. He was under.


Dan pinched the torch wick between thumb and forefinger. The darkness rushed in. A bitter tang of spent wick hung in the air—parting's small, particular smell. He held his bearings by memory and the single slot of moonlight through the narrow wall slit, barely a handspan wide, that cut a gray line across the corridor floor.


Three nights ago Aviel had hissed across their fire: "If you are still searching for your source, boy, pick up a stone and give it a name. Seek answers from a power you can touch."


He refused the stone then. He refused it now.


Through the wall slit, the stars of Hebron burned in their old patterns above the hills. His mother had taught him they were the closest thing to the unseen source—that original light from which everything borrowed its brief existence and to which everything returned. And his dreams had always found their way there. Not the shapeless kind. Deliberate. He had dreamed of climbing endless stairs in the dark, his legs never tiring, the air growing thinner and cleaner with every step. Of jumping and not coming back down. Of spreading his arms and rising until the whole dark earth curved below him like a broken plate. Everyone who has ever woken from that kind of dream and found a ceiling above them instead of open sky will understand what he was after.


He stood one moment longer, watching the light through the slit.


Up, he thought. Even if it kills me. Even if I'm ground to dust on the way. Up.


But this night's work was not for himself.


---


His mother, Zuna, had been a dreamwalker—rarer than it sounds and considerably more dangerous than it looks. She had taught Dan the same capacity before she went, which is to say: she had taught him to enter another person's sleep and move within it with deliberate intention. Some say such an ability belongs to those who consort with certain patient, curious watchers of the world—creatures of thread and careful observation whose tales are considerably longer than a ball of yarn. I will leave it at that.


What matters is that Dan had the gift. Had used it twice before. And tonight, for reasons that were not entirely about his father—he wanted to know if he could still do it, wanted to test the power the way young people test things they have been told are dangerous—he was going to use it a third time.


He knelt beside his father's cot. The stone floor seized his bare knees with a cold that went straight to the bone. He slowed his breath, letting the frantic voices dissolve until only the emptiness remained.


Her voice in the silence. Her hands rough and reddened from the lye, holding a clay pot up to the afternoon sun.


"The last scrubbing is the one that matters, Dan. Do not fear the emptiness." Her eyes always fixed on something beyond the walls. "Make the vessel empty before you try to use it."


He let go. The stone beneath his knees dissolved—not instantly, but with a slow horrifying certainty. In that terrifying, perfect stillness—that necessary, terrible emptiness—he stepped sideways, and into the Dreamscape.


---


The journey began not with a lift but a vertical commitment.


He stood at the base of a rope—thick, rough-woven hemp, blood-warm and slick with something between sweat and oil, vibrating with the hum of the emptiness it pierced. Each grip burned with tiny splinters. He climbed. The air grew thin, then cold, then ringing-silent. A moment of doubt arrived and lodged in him like a stone: some barriers are meant to stand. Some fathers are meant to stay broken. He climbed through it. His arms ached and his breath came in sharp urgent gasps and he climbed through that too, until his hand found an edge—solid, unmoving—and he pulled himself up and over.


He had arrived. Not easily. Not gracefully. But he had arrived at the top of something. It had cost him. But he was there.


The Dreamscape spread before him in ethereal gray light, featureless and vast.


Formless void, darkness on the deep—as things must have been before Time, when the first words of truth were spoken.


He turned toward his father's presence and found the barrier immediately: a wall dense and pulsing with old bruise-light, sick and humid. It bled through its surface: a child's face screaming in smoke, a woman's hand slipping from a grasp, blood on white stone, a city's final scream before silence swallowed it whole. His father's private ledger of failure and loss, stacked and mortared together into a bruise that had achieved consciousness and learned to hate everything that was not pain.


He pressed through. The barrier gave way—not with a burst, but with a painful inward implosion, like a collapsing lung—and then there was silence and release.


Aviel stood alone in the gray, waiting to dream.


Dan lifted his palm toward the emptiness above. Intent was enough. "Let it rain."


The creation was instantaneous and corrupt. Glittering motes fell from nowhere, hardening—smooth pebbles first, then stones, then rubies like frozen blood and sapphires like captured sky and emeralds like distilled envy, gold and fine silver and copper, oil jars and bolts of linen, everything Aviel had ever catalogued in his hunger. The noise of his hidden greed was a soundless scream. He grasped and shoved and gathered—pockets, then clothing, then his arms wrapped around the growing pile—and his lips barely shaped the word: "Mine." Half prayer. Half curse.


He gathered until his form was distended and crushed—a silent column in a barren field, the accumulated density squeezing the breath from his lungs. His hands locked in the posture of hoarding. The suffocating mass silenced him.


Then Dan summoned a wind. Cold, scouring, relentless. It stripped the field clean—all that gathered, all that hoarded—carrying everything back into nothingness. The shaping left Dan hollow, a sharp ache behind his ribs, the first installment of tonight's debt.


Aviel looked up. A single star pierced the gray, impossibly bright. But even there, in the star's radiant core, a colder light struggled to be born—a subtle resentment prefiguring the first fratricide, the first envy of a younger fire for an older, the first burning carried forward into every age that followed. Dan did not name it. He pressed his remaining intent into the image instead: The boy must depart. The father must prepare his heart. He held it there and then, gently, released it.


---


The stench hit him first—rot and surrender's sweet perfume—before the cold stone reasserted itself beneath his feet. He was back. Smaller than before. The expenditure registered in every tight muscle and in the way his legs moved like they belonged to someone who had been walking for much longer than one night.


He found the hearth coals again. Coaxed a flame from the embers with a piece of kindling. The torch caught with a quiet hiss. And with new, rawer purpose—smaller and more honest than the purpose he'd started with—he left the main room.


He closed the door of his own room behind him.


Of all the rooms in that house of surrender, this was the only one that did not shame him. Meticulously ordered. A walking stick. A folded blanket. Each possession placed with care. A small corner of perfect order—hard-won proof that he had put his own house in order before daring to criticize the world.


He lay down on the mat. The torch guttered in its crack in the wall. His shadow, thrown large and hunched by the dying light, looked like a man who had been emptied out and not quite refilled. Sleep was not rest. It was summons.


---


He stood on a shore of glittering black sand, the water before him unnaturally calm—a sheet of polished glass. A small boat sat at the water's edge; he dismissed it with a thought. He stepped out onto the surface, savoring the impossible ease of it. This was his dream, and he decided how things worked here.


He was halfway across the cove when the glass shattered.


It cracked from beneath his feet outward in every direction at once—beautiful, in a terrible way—the fracture lines racing out through everything he'd trusted. The peaceful blue bled to bruised purple. The sea erupted. Not weather. Something far older. A maelstrom of liquid stone and bitter brine, a churning so vast it made the previous night's dreamwalk seem like a puddle. He found himself clinging to a single inadequate plank of wood. Waves the size of walls crashed over him. The cold stench of the deep invaded every breath. The sky above was a suffocating churning blanket of obsidian blackness that offered no exit and no stars. Visions of blue and black and white foam conspired to consume him.


Then, from the impossible crushing depths, something rose.


At the southern edge of the world, where the horizon bled into an endless turbulent gray, a colossal head emerged from the waves. The face was carved from drowned stone—ancient, impassive, radiating an inhuman profound cold. Eyes wide as abysses, blacker than the deepest trench in the sea floor. Seaweed, thick and rotting, draped the chin like a hideous beard. Barnacles, sharp and encrusted, clung to the cheeks like jewels of corruption. The body stretched boundlessly beneath the waves, connecting the stone face to a massive serpentine form so far to the south that Dan could not see it but knew it with dream-certainty, spanning the width of the world.


The plank tilted.


The voice did not come from the mouth. It came from the depths of Dan's own skull—a resonant hum that shook his teeth and set the floor of his mind ringing.


The air choked with salt. The cold stench of the deep pressed in from every direction.


"I am Dagon. The power your father has chosen. Lord of Abundance." A pause the length of a slow tide. "Ask, and receive. My path is ease."


"I didn't call you," Dan choked out, brine burning his throat.


"Your flesh calls still, ceaselessly, seeking its appointed rest." The voice had no hurry in it. No offense. The patience of bedrock. "And this night have you sinned—daring to undo my work in your father's house. Do not mistake your petty will for a living offering."


"Abundance of things?" Dan spat, his voice cracking with brine. "Why devote my life to things I cannot carry into death?"


Dagon leaned closer. The movement displaced mountains of water, the secondary wave crashing over the plank and over Dan.


"I offer you a raft, so that the friction of death shall lose its sting. Follow me. I shall restore your mother's voice to you in the night—dreams in which she speaks comfort unceasing. I shall give you a wife whose hands need not be reddened by lye. Sons who will never know the stench of their grandfather's shame. The sluggish, sweet warmth of a life forgotten by the cruelties of the heights. The life your mother desired for you—peaceful, whole, and forgotten by the terrible heights that demand all."


Dan wavered. The vision of it—a whole, unhaunted family, warm and ordinary and unhaunted—struck him like a blade of light against everything cold and hard in his reality. The hunger of it was a hole that went straight through to the other side of the world.


"Your temple fell because of a blinded man," he snarled, the hesitation hardening. "You crumpled before the sacred chest. Why should I serve you rather than El, who dwells higher?"


Something moved through the vast stone face—not expression, nothing that simple. A reorganization.


"My temple fell not for want of strength, boy. There exist boundaries—ancient lines that must not be transgressed. The realm of the living cannot trespass too far into the realm of the dead. We are bound by different laws. Mistake not my ancient restraint for impotence."


The vast head leaned further in, seaweed beard dragging across the chaos below.


"Look at the war within you. Look at the wars between peoples. Death is not a distant shore. It is the very sea in which you are standing at this moment. And I am the very earth those mountains are rooted in, boy. Your struggle is my foundation. Every step you take upward is a step I must sustain."


"You offer me a raft for a life of soft lies?" Dan shoved the salt water from his eyes. "I just saw what that bought my father. I'd rather drink the sea and sink than live one more day in your filth."


The laugh that followed was the sound of mountains of bedrock grinding against each other in the deeps of the earth—slow, foundational, utterly certain it would arrive.


"The one who pursues the heights becomes the monument—cold, isolated, and alone." The voice dropped further, a crushing personal judgment that bypassed his ears and lodged directly behind his sternum. "Choose the heights if you must. But know this: the silence of the cosmos awaits you. The star you become shall burn alone.


"When you speak, no man shall understand. Your instrument shall be shattered. Your tongue shall be made a heavy thing, speaking a foretold truth that is only ever heard as noise—the worthless babble of the judged.


"You will have no witness."


The words settled into him like stones dropped into still water, sinking before he could stop them.


Dagon's form surged forward—a tidal wave of darkness. The dream began to fracture. Lines of blinding white-hot light cracked through the suffocating black, the nightmare diminuendoing toward its final unresolved seventh—the phantom opera of stone against stone in the deeps below the world, collapsing toward a chord that would never resolve.


"If ever you should alter your tone," Dagon said, his voice the sound of bedrock grinding in the dark below the roots of mountains, "merely knock three times, ask twice, then wait. I shall come. I am the cadence you seek to outrun."


---


Dan woke with a strangled, silent cry.


The sea's phantom opera still echoed—a static cry of brine and chaos. He sat up gasping. The familiar stench of the house returned with sickening clarity. Dawn scratched a cold gray line through the narrow wall slit.


He was not merely terrified. He was shattered. The muscles clenched to rigid, jaw locked, breathing a frantic high-pitched whistle. He had poured his will into shaping his father's dream and then spent every reserve defying something that had been old when Hebron was new.


He lifted an unsteady hand. The fingers thick, sluggish. He tried to say the simplest word—Mother—and the tongue went heavy in his mouth. Not silent. Not yet. But dense, forewarning, a cold thing lodged where certainty used to live. The terror was not the physical sensation. It was what it foretold: to be a speaker whose truth was only ever heard as noise.


No witness, still ringing like a bell struck once in stone.


He moved to the wall slit and looked up. The sky was turning from black to a bruised and hopeful purple.


"Stardust to stardust," he whispered.


His voice was still there. For now.


He lay back down. His shadow against the wall was enormous and hunched—a shape barely recognizable as a boy. He had chosen the cold fire of the star over the warm rot of the sea. The house was divided against itself, and so was he. He had not come here tonight to save his father. He had come here to test whether the power still held, and it had held, and the cost of testing it was written in every tight muscle and leaden limb. Dan's sleep would bring him no more rest than his father's. They were sleeping the same sleep now.


No witness.


Below his room, the bedrock groaned—a low, grinding claim. Dagon, settling into the earth beneath the house, making his weight known.


But at his chamber door, something else stirred. A soft sound—not quite tapping, not quite rapping—a gentle insistence at the threshold. A purr, low and patient, neither threat nor comfort. Something curious. Something waiting.


Dan closed his eyes. Two presences now: one below, ancient and crushing. One at the entrance, watchful and strange.


He pulled the thin blanket to his chin and tried not to think of what might be gently entreating entrance at his chamber lattice in the dark.
