Chapter 1: Stardust to Stardust

The torch light, clenched in the boy's hand, trembled, casting suspicious shadows across the narrow hallway. The frail flame scattered light like cheap copper. It shivered not from wind, but from the erratic tempo of the boy's muffled heart, a frantic drummer in a condemned dwelling.

Each barefoot step on the cold, unforgiving stone was a profound, private wager, where the slightest misstep risked the betrayal of a clank, a dry scrape, or any clatter that might announce his small, forbidden errand. This was no mere silence; it was a coiled, ancient emptiness that smelled of sulfur and expectant soil—the still patience of serpents in a garden. This was no home, but the heavy, unmoving lair of a man devoted to the earth's sheer, magnetic pull. Therefore, the boy's every calculated movement toward his father’s room was an act of quiet, necessary heresy.

The four-room house, which had once been a space warmed by a mother's songs, had ceased to be a simple structure. It had spread not merely into a ruin, but a physical testament to Father Aviel's catastrophic unraveling since her disappearance. This house had been built in the days of a great king, but the man had brought his own gods with him from the Western plains. This was a vile, intricate shrine, a temple built of forgotten things, consecrated by a scholar who willingly surrendered his life to gravity's claim. The house was cold, but with the specific, internal chill of a place where the sun has never reached. The entire structure felt like a pressure applied inward, seeking to crush its inhabitants back into the formless dust from which all flesh begins.

Navigating its turns was not simple walking; one was forced to pick a path through the snare of matter, feeling its terrible, rooting gravity with every breath, as if the air itself possessed a viscous debt. Piles of broken pottery shards and failed instruments—things that had betrayed their original purpose—loomed like small, silent monuments to futures that died stillborn. Rotting textiles, Mother Zuna's sacred threads left mid-shuttle, had unraveled into mildew and waste, a promise broken not by time, but by overwhelming grief. The decaying fabrics sloughed off the looms, draping over discarded, brittle animal bones, making a horrifyingly dry rustle—the sound of all vitality surrendering to the material.

The failed records lay everywhere: Fragments of parchment and crumbling wax tablets, once holding records of birth, debt, and lineage, were scattered beneath the debris, meticulously stripped clean of meaning by unseen, hungry mold. Withered sandals and rusted bronze tools lay scattered like the cast-off implements of a defeated messenger. Desiccated fruits, once heavy with the promise of life, now clung to their seeds like mummified burdens, a stark testament to a harvest withheld. The water skins were brittle and cracked, smelling of stale leather and profound thirst, mocking the very concept of living water. Even the well, the house's source, had stopped remembering how to hold water. Thirst was the final state of everything here, a physical manifestation of a profound internal craving.

Dan, the boy with black hair and kind eyes that had witnessed too much raw, exposed suffering, picked his way through the refuse. A bone-deep chill settled on his spine, born not just of the filth, but from the corrupt heart of a man who saw treasure in rot and comfort in pity. Father Aviel had been a scholar whose mind was sharp as flint. He had traded that bright, clean currency for the slow, sticky joy of letting go. He had ceased the daily rites of cleaning and maintenance, the small, repetitive acts that hold the chaos of the world at bay. This deliberate cessation of order, this philosophical embrace of collapse, was the true horror. It was the choice to stop fighting gravity, a choice that makes the earth cling to you a little too much.

Dan had to leave this place of reckoning before the material decay claimed him, too. He knew this house was a place consumed, a silent monument to what was lost in the fires of the cities of the plain. Turning back was not an option—it was a history of failure written in the first book of all beginnings. He paused by the hearth in the main room, where the glowing coals pulsed like a small, exhausted red heart in the surrounding blackness. His anchor, clutched in his hand, was the Zuna stone: a river rock his mother had rendered perfect and cool against his ribs. It was the one truly simple thing he owned in a house dedicated to the beautiful, horrifying complexity of the messy.

Dan crept to Aviel's doorframe and listened. A low, ragged snoring, the sound of a man trying to outrun his own breath, issued from within. The boy held his breath, and with a quiet snuff, deliberately extinguished the torch. He plunged the area into a deep, choking darkness, relying only on the sharp memory of light and the faint moonlight filtering through a narrow window slit in the hallway wall. The pungent smell of burnt wick lingered, a bitter tang of parting. 

 The ache in Dan's chest was a cold, dull throb—the physical cost for the upward path he was about to take. He remembered his father's final, damning dismissal from three nights past:

"If you are still searching for a god, boy," Aviel had hissed, "then pick up a stone and make it so! Seek your answers from a power you can touch."

The words were a bitter acid, a justification for his father's descent into matter-worship. But Dan refused the stone, and the earth it represented. His gaze instinctively sought the open sky, searching the vast, starry canvas above. He knew the true answers lay not in the heavy ground, but in the heights, because his mother, Zuna, had taught him that all things returned to stardust, and the stars themselves were the closest thing to the unseen source's unwavering truth. His dreams had always taken him to the high places—not fleeting images, but exercises of will where he flew or climbed endless stairs.

But this night's work was not for his own ascent. He was entering the Deep Dreamscape, which was nothing less than the sleeping mind of the world, a boundless place whose full tale was longer than all the threads in his mother’s unfinished spool of yarn. This mastery was the perilous art of commanding raw elements with nothing but a thought. This gift was often feared, whispered to belong only to certain witches who consorted with the shadows and silent, watchful cats. This rare capacity was a gift shared with his mother, and it was the means he needed to access his father's mind.

Dan knelt by his father's cot. The cold stone of the floor immediately seized his bare knees, the sensation sharp and immediate. He slowed his breath, silencing the frantic voices in his inner self as Zuna had taught him.

He remembered her hands: "The last scrubbing is the one that matters, Dan. Do not fear the emptiness. Make the vessel empty before you try to use it."

He forced his mind empty, chasing the last fragments of earthly attachment away. He let go of the phantom scent of Zuna's memory and the comforting shadow of Aviel's history. The pressure of leaving the familiar was a sharp agony, a deep shedding. The stone beneath his knees dissolved, not instantly, but with a horrifying, slow certainty, as if the floor itself was sinking into quicksand. He waited through a full breath before the sensation of matter completely vanished. In that terrifying, perfect stillness—that necessary, terrible emptiness—he stepped sideways and into the Dreamscape.

The immediate change was the silence. The Dreamscape was a place of endless, ethereal gray, a formless void. It was as things must have been before Time, when the first words of truth were spoken.

He found himself standing at a shimmering border—the veil of his father's sleep—a wall of shattered, disorganized memories and the heavy, black weight of regret. This was the guarded perimeter of his father's internal world, dense with years of self-deception. He could not fight it; he could only pass through.

Dan drew a breath of pure intent, and a primal surge of focus flowed through him. The barrier to his father's mind—the Veil of shattered, disorganized memories and the heavy, black weight of regret—did not dissolve with grace. Dan hit it like a wall of cold, viscous tar. For a terrifying, suspended breath, he was crushed under a sensory onslaught: a rush of profound, paralyzing grief that was not his own, and the sickening, sugary stench of years of self-pity and rot. The intrusion felt like a psychic violation, forcing him to momentarily shoulder his father's crushing internal debt. He channeled his will forward, demanding passage. The boundary gave way, not with a burst, but a painful, inward implosion of pressure. Then, silence and release. He was inside, witnessing a featureless field of raw, indistinguishable material.

Aviel stood alone in the gray waiting to dream. His formless void teeming with potential, as things were before Time when the first words of truth were spoken.

Dan exhaled, a soundless act of command:

“Let it rain.”

The creation was instantaneous and corrupt. Glittering motes rained down, then knit together, hardening, forming smooth pebbles, and then heavy stones that accumulated. It was the unnatural, accelerated genesis of devastating accumulation. By Dan's will and word, father Aviel saw gold. His hands moved through the stones, frantic, the noise of his hidden greed a soundless scream in the dream. He shoved them into his pockets and then beneath his clothing.

"Mine," he whispered, hoarding his own delusion.

He gathered the ever-burgeoning accumulation until his very form was distended, crushed. He became a lonesome pillar of stone in a barren field, the accumulation squeezing the breath from his lungs. The father's eyes glazed over, his mouth open in a soundless gasp. The suffocating mass of his possessions silenced him, permanently locking his hands in the posture of hoarding.

Then, with a final, deliberate breath that was both a sigh and a judgment, Dan summoned a wind, a quiet, purifying current. The shaping left him hollow, leaving a sharp, internal ache behind his ribs. The wind swept across the field, gathering the grit and its crushing creations, carrying everything back to the nothingness. In that brief moment of relief, the father looked up and saw a single star pierce the gray, impossibly bright. But even there, in the star's radiant core, a colder light struggled to be born, a subtle resentment prefiguring the final measure.

Dan poured his final, potent intent into that fleeting vision: Go and tell the truth. He held the image of the star, imprinting it upon his father's mind. Then, gently, he released his awareness.

The stench hit him first—rot, waste, surrender's sweet perfume—before the cold floor registered beneath his feet. Drained but with renewed sense of purpose, Dan left his father's room. He sat up, gasping for breath, the coldness a physical ache that settled in his bones. The darkness of the hallway was a sudden, heavy, oppressive presence. He felt smaller now, his confidence spent.

He knelt by the glowing coals in the hearth. He extended a steady hand, coaxing a flame from the embers with a small piece of kindling. The flame caught with a quiet hiss. He lit his torch, its light a familiar, hopeful presence in the gloom, and with new, resolute purpose, left the main room.

Back in his room, the door closed behind him. Dan's small room was a meticulously tidy corner of perfect order—the hard-won proof that he had put his own house in order before daring to criticize the world. This was his sanctuary, his final act of self-possession. His body was a raw, aching weight after the spiritual exertion; he crumpled onto the cot, allowing the simple linen and the welcoming darkness to pull him toward oblivion. Yet, even as the darkness claimed him, a chilling dread coiled around his fading awareness. Sleep was not rest but summons.

He found himself standing on a shore of glittering, black sand. The water was unnaturally calm, a sheet of polished glass. He dispersed the small boat with his will. He stepped out onto the water, and it held him, savoring the impossible ease of his stride. He was halfway across the cove when the glass shattered with a deafening crack, pulling the false foundation out from under him. The peaceful blue instantly bled into an angry, bruised purple. The sea did not merely roar; it was a maelstrom of pure, liquid rock and bitter brine.

He found himself clinging to a single, inadequate plank of wood. Colossal, black waves crashed over him, the cold stench of the deep clinging to the foam. Visions of blue, black, and white—water, night sky, and foam—twisted together, trying to consume him. The sky, a suffocating, churning blanket of obsidian blackness, offered no escape.

The face was impassive, carved from drowned earth and time, radiating an inhuman, profound cold. His unblinking eyes, vast and black like the starless void, locked onto the boy's. The staggering scale of the entity defied light and reason, an entity of absolute material chaos, the Leviathan of the deepest waters.

He was not merely terrified; he was shattered. The cost of his defiance was immediately registered in his physical being: his muscles were clenched, his jaw was locked, and his breathing was shallow, a frantic, high-pitched whistle. He had chosen the cold fire of the star over the warm rot of the sea. The suffering was absolute. The struggle was eternal. The climb had begun.
