Chapter 3: Lift Up 

Something was wrong. The well water, just moments before a promise of cold clarity, had twisted into a mirror for something else entirely. It was a phenomenon so unsettling, so utterly otherworldly, that Dan's weary mind, already pushed to its limits by the day's trials, struggled to process it. The image that had coalesced was not merely a reflection, but a shimmering, impossible vision, rising from the dark, still depths.
A being of impossible beauty, a form perfectly sculpted from the very dreams one dared not whisper (she too would appear to you precisely as your heart's most secret longing). The air thickened around him, sweet with the scent of jasmine and ripe pomegranates, and a strange, profound lightness, a dizzying emptiness, settled in the pit of his stomach. It was the feeling of a profound hunger, but not for food or water. It was a hunger for rest, for softness, for the quiet end of his struggle—the ultimate Snare. They were adorned not merely with jewels, but with the very essence of them: rubies that pulsed with the warmth of a lover's heart, emeralds that gleamed with Spring’s promise of green pastures, and sapphires that held the cool, deep mystery of the night sky. Symbols of fertility and abundance, carved from ancient bone and polished stone, seemed to float around them, each one humming with a silent, vibrant energy that sang a promise of solace, of softness, and of rest. Their eyes, luminous and ancient, burned with an intensity that dwarfed any simple allure; they were pools of starlight and shadowed desire, drawing one in, promising the culmination of every secret longing. This was no mere flesh and blood. This was a goddess woven from the very fabric of life's deepest hungers.
“I am Lady Astarte,” the figure boomed, their voice a coiling, inescapable tide, dragging Dan into its fertile, intoxicating depths. “I am the Lord of Fertility. For this purpose was I wrought, and for this purpose have I come. I bring unto [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] the Truth of [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] barren striving, and the true path to deliverance.”
Their presence pulsed from the water, a silent, powerful energy, a force that resonated with the very lifeblood in his veins, a hum that seemed to vibrate in the hollows of his bones. And they spoke, their voice a sound woven from moonlight and ripe fields, from the laughter of lovers and the cries of newborns, from the rustle of silk and the murmur of ancient rivers. It was a sound that bypassed his ears entirely and echoed directly in the deepest chambers of his soul, a melody both terrifying and irresistibly sweet.
They smiled, a slow, devastating expression that promised every earthly delight, every comfort, every forgotten joy. "[PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] didst reject my follower on the road," they purred, their voice laced with cold amusement and simmering fury, a subtle, dangerous undertow beneath the honeyed words. "That one was a vessel of My power, a taste of the abundance I offer. And [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] didst turn away. [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] didst scorn the flesh, the very fount of life and joy. For this cause am I here, child. To reveal unto [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] the folly of [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] chosen path."
The well water swirled faster, the reflection of Astarte growing clearer, more vibrant, almost painfully so, as if the very air was being drawn into her image. The scent of sweet jasmine and rich earth rose from the well, mingling with the dry desert air, a heady, disorienting perfume that promised oblivion. Dan stumbled back, his hands scraping on the rough stone edge of the well. His mind reeled. This was not a dream. This was real. And it was far more powerful than any vision his father had ever described. His body, already weary from the long day's march, from the relentless sun and the gnawing thirst, felt a sudden, profound weakness, a vulnerability that craved nothing more than relief. He had walked alone, and the solitude, though chosen, had begun to gnaw at the edges of his resolve.
"[PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] path is one of emptiness," Astarte continued, their voice a silken ribbon unspooling in his mind, drawing him in even as he tried to resist. "Of denial. [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] seekest to shed the bonds of the world, to ascend to some barren, distant height. But I tell [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE], sweetness is found in the depths, in the full embrace of life. The soil, the vine, the blossoming womb – these are my domain. And they are joy." The words were not merely heard; they were felt, a warm current flowing through his veins, whispering of ease. "Why seek the cold, distant stars when the warmth of human touch awaits? Why strive for lonely heights when the softest bed, the fullest meal, the laughter of companionship, can be thine for the taking? This journey of yours, this solitary quest, it is a foolish thing, a denial of the very essence of what it means to live. Life, dear boy, is found in connection, in the weaving of souls, in the shared warmth against the night's chill. These are the gifts I offer, the true abundance."
The image in the water pulsed, and Dan felt a sudden, intense yearning bloom in his chest, a hunger that went beyond mere physical thirst. It was a desire for comfort, for warmth, for the softness of skin and the sweetness of ripe fruit, for the simple, undeniable solace of not being alone. The parched dryness of his journey seemed to cry out for the very things Astarte offered. It was a tempting vision, seductive beyond words, a return to all the senses he had just tried to deny. For a fleeting, desperate moment, he wanted nothing more than to dive into that well, to drown in the intoxicating warmth of Astarte’s promised abundance. Indeed, Dan almost fell into the well that day. (For even the stoutest heart, when faced with such overwhelming sensual delight after a long and arid road, might falter, and refuse to engage in the Curiosity of its own Internal War.)
But then, a grounding image flashed in his mind: his father, trapped in the dusty squalor of his own choices, suffocated by the very weight of "things." The stagnant air, the rotting piles, the festering spiritual decay—the crushing reality of Aviel's Rot. He remembered the clarity of the starry night from his own visions, the feeling of soaring, of shedding burdens, of finding the unburdened way. He remembered the chill, pure air of the peaks, the vast, boundless sky.
He lifted his head, forcing his gaze away from the swirling depths of the well, fixing his eyes on the distant horizon, where the faint glimmer of the first stars was beginning to appear against the fading twilight. "I'm not seeking a settled life, Lady Astarte," Dan said, his voice raw but firm, cutting through her potent allure. "Your abundance… It feels heavy. It clings." Astarte's beautiful face in the water contorted, ever so slightly, a flash of pure, cold fury passing through their luminous eyes. The seductive purr in their voice hardened, gaining an edge of ancient, terrible power. "Heavy? Nay. It is the very tapestry of existence. [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] art but a thread, and [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] dost presume to sever thyself from the weave? Foolish boy! [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] pride shall be [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] undoing. To ascend to the barren, to float among the cold, distant lights… is to know truly nothing. It is to know the bleakest of all solitudes." The well water began to boil, sending up plumes of fragrant steam that coated Dan's face like a shroud, burning his skin. Astarte's beautiful, terrible image expanded, filling his vision, their face contorting into something monstrous. The air grew thick and heavy, each inhale a struggle against an unseen weight. He felt an invisible pressure pressing down on him, a crushing force that threatened to drive him back into the earth, a feeling he'd crafted for his father's nightmare, but amplified a thousandfold. The very ground beneath his feet seemed to tremble with her wrath. "[PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] art a frail, trembling thing!" Astarte’s voice boomed, no longer a purr but a resonant gong striking against the inside of his skull. "To turn from the warmth of the soil, the embrace of the Living, for what? For empty air? For the cold, indifferent gaze of distant stars? I tell [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE], boy, the cosmos cares not for [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] puny aspirations! [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] art born of the earth, and to the earth shalt [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] return!" The pressure intensified, forcing him to his knees beside the well. His head swam, and black spots danced before his eyes. He felt a profound sense of loss, a vital cord severed, as though something essential were being ripped from him, piece by agonizing piece. This was the sharp cost of refusing such overwhelming power. The path upward was a lonely one, and it demanded everything. But even as the darkness threatened to consume him, a grounding image pierced through the terror. Not from the well, but from deep within his own mind. The memory of the single, distant star from his vision, the one his father had dismissed, now grew brighter, pulsating with a gentle, inviting light. He heard Zuna’s voice, faint but clear: “Up, my little pigeon. Always up.” He remembered the suffocating weight of his father's house, the clinging burden of things. He gritted his teeth, digging his fingers into the rough stone of the well-curb. His muscles screamed with the effort, but he refused to fall entirely. His head was bowed, but his spirit was not broken. He remembered his purpose. "No," Dan whispered, his voice hoarse, barely audible above the booming wrath of Astarte, but resolute. "I will not return. I seek the light. I seek the Truth. I will not be pulled down by the burdens of the flesh, nor the comforting lies of the earth!”
As Dan uttered those words, a furious, chilling shriek tore through the air from the well, a sound that cracked the very stones and sent a shiver of primordial dread down his spine. It was the sound of ancient power denied, of cosmic rage unleashed. The image of Astarte in the water flared, their face dissolving into pure, blinding light, then twisting into a vortex of swirling dark water. "If ever [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE] shouldst need me, child, merely knock thrice, ask twice, and I shall—" The scent of jasmine and earth curdled into something acrid, like burnt offerings and decaying flowers. The well water roiled violently, surging over the edge, drenching Dan in a wave of cold, muddy spray that smelled not of life, but of death and corruption. He gasped, choking, spitting out the foul water.
When he looked up, gasping for breath, the well was still. The water was once more placid, reflecting only the distant, innocent stars beginning to pierce the twilight sky. The air, though still holding a faint, metallic tang, had lost its oppressive weight. Astarte was gone, her challenge met, her ancient power rebuffed.
Dan stood, shaky but resolute, his clothes plastered to his skin, shivering despite the lingering heat of the day. He had faced a god in the light of day or something very much like one, and had not broken. The exhaustion was bone-deep, but beneath it, a strange, exhilarating lightness began to hum within him. The very ground felt different under his feet, less like solid earth and more like a launching pad.
"Harlot!" he spat, the word raw and trembling, directed at the lingering presence, the fading image, the sheer audacity of the temptation. He had refused a goddess.
Twice in one day, the world had presented him with paths of ease, cloaked in power and comfort, and twice he had turned his back. The confrontation with Astarte had drained him more than the walk from Hebron, leaving him trembling, but a strange, unyielding core had hardened within him. A new strength, sharp and unyielding, seemed to hum just beneath his skin, a force forged in the crucible of divine temptation. Without filling his water container, Dan continued north.
He reached Bethlehem as the light faded and night began to fall, the hills around the town softening into shadow like sleeping giants. He found a shepherd's shelter willing to offer him a corner for the night and shared a meager meal of bread and cheese with the family. The low murmur of voices from nearby homes and the distant bleating of sheep on the hillsides, sounds as comforting as an old lullaby, drew him outside the shelter for a moment.
Bethlehem, even as evening settled, felt older, tied to the quiet rhythm of flocks and fields, but also expectant, alive with whispers of the times. It felt like a place rooted in the soil, dreaming ancient dreams. He saw a few townsfolk gathered near a lamp hanging by a doorway, their faces illuminated in the soft glow, looking like faces carved from warm wood as they spoke in hushed tones. Curiosity, that familiar companion, drew him closer. He lingered just within earshot, listening.
Dan listened, the talk of leaders and uncertain futures an echo against the battles he had just fought. He heard a man declare, "King David! A true warrior, that one! He'll bring us peace, mark my words!" A woman's voice, softer but firm, added, "No more Philistines raiding our threshing floors, not with him on the throne! The High Power is clearly with him, guiding his hand!" Another chimed in, "Born right here, in Bethlehem soil, he was! He won't forget us!" The quiet talk wove through admiration for his deeds and a soft anticipation of what his continued leadership might mean. "A brighter future, that's what he promises," someone murmured, "a true king for all Israel!" He stood there for a while longer, absorbing the quiet hope and underlying anticipation of the town, feeling the weight of their collective outlook, like carrying a basket full of wishes. He then returned inside the shelter, lay down on a rough cot, the conversations of the townsfolk fading into the background like distant music, soft and indistinct. The exhaustion was immense but the strange lightness, the exhilaration born of resistance and resolve, still shimmered beneath it. He had held his ground against the very forces that sought to pull him down or root him firmly in the earth, and that defiance felt like wings. Sleep claimed him instantly. It was a deep sleep, deeper than any he had known before, uncluttered by the anxieties of the house or the lingering presence of dark gods. He slipped effortlessly into the Dreamscape, but this time, he was not navigating a desolate field or a stormy sea. He was already moving. Up. He rose with incredible speed, leaving the sleeping city of Bethlehem far below, a tiny cluster of lights on the back of the world. The ground fell away with dizzying speed, the familiar hills flattening. He shot through the heavens, the air growing impossibly thin and cold, the sounds of the world fading to an absolute, profound silence. He shot past clouds, vast and swirling like celestial oceans, painted in impossible colours by the sun he was rapidly approaching. The moon, a colossal pearl, grew rapidly in his vision, a pockmarked, silent world hanging in the blackness. He passed it in an instant, and he noticed, with undeniable certainty, that the moon smiled at him. He felt a pull, a strange, insistent tug, as if the moon were trying to draw him in, like a great stone in the sky pulling at the water below. But the very core of him, hardened by refusal and lightened by shedding, offered no purchase to its ancient grasp. He was able to go even higher. Then the sun itself began to shrink, becoming just another bright star as he left the dominion of its light. As he soared past it, a flash of red light from its surface revealed, for just a single heartbeat, the fleeting illusion of a face, one so familiar yet twisted by a chilling, resentful fury. Then it was gone, swallowed by the silent vacuum of space, leaving behind a cold shadow. And then, the countless stars erupted around him. This was new, higher than he had ever reached before. They didn't appear one by one, scattered across a distant canvas. Instead, as his speed surged, the very air around him seemed to stretch and twist, pulling him forward. The stars ahead, towards which he was hurtling, didn't just get brighter; they converged, their light shifting into piercing, brilliant points that crowded together, forming a blinding, shimmering field directly in front of him. It was like plunging into a waterfall of pure light, each drop a distant sun. The stars to his sides, meanwhile, seemed to stretch and smear into long, thin streaks of light, like cosmic rain falling past him with impossible speed. He flew through their midst, sensing the chilling emptiness around him, yet completely bathed in their ancient, pure light. The silence was absolute, broken only by the silent rush of the light streaks as they blurred past. He saw celestial clouds not as distant smudges, but as vast, swirling cathedrals of gas and dust, painted in colours that had no names in the waking world – electric blues that hummed with unseen energy, fiery oranges that burned with silent heat, deep, velvety purples that swallowed light – structures so immense they dwarfed any mountain, any city, any world he had ever known. He was among the stars which had been waiting patiently, the ones his people had named and told stories about for generations. A tremor of pure excitement coursed through him, a wonder so vast it made his heart ache. He felt utterly insignificant, a tiny speck, yet perfectly at home in this cosmic ocean. He was a vessel, emptied and ready to be filled with this light, a son returning to his true source—The Unmanifest. He felt no fear here, only awe and a profound sense of belonging. This was the threshold of a new life, a place of lightness and clarity, utterly beyond the suffocating weight of his father's house. He had cast off those earthly entanglements, and his dreams, fueled by his newfound will, had carried him here. He had reached the stars. He hadn’t gone past them, not tonight. This was enough for now. To simply be among them, to witness their silent, rushing glory, was more than he had ever hoped. The thrill of it made his spirit soar even higher. He still didn't understand the full meaning of his journey, nor the ultimate destination of his ascent. He didn't know what lay ahead, or what other ancient beings might test his resolve. But he knew, with a certainty that resonated in his very soul, that he was no longer just running from something. He was moving towards something. Something immense and True and utterly beyond the confines of earthly desires. He was moving towards the stars, and the stars, he now knew, were moving towards him. The night was young, and the road, though unseen, called to him with a new, quiet urgency. The boy from Hebron, whose life had been measured in burdens and entanglements, looked up at the boundless expanse above him, and for the first time, felt truly, gloriously free. But a chill, sharp as a winter wind, touched him even in that moment of triumph. (And from a place far older than starlight, there was a quiet, purring contentment as the boy, a nascent judge, began his charted climb.) Freedom, he suspected, was just another kind of vastness, and the stars, for all their beckoning light, were cold, indifferent eyes in the endless dark. He was aiming up, all right. But what, exactly, was waiting at the top?
