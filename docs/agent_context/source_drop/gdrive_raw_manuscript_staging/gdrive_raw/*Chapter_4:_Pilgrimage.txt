Chapter 4: Pilgrimage

The dark, it has a way of seeping in. Not just the absence of light, but the quiet dread that settles in the corners of the mind when the world goes silent. It just waits, patient as a spider in its web, for the moment one lets their guard down, for the sliver of primal fear that creeps in when the light begins to fade. And for young Dan, the night had been a long, whispering journey indeed, though light danced in his vision from distant stars, bright sentinels in the cosmic dark.
He stirred slowly beneath the thin, scratchy blanket, a heavy weariness clutching at him more stubbornly than any dream's fading tendrils. The straw beneath his blanket, sharp as an ill-tempered porcupine, pressed its coarse truth against his tired bones, a constant, nagging reminder that sleep had been a wrestling match with unseen adversaries. The exhaustion was not merely the kind that settles in one's legs after a long day of fetching water. No, this was the deep, marrow-aching sort, a weariness born of a strange, arduous journey. For in his dream, his mind, nimble as a fox through thorny undergrowth, had soared upward, leaving his earthly vessel far below, straining towards heights that defied the very concept of "up." That arduous, invisible ascent, stretching his mind's eye towards the glinting pinpricks of distant stars, had drained him utterly. His mind felt worn thin, like a threadbare tunic, and his body, consequently, mirrored that profound fatigue with a weary sigh. He blinked against the shy, dim light that crept, cautious as a thief, through the small window, squinting as if to scrub away the haze still clinging stubbornly to his thoughts. The room smelled faintly of dust and ancient, cool stone, a scent of ages. He had not truly rested. He thought of his father, still in Hebron, and wondered if this weariness was a taste of what Aviel had endured for years, a spiritual drain far more debilitating than any physical exertion—the quiet, insidious victory of Aviel's Rot.
Dan threw his legs off the edge of the cot, a low groan escaping his lips, and stretched, muscles protesting with tiny, muffled complaints from the night’s unrest. He dressed quickly, feeling the persistent chill of the stone room seep into his skin like a silent complaint. He wrapped his rough wool cloak tighter, pulling the fabric close as he moved towards the doorway. Outside, the first blushing whisper of dawn began to settle softly over the rooftops of Bethlehem, painting the distant hills with a fleeting, golden promise. It was still quite early, the sort of time when the world feels like it’s just waking up. The sun, a shy, tentative golden orb, was still hugging the eastern horizon, its light spreading like spilled honey.
He held a direction in his mind, clear and unwavering as the sky overhead. His journey led him north, towards the great, rugged heights of the Jebusite lands—a place he had only glimpsed in the strange, shimmering tapestry of dreams. Dan had never traveled this far in flesh, only as far north as this very town of Bethlehem. The road ahead would be long, and indeed, the journey already felt vast, the hours stretching thin and quiet, measured only by the steady rhythm of his wandering thoughts, a low, constant murmur in his mind.
The path led him first through the highlands of Gilo, a place where the air grew thin and crisp, sharp enough to make his lungs ache with cold exhilaration. He passed ancient watchtowers, like silent sentinels carved from the very rock. Below, a vast valley stretched, a place whispered about in hushed tones, where the very soil seemed to hum with old, forgotten stories. He moved steadily onward, hour after hour, the land slowly beginning to change under his feet. The familiar, soft earth of Bethlehem gave way to more stone, rougher and more defiant. The scent of dry earth, warm and comforting, now mingled with the sharp, wild tang of unknown rituals carried on the breeze, a scent that prickled the back of his throat.
And then, there it was, a formidable silhouette emerging from the morning haze, growing more distinct with every step. The city of Jebus, perched atop its towering cliffs and jagged rock faces like a falcon on its lofty nest, seemed almost unreal in his mind – a place of ancient power and whispered secrets. He had heard tales of its towering temples and the strange, solemn rites that unfolded beneath the watchful, silent gaze of powers whose very nature seemed to twist the divine, their hungers not for piety but for a darker sort of obedience. He thought of the seductive nature of Astarte, whose beauty was meant to lure him into a lesser contentment. He had refused that easy path, and now he saw an entire city that had built itself upon such a temptation—a sprawling, stone-carved Snare.
As he moved slowly towards the city, its formidable walls and terraces, built high upon the natural rock, began to loom into view. Even before reaching the gates, he began to see unmistakable markers of their faith: carved figures, grotesque and ancient, set into solemn niches along the paths leading up; standing stones, rough-hewn and silent. Small shrines nestled into the rocky slopes. These weren't merely decorations; they were declarations, bold pronouncements marking this territory as being under the ever-present, watchful eyes of their mysterious gods. A silent, potent promise to anyone who dared to approach, a warning etched in stone and shadow.
He entered the city proper through a gate, a dark, gaping maw carved directly into the rock face itself, swallowing the morning light. The air inside was different – thicker, warmer, a heavy shroud woven from the cloying, sweet scent of incense, the sharp tang of human sweat, and the acrid, earthy odor of animal waste. The streets were a dizzying ascent, a spiraling path carved directly into the very hillside, winding steeply upwards like the coils of a sleeping serpent. The very stone felt alive with history, vibrating with the echoes of countless lives. Everywhere he looked, within the city walls now, there were more markers of their faith: statues of strange, angular gods carved into niches, their stone eyes following his movements; pillars rising tall, some topped with symbols he didn't recognize. The city felt dense, layered, built not just on the hill but of it, a living, breathing monument to its strange, ancient powers. It reminded him of Dagon who was worshiped in his father's house. These were gods of the low places, and he had come to an entire city built to honor them. He was not here to linger. He had a different ascent in mind.
He needed to find the way towards the highest ground. He asked a passing townsman for directions. “Just keep north,” the townsman said with a tired smile, “and the heights will guide you. They say the powers live up there still, watching. Always watching.” (The townsman offered a direction, but not the Truth, for no one in this city dared to be truly Curious about the Internal War raging between flesh and spirit, preferring the solid chains of the low gods to the terrifying freedom of the high ones.)
Dan thanked him and continued on, the path rising steadily beneath his feet. The hours stretched long, measured only by the sun’s steady crawl across the sky. The soft earth gave way to rocky outcrops and gnarled trees that twisted like ancient dancers. The air grew colder, carrying a faint saltiness from distant, unseen seas. Occasionally, Dan glimpsed the figures of others on the paths ahead. Tall, lean, and quick of foot, the Jebusites seemed built on a different scale than those he knew from the southern lands, as if sculpted by the very mountains they inhabited. No one spoke to him, but their eyes held a wary curiosity.
As he climbed higher, Dan found his breath coming faster, the cool air filling his lungs with a sharp, invigorating clarity that sharpened his thoughts. The view opened up behind him — the city he had left shrinking into a cluster of tiny rooftops. A profound emptiness settled inside him, not of loss, but of anticipation. It was a hunger only the northern road could feed, a new kind of pilgrimage that began not with his feet but with his very soul.
He passed through the northern gate, Jebus simply a necessary stop, a stepping stone on a much longer journey. The mountain’s rugged embrace finally loosened its grip as he left the city behind. The path led him across a winding ridge and into rolling hills and small, gentle valleys. Shepherds’ calls echoed across the quiet, sun-drenched landscape, a timeless sound. He nodded to other travelers whose faces were etched with the familiar weariness of the road, and skirted small, silent outposts manned by a few soldiers. Here and there, stark reminders of old conflicts littered the landscape—scattered stones bleached by the sun, a broken spearhead half-buried in the parched earth, a faded stain on a rock that might have been blood.
The hours flowed like honey, slow and golden, as he caught a glimpse of the town of Nob, a cluster of houses clinging precariously to a hilltop, and later, the more imposing silhouette of Mizpah. The sun began its slow, majestic descent, casting longer, softer shadows just before another settlement, perched upon a distant hill, finally came into view:
Gibeah. As he entered the town, he was met with a scene of utter, jarring Chaos. The streets were filled with distraught people, their faces twisted with grief and raw, guttural anger, their frantic movements a dance of mourning. The air was thick with lamentation, a suffocating shroud, and the sounds were a dreadful symphony of wails, cries, and angry shouts directed at the indifferent sky.
A beggar, his clothes in rags, his face a roadmap of hunger and despair, reached out a skeletal hand. "Alms, traveler? For the love of the High Power, a morsel to fill this empty belly?"
Dan’s kind heart, a legacy perhaps from his mother, Zuna, couldn't help but respond. He reached into his small pouch and pulled out a tiny, gleaming Gerah, pressing it gently into the man's trembling hand. The beggar’s eyes, dull moments before, widened slightly. "Blessed be," he croaked, then added, almost to himself, "I pray our next true leader is as gentle as you. Oh, how we yearn for a king! A true king!"
Dan’s brow furrowed with genuine puzzlement. The beggar’s words echoed the same desperate cries he was hearing everywhere in Gibeah. A hunger for a king. A ruler. A man to fix their problems. He thought of his father, the madness in his eyes, his house filled with Chaos and devoted to an abundance of earthly treasures. Here, in this city of lamentation, Dan saw that the yearning for a king was a similar hunger, a desperate reaching for an earthly solution. This desperate reaching for an earthly solution was simply a refusal to fight the Internal War of Flesh and Spirit.
The city, reeling from the brutal, tragic death of its recently fallen leader, was a testament to the void—a city consumed by The Chaos and a palpable, pervasive sorrow. He lingered only briefly before the urge to move on became a desperate need.
The sun, by now, was well into its downward arc, painting the sky with deepening hues of orange and purple, like a bruised peach. The road from Gibeah to the next city was not long. He soon saw Ramah, a cluster of buildings clinging to a hill, its high place or altar visible even from this distance. It seemed a quieter place, perhaps, but even from afar, Dan felt a subtle chill, a premonition. The air around the town was not peaceful; it felt hushed, a silence that seemed to hold its breath. This wasn't the kind of quiet that follows a storm, but the kind that precedes it. From a distance, the houses looked like tired, hunched shoulders, and the smoke from the cooking fires seemed to curl not up to the heavens, but to hang low over the rooftops, as if it feared the open sky. He walked on, past Ramah, and as the sun dipped a considerable distance toward the western edge of the world, painting the sky with long streaks of amber and rose, he was tired, yes, his muscles a symphony of aches.
He reached Bethel as the last of the sun's light faded, a town that, unlike others, did not feel like a place he was passing through. Its air was lighter, a cool, invisible current that rippled through him, a melody of peace he hadn't known he was missing. It was amazing; utterly unlike the heavy, humid air of the lowlands, and not the oppressive, suffocating weight of Dagon, nor the vibrant, earthy pull of Astarte. The very stones of Bethel seemed to glow with an inner light, a soft, inviting promise of solace.
He walked into the heart of the town. His body was a symphony of aches, his throat parched, but he was more aware of his exhaustion now, as a separate thing from his spirit. He found a well, its stone cool to the touch, and drew a bucket of water. He drank deeply, the coolness a shock to his system, before sitting down in the shade of a small pomegranate tree. The people of Bethel moved with a quiet, purposeful calm, their voices a soft hum, a counterpoint to the chaotic wails of Gibeah. They seemed to be always looking up, their gazes aimed at the gathering stars. They too, he saw, had found a way to look to the heavens.
It was then he noticed her. An old woman, with a face like a dried riverbed, etched with countless stories, and hair like spun silver, she was stirring a pot over a small fire in her courtyard. She had her head tilted, speaking to the sky in a soft, murmuring voice, a private prayer. He saw her raise her hands, palms up, as if holding the night itself. The sight of her sent a jolt through him, a memory of his own mother, Zuna, who had often stood just so. He watched, captivated, and when her eyes turned to him, they held the same ancient, knowing wisdom. A flicker of recognition passed between them, a silent current that transcended words.
"You look hungry, my son," she said, her voice a low, raspy whisper that sounded like the rustling of leaves.
He nodded, a boy again, stripped of his grand spiritual journey, his grand ascensions. Just a traveler, hungry and in need. She didn't ask his name or where he was going. She just offered him a bowl of lentil stew and a piece of bread, and he sat and ate, the food rich with the taste of earth and fire, restoring him. As he ate, she spoke, her gaze returning to the sky.
"The wind blows through an empty house, my son," she mused, her voice soft, "but if the house is cluttered with things, the wind finds only obstacles. The light has to be given a space to shine."
He felt the Truth in her words, the simple wisdom that mirrored his mother's. He thought of his father’s house, filled with hoarded refuse, and the empty, vast sky he had been traveling toward.
"You carry a thing of hope," she said, her eyes meeting his once more, and he instinctively closed his hand around his smooth river stone. "But hope," she continued, "is merely a wish, a shallow thing on the surface that things might play out in your favor." She reached into a small satchel at her waist and produced a smaller, smooth stone, a bit of carnelian that glowed with a deep, fiery red. She placed it in his hand, a weight of surprising substance. "Here is a thing of faith. For faith is the deep knowing, down to the core of your being, that all things will work out, in everyone's favor, by Time’s end."
He stayed in Bethel, drawn in by her presence. She was not a companion in the way of a friend, but a spiritual anchor, and her words were like his mother's, full of quiet wisdom. He felt himself settling, the wild, desperate energy of his ascent slowly fading, replaced by a profound peace, a feeling of being home.
He spent days observing the townspeople, their faces turned upward like his, their eyes searching the heavens for answers, for direction in the stars. Yet he felt a subtle dissonance, a deep-seated cling to the Earth. Their homes were well-kept, their possessions clean, their leaders revered. They looked up, yes, but they were rooted deeply to the ground, their comfort a chain tethering them to the very thing they sought to transcend. This was a place of deep contentment, but it was a contentment of the body, and the Earth, not the spirit.
When dawn, a soft whisper of pink and gold, returned to the sky for the third time, the realization came to him. The quiet hum of Bethel, the profound sense of belonging with the woman, was the softest, deadliest Snare. He felt a fierce, aching longing to stay, to simply be, to settle. He saw a fleeting image of his father’s chaotic house, his mother's death, and his father’s obsession, and he suddenly understood. This feeling, this pull toward home, was the same kind of earthly tether he had been running from... a kind of good that was not the highest good. An attempt to build his own little heaven on earth. The sharp, physical ache of a soul being rended settled in the marrow of his chest. This was the cost of a Living Sacrifice. His hands instinctively closed around the two smooth stones. The coolness of his mother's stone and the warmth of the woman's stone were a small, stark comfort against the raw ache in his chest, a reminder of the path he must choose. And so, he walked on.
He walked out through the gate, the city of Bethel shrinking behind him with every step, its golden light dimming in his memory, the road stretching north, beckoning him onward. He looked up at the sky, at the first streaks of dawn, and his lips formed the words, "But this is not high enough." It was a quiet promise breathed into the chill morning air, a renewed resolve settling in his heart, even as the heartbreak, quiet and profound, settled within him too. Sometimes, even when one finds a little piece of heaven on earth, a place that shines with a soft, True light, it’s still not enough. And that is a sadness that can break a boy’s heart clean in two, leaving him to walk on, alone, toward a higher, colder truth. (And the truth of his heartbreak was simple: he was leaving not because Bethel was bad, but because it was good enough to trick him into settling, just as the reader, watching, refuses to ask if their own gentle contentment is merely a comfortable Snare.)
