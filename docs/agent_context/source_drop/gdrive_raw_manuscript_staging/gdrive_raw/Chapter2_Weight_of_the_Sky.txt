CHAPTER 2: LIVING SACRIFICE

The scream arrived before the dawn did.

Not a sound of pain — the distinction matters, and I have been cataloguing the screams of the upright
long enough to make it reliably. A sound of pain comes from the body's reporting system, which is
efficient and specific and does not waste energy on volume beyond what is required to communicate
urgency. What tore through the house in the hour before first light was something else: the sound of a
mind that has encountered, in its sleep, a truth it had spent seven years constructing excellent reasons
not to encounter, and which has now encountered it anyway.

This is, in my experience, the loudest sound a person makes.

Dan was off the mat before the echo finished. His feet found the cold stone. His hands found the torch.
The motion from horizontal to purposeful took less than three breaths, which tells you something about
the quality of sleep he had been managing. He moved through the corridor the way he always moved
through the corridor — by memory and by the particular attention of someone who understands that the
refuse around him is not neutral, that it has its own intentions toward noise — and reached his father's
door.

Aviel sat upright on the cot, wild-eyed in the gray pre-dawn, his chest moving in the specific rhythm
of a man who has run a great distance without moving. His hands worked at the bedding, pressing and
releasing, pressing and releasing, as though testing whether the material was still real.

"Dan." The word came out worn thin. "You are here."

"I am here."

Relief moved through Aviel's face and then something else arrived behind it, sharp-edged and cold:
the particular expression of a man who is beginning to understand not only what he dreamed but what
the dream implies about who sent it.

"I had a vision." Aviel's voice had recovered some of its authority, the scholar's instrument finding its
footing. "A field. Gray. Stones falling from nowhere — pressing, pressing — then wind. And a star."

He paused. He looked at Dan with the eyes of a man who taught himself to read texts and who is now
reading one he would have preferred not to open.

"The star pointed north," Aviel said. "Toward the heights."

Dan said nothing. In this family, in this room, in this specific hour, silence was the only honest
response to what was unfolding.

"It was for me," Aviel said. Not a question. Then, quieter: "Or you made it seem so."

The space between them held that claim with the patience of stone holding a seal's impression.

"You entered my sleep." The scholar's voice now. Precise. Without the warmth that had occasionally
lived in it, before. "You moved through my dreaming without my invitation. You shaped what I
witnessed. And now you are standing in my doorway with the posture of a man who has finished
preparing for a journey."

"Father —"

"You dare." The word came out quiet, which was worse than loud would have been. "You dare interfere
with what a man has chosen. You dare reach into the place where a man is most himself and rearrange
the furniture. For what? To correct me? To improve upon the arrangements I have made with the lord
of this house?" Aviel rose from the cot, and he was still, despite everything, the man who had once
been sought out for his thinking, and that man's authority was still present in the body, even if the
body had been living badly. "You are sixteen years old. You dreamwalk with your mother's stolen
gift, which was not yours to inherit without her here to teach you its weight. And you presume."

"The vision was true," Dan said. "What you felt in it was true."

"What I felt was manufactured."

"The feeling was real. I gave it the shape to be visible, but the feeling was already in you. I only —"

"You only." Aviel's voice dropped to the register that meant the conversation was nearly finished.
"You only violated the single space I had left. You only reached into the last room of a man's house
and decided it needed redecorating." A breath. "You want to leave. You have always wanted to leave.
Your mother's blood runs north in you and I have watched it your whole life and I have said nothing,
because a man does not argue with blood, he simply watches it go where it must go. But you will not
dress this departure in the language of my healing. You will not make your leaving into a gift you are
giving me." He pointed — the gesture of a scholar identifying the exit from an argument. "Go. Take
what you have prepared, which I can see by the weight of your posture is already packed. Leave this
house. Leave Hebron. And do not tell me the star sent you."

Dan stood with this for the length of several complete breaths.

Then he said: "The star was real, Father."

And left.

---

He gathered what he had already gathered: the tunic rolled tight, the cloak, the flint and pyrite, the
pouch of dried olives and salt. His mother's carving stone, smooth as a promise kept. The leather pouch
of twenty shekels — the earned kind, weighed and real. The walking staff from its place against the
wall, the rope coiled at its base.

He stood at the outer threshold.

Aviel appeared at the corridor's end. In his hand: a second pouch, smaller, extended without warmth
and without ceremony, the gesture of a man discharging an obligation he had not chosen. "Five
reserve shekels," he said. "For bribes. The road is not kind to those who carry the upward direction."
A pause that held something it could not name. "A skin of water. This bottle of wine — a dreadful
vintage, I warn you, a colleague's gift I never opened." The items placed on the floor between them,
not handed across. "Go north. The city of Dan, at Hermon's base. Your mother believed the veil was
thinnest there." Another pause. "I do not know what I believe. I know what I have built here, and I
know it has not served either of us."

Dan picked up the water skin, the wine, the reserve pouch. He added them to what he carried.

"Father," he said.

Aviel had already turned back toward the corridor.

The outer door opened. Hebron was still and dark and the hills beyond the gate held their breath in the
manner of hills that have been watching human departures for several thousand years and have stopped
finding them dramatic.

---

The gate watchman let him through without comment. This is the courtesy of gate watchmen at
this hour: they have seen enough of predawn departures to understand that the people making them
have made their decisions already and do not require the watchman's opinion of those decisions.

The northern road began where the paving stones ended.

Dan walked. The city behind him reduced itself to sound — the first stirring of donkeys, the distant
rhythm of a weaver's loom beginning its day, the particular quality of a city that has not yet committed
to being awake — and then reduced itself further to silence, which was not actually silence but the
road's own language: the dry scratch of his sandals on packed earth, the tap of the staff, the wind
moving through the sparse grasses on the hillside's edge.

He had gone perhaps four hundred paces from the gate when he saw the boy.

The cord was knotted to a ring driven into the base of a wall beside the road. The cord ran from the
ring to the ankle of a child of perhaps eleven or twelve — the age at which the body has begun its
argument with childhood and has not yet settled the outcome. The child sat against the wall in the
posture of someone who has been sitting in this posture for long enough that the posture has stopped
feeling like a temporary arrangement and started feeling like a permanent condition.

Dan stopped.

The child looked up. The assessment in that look was the rapid, practiced triage of a person who has
learned to read strangers quickly and draw accurate conclusions from incomplete evidence. The
conclusion took less than a breath. Then the child looked back at the road.

"Since when?" Dan said.

"Middle watch," the child said. Without complaint. As information.

"Where is the man who set this?"

"Went to find his breakfast." The child's voice had the specific flatness of someone who has removed
inflection as a survival strategy. "He'll be back."

Dan looked at the cord. He looked at the ring. He looked at the twenty shekels of earned silver in the
pouch at his belt, which were twenty shekels of road, twenty shekels of northward motion, twenty
shekels of the distance between himself and what the dreamscape kept showing him. He looked at the
child.

The pull in two directions was not comfortable. The upward path requires, among other things, the
willingness to pass things one cannot fix, because the alternative is to stop entirely — and there is a
specific cruelty in stopping entirely that masquerades as conscience. He knew this. His mother had
been clear about it, in the way she was clear about things she considered load-bearing: directly, without
comfort, without the softening that would have made the clarity easier and therefore less useful.

He also knew what it felt like to be the cord attached to the ring.

He took three shekels from the road-pouch. He set them on the paving stone beside the ring — not in
the child's hand, because the transaction needed to be legible to whoever came back from breakfast. He
put his knife to the cord and cut.

The child stood. Rubbed the ankle. Looked at the shekels. Looked at Dan.

"That's less than I'm worth," the child said.

"I know," Dan said. "It's what I have to give without stopping."

The child picked up the shekels. Something shifted in the child's face — not gratitude, exactly, which
is a thing that requires safety in order to be felt, and the child was not in a position of safety — but
something adjacent to the recognition that the world contains, occasionally, a person who makes the
less convenient choice. Then the child turned south, toward Beit Zur, and was gone.

Dan stood with seventeen shekels and the particular weight of a decision that was both right and
insufficient. Then he turned north and walked.

---

The road taught him things. This is what roads do, when you have enough of them under your feet and
enough silence to receive what they're saying. The road from Hebron to the northern territories is an
old road — old enough to have opinions about the people it has carried, old enough to have developed
the patience of something that has seen every kind of traveler and has stopped being surprised by any
of them. It ran through the vineyard country first, the terraced hills heavy with the late-season growth,
the smell of turned earth and cut stems carrying in the thinned morning medium. Then the terrain
hardened: limestone outcrops, the specific dry scrub of the hill country, the sky opening as the road
climbed and the city's familiar silhouette compressed itself into something too small to be the place
where he had spent sixteen years.

He passed a donkey seller at the road's first junction, already at his post in the early light, whose price
for a single animal descended rapidly when Dan showed no sign of stopping. He kept walking. He
passed a procession — a merchant's household moving south, musicians and servants and the merchant
himself carried in a chair between two poles, his expression the expression of a man who has arranged
his life so that the road does not touch his feet — and watched it pass with the attention of someone
cataloguing what he was choosing not to want.

Could that man go up? Probably not, Dan thought. Not because wealth was an absolute barrier — his
mother had never said that — but because the accumulated tethering of it, the ten thousand decisions
made in service of its maintenance, would have to be shed before the ascent could begin, and the
shedding of ten thousand decisions at once was a different kind of work than the shedding he was
doing. The merchant had his road. Dan had his.

He walked through the hours as the sun climbed and the morning's coolness burned away and the road
became a hot and serious thing. He passed Bethlehem's outskirts — the smell of barley bread and
animal pens, the distant sound of a gate argument about livestock — without stopping. The water skin
lightened. The shekels at his belt made their small dense claim against his hip with every step.

By the time the road descended toward the valley spring country south of Jebus, the sun was well past
its highest point and his throat had developed the specific dry insistence of a thirst that has moved past
urgency into the philosophical.

He saw the well from a distance. The cluster of fig trees around it. The small group of figures in the
shade.

One of the figures detached from the group and moved toward him before he reached the well's edge.

She was not young — or rather, she held herself with the specific quality of someone for whom youth
was no longer the relevant category. Dark hair braided and pinned with a bone comb. Clothing of the
border-country weave, ochre and deep red, the Edomite pattern he recognized from the traders who
passed through Hebron's market. A bronze disk at her throat. She walked toward him with the
directional certainty of a woman who has decided something, and the decision was visible in the angle
of her shoulders before she had covered half the distance.

"You are very dry," she said. It was not a question or an offer. It was an observation delivered with
the precision of someone who has been observing things professionally for a long time.

"I am," Dan said.

"Come to the well." She smiled — the specific smile of someone who knows exactly what their smile
does and is deploying it deliberately, which is not the same thing as a false smile, but is adjacent
to it. "Let me draw for you."

He went to the well. She drew water with the practiced economy of a person for whom this action is
entirely natural, which made it harder to identify the part of it that was not natural. He drank. The
water was cold and clean and exactly what his body required, which was itself a form of argument.

She stood close enough that the warmth from her was present as a fact, the way warmth from a fire is
present even before you've decided whether to move toward it or away from it. Her hand brushed his
when he returned the cup — not an accident, and not an assault, but a contact that carried within it an
offer whose terms had not yet been stated.

"The road is long," she said. Her voice had found a lower register, the one that carries best in close
spaces. "And there are shorter paths. Paths with shade, and rest, and the kind of company that makes
the rest feel earned." Her eyes held something that was not quite a question, because the question
already knew its preferred answer. "You are going north. The north will still be north tomorrow."

The warmth of her presence had the quality of something that was also a weight — not an unpleasant
weight, which made it more rather than less dangerous. Dan stood with it. He let himself feel it fully,
which his mother had also been clear about: the thing you refuse without feeling it fully is the thing that
follows you.

The weight was real. The warmth was real. The offer was genuine, in the way that a current in deep
water is genuine: it will take you exactly where it is going, and where it is going has nothing to do with
where you intended to go.

"Peace to you," he said. "My path is elsewhere."

She watched him. The smile did not disappear — it shifted into something older and more assessing.
"The heights," she said. Not mocking. Accurate.

"Yes."

"They are cold up there," she said. "I have known people who reached them. They did not come
back warm." She stepped back with the grace of someone who knows how to exit a scene without
losing its final word. "But perhaps cold is what you are looking for."

She returned to the group under the figs. Dan turned back to the well.

He needed more water. He lowered the bucket — the rope was old, fraying at the knot, the kind of
rope that a more cautious person would have replaced — and drew it up and drank again and filled the
skin and stood for a moment at the well's edge with the bucket rope still in his hands and looked down
into the water below.

His reflection looked back.

Then his reflection changed.

It did not change the way reflections change when the surface is disturbed — not a ripple outward from
a central point, not the gentle spreading dissolution of a face into abstraction. It changed from below:
something rising through the water's depth, something that had been present in the water before he
arrived and was only now deciding to be visible.

She rose slowly. The form that assembled itself in the dark water below his face was not a woman —
or rather, it contained womanhood the way a country contains a city: as one feature of a larger
geography. What rose was older than the word for woman, older than the word for goddess, older than
the word for the specific hunger that builds civilizations and then devours them when they stop serving
it. Her beauty was the kind that arrives as a physical fact, the way a stone arrives when dropped:
without negotiation.

She looked up at him from the water and she was not looking at his face. She was looking at whatever
is behind a face. At the thing that uses the face the way a hand uses a tool.

Dan's legs stopped working.

This is the accurate description: they stopped. Not from damage, not from a decision made by the part
of him that makes decisions — but from the withdrawal of the permission that the upward path extends
to the body when the body is cooperating. The permission withdrew and his legs became weight without
direction, present but not operational, and the well's stone lip was the only reason he did not go down.

His hands gripped the coping stone. The rope was still in his hands. The water below him was still
and dark and patient.

She smiled.

The water moved.

Not toward him — upward. The level in the well shaft began to rise, slowly, with the inexorable
quality of something that has decided the distance between itself and the thing it wants is a problem it
intends to solve. Dan watched it come. He could not look away. This was the other thing she was: not
only the pulling-toward but the inability to turn from. The water rose and she rose with it and her face
in the rising water was getting larger and more specific and more itself, and the smile on it was not
cruel — cruelty is a thing that requires the object to matter to the subject, and he did not matter to her
in that way, he was simply a direction she had decided to move — and the water reached the coping
stone and ran across his hands, cold as deep places, cold as the bedrock below the limestone below the
city below the road he had left this morning.

She was still rising.

Her smile was the smile of something that has waited this long and does not mind waiting further.

The water ran over the stone and down the wall and the choice — if it was still a choice, if the word
still applied to whatever was left of his capacity to do anything except grip the stone and look down at
what was looking up — was still his.
