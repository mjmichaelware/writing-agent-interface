CHAPTER 10: FORSAKEN

The interior of the goat-hair sanctuary hung heavy with the residue of the unuttered, like the flaking skin of dead gods caught in a tomb where the light of the zenith had long since been refused entry.

Now, you must understand—and I say this with the slow, rhythmic blink of a creature that has watched the foundations of the Levant settle into the mud—that for a daughter of the Peleset, this stitched sanctuary was a secondary skin. It was a dark womb built to hide the frantic, staccato vibration against the ribs from the cold, judgmental eye of the deep sky. A space where she could pretend that Ba'al-Zebub of Ekron still leaned in to listen, even as the silence began to ripen like rot on a fallen fig. This silence—once Kasha's most potent ally—had settled now in the tent's corners the way moth and rust settle into neglected gold, consuming the certainty she had built over thirty years until only the tarnish remained. Such is what abandonment does, if you are patient enough to watch it work. It does not arrive like a flood. It settles like dust, one patient layer at a time, until the morning you press your palms into the soil and find that the warmth you have always felt there has quietly, without announcement, withdrawn. I should know. I was sitting in the corner when it happened.

It had begun, as such things always do, imperceptibly. Her fingers, usually steady as she pressed marks into wet clay or cast the smooth knucklebones across the mat by lamplight, now drifted—a frantic, aimless wandering that rippled through the tent's bones. She would reach for a sprig of hyssop and find her hand resting on a shard of basalt instead, her mind a swarm of locusts devouring the sequence of her intent. The morning had grown indistinguishable from the dusk, the hours blurring at their seams, the mind losing its anchor—she would register the sun's shifted position through the canvas weave and find herself genuinely surprised that the world had continued while she remained suspended in a thought of the boy. The figs in the clay jar were gone. She could not say when she had eaten them.

She had been watching the boy for the better part of a year through the bronze scrying vessel—not with intention, not at first. He had wandered into the edge of her water when she was attending to something else entirely, a small, solitary figure on the north road with the specific quality of a man paying a debt with every step. What drew her was not brightness—that quality she associated with the hill-people and their upward-reaching religion, which gave her teeth a specific kind of ache. It was the weight of his direction. His aim registered in her bones the way a heavy stone registers in the palm: by its pressure, its refusal to pretend it is lighter than it is. He did not bargain with the road. He paid and kept walking. She had intercepted his dreamwalk into his sleeping father's mind—a frantic, rhythmic call through the shifting gray, dust shaped into the weight of his meaning—with a mixture of awe and resentment she had not planned on feeling. The message had been an echo of the beginning, a reach for the heights; she, a daughter of the dust, could read it only as a threat to the stability of her pastures of gray. She had watched him through the hill towns and the hostile city and the slow, deliberate losing of his voice and his sandals and his eye—had watched him give everything away with the completeness of a person who has decided he is not carrying anything through the last door—and had felt a cold, clean fury at the sea-men who took the eye that had not asked her permission to arrive and could not be made to leave.

She had watched him reach the mountain's base. Watched the giant's face in the moment of receiving what Dan gave him—a man given back a thing he had stopped believing he would hold again. Watched the boy begin to climb. Then the water had gone black and still as the eye of a drowned man, and the spirit of the deep had not moved upon those waters, and her own reflection had stared back from the bowl: a stranger's face, etched with the failure of her house of dust and the silence of the mother who was nameless there forevermore.

Five weeks of silence from a patron is not, by itself, unusual—or so Kasha had told herself on the first day and the fifth and the twentieth, with the diminishing conviction of a woman who knows precisely what she is doing. Ba'al-Zebub served purposes larger than her immediate need; the deep currents bent to rhythms older than her thirty years of petition. She knocked three times on the bronze rim—once for acknowledgment, once for approach, once for petition, the sequence her teacher had pressed into her in the first year, the sequence she had used in every address since—and asked twice, pressing her forehead and both palms against the cold soil beneath the mat in the full prostration her teacher had called the offering of the skull. She held the posture until the cold worked into the bone of her brow and the mineral smell of packed earth filled her lungs.

"You will answer me," she said into the ground. Not a question. Kasha did not ask the ground questions; she told the ground things and waited for the current's response, the way you press a seal into clay and wait for the impression to hold. "I have given thirty years of work into this soil. I have pressed my palms into this valley's floor while kings rose and fell above it. I have spoken down when every bone in my body said up." Her jaw set hard against the mat's weave. "I am owed a response."

The ground received this and gave nothing back, which was within its ancient rights—but there had always been a warmth in the receiving before, a vibration beneath the silence that meant the address had been registered, that something below knew the weight of what pressed above it. Tonight the soil was only soil: cold packed earth and the dense, mineral patience of the valley floor and silence as complete and unyielding as a brass ceiling, all the way down.

Outside, the Peleset camp had become its daily symphony—dry rock grinding against bedrock, the steady massive labor of warriors sharpening iron blades against Cretan whetstones, the dull percussion of bronze greaves on packed earth, the guttural chanting of men whose theology was the weight of what they could lift and the edge of what they could put against another man's neck. Kasha felt it in the marrow of her long bones: the patient declaration of the world's basement, asserting that all things made of dust must return to it, that the iron's new law had no interest in the whispers of a woman whose patron had gone silent. She had been assuming Ba'al-Zebub was busy at the war's heart—bending the valley's tides from beneath, doing what he always did when the nations ground against each other in this basin that had swallowed so many armies it had grown fat on them. She had been assuming this for five weeks now. The comfort was wearing thin in the particular way that assumed things wear: not breaking, only becoming transparent.

She tried the oil. The copper bowl, fat pooled on dark water, had shown her the bloom of pattern for three decades—swirling eddies and spirals, a living text that moved and self-revised, telling her what the ground knew about the hours ahead. She watched the golden film spread across the surface. It sat in flat, stubborn rings, refused to mingle, mirrored the ceiling above her and nothing else. Nothing formed. She stared at its flatness until her own reflection stared back at her from the dark water—a stranger's face, etched with thirty years of faithful service rendered to a source that had, for the moment, turned its back. The dust was claiming its own.

She went to the scrolls. Secondary channels first—pathways through the craft's deep substrate that moved beneath the patron-connection—and found a warmth that arrived and faded before she could source it, a shimmer that dissolved before it resolved into meaning. She threw the astragali: the four pale knucklebones clicked against the mat and lay in the pattern of an attenuated current, present but thinned to thread, which was exactly what she already knew and had been knowing for five weeks. She tried the old petition-form, the libation of cedar oil on the bronze rim, the invocation in the chest register—the voice from the body's deep cavity where breath becomes intention—and pressed her forehead to the mat again and stayed there. Silence. The tent around her held the particular quiet of a space whose current has withdrawn, the way a well sounds wrong when the water has dropped below the rope's reach.

She reached for the hyssop bundle—the fresh preparation she had been meaning to complete for two weeks—and found her hand resting on the basalt shard instead. Stared at it. Set it down. Her mind was attending to everything simultaneously, which is another name for attending to nothing: the hyssop, the scroll's re-oiling, the midday casting she had not done, the fig jar empty again with no memory of the emptying. The sun had moved considerably since she had last registered its position.

She pressed both palms flat against the floor again, cold soil through the mat's weave, and said to it—lower now, the formality spent, only voice remaining: "I have done the work faithfully. I have kept every agreement. I have maintained every working without complaint. I have not looked up when I was told to look down." She pressed until the mat left its impression in both palms. "Where are you." Not a question. The grammar of a woman who has given thirty years into a compact and finds the other party gone.

The ground held its ancient patience and gave nothing back.

Now—and I tell you this with the particular urgency of a creature whose new capacity for knowing things has arrived alongside an equally new and somewhat overwhelming need to tell you all of them—Ba'al-Zebub, the Lord of what flies and swarms and multiplies in the low places, was in Ekron. He had been in Ekron since the last campaign's end, doing nothing with the practiced ease available only to beings of considerable standing and no immediate necessity. He was broad-shouldered and dark and handsome in the manner of things that acquired beauty as a function rather than possessing it as a quality—the beauty of a thing that learned it as a tool. He lounged in the temple courtyard watching the flies on the warm stone with the mild satisfaction of a craftsman watching good work maintain itself without him, rubbing his temple every quarter-hour with two deliberate fingers, an old gesture that had nothing to do with headache and everything to do with a very old wrong turn he could not locate precisely but could feel the distance of continuously.

He had given the Peleset their iron knowledge—not earned, acquired; the distinction matters exactly as much as you think—and they had used it with the thoroughness of people who believe the tool is the victory and not merely the route to one. They had also, in the confidence of this belief, taken the hill-people's sacred chest—gold-plated acacia wood, two carved figures facing each other across the cover, the portable seat of the one whose name Kasha had nearly spoken tonight and very nearly not survived speaking—and brought it into the house of the fish-god, and found the fish-god prostrate before it three mornings running, the third time with both hands broken off at the threshold. The burning that followed had moved through the coastal cities with the conviction of a thing that has been making its point for a very long time and has decided to stop being gentle about it. The chest had been returned on a cart pulled by cows that walked in a straight line when every animal instinct said turn back, because the object on the cart had opinions about its destination that overruled the cows entirely. Beelzebub had watched all of it and rubbed his temple and determined that the Peleset were destroying themselves with iron they had not understood and pride they could not carry and required no further arrangement. He was not refusing Kasha's calls. He was simply absent from them, the way a man absorbed in a fire does not hear his name called from the far end of a dark field. The call arrived. There was no one present to receive it.

Kasha did not know this. A woman of her faithfulness does not readily consider that the silence might be inattention rather than intention—that the thing she has served carefully for thirty years has simply, for the moment, forgotten to look in her direction. I want you to understand that this is not a failure of intelligence. It is, in fact, the specific and bitter cost of faithfulness: the inability to imagine that the thing you have served could be absent without meaning something larger by it. Faithfulness makes you a poor diagnostician of neglect. This is its particular cruelty, and it is entirely unfair, and that, I'm afraid, is exactly the sort of detail the universe tends to leave out of the original arrangement.

She reached for the oldest scroll.

It lay at the table's back wrapped in cedar-oiled linen, in a script older than any writing system she had been taught, marks pressed rather than drawn, running center-outward in concentric rings from before the decision to write had been made permanent. Her teacher had given it with the gravity of a person transmitting something they considered dangerous. Use this only when everything else is exhausted. Then her teacher had not mentioned it again, which is precisely how teachers indicate that a thing is serious. Kasha had been working through the inner third for eleven years; the outer two-thirds maintained their patience, and she had long suspected they were waiting for a specific question rather than withholding information on principle.

She unwrapped the linen. Found the passage for the third form of deep contact: the sound that addressed not the patron but the source from which the patron drew his authority—the root-form, older than the liturgical name, from the time before the name had been separated from what it named. The pitch she had to find without first tuning for it, the way you find a dropped thing in complete dark by trusting the memory of where your hands were when it left them.

She found the first syllable. The second.

On the third her throat closed wrong—and the sound that came out was close, the way a fired vessel is close to the vessel intended when the kiln ran too hot. Close, but split at the seam where the syllables should have fused into something much older and more intact.

"Yah—Vah," she whispered, the sound catching like flint on steel.

The scroll went hot in her hands. Then very hot. She dropped it. In the gap between releasing and landing—the interval that is not nothing, however brief—a fragment of something that predated the current arrangement of things tore free from the broken path and crossed the tent's interior and arrived in me.

Every question I had never known I was not asking became, simultaneously, necessary. The crease in the goat-hair where the canvas had been folded for transport: how many times folded, in what season, by whose hands, and does the folding persist in some form beneath the fiber's surface? The basalt shard on the table: what formation, what pressure, what depth, did anything witness the moment of its making? The oldest scroll: who first pressed these marks, in what year, in what light, with what intention behind the pressing, and is the intention still there—does what a thing was made to say remain inside it after the maker is gone? I sprang upright, black fur at full extension, golden eyes gone very wide.

Kasha spun from the table.

"By the Ancestors' beard," she said, in the specific quiet of a woman whose composure is working very hard, "what was that." She stared. Her eyes found my frantic, open gaze and stayed there, the way eyes stay on something that has changed when the change has not yet been categorized. Something behind her expression shifted—slowly, the way a stone shifts in deep water. She shook her head. A small, perplexed smile touched the corner of her mouth, the first smile I had seen on her in five weeks. "Such—Sak'ranut," she murmured, using the old word for the quality of relentless, consuming inquiry—the word you give a thing that has begun opening every cabinet simultaneously and cannot be stopped. She did not yet understand what she had named. A deity of Curiosity, even a small one, even one wearing a cat, does not get named accidentally. Or perhaps all naming is accident. I was still working through this particular question when she pointed at the mat.

"You are my beast," she said. Her finger was steady. "Not my master. You do not look at me that way. Sit."

I sat. I continued looking at her in exactly the manner she had objected to, because the quality of the look was not a decision I was making but the condition I had entered, and there was no path back to the condition I had been in before the syllable broke. You may as reasonably ask the river to stop being water.

She held my gaze. She looked away first—at the dropped scroll, at the bowl, at her own hands. In the moment before she turned, the expression on her face was not the expression of a woman who has won an argument. It was the expression of a woman who has decided this particular dispute does not merit the available energy, which is a different thing entirely, and both of us knew it, and I filed the difference carefully.

"What was in the third syllable," I said.

Kasha went still.

"Because whatever was in it is in me now," I said, because a question that has started has no mechanism for stopping, this being one of several things about my new condition I was actively discovering, "and I find I have considerable views about what I am made of. This seems reasonable. Most things want to know what they are made of. Even the ones that end up not preferring the answer." A pause. "Also: I can feel the current through the ground. Thin as thread where it should be rope, but present. Moving west toward the coast. Your patron is not absent from this world. He is absent from you specifically, which is a different kind of problem, and the kind that has a direction to look in."

She stared at me for a long moment with the expression of a woman who has managed a great many unexpected developments with professional composure and has just received one too many. Then she sat down on the mat and pressed both palms against the cold soil beneath it—not the petition posture, not the invocation register, just contact—and said, to the earth below her hands, in the voice below her voice: "I have been knowing it for five weeks. I know the current has thinned. I know the bowl shows me nothing. I know what this means and I have been telling myself it does not mean what it means." Her palms pressed harder. "I will not be abandoned. I have given thirty years of faithful work into this soil and I will not be left without an answer." She pressed until her knuckles whitened. "I have held every agreement."

The ground received this with the patience of the ground, which is to say it received it into the record it has been keeping since before records had a name, and held it without reply—in the manner of a thing that has been given everything and returned very little and has no defense for this except that it is very old and was not built to respond to urgency, and this, too, is exactly the sort of arrangement that gets made without the terms being made clear at the outset.

Her hands lifted. She looked at the impressions they had left in the mat.

There was another way—deeper, older, reserved for the moments when every other channel has gone dry: the gray wilderness of sleep, the Sea of Potential, the shifting expanse where the earliest words were still echoing in the mist and reality waited for a will to press meaning into it. Kasha's jaw tightened at the thought. She was not afraid of the gray wilderness's creatures—she had faced the shapes that assembled at the seam between sleeping and waking and fed on the unguarded, and she had found them manageable. Her fear was subtler and more honest than that: it was the fear of a woman who has spent thirty years knowing with absolute certainty where the floor is, suddenly asked to exist in a place where the floor is a decision you make rather than a fact you stand on. She was a woman of the horizontal world—the world of weight and density and the patient accumulation of what presses down. In the gray wilderness, down was something you constructed. Her will had been built for holding, not for making. She had gone there twice in her life. Both times she had come back, and both times she had come back changed in ways that were not entirely comfortable, and the ground had held her when she returned, which was the only thing that had made the returning possible.

But Ba'al-Zebub had spoken to her from the gray wilderness before—had condensed from its ambient dark with the ease of something that had always been there, more himself in that formless expanse than anywhere, the deep currents moving visibly through the gray the way hidden stone shows through clear water from above. If he was findable at all tonight, he was findable there.

And what was behind her—five weeks of silence, the thinned current, the oil that would not bloom, the ground that kept receiving and not responding—was worse than what was in front of her. That is, I have observed, the specific texture of the bravest choices: not the absence of fear, but the presence of a worse alternative.

She snuffed the oil lamp. The tent went dark save for the thin slivers of starlight pressing through the canvas weave, cold and distant, barely enough to name the shapes of things. She laid herself on the worn mat with the deliberate straightness of a woman who is afraid and has decided the fear does not alter what needs doing—a rigid line against the ground, every joint set with intention, the body picked up like a tool for difficult work. She pressed her palms down one final time: low, direct, no ceremony left, only contact. "You will hold me," she said to the soil. "When I return. You have always held me. I am coming back." She held the contact a moment longer—long enough that the cold of the ground came fully into her palms—then she began.

The wordless chant built in the center of her chest, the vibration that extends outward until it finds the body's boundary and reverses, pressing inward toward the central knot where the self is bound to the flesh, working it loose the way a well-packed bundle comes apart when the right knot is found and undone—each cord releasing in patient sequence, the body's grip on the spirit loosening layer by layer without force. I have watched this many times. From outside it looks like a woman going still. What it is, is a woman going somewhere that is not here. The tent poles stretched, the canvas walls drew inward, the scent of dried herbs thinned into the sharp metallic tang of the threshold, and the shadows grew long and specific, twisting into the shapes of things that live at the world's edges and wait, without urgency, for the moment when the boundary between what is and what might be grows thin enough to step through.

She crossed.

The lockdown arrived without announcement, which is its nature and the source of its advantage over every other kind of claim. It does not approach. It is simply the retroactive knowledge that it was always already present—that you have been in its possession for longer than you realized, that the possession preceded the awareness of it. First: the weight on the sternum, not the weight of stone but heavier, the specific density of absolute certainty pressing down with no interest in argument or appeal. Then: the limbs, which did not grow heavy but held—and these are not the same thing. A limb that rests chose to stop; a limb that is held was stopped between one moment and the next, pinned in the interval where will and flesh were being separated with the calm precision of a craftsman who has performed this work before and finds it unremarkable. Her throat sealed. Her eyes would not open. The chest continued its minimum—breath as technical necessity rather than living act, the cold economy of a body maintaining itself in the absence of its mind's participation.

She was awake in her spirit and a sealed jar in her flesh, unable to move a finger, a prisoner in a house of clay that had forgotten how to open its own doors. The staccato vibration against her ribs was the only proof of the frantic furnace within, a rhythmic drumming no one outside the tent could hear. Somewhere in the tent's upper dark, where the canvas sagged between the poles, a raven of the mind began its slow and patient circle—silent, certain, the nevermore of a craft built over thirty years hanging in the air above her with the unhurried authority of a thing that intends to be present when the reckoning arrives. She lay there, a witness to her own entombment, awake and sealed and unable to do anything but fall toward what was waiting for her in the gray.

I stepped onto the center of her chest and pressed my small mass into the weight-claim that pinned her to the mat. I want to be precise about what this was and was not. This was not a cat doing what cats do to sleeping bodies, though I will not blame you if it looked like that from the outside. I pressed my weight down and I purred—the vibration moving through my ribs into my paws into her still chest into the mat into the cold ground below, which received it the way the ground receives everything: without ceremony, without reply, into the record it has been keeping since before records had a name. The sound meant: I am still here. The ground is still here. You have come back before. The cord you are on has not yet run out.

In the gray wilderness—that blank and waiting canvas of shifting grays, that earliest possible light before the first word of the first morning was spoken, where reality held itself in suspension with the patience of something that has always known a will would eventually come to press meaning into it—Kasha stood in the posture of her planting. Feet pressed to the shifting floor. The memory of solid ground held in the structure of her bones, the only solid thing available. The expanse surrounding her in every direction began, with the movement of something that has been waiting and does not find waiting difficult, to resolve. Not approaching—simply becoming more present, the way an image comes forward out of darkness when the eye has finally adjusted. Something assembled from the ambient density of the gray, taking on particularity, taking on weight, choosing with the careful confidence of a craftsman who has performed this work many times for many people which face to wear for this one first.

The first of the six princes stirred in the pastures of gray beyond the veil, and I sat at Kasha's side with my golden eyes open in the dark, pressing my small warm weight against her still arm, purring steady into the cold ground below—because hope, at its most honest and its most threadbare, is not a feeling. It is a decision to remain present while the outcome is still open. It is the small insistent warmth of a thing that has decided not to leave. It is the sound made in the dark by something that believes the person it sits beside will, when the floor dissolves and the gray closes in and the first of the offered faces turns toward them wearing exactly the face they most need to see—will choose, even then, to come back.

Outside, iron ground against stone, patient and indifferent.

Inside the tent, the ground held everything placed upon it, as it always has, as it will when the iron and the tent and the valley's long argument with armies have all been pressed into it and the pressing has begun again.

The choice—as all the true ones are, in every age, in every tent, in every gray wilderness where a human soul has stood alone and felt the weight of what is offered—was entirely hers.
