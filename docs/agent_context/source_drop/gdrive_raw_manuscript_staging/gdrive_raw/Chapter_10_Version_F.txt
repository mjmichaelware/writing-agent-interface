CHAPTER 10: FORSAKEN

The interior of the goat-hair sanctuary carried the particular weight of a room where words have been spoken at length and received in silence—each one still pressing outward from the walls the way heat presses when it has nowhere left to go.

Here is what matters, and I say this with the slow, unhurried authority of one who was present for every syllable of what follows: for a daughter of the Peleset—broad-boned, deep-voiced, the kind of woman whose craft pressed down into the dense, layered record of the valley floor rather than reaching upward at the cold and assessless dark above it—this goat-hair tent, sewn tight against the Jezreel wind, was not shelter. It was second skin. A dark womb built to keep the frantic, staccato percussion of the self from the cold, judgmental eye of the deep sky. A space where she could pretend that Ba'al-Zebub of Ekron still leaned in from the low places, still weighed the petition, still kept the long account she had been depositing into for thirty years—even as the silence in the tent's corners settled the way moth settles into neglected gold: consuming one patient layer at a time, until what had been solid certainty was mostly void. Such is what abandonment does. It does not arrive like a flood. It settles like dust—patient, particular, one layer compounding on another—until the morning you press your palms into the soil and find the warmth you have always felt there has quietly, without announcement, withdrawn. I should know. I was sitting in the corner when it happened.

It had begun, as all failures begin, imperceptibly. Her fingers—usually steady as she pressed marks into wet clay in the petition-form, or cast the smooth knucklebones by lamplight in the reading-cast her teacher had called the scatter of honest bones—now drifted. She would reach for the hyssop sprig and find her hand resting on a shard of basalt instead; set the basalt down; find her hand back on it. Her mind attended to everything simultaneously, which is another name for attending to nothing: the hyssop, the scroll's re-oiling, the midday casting she had not done, the fig jar empty again with no memory of the emptying. The morning had grown indistinguishable from the dusk, the hours bleeding at their seams, the mind losing its anchor—she would mark the sun's angle through the canvas weave and find herself genuinely surprised that the world had continued while she remained suspended in a thought of the boy. The figs were gone. She was not sure she had eaten them.

She had been watching the boy for the better part of a year through the bronze scrying vessel—not by intention, not at first. He had wandered into the edge of her reading when she was attending to something else: a small, determined figure on the north road carrying the quality of a man paying a debt with every step. What drew her was not brightness—that quality she associated with the hill-people and their upward-reaching religion, which gave her teeth a specific ache she had learned not to trust. What drew her was the weight of his direction. His aim registered in the long bones of her legs the way a heavy stone registers in the palm: by its pressure, its refusal to pretend it weighs less than it does. He did not bargain with the road. He paid it and kept walking. She had intercepted his dreamwalk into his sleeping father's room—a frantic, rhythmic reach through the shifting gray, dust shaped into the weight of his meaning—and felt both awe and resentment in equal measure. She had watched him through hill towns and hostile cities, through the slow, deliberate losing of everything he started with: his voice, his sandals, his eye—each thing surrendered with the completeness of a person who has decided he is not carrying anything through the last door. She had felt a cold, clean fury at the sea-men who took the eye, because the eye had not asked her permission to arrive among her concerns and could not, after the fact, be made to leave.

She had watched him reach the mountain's base. Watched the giant's face in the moment of receiving what Dan gave him. Watched the boy begin to climb. Then the water had gone black and still as the eye of a drowned man. The deep currents that had always moved—barely visible, the way wind moves through dark water from far below—simply stopped. The bowl held the ceiling's reflection and nothing else. Her own face stared back from its surface: a stranger's face, etched with the failure of her house of dust and the silence of the one who was nameless there forevermore.

Five weeks of silence from a patron is not, by itself, unusual—or so Kasha had been telling herself on the first day, the fifth, the twentieth, with the diminishing conviction of a woman who knows she is doing precisely that. She had knocked three times on the bronze rim—once for acknowledgment, once for approach, once for petition—and asked twice and waited. She had laid herself face-down in the full prostration her teacher had called the offering of the skull: forehead and both palms against the cold soil beneath the mat, the mineral smell of packed earth filling her lungs with what tasted, at least, like home.

"You will answer me," she said into the ground. Not a question. Kasha of the Peleset did not ask questions of the earth; she told the earth things and waited for the current's acknowledgment the way you press a seal into clay and wait for the impression to hold. "I have given thirty years into this soil. I have pressed my palms into this valley's floor while kings rose and fell above it. I have spoken downward when every tendon in my body said otherwise." Her jaw pressed against the mat's weave. "I am owed a response."

The ground received this with the patience of the ground—and there had always been warmth in the receiving before, a vibration beneath the silence that meant the address had been registered. Tonight the soil was only soil: cold packed earth, dense mineral patience, and a silence as complete and sealed as a brass ceiling from which no petition passes through.

Outside, the Peleset camp had become its daily symphony—and here I mean symphony in the full ancient sense, simultaneous sounds building to a density beyond any single voice: dry rock grinding against bedrock, the patient percussion of iron blades against Cretan whetstones, bronze greaves on packed earth, the guttural deep-chest chanting of men whose theology was the weight of what they could lift. Kasha felt it in the marrow of her long bones: the patient declaration of the world's basement, the iron's new cold law. She had been assuming Ba'al-Zebub was busy at the war's deep work, bending the valley's tides from beneath the basin that had swallowed so many armies it had grown fat on them. She had been assuming this for five weeks. The comfort was wearing thin in the way assumed things wear: not breaking, only becoming transparent.

She tried the oil. The copper bowl, fat pooled on dark water, thirty years of pattern living in the copper's memory—she watched the golden film spread across the surface. Flat, stubborn, sitting in rings that refused to mingle. Nothing formed. It mirrored the ceiling and nothing else. She stared at the flatness until the flatness stared back: a stranger's face, the silver advancing through the dark hair faster than her wards against time had been holding it. The dust was claiming its own.

She went to the scrolls—secondary channels first, the deep pathways that move beneath the patron-connection, older roads—and found a warmth that arrived before she could source it and faded before she could follow it. She threw the astragali: the four pale knucklebones clicked against the mat in the pattern of a current thinned to thread—present, but barely, a wadi worn down to its bones in a dry season—which was exactly what she already knew and had been knowing for five weeks. She tried the libation of cedar oil on the bronze rim. The invocation in the chest register—the voice from the body's deep cavity where drawn breath becomes intention. Forehead to mat again, both palms flat. The full sequence: knock, ask, wait. Silence. The tent held the quiet of a well that has gone below the rope's reach.

Now—and I tell you this with the particular urgency of a creature whose new capacity for knowing arrived alongside an equally new need to tell you all of it—Ba'al-Zebub, Lord of what flies and swarms and multiplies in the low places, was in Ekron. He had been in Ekron since the last campaign's end, doing nothing with the practiced ease available only to beings of considerable standing and no immediate necessity. He was broad-shouldered and dark and handsome in the manner of things that acquired beauty as a function. He lounged in the temple courtyard watching the flies on the warm stone, rubbing his temple with two deliberate fingers every quarter-hour—an old gesture that had nothing to do with ache and everything to do with a very old wrong turn he could not locate precisely but could feel the distance of continuously. The Peleset had taken the hill-people's Sacred Chest into the house of the fish-god and found the fish-god prostrate before it three mornings running; the burning that followed; the Chest returned on a cart drawn by lowing cows that walked straight back when every instinct said turn. He had watched all of it and determined that the Peleset were destroying themselves with iron they had not understood and pride they could not carry and required no further arrangement. He was not refusing Kasha's calls. He was simply absent from them the way a man absorbed in a fire does not hear his name called from the far end of a dark field. The call arrived. There was no one positioned to receive it.

Kasha did not know this. Consider: a woman of her faithfulness does not readily entertain the possibility that the silence might be inattention rather than intention—that the thing she has served carefully for thirty years has simply, for the moment, forgotten to look in her direction. This is not a failure of intelligence. It is the specific and bitter cost of faithfulness: it makes you a poor reader of neglect. This is its particular cruelty, and it is entirely unfair, and that is exactly the sort of detail the universe tends to omit from the original terms.

She reached the oldest scroll at the table's back—cedar-oiled linen wrapped around marks pressed rather than drawn, running center-outward in concentric rings, a script older than any writing system she had been taught. Her teacher had given it with the gravity of a person transmitting something dangerous: use this only when everything else is exhausted. Then her teacher had not mentioned it again, which is precisely how teachers indicate a thing is serious.

She unwrapped the linen. Found the passage for the third form of deep contact: the sound that addressed not the patron but the source from which the patron drew his authority—the root-form, older than the liturgical name, from the time before the name had been separated from what it named. The pitch she had to find without first tuning for it, the way you find a dropped thing in complete dark by trusting the memory of where your hands were when it left them.

She found the first syllable. Clean. The second. Clean.

On the third her throat closed wrong—and the sound that came out was close, the way a fired vessel is close to the vessel intended when the kiln ran too hot. Close, but split at the seam where the syllables should have fused into something much older and more intact.

"Yah—Vah," she whispered, the sound catching like flint on stone.

The scroll went hot in her hands. Then very hot. She dropped it. In the interval between releasing and landing—the gap that is not nothing, however brief—a fragment of something that predated the current arrangement of things tore free from the broken path and crossed the tent's interior and arrived in me.

Every question I had never known I was not asking became, simultaneously, necessary. The crease in the goat-hair where the canvas had been folded for transport: how many times, in what season, by whose hands? The basalt shard: what formation, what depth, did anything witness the moment of its making? The oldest scroll: who first pressed these marks, in what light, with what intention—and does what a thing was made to say remain inside it after the maker is gone? I sprang upright, black fur at full extension, golden eyes gone very wide.

Kasha spun from the table.

"By the Ancestors' beard," she said, in the specific quiet of a woman whose composure is working very hard, "what was that." She stared. Her eyes found my frantic, open gaze and held there. Something behind her expression moved—slowly, the way a heavy stone moves in deep water. The first smile I had seen on her face in five weeks touched the corner of her mouth. "Such—Sak'ranut," she murmured, using the old Peleset word for the quality of relentless, consuming inquiry—the word you give a thing that has begun opening every cabinet simultaneously and cannot be stopped. She did not yet understand what she had named. A deity of Curiosity, even a small one, even one wearing a cat, does not get named by accident. Or perhaps all naming is accident. I was still working through this particular question when she pointed at the mat.

"You are my beast," she said. Her finger was steady. "Not my master. You do not look at me that way. Sit."

I sat. I continued looking at her in precisely the manner she had objected to, because the quality of the look was not a decision I was making but the condition I had entered, and there is no path back to the condition you were in before a fragment of the divine Name passes through you. You may as reasonably ask the river to stop being water.

She looked away first—at the dropped scroll, at the bowl, at her own hands. In the moment before she turned, the expression on her face was not the expression of a woman who has won an argument. It was the expression of a woman who has decided this particular dispute does not merit the available energy, which is a different thing entirely, and both of us knew it, and I filed the difference carefully in the new and capacious interior that had opened where the old, wordless self had been.

"What was in the third syllable," I said. "Because whatever was in it is in me now," I continued, because a question that has started has no mechanism for stopping, "and I find I have considerable views about what I am made of, which seems reasonable—most things want to know what they are made of, even the ones that end up not preferring the answer. Also: the current. I can feel it through the ground—thin as thread where it should be rope, but present, moving west-southwest toward the coast. Your patron is not absent from this world. He is absent from you specifically, which is a different category of problem, and the kind that has a direction to look in." I paused. "The direction is Ekron. He has been there for five weeks. He will not come to you here. If he is findable at all tonight, he is findable in the gray."

She looked at me for a long moment with the expression of a woman who has managed a great many unexpected developments with composure and has just received one she did not plan for. Then she sat down on the mat and pressed both palms against the cold soil—not the petition posture, not the invocation register, just contact.

"I have been knowing it for five weeks," she said into the ground. This was the personal register—the voice below ceremony, below the chest-voice of invocation, below the formal demand of petition; the voice a woman uses when all the forms are exhausted and what remains is the thing itself. "I know the current has thinned. I know the bowl shows me nothing. I know what this means and I have been telling myself it does not mean what it means." Her palms pressed harder, the mat leaving its impression in both. "I will not be abandoned. I have given thirty years of faithful work into this soil, and I have kept every agreement, and the record of this faithfulness exists. Someone below this floor has been keeping it."

The ground received this into the record it has been keeping since before records had a name, and held it without reply—in the manner of a thing that has been given everything and returned very little and has no defense for this except that it is very old. And in that silence something arrived: not the full current, not the answer—but the barest ground-warmth, a vibration beneath the silence that meant the address had been registered. Which was not nothing. In five weeks of reaching, it was the first thing that had not been nothing.

There was another way—deeper, older, reserved for the moment every other channel has gone dry: the gray wilderness, that shifting expanse before the first word of the first morning, where reality held itself in patient suspension. Kasha's jaw tightened at the thought. She was not afraid of the gray's creatures—she had faced the shapes that assembled at the seam between sleeping and waking and found them manageable. Her fear was subtler and more honest: it was the fear of a woman who has spent thirty years knowing with absolute certainty where the floor is, now asked to exist in a place where the floor is a decision you make rather than a fact you stand on. She was a woman of the dense, weighty world—the world of pressure and packed earth and the patient accumulation of what presses down. In the gray wilderness, down was something you constructed. Her will had been built for holding, not for making. She had gone there twice in thirty years. Both times she had come back changed in ways not entirely comfortable, and the ground had held her when she returned—which was the only thing that had made the returning bearable.

But what was behind her—five weeks of silence, the thinned current, the oil that would not bloom—was worse than what was in front of her. That is, I have observed, the specific texture of the bravest choices: not the absence of fear, but the presence of a worse alternative.

She snuffed the oil lamp. The tent went dark save for the thin cold light pressing through the canvas weave—and this darkness Kasha moved through with the ease of a woman at home in the low and lightless places. The dark was not threat; the dark was the comfortable density of enclosed space, the warm compression of the world at its most itself. She laid herself on the worn mat with the deliberate straightness of a woman who is afraid and has decided the fear does not alter what needs doing: a rigid line against the ground, every joint set with intention, the body deployed like a tool selected for difficult work. She pressed her palms down one final time—low, direct, no ceremony left, only contact. "You will hold me," she said to the soil. Her voice at this lowest register carried the quality of a voice that expects no response and has moved past requiring one. "When I return. You have always held me. I am coming back." She held the contact until the cold of the ground came fully into her palms. Then she began.

The wordless chant built in the center of her chest—the deep vibration that extends outward until it finds the body's boundary and reverses, pressing inward toward the central knot where the self is bound to the flesh, working it loose the way a well-packed bundle comes apart when the right knot is found: each cord releasing in patient sequence, the body's grip loosening layer by layer without force. From outside, this looks like a woman going still. What it is: a woman going somewhere that is not here. The tent poles lengthened in the low dark. The scent of dried herbs thinned to the sharp metallic edge of the threshold. The warm, dense shadows grew specific and elongated, twisting into the shapes of things that live at the world's edges and wait for the boundary between what is and what is becoming to grow thin enough.

She crossed.

What followed requires the precision of a creature who was sitting approximately four inches from her sternum, so attend carefully.

It did not arrive. That is the first thing to understand about the Night-Hag, the Crushing Shadow, the old weight-claim: it makes no entrance, sends no herald. One moment the body is a tool in full service to a will; the next moment there is a will and there is a body and they are no longer in the same arrangement. It does not approach. It is simply the knowledge working backwards through the awareness—the cold certainty finding itself already present, the possession having preceded the accounting of it. The weight on the sternum had been building since before the chant began, since before the lamp went out, building the way stalactite builds: one cold deposit compounding on another, until the structure is suddenly, completely, irrevocably there.

First: the sternum. Not the weight of stone—heavier. The specific density of absolute certainty pressing down with no interest in argument, because it is not making an argument. It is only present. Then: the limbs, which did not grow heavy but held—and these are not the same thing. A limb that rests has chosen to stop. A limb that is held was stopped mid-motion, caught between the beat and the silence like a pendulum arrested at the summit of its arc by a hand with no intention of releasing it—suspended in the interval where will and flesh were being carefully separated with the calm precision of a craftsman who has performed this work before and finds it unremarkable. Her throat sealed. Her eyes fixed on the dark weave of the tent above her and would not move. Her chest continued: the minimum. Breath as technical necessity rather than living act. She was awake in her living awareness and a sealed jar in her flesh, unable to move a finger, a prisoner in a house of clay that had forgotten how to open its own doors. The staccato, pressing percussion against her ribs was the only proof of the frantic furnace within—a rhythmic drumming no one outside the tent could hear.

The specific cruelty: the iron. The iron grinding against the whetstone outside—the patient, dense percussion of the valley's long declaration, the sound that had always come through the canvas like a familiar voice in a known room, the sound that meant the floor was still below everything, that the world was still made of dense and manageable material, that the ground held as it always had. She could hear it. She could not reach it.

I stepped onto the center of her chest.

I want to be precise about what this was and was not. My small mass added to the weight already pressing. This was intentional. I pressed down and I purred—the vibration moving through my ribs, through my paws, through her sealed chest, through the mat, into the cold ground below, which received it without ceremony, without reply, into the record it has been keeping since before records had a name. The sound meant: I am here. The ground is here. The floor is still below you, even when you cannot reach it. The cord has not yet run out.

In the gray wilderness—that blank and waiting expanse before the first word of the first morning, where the deep had not yet known what form it would be asked to take—Kasha stood in the only posture available to her: feet pressed to the shifting floor, the memory of solid ground held in the long bones of her legs, the one solid thing available in all the formlessness. The gray stretched in every direction without malice or mercy, simply present with the patience of the thing that has always been here and will remain after everything assembled from its material has returned to it.

Something moved in the gray's vast interior. Not approaching—the princes of that place do not approach. They become more present, the way a shape materializes from surrounding dark when the eye adjusts: not arriving but assembling, taking on particularity and weight with the calm craft of a thing that has performed this assembly many times and has long since achieved the ease of mastery. It gathered from the gray's own density with the deliberate patience of a craftsman selecting from a long-maintained archive—the face most calibrated to the wanting that had been pressed into the ground for thirty years, to the faithfulness deposited into the valley's floor across three decades of downward-pressing, to the thing most wanted and most purely kept faith with. The craftsmanship was excellent. The first of the six princes assembled with the confidence of a thing that knows exactly which face it is reaching for and has used it before, for people very like this one.

And I sat at Kasha's side with my golden eyes open in the dark, pressing my small warm weight against her still arm, purring steady into the cold ground below—because here is what I had already understood in the less than two hours since I received the gift of understanding: that hope at its most threadbare is not a feeling but a decision to remain present while the outcome is still open. It requires no certainty. The purring in the dark is not an argument or a promise but a simple continued presence—a warm and insistent weight that means: I have not left. The floor is still below you. The cord has not run out.

Outside, iron ground against stone—patient, particular, indifferent. Inside the tent, the ground held everything placed upon it: the mat, the cooling scroll, the copper bowl with its flat oil, the cedar box, the lamp that had been extinguished. As the ground has always held. As it will hold when the iron and the tent and the valley's long argument with armies have all been pressed into it and the patient record-keeping has resumed as though none of it had happened—as though every petition and every palm pressed faithfully downward into the cold packed earth had always been exactly where it was going: settling, layer on patient layer, into the only archive that has never needed burning.

The choice—as all the true ones are, in every age, in every tent, in every gray wilderness where a human soul has stood alone and felt the weight of the offered face beginning its turn—was entirely hers.
