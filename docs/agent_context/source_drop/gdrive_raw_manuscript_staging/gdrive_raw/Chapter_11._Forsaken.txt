Chapter 11: Forsaken

Since you've journeyed with me this far, through dust and starlight, through the echoing halls of Hermon and the becoming of a boy, you've surely earned a secret. A very old secret, one I've kept tucked away beneath the grand tapestry of this tale, a thread interwoven so subtly that only the keenest eye might follow its glint. For not all who observe stories are merely voices from thin air, nor are all companions quite what they seem. Some have been here all along, present as the breath drawn, curled at Kasha’s feet, a silent shadow in her tent, just as you may remember from those earlier chapters. Indeed, I've been with her since the days when the light of Hermon first kissed the peaks of her ambition, a silent witness to every tremor and triumph, to every desperate gamble she's made under the fickle gaze of the heavens. Yes, it was I, the very cat you glimpsed beside her — watching with eyes that see a little more than the average pair, eyes that have peered into the twilight chasms where forgotten gods sleep and the dawn where new truths are forged. My lineage is not of the common housecat, but of the ancient ones, those who walked the earth when mountains were young and the great rivers carved their first paths. My paws know the ancient rhythms of the world, not merely the soft tread on earth, but the subtle thrumming of the great cosmic loom, the ebb and flow of power across the land, older than any king or creed. I sense the ley lines that crisscross the globe, the slow, tectonic shifts of magical currents, the very pulse of creation and decay. I move with quiet grace, a soft brush against the dust, ears twitching to subtle shifts in the air, listening to the unseen currents that pull at the fabric of destiny. Sometimes I settle in the folds of her robes, feeling the tremor of her restless heart, a drumbeat of longing and unease, sensing what even she cannot yet name. Humans, with their grand designs and frantic striving, often forget the vast, indifferent expanse of time that truly governs all things. I am the silent keeper of her solitude, the one who doesn't judge, but simply observes. My purpose, too, is woven into this tapestry, a guardian of thresholds, a whisper of what might be. Presence is many things, dear reader. Sometimes it is simply waiting—silent, still, never truly passive. Like ancient stars waiting indifferently, it is a vigil, a quiet witnessing, a holding of space for what is to come, or what has irrevocably passed. Like the enduring mountains that watch epochs rise and fall, I have waited, seeing the dust of ages settle upon even the most formidable figures.

The air within the tent, when all was laid bare, hung thick with unspoken things: the scent of dried herbs, their usual sharp aroma now a cloying sweetness from simmering potions gone cold, a heavy, almost sickly perfume. It was the odor of stale magic, of spells left unfinished, of hopes curdled. This mingled with the taut, nervous energy of a woman whose world felt suddenly unmoored, its anchor dragged from the muddy bed of certainty. A subtle tremor, not of the earth, but of Kasha herself, vibrated through the very ropes holding the canvas taut. The familiar hum of her protective wards, once a comforting presence, was now a faint, high-pitched whine, barely audible to human ears, a dying breath. Outside, the Jezreel Valley wind tore at the worn canvas, a relentless, biting thing, a tempest of unseen forces, its harsh whisper a sharp counterpoint to the humid stillness Kasha usually cultivated within her sanctuary. This valley, a crucible of nations and the grave of empires, seemed to sigh with a thousand forgotten conflicts, its breath chilling even through thick canvas. The very soil beneath the tent felt cold, hardened by centuries of bloodshed and forgotten ambitions, a silent witness to the cycles of triumph and despair. I sat near the entrance, tail curling and flicking, a pendulum measuring the relentless passage of her anxiety. Tiny dust motes, like scattered dreams, danced in the shafts of fading sunlight that pierced the tent’s weave. A thin layer of dust, fine as powdered bone, coated the scattered scrolls across the low table, a silent testament to neglected work and dwindling purpose. The very air, though scoured clean by the ceaseless gusts, felt hollow, devoid of the familiar currents of power she once commanded, a space where vital energies had once coalesced, now only a vacuum. My whiskers twitched, sensing the emptiness where once flowed the subtle energies that nourished her craft, a void that was both physical and spiritual. Her hands, usually steady as she prepared tinctures of potent herbs or drew intricate designs, now trembled—a faint, almost imperceptible shake that nonetheless rippled through the tent’s bones, a tremor of an inner earthquake. Her fingers, once nimble and precise, felt clumsy, almost alien. She had begun to notice the subtle lines etched around her eyes, deeper now, and the faint silver threads that occasionally glinted in her once uniformly dark hair – undeniable marks of the very passage of time her magic had so long defied. A persistent ache settled in her joints, a tell-tale sign of her failing wards against age. I rubbed against her ankle, soft as a whispered comfort, a gesture she might once have acknowledged with a gentle stroke, but now she barely noticed, her gaze fixed on something distant, something lost, a ghost of her former power. Kasha sank heavily onto the worn rug before her scrying bowl, her movements jerky, desperate, like a marionette whose strings had frayed. Her eyes, once sharp and calculating, fixed on the murky water—one of her few remaining conduits to the unseen, a fragile window to the ethereal—the last device still whispering faint echoes of her patron’s presence. This vessel, crafted from ancient bronze and etched with symbols that once pulsed with latent power, now seemed merely a bowl of stagnant water. It was an empty vessel, reflecting an empty hope.

I leapt lightly onto the table edge, careful not to disturb the fragile scrolls or the dried remnants of powerful incantations, and dipped a paw into the cool spring water she had just poured. The ripple vanished as quickly as it came, swallowed by the stubborn stillness, as if the water itself refused to acknowledge even the slightest disturbance. It was as flat and unyielding as a stone. The spirit did not hover over these waters.

She began to chant, a low, throaty hum meant to coax the unseen currents, to draw forth the presence she craved. Her voice, usually a deep resonant timbre that could command elements, was now reedy, strained, a desperate plea rather than an invocation. The words, once flowing effortlessly, now caught in her throat, tasting of ash and failure. Each syllable was forced, a physical exertion, as if she were speaking through a throat parched by desert winds. But the water remained stubbornly flat, reflecting only the dim flicker of the oil lamp and her own anxious face, a mirror to her growing despair. The air itself seemed deadened, refusing to bend or bow to her will, a thick, unresponsive veil drawn between her and the source of her strength. It was as if a great, silent wall had risen, deaf to her pleas, mirroring the silence that had fallen upon her heart. I twitched my whiskers and curled tighter beside her, the weight of her frustration settling into the tent like a tangible shadow, a miasma of failure. Outside, the wind rattled the tent poles, a restless, mournful sound, echoing her profound unease, a lament for what had been lost. It felt like the valley itself was mocking her, its ancient breath carrying the scent of triumph—not hers. A flush crept up Kasha’s neck, a familiar fire of rising pique tightening her jaw, a physical manifestation of her indignation. But beneath the anger, a cold dread gnawed in her gut—a sensation she seldom allowed herself, a bitter taste of helplessness. The profound silence over the usual channels of power was like a locked door with no key, a vast, unyielding chasm between her and the divine. The whispers that once flowed like a river were now a parched desert, and the warmth of her patron's attention, once a comforting blanket, had become the chill of utter abandonment. Her connection to Beelzebub was not merely a source of power; it was the very bedrock of her identity, her purpose. Without it, she was a discarded tool, a withered branch, stripped of the grandeur that had defined her for decades. The fear was not just of losing magic, but of losing herself, her very essence, like a vessel emptied of its sacred oil.

Kasha saw her people celebrating their conquest over the Philistines in the scrying bowl, seeing the recent past but no hint of present or future, and certainly no sign of her patron. Then came a different sound — faint but carried through the canvas by the biting wind. Not a whisper, but a distant, visceral roar. Not the clash of men or bronze on iron, though that too had lately filled the air, but something older, primal — the deep thrum of a world reshaping itself, groaning under the weight of cosmic shifts. It was the heavy echo of battles long past, victories carved into dust and memory, a mournful dirge for the fallen and a triumphal pean for the victors. This sound resonated with the very earth of the valley, a geological sigh of triumph and defeat. The land itself seemed to remember the days when chariots of iron had swept across these plains, crushing the meager forces aligned against them, leaving behind a legacy of ash and lamentation. Kasha knew her patron had been deeply entwined in this ancient struggle, his task in the valley fulfilled. He was not a god of men, but of the currents beneath men, a force that swayed the tides of battle and the hearts of nations, harvesting the energies of conflict. Kasha knocked thrice, asked twice, and waited for her patron, but he did not come. The whispers she had received hinted at a new force rising among her people, a king whose shadow stretched far and wide, meant to challenge the Philistine dominion. A fragile, fledgling kingdom, born of desperation and the remnants of a fallen house, a testament to the enduring, if sometimes faltering, spirit of Israel. But here, in this tent, the truth was clear — her patron was needed elsewhere, in the heart of Philistia, where its strength was greatest, where the raw currents of human ambition and divine will were most volatile. Kasha believed her calls went unanswered because Beelzebub was having a very long Sabbath, sitting idly in the city of Ekron. That was why her invocations were hollow. She couldn't fault him. He simply served a purpose larger than her immediate needs, a terrible cosmic agenda that paid no heed to the small desires of a solitary sorceress. He was a force of nature, not a comforting father.

Her gaze returned to the scrying bowl, a desperate resolve hardening her features, chiseling away the anxiety, leaving behind only grim determination. If direct calls were unanswered, if the very air refused to carry her pleas, perhaps the water would show the way, a glimpse through the veil. Her fingers, still slightly trembling but with renewed purpose, traced the ancient symbols etched around the bowl’s rim. She whispered once more, coaxing, willing the surface to cloud or ripple, to reveal a path, a sign, a whisper of guidance. But the water remained as stubbornly clear as ever—a mirror to the empty tent ceiling and her own strained reflection, a perfect, mocking stillness. It offered no counsel, no path, only the unyielding face of her own self. The water, like the sky, had become a brass ceiling above her, impenetrable and silent. I shifted closer, brushing against her wrist, a soft reminder of presence in a silent room, a quiet, reassuring weight. Even my purr, low and steady, a vibration against the very earth, couldn't stir the stillness, couldn't penetrate the cold, hollow space that had opened around her. My ancient rhythms seemed powerless against this desolation, a testament to the sheer magnitude of her predicament.

There was another way, a deeper one, reserved for moments when all else failed: the Dreamscape. A place of raw, untamed power where thought gave substance to shadow and reality’s edges blurred. Yet a place inviting danger, where even the strongest mind could lose its way — swallowed by illusions or ancient entities lurking beneath shifting currents, entities that fed on the unwary, that saw human consciousness as mere fodder. It was a perilous journey, a crossing of the threshold into the very chaos that preceded creation. A place where the mind was its own master, and its own greatest betrayer. I myself had once fought a great serpent in that churning void, a battle I recall with a shiver, but that is a tale for another time.

Kasha recoiled at the thought, a shiver running down her spine. Unlike others, she did not fear the Dreamscape’s horrors as such. She had faced monsters born of collective nightmares, walked through fields of screaming specters, and witnessed the primordial forms of fear itself. Her fear was more subtle — the treacherous shifts in perspective, the unstable seas where even the surest step might betray, where the very ground beneath one's feet could dissolve into nothingness. In that place, perception is the key, the only compass. One false glance could turn friend to foe, shadow to monster, truth to an enticing lie. It was the fear of losing herself not to an external monster, but to an internal deception, a mirage of what she believed she wanted. Still, with no other choice, and the silent veil drawn over her scrying, her need to find him gnawed too fiercely to be ignored. It was a hunger in her gut, a thirst in her parched soul, a desperate ache that outweighed all caution. This was the precipice of her existence.

Her magic, once fluid and fierce, a torrent of power that flowed through her veins like lifeblood, dried up like the desert wind outside — the warmth of youth slipping from her skin, taking with it the vibrant glow that had defied the years. Her skin, once smooth, now showed the faint map of time, the subtle lines deepening around her eyes. The power that kept her face unlined, her stride confident, was fading, revealing the subtle etchings of time, the faint marks of worry she had long kept at bay. The tent, once a refuge, now felt cold, brittle, worn, its very fabric frayed by the onslaught of reality.

With a heavy sigh, Kasha snuffed out the oil lamp, plunging the tent into near darkness save for faint starlight filtering through canvas, thin slivers of distant light that seemed to mock her dwindling hope. She laid herself on the worn mat, her body stiff with apprehension, a rigid line against the ground, and began a slow, deepening breath—a chant without words, steady and measured, designed to unravel the self. It loosened the bonds of flesh and bone, lifting her spirit bit by bit from the world of touch and sight, a gradual separation of spirit from form, a journey of the soul into the unmanifest. Sounds dimmed to silence, then dissolved altogether, and the scent of herbs faded into a sharp, metallic tang—the scent of the Dreamscape itself, cold and clean and utterly alien. It was the scent of raw potential, of unformed ideas, of nothingness before creation. A cacophony of disjointed whispers assaulted her, then receded into a vast, echoing void, like the silence of a tomb. The transition was always the same: a dizzy tumble through shifting images and sounds, fleeting and kaleidoscopic, a million impressions born and dying in an instant, until awareness settled here, in a vast gray realm where shadows held weight and colors drained away, leaving only the starkness of existence. The Dreamscape stretched before her, a desolate expanse, blank as the earliest dawn when first words of truth were spoken, when the very fabric of existence was still being spun. Reality was a canvas of shifting grays — empty, waiting for will to paint meaning and form, for the whisper of intention to bring forth substance. It was a place where belief was creation, and doubt, dissolution. She called out, voice a clear echo in the vast emptiness, reverberating into the infinite, “Beelzebub! O Beelzebub! Wherefore art [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE]?”. Her words drifted, swallowed by the unbroken silence, devoured by the sheer scale of the void, receiving no answer, no sign. It was as if she shouted into a starless night, and heard only the echo of her own despair. A chilling wind, not of the Jezreel but of the void, seemed to buffet her, carrying the scent of cosmic indifference. She pressed forward, a ghost in a ghostly land, urgency heavy on her shoulders, a crushing weight of desperation. She sought the familiar buzz that heralded her patron’s arrival, the low frequency that always vibrated in the deepest recesses of her being when he was near, but the Dreamscape offered no comfort.

Then the world itself conspired to bind her, the light dimmed further, her limbs grew heavy, impossibly weighted, and breath turned shallow, ragged, a desperate gulping for air. She awoke in her body but could not move it, caught in a prison of stillness where the tongue faltered, unable to form words, and eyes flickered in silent terror, desperate to make sense of the encroaching darkness. This was the deepest horror, the mind awake and aware, but locked within a corpse. A cold dread, like iron bands, tightened around her chest, forcing the air from her lungs in shallow gasps. The heart hammered loud in the cage of ribs, a frantic bird seeking escape; the chest rose and fell in desperate rhythm, a futile attempt to expand against an unseen pressure, yet no limb would heed the command, no muscle would twitch. A whisper of shadows crept at the edges of her vision, amorphous forms that suggested ancient horrors, the cold pressing down, a crushing weight, the silence louder than any cry, a deafening roar of nothingness. Panic curled like a serpent around her mind, tightening its coils, squeezing out all rational thought — yet she was locked in place, the darkness closing in with patient inevitability, a slow, inexorable descent. Her breath caught, caught again, a choking gasp, the pounding still relentless, a terrifying drumbeat of impending doom. Time blurred, stretched — seconds felt like hours in this stilled purgatory, this frozen moment between worlds, where the mind screamed but the body remained a tomb. I felt a low growl rumble in my throat, a primal instinct to defend, though my physical form could do little against such an ethereal assault.

It was in this paralysis that the vision came—six figures, made well like princes, draped in false beauty, their faces veiled by sheep’s clothing that hid their true forms. They appeared to Kasha in the fragile half-place, where waking meets sleep, where body and spirit teeter on the precipice of distinct realms. That terror stayed with her still, the memory of helplessness in the deep dark, of being caught between worlds, unable to cry out, unable to fight. The memory of that stillness, that vulnerability, was a deep, unhealed wound.

The first figure stepped forward, robed in blinding white, a vision of purity that radiated cold, sterile emptiness. Its face was serene, hollow where desire once lived, its eyes like twin pools of still water, reflecting nothing. A thin voice, like the whisper of dry bones in the wind, echoed not in her ears but in the core of her spirit, “All desire is sin. All pleasure a snare. True freedom lies in emptiness. Cast off the burdens of the flesh, embrace the void, and know peace”. Kasha felt a sudden lift, a release from earthly want — a lightness almost intoxicating, a deceptive peace that promised liberation from all struggle. Her own past, filled with ambition and longing, seemed petty, a childish folly. She felt a profound weariness for the endless striving, a temptation to simply let go of all earthly burdens, to dissolve into the ether. But the air around this presence shimmered, distorting, a trap for those who sought to erase hunger in cold perfection, a beautiful cage for the spirit. It was the lure of oblivion, of self-annihilation disguised as transcendence, a promise of peace that would leave her less than nothing. Before she could falter, before the insidious emptiness could claim her, the next emerged — a figure wreathed in the dark splendor of absolute power, its eyes burning with possessive fire, its touch promising dominion over realms, wealth of worlds, absolute control. Its form shifted, momentarily appearing as a crowned king on a throne, then a vast, conquering army, its shadows stretching across continents. “All belongs to me,” it hissed, a silken caress curling around her soul, a voice that spoke of ancient kings and the iron will of conquerors. Her heart thundered with ambition, the thrilling rush of control, the intoxicating promise of power without limit, but also a choking bind tightening fast, a chain disguised as an embrace. A vision of herself, crowned and unassailable, flickered before her eyes, then dissolved into the image of countless souls bowed in endless servitude, a chilling price for such dominion. It was the lure of tyranny, the seductive call of boundless might that demanded absolute submission, to become its puppet on a vast, bloody stage. One by one, the others drifted into view — each a test, a voice coaxing her to surrender, bend, give in. One, cloaked in ragged shadows that writhed with unseen torment, promised vengeance, its eyes burning with the fires of righteous fury, offering to turn her bitter defeats into crushing victories over her enemies, to lay low those who had ever scorned her, their faces contorted in agony at her command. Kasha imagined the sweet release of old grievances satisfied, the humiliation of rivals turned to dust. Another, draped in silken veils woven from spun dreams, whispered of eternal pleasure, sensations that would drown out all sorrow, all pain, offering endless indulgence, a feast for every sense that would erase all memory of hardship. She felt the phantom brush of silk on her skin, the taste of forbidden fruit on her tongue, the whisper of endless comfort, a seductive balm for her weary soul. Another, a master of deception, its face a shifting kaleidoscope of sincerity and betrayal, whispered lies cloaked as truth, weaving intricate tapestries of false righteousness, offering to redefine reality itself to suit her desires, assuring her that her greatest sins were merely misunderstood virtues. It offered a perfect narrative for her life, where every failure was a lesson, every compromise a calculated step towards greatness, absolving her of all guilt. Another still offered knowledge, secrets of the cosmos, the deepest mysteries of creation, but with the subtle implication that such knowledge would render her utterly alone, adrift in a sea of terrifying truths, too enlightened to ever connect with mere mortals. Each was a mirror of a part of herself, a hidden longing, a dormant fear, magnified and presented as a path to salvation, each a siren's song designed to lure her from her purpose.

Six figures, made well like princes, in all, each a carefully crafted illusion, a personalized snare designed to exploit the deepest crevices of the human soul. And though Kasha sensed the seventh — the Lord of the Flies — his presence faded like smoke whenever she struggled to fully wake, whenever she reached for him, always just beyond reach, a phantom limb of her power, a promise unfulfilled. His absence was a presence in itself, a chilling reminder of her isolation. He was a god on a higher war, one too busy for her small crisis, a distant thunder in the cosmic storm, leaving her adrift in the quiet tempest of her own soul. She fought to hold her center, the fragile thread of will that anchored her to reality, to herself. The vision’s pull was sharp, sweet, deadly, each temptation a barbed hook tugging at her very essence. The Dreamscape whispered that all power came at a price: submission to their rule, a loss of self to claim greater strength, a complete erasure of her individuality in exchange for a false form of omnipotence.

Then, a flicker of something else — a presence, soft and sure, distinct from the malevolent pull of these figures or the mocking absence of her patron. It was something older, quieter, a thread of light in the encompassing dark, a beacon in the storm. The paralysis slowly loosened, fingers twitching, toes curling. Movement returned like a flood — sudden, overwhelming, a rush of blood and sensation. She gasped and blinked into the dim glow of dawn creeping beneath the tent flap, a grey, washed-out light that promised another day, even if it brought no answers. Kasha lay trembling, her body and I was there still, curled beside her, eyes half-lidded, watching, waiting. I knew more now, details she had missed, nuances of the dreamscape she couldn't grasp. And so did she, in her own way. The raw, terrifying truth of her own vulnerability, the sheer, crushing weight of her patron's absence, and the unsettling clarity of her own hunger reflected back at her. It was a stark, brutal awakening, a stripping away of all illusions. She felt a profound, chilling emptiness, yet paradoxically, a spark of defiant self-reliance ignited within that void. The gods might be indifferent, but she still remained.

But oh, what a terrible thing it is, dear reader, to look for a god and find only reflections of your own hunger, to call out into the void and hear only the echo of your own desperate, beating heart. It is the beginning of a different kind of journey, one where the answers lie not in the heavens, but in the desolate, untamed landscape of the self. And I, the silent watcher, remained to see where that journey would lead. For even in abandonment, a new path can be forged, perhaps one of unexpected, solitary strength, a path where even a broken spirit can find its own unwavering light.
