CHAPTER 1: STARDUST TO STARDUST
Version O

A four-room house in Hebron had been built in the shape of a man divided against himself — four walls holding four different conclusions, the rooms arranged so that no two shared a threshold without requiring the occupant to choose which version of himself would walk through it. You might ask why a dwelling would come to resemble something so treacherous as a human heart refused — such a question, posed while reading this, means you and I will get along. I tell you this as one who stood at that threshold on the night this story begins, and who has not left since. What was happening in that house had been building for seven years, compounding the way debt compounds: invisibly at first, then all at once, then in a manner that made invisibility seem generous.

Father Aviel had been a scholar — sharp as fresh-knapped flint, sought out by those whose questions twisted beyond their own unraveling. But when Mother Zuna passed — nameless there forevermore — something ceased within him: not the clean cessation that would have been kinder and quicker, but the stillness of a cart whose axle has been shattered so completely that no carpenter in the province can name the repair. The gods he had gathered from the western plains — brittle bronze forms of forgotten flame, of fading fortune, of fertility that promised without delivering — could not fill what had gone hollow in him. He swept them away and kept only one: the lord of limitless dust and earthly plenty, who whispered that hoarded objects might hold back time himself, just to spite the things made. That one stayed. Seven years it had been staying.

On a night when the moon rode full and high in its third day, the son moved through the monument his father's ruin had made. They had called him Dan once, when names still mattered in this dwelling that hardly stood. Sixteen years old: kind eyes that held more sorrow than a boy should carry, and unruly black hair that wrestled with simple commandments. The torch trembled in his hand — the frail flame guttered not from any wind, but from the staccato percussion against his ribs, casting suspicious shadows across the narrow corridor. Each barefoot step through the refuse was a wager against sound: one shard of pottery, one brittle bone snapping beneath his weight, and the house would know. He was moving toward his father's room. Tonight he would enter the dreamscape to show what could not be undone.

Now — I should note, before we go any further, that the corridor Dan moved through was not merely disordered. It was wrong in the specific way that only patience and abandonment, working together across years, can make a place wrong. Pay attention to this. It matters considerably more than it appears to.

The corridor choked with debris. Broken pottery lay scattered like the shards of failed offerings — Dan had counted eighteen visible pieces one morning before stopping, unable to push past that number. Fragments of parchment and brittle wax tablets, once holding records of lineage and covenant, lay stripped clean by ravenous mold. Ruined brass astrolabes gathered in shadowed corners — instruments that once measured the heavens, now unable to find even north, their rings fused into grotesque shapes, instruments of navigation that had stopped navigating. Desiccated fruits clung to their seeds the way the dead cling to the living: with a grip the living cannot name and cannot loosen. Water skins lay cracked and hollow, reeking of stale leather and the sharp mineral tang of total dryness.

The smell arrived in layers, as catastrophe arrives in layers — the outrider first, and you think: this is already bad enough; then the second wave, and you revise considerably. Ammonia sharp enough to sear the passages and coax tears from unwilling eyes. Then the sweetness beneath it — decay in its middle stage, overripe and cloying, the scent of a thing that has crossed from almost-rotted to fully-committed and found the crossing comfortable. Beneath that: herbs that had surrendered whatever virtue they once held, gone from medicine to powder to the ghost of both. And beneath all of it, below even the reek — the mineral cold of disturbed earth, of things buried being dragged back to light where they had no business.

Where the corridor narrowed toward the central room, the debris had fused into something almost deliberate: a wall of accumulated abandonment leaning inward at a precise angle, as though the house itself were attempting to close the path the way a body closes around a wound it has given up healing. Maggots worked the rotted cloth in the corners — pale as mushroom flesh, fat as thumbs, moving with the unhurried industry of creatures who have found their purpose. The only living things, here, that still had any. Flies rose and fell in living clouds, their droning a dirge composed in a register no priest had ever notated, for gods no priest had ever named. There was a smell beneath the smell — a cold mineral edge with something underneath it that registered not in the nose but in the back of the throat: present and wrong, a persistent wrongness that had soaked itself into the limestone walls, into the pores of the stone, into the dark between the stones. The house breathed it. The house had been breathing it for some time.

The torch Dan carried was burning, and it is worth explaining how.

He had lit it at the hearthstone near the corridor's far end — crouching over the cold fire-pit with a chip of flint and a nodule of pyrite, striking a spark into the tinder pile he had prepared the night before, because Dan prepared things the night before. This was his nature. The torch caught; he straightened; he passed through the corridor with the flame held high and one hand trailing the wall, and he did not run, because running was sound, and sound was how the house woke.

I have been watching this family for seven years. I want to be transparent about that. I was present when the accumulation began — I watched the first thing brought in, a bronze astrolabe cracked along its outer ring, purchased from a passing merchant for what seemed a reasonable price — and I understood at once what it was. Not the astrolabe. The price. A man always pays a reasonable price for the first thing he does not need. After that the prices grow more complicated.

Some are born with the specific capacity to step through that thin membrane between waking and the vast interior where every sleeping mind wanders — that shifting expanse where the dreamer moves without anchor or guide unless someone has been there before and knows the paths. Dan's mother had this capacity. She had taught him when he was very small, in a room that smelled like cedar and olive oil. The TECHNIQUE is simple, which is not the same as easy: forget yesterday — dissolve it the way salt dissolves in water, leaving only the faint mineral trace that memory always leaves. Forget tomorrow — release both obligation and hope, which live in the same corridor of the body. Forget now — release the body itself, become as still as deep water so that what is true may be reflected in you. He had practiced this for five years in secret, in this house, in this room. Tonight the practice would be tested against the thing it was prepared for.

He reached his father's door. Pressed his back to the wall beside it and held the torch low.

Four counts in. One held. Six out.

He listened — not for silence but through it. From behind the door came the particular rhythm of a man trying in sleep to outrun what had learned his pace: short catch-inhale, long ragged exhale, the breathing of someone fighting even here, even now. Not deep enough. Dan waited. He said something at the door — three syllables, barely breath, the way a boy speaks toward a thing he cannot afford to wake. He said it the way his mother had told him to speak before entering another dreamer's space: asking permission, not expecting an answer, but asking regardless. The wood did not respond, which was its nature; but something in him settled at having asked.

He extinguished the torch.

The darkness arrived completely, the way it always does when the last light goes — not gradually, but all at once, the dark having been patient the whole time. Dan held the dead torch and let his eyes adjust and listened. The breathing behind the door lengthened. Smoothed. The catch-inhale unwound into the long even pull of a man who has, finally, stopped running.

He sat against the wall. He closed his eyes — which is slightly redundant in complete dark, but nonetheless necessary for the TECHNIQUE, as anyone who has attempted it will confirm. He released yesterday first. It dissolved slowly, which suggested he had been holding it harder than he knew. Tomorrow went more easily. Now was the last and hardest, as it always is: releasing the body's cold insistence on its own existence, the wall behind his back, the limestone biting the backs of his legs, the dead torch in his lap. He let them go one by one, the way you lower a weight rather than dropping it, because dropping it wakes the house.

The darkness behind his closed eyes shifted and thinned.

The world as it is, is a thick thing: dense with the accumulated pressure of everything that has happened in it and everything that has refused to resolve. The place Dan crossed into was where that pressure goes before it takes form — the vast interior where every dreaming mind wanders, each in its own dim corridor, none aware the others are there. Most dreamers move through it like fish move through deep water: by instinct, without awareness, following whatever the day left in them. The rare few — and they are rare, I have met perhaps a dozen in a very long time of watching — can move through it with their eyes open.

Dan moved through it with his eyes open.

The formless deep churned beneath him, void waiting to be told what to become, the way it had waited before the first word of the first morning was spoken. Here the architecture pressed itself into its own surface the way a seal is pressed into wet clay — the same mark repeating in every layer, to a depth that had no bottom, each impression carrying the whole design. He turned toward his father's dream and found the barrier: a wall pulsing with sick light, lurid as a bruise that had learned to hate. He held still against it. He pressed his intention against the surface without forcing — not a command, but a statement of presence. He was here. He understood what was behind this. He was not here to judge.

The wall broke.

Sound exploded outward — screaming in an enormous distance, stone crashing, the frequency of a weeping that has stopped caring who hears. Images pressed through like hands through torn cloth: faces contorted past recognition, his mother's shrouded body carried toward the dark earth, the particular blue of her veil against the pale limestone chips of the grave. Every step forward cost what muscle does not spend — a fixed, cold drain from the thing that had aimed him here — and the barrier squeezed from all directions until his sight went white, then black, then white again.

He fell through.

The dreamscape on the other side held the particular exhausted colorlessness of a place where hope has not been present in a long time: a bleached sky above a bleached plain, the soil like ash, the light like the memory of light rather than the thing itself. Father Aviel stood at the center of it — shoulders hunched, arms wrapped around himself as if to hold his own shape in, a man who had been waiting so long he had forgotten what he was waiting for. Possibly for this. Possibly for anything.

Dan raised his palm toward the empty sky. Intent moved through him — not a word, not a command, simply the thing he needed this place to do, aimed upward the way you aim a tool at the work. The sky received it.

Dust swirled upward, darkening, gaining weight. Stones fell from the lightless above — and then, in the peculiar logic that belongs only to these places, transformed. Gold first, heavy and pure, carrying the specific weight of what men will abandon their families for. Aviel's eyes went wide. More fell: copper pieces, silver rings, gems in impossible colors — rubies like frozen blood, sapphires like captured sky, emeralds like want made mineral and cold. He began to gather with the frantic efficiency of a man who has been waiting his entire life for precisely this permission.

"Mine," he whispered. Half prayer, half curse.

He shoved treasure into his belt, under his tunic, into the space between his skin and his robe. The pile grew past his ankles, past his knees, past his waist. His hands would not stop. The hands of a man who knows this is wrong and cannot stop were always the hardest thing about this dream to watch — and Dan, watching, kept both his hands fixed at his sides and breathed through it, because the watching cost him something and he needed what he had left.

The treasure reached Aviel's chest. His neck. He was buried to the chin, hands locked mid-grasp, fingers fused around metal that had grown into his palms. His face cycled through red and then the deep suffocating purple of a thing being pressed closed from outside. His mouth opened: a silent accusation at the empty sky. Dan held his aim. Then he raised his palm one final time.

Cold current swept the plain. The wind gathered coins and gems into a spiral, scattered them back into dust, dissolving as they fell. The treasure vanished. Aviel knelt alone with his hands still shaped for holding. His eyes cleared. He looked up at the sky — and something appeared there: a star, small and distant as the truth things tell only when no one is watching, cold and fixed against the bleached above. He stared at it with the expression of a man discovering he has been forgiven for something he never confessed. Something cracked in him — not broke entirely, but admitted one thin sliver of light, the way a sealed room admits one thin sliver of dawn before anyone has decided to open it.

Dan released his aim. The star faded. The plain dissolved. Aviel drifted back into dreamless sleep — the first, I noted, that he had known in months.

Dan came back to his body.

His knees were on cold limestone in the dark corridor, his hands were shaking, the dead torch lay where he had set it, and the taste in his throat was the mineral cold of the dreamspace still draining out of him. From behind his father's door: a long, deep breath. Even. Settled. The breathing of a man sleeping without running for the first time in a long time.

He tried to stand. His legs declined. He pressed one palm to the wall and moved that way — hand on limestone, one foot, then another, the cold floor colder now than it had any right to be, as though it had been collecting heat from him the whole time he was elsewhere. The body moved like a cart overloaded on a bad road. Still, it moved. One step, then the next. The dark was complete; the corridor was obstacles he knew by position; he navigated them by memory and the trailing hand and the fact that falling was not acceptable tonight.

He reached the hearthstone.

He knelt. His hands found the flint and pyrite in the dark, found the tinder by touch, struck three times before the spark caught. When the torch finally steadied he said something at the fire — a short thing, said in the direction of the flame the way a person speaks toward something rather than directly at it. The complaint first, exact and honest. The plea, smaller than the complaint. Then the space where resolution either comes or doesn't. He said it the way his mother had told him the psalmists said hard things: without softening what was hard, without pretending resolution had arrived before it had.

The flame burned steadily.

He moved back through the corridor, one palm on the wall, the torch casting moving shadows over the debris that was always there. He did not look at it. He moved through it. He reached his room, pressed the door shut behind him, set the torch in its crack in the wall where it had always gone.

Here is where I must ask you to pay particular attention — and I am aware that asking for attention is the surest way to lose it, which is why I have waited until the moment when the thing I am asking you to attend to is directly in front of you. Dan's room.

The house was ruin: seven years of accumulated wreckage pressing at every wall and every threshold, the whole structure groaning with what had been brought in and never taken out. But Dan's room was different. The blanket folded with corners crisp as an argument that has already won. The floor swept clean, each morning, without being asked. His possessions arranged with care against the far wall: a clay cup for water, a wooden comb his mother had carved — nameless there forevermore — from the olive branch she had cut the year before she passed; a flint stylus for scratching words into stone; and one small window kept clear so that morning light could enter, and did. He had been maintaining this order since he was ten years old. One might say that Dan had put his own house in order before daring to criticize the world — though this was not quite the right framing, because it implied a confrontation that had not yet arrived. What he had done was simpler and harder: he had made the one claim available to him. This corner. This small insistence. Proof, hewn by six years of quiet discipline, that what surrounded him was a decision and not a fate.

He lowered himself onto the cot with the sigh of a man old in his bones and young in his obligations. He wedged the torch into its crack. He lay back.

The stone above him was just stone: unchanged, offering nothing, making no argument in either direction. That indifference was easier to receive than the dreamspace, where everything bent to the shape of intention whether that intention was ready or not. There, in his own dream, Dan moved with his eyes open — aware of the machinery, able to aim it — the way a man who has practiced something for five years can practice it, which is not the same as mastery but is further than most people think to go. Tonight the dreamspace had packed him with what it had. The tide came for what the beach was holding. He did not negotiate. He stopped.

He slept.

He stood on water.

Not floating — standing, bare feet on a surface both liquid and solid depending on how much attention you gave it, which is the kind of paradox that belongs only to this particular expanse where all sleepers go and most sleepers never notice they have gone. The sky above held that particular blue — the one that exists nowhere in the waking world, carrying no equivalent in any daylight color, a blue that is only possible when the constraints of what is real are temporarily loosened. He took a step. The surface held. Perfect circles spread from beneath his feet to the horizon, where the water became the sky, where the sky became the water.

He dispersed a boat in the distance with a thought. In his own dream, he was the architect.

The water changed beneath him. It thickened, took on solidity, became glass — clear as polished obsidian, harder than the limestone of the corridor above. He looked down through it. Depths with no floor. Shapes moving far below: enormous, patient, slow with the slowness of things that have been patient longer than memory reaches.

Then: the snap.

A crystalline fracture from directly beneath his feet — tiny as a breath, precise as a blade. He had time to look down. Fracture lines raced outward with the speed of absolute conviction — not spreading, detonating, a web of cracks expanding in every direction until the entire surface was compromised and the roar of it built from whisper to the sound of the world being reorganized at its foundations.

The glass exploded upward. The water came with it. The dream-blue sky bled instantly into bruised purple, then black, then the specific deep red that Dan had no waking name for and which registered not in the eye but in the long bones. A presence rose from below with such force the dream itself shuddered — not the dream's contents but the architecture behind the contents, the structure underlying the scenes, shaking the way a building shakes when something very large moves beneath its foundation.

Dan seized a plank. The sea crashed over him in cold black walls. The stench of the abyss clung to the white foam — bottomless, mineral, the smell of things that have never known light pressing up from below to claim the surface as though it had always been theirs. The sky churned to absolute black and the waves came in sequences of three, each larger than the last, each carrying the particular patience of something that does not need to hurry because it has already won.

Then, from the heart of the storm, it rose.

At the southern edge of the world — which in this dream, as in the old cosmology, was also the northern edge of the abyss — a colossal head emerged from the waves. Not the stone of quarries: the stone of ocean floors, of things that have been pressed by deep water for longer than memory reaches, ancient past reckoning, smooth past feature. Impassive as a wall that has absorbed ten thousand storms and has not been interested in any of them. Its eyes were open — wide as abysses, blacker than the deepest trench, the black of a thing that has never needed light and has spent no time missing it. They found Dan with the gaze of something that does not need to hurry.

Seaweed draped its chin. Barnacles in archipelagos across both cheeks. Somewhere impossibly far to the south — past the edge of what the dream could show, known only by the dream's own certainty — a massive tail broke the surface, stretching from one horizon to the other.

The voice came from inside Dan's own skull. Not from outside. From inside — a resonant hum that located itself behind the back teeth and shook outward from there, vibrating through the roots of each tooth in sequence, climbing through the jaw, through the temples, pressing against the backs of the eyes from within.

"I am Dagon. The power your father has chosen to serve. Lord of Abundance [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE], Master of Accumulation Eternal. Ask and receive — for my path demonstrates ease beyond mortal comprehension."

"I did not call you."

"Always have you called." Patient. Absolute. The patience of deep water that has outlasted every shore it has ever touched. "Ceaselessly does your flesh cry out for its appointed rest. But this night have you sinned most grievously — daring to undo my work established within your father's dwelling through seven years of patient cultivation. Do not mistake your petty resolve — transient as morning mist — for a living offering worthy of divine consideration."

"Abundance of things." Dan's voice came out steadier than he expected. "Why should I devote my life to what I cannot carry through death?"

Dagon leaned closer. Mountains of water displaced; the ocean pushed aside by the fraction of attention the thing below was extending toward him. The waves redoubled.

"I offer you a raft substantial enough that death's friction shall lose its sting. Follow me into the comfortable depths." The voice settled lower — not louder, simply more present, the way the ocean is most present not when waves crash but in the held moment just before they crash. "Your mother's voice I shall restore — in the night visions, speaking comfort unceasing. I shall give you a wife whose hands need not be reddened by lye, and sons who shall never know their grandfather's shame. I offer the sluggish, sweet warmth of a life entirely forgotten by the cruelties which the heights inflict, without mercy, upon those foolish enough to seek them."

The drag registered in Dan's long bones — sudden, enormous, a pull with the specific character of something bottomless asserting its claim, the way a debtor feels the sum of every unpaid oath arrive at once, all interest compounded. Something in his chest answered it before he could redirect. The answer was not yes. It was something older than yes: a recognition. He had felt this pull before — in the corridor, moving through the debris toward his father's door. That same invitation to stop. To let the house close around him.

His hand found the stone against his chest. His fingers closed around it.

"Your Temple fell," Dan said. "Because of a blinded man. You crumpled before the Sacred Chest."

"My temple fell not for want of My weakness, boy." The patience in the voice did not waver. A continent does not waver. "My statue crumbled not for absence of My power. There exist ancient boundaries which may not be transgressed — the realm of the living cannot trespass into the realm of the dead without cost. We are bound by different laws. Mistake not My ancient restraint — discipline practiced across eons — for weakness. It is the patience of ages. Not the failure of a moment."

"Then you admit it. You are a Being of Death."

Something shifted in the black water. The eyes found a different angle — not moving, but reconsidering.

"Look about you, boy. Look at the war within you. Look at the wars between men. Death is not a distant shore — it is the very sea wherein you swim ceaselessly. The question has never been whether you swim it. The question is whether you drown standing or lying down."

"Why should I serve you and not El or Baal who dwells higher?"

The eyes flashed — not with light, but with the absence of light, the way certain deep things register attention not by illuminating but by making the dark more complete.

"The one who pursues the upward path," Dagon said — and now the voice dropped to its lowest register, the frequency that lives below sound in the chest cavities of mountains, the note that makes foundations shift — "becomes the monument isolated. Cold, absolute, alone." A pause. The precise interval between the lightning and the thing the lightning makes inevitable. "You might survive, yes — where others have failed. But those you love shall live too, and in their living find only hardship. When you speak, no man shall comprehend. Your instrument shall be shattered beyond repair. Your tongue shall become a heavy thing — speaking foretold truth heard only as noise, the worthless babble of the judged, like chaff that the wind drives away."

A wave broke over the plank. Salt filled Dan's mouth, his nose, the back of his throat.

"You will have no witness."

"You offer me a raft for soft lies." Dan wiped salt from his eyes. "I just watched what that bought my father. I would drink this whole sea before I spent one day in your filth."

Dagon laughed — the low, foundational sound of an ocean finding a new shore. Patient. Certain. Final in the specific way that means: not tonight, but eventually, and I can wait.

"A brave little mouse. You choose the high places? Very well." The enormous face began to submerge — slowly, with the measured authority of a thing for which time is a resource it cannot exhaust. "I am the very earth those mountains are rooted within. Your struggle ascending is my foundation sustaining. If ever you should alter your tone — merely knock thrice, ask twice, then wait. I shall come with certainty absolute. I am the cadence you seek to outrun."

The visions came as the face descended: three, in rapid sequence, each printed behind Dan's eyes with the force of a brand pressed to flesh. White first — the blinding white of a future he could not yet read, painfully luminous and wholly silent. Black second — absolute, the black of the trench, the black of the abyss, the black of a thing that has never needed light and has never missed it. Then blue — that impossible dream-blue, the color of the sky above a sea that was about to kill him, cold and unbearably still.

Black water filled his mouth. His nose. His lungs. Everything went dark.

Dan woke gasping.

The frantic furnace within announced itself against his sternum — staccato, urgent, sweat soaking through his tunic until the cloth clung flat against his skin. For a terrible moment he could not separate the salt in his throat from the ocean and from the night's cost. Then the details arrived one by one, the way reality restores itself after a hard crossing: the clean-swept floor. The arranged possessions. The torch in its crack in the wall, burning low but burning. The window showing the first bruised hints of the sky's turn from black toward something not yet decided.

He stood and walked to the window. The sky was moving from black to that particular dark purple the sky becomes just before it decides to be dawn — uncertain of itself, carrying both. He pressed his palm to the stone sill and held it there. The cold came into his palm slowly, the way cold enters stone that has been holding it all night, the way it always does, the way it will keep doing after everyone in this house has been held by the earth they stood on.

He said something at the window. Short — three phrases, the kind you say when you are sixteen and have just done the hardest thing you have done so far and are not yet certain what it means. He said it toward the glass of sky the way the psalmists said such things: the complaint first, exact and unsoftened. The plea, smaller than the complaint. Then the thing you say when you are not sure resolution has arrived but you cannot keep standing at the window waiting for it.

"Stardust to stardust." His voice came out smaller than he intended. "Tell me it means something. Tell me I did not waste it."

He lay back down and pulled the thin blanket to his chin, though he was neither cold nor warm — only hollow in the way that follows a cost too large and too private to be witnessed by anyone. He no longer cared, precisely, whether his father woke healed or broken, whether the house concluded its long argument with its own foundations, whether the sun pulled itself above the horizon as it always had or the dark simply continued. Everything required too much. Like chaff that the wind drives away, he thought — and was not certain whether the thought was resignation or quotation or both.

His shadow on the wall, in the last of the torchlight, was hunched at the shoulders. The shape of a boy who has carried something a long way and set it down.

Then he heard them: from two directions at once.

From below — deep below, beneath the house's foundation stones, from the patient dark that has always lived at the world's basement — a sound like grinding. Slow and foundational, the percussion of certainty that does not need to announce itself because it has always been precisely where it is. The sound of something that has no need to hurry because the time it is waiting for is the time that comes for everyone.

And from the door — his door, the door of the one ordered corner in the house divided against itself — something that was not quite tapping and not quite rapping: a sound at the chamber's lattice like a late visitor, curious as well as patient, gently entreating entrance at his chamber lattice.

The house is divided, he thought. The house is divided and cannot stand.

The torchlight guttered. Shrank. The shadows at the room's edges gathered themselves inward.

*No witness* — not his voice, not the voice from the water, but the plain fact of it, settling into him the way cold settles into stone — *no witness* — as the last light failed.
