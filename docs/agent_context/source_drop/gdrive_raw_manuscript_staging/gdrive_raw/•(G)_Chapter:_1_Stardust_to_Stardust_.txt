CHAPTER 1: STARDUST TO STARDUST

Let me tell you about a boy named Dan, and the night he decided to save his father by breaking into his dreams.

This is considerably harder than it sounds.

The torch in Dan's hand was a nervous, sputtering thing. It cast a sick, copper light that barely pushed back the darkness of the hallway. The flame didn't so much burn as it was *squeezed* by the dark—a living, pressing weight that seemed to lean in from all sides like a huge, patient animal. The shadows it threw were long and suspicious, jittering across the limestone walls like thieves debating an ugly deed.

Dan's heart hammered an unsteady rhythm against his ribs. The torch shivered with every beat, as if his fear had crawled right up his arm and into the flame itself.

He was sixteen years old. He had kind eyes and messy black hair that fell across his forehead no matter how many times he pushed it back. Right now, those eyes were fixed on the door at the end of the hallway—his father's door—and they held the particular determination of someone who knows they're doing something dangerous but can't see any other choice.

The house, you must understand, had not always been like this.

Once—seven years ago, when Dan's mother was still alive—these four rooms had been filled with warmth. Zuna had sung while she worked her loom, her voice carrying through the limestone walls like sunlight through water. The air had smelled of fresh bread and wool and the sharp, clean scent of lye soap. Visitors had come often, seeking Aviel's counsel, for Dan's father had been the cleverest scholar in Hebron. His mind was sharp as new flint, and he could solve disputes and interpret texts with a wisdom that seemed almost prophetic.

But then Zuna died.

And something inside Aviel died with her.

Or perhaps it's more accurate to say that something inside him *stopped*. The part of a man that moves forward, that builds and plans and hopes—that part simply ceased its motion. In its place grew something else: a terrible, rooting grief that expressed itself not in tears or wailing, but in accumulation.

If you've never lost someone you loved—and I hope you haven't, though the odds are against us all—it's hard to understand what happened to Aviel. Grief is a strange beast. It can make a man want to hold onto *everything*, as if by clutching enough objects, he might clutch back time itself. As if by refusing to let go of broken pottery and rusted tools and scraps of worthless parchment, he might somehow refuse to let go of her.

Aviel had fled Philistia seven years ago, bringing his gods with him—the sea god Dagon, chief among them. After Zuna's death, Dagon became his only anchor. He built a shrine in the back room, a vile, intricate thing constructed from forgotten objects and desperate prayers. And slowly, TECHNIQUEically, the house transformed.

It became a tomb.

But not a clean tomb—not one of those neat, whitewashed sepulchers you see dotting the hillsides. This was a tomb of *refusal*, a monument to one man's absolute unwillingness to move forward into the terrible, empty space his wife had left behind.

The four rooms filled with debris.

Broken pottery shards formed small mountains in the corners, each one a failed vessel, a dream that had shattered and been preserved in its brokenness. Ruined brass astrolabes lay scattered like the corpses of dead stars, their mechanisms frozen, no longer capable of measuring the heavens. Cracked wooden compasses pointed in every direction and therefore no direction at all.

Zuna's loom stood in the main room, and on it her final work remained: sacred threads left mid-shuttle, now unraveled into mildew and decay. They draped over the frame like the skin of something that had tried to live and failed. The fabric had rotted, and in the rot lived pale, wriggling maggots—soft as mushroom flesh, busy with their small work of reducing all things to their simplest components.

The smell was extraordinary in its awfulness.

It was a layered stench, an archaeological record of decay: the sharp ammonia of old urine mixed with the sweet rot of food gone bad, overlaid with the dusty musk of dry herb and bone, and beneath it all, the sulfurous reek of something that shouldn't be in a house at all—the smell of disturbed earth, of graves opened, of things that belonged buried being dragged back into the light.

Swarms of flies rose and fell over the piles like living smoke. Their buzzing was constant, a low morbid hum that had become the house's new voice—the sound of active corruption, of matter in the process of forgetting what it used to be.

Dan had lived with this for seven years.

He had watched his father—that brilliant, sharp-minded man—slowly disappear into the accumulation. Aviel barely ate now. Barely spoke. He spent his days sitting in his room, surrounded by his hoard, whispering prayers to Dagon, the god of abundance, the deity of the downward pull, the lord who promised that death could lose its sting if you just held tight enough to the things of earth.

Dan couldn't watch anymore.

Tonight, he would do something about it.

But first, he had to reach his father's room without being heard.

Every barefoot step on the corridor's limestone floor was a calculated risk. The stone was cold—that deep, bone-aching cold of a place the sun had given up trying to warm. He moved slowly, distributing his weight with the care of a thief, because the slightest wrong pressure might send something clattering: a loose shard of pottery, a brittle bone, any of the thousand small landmines of debris that littered the path.

The house itself seemed to resist his movement.

That sounds like poetry, I know, but I mean it quite literally. The structure had taken on the character of its occupant. The very air felt thick, viscous, as if he were wading through something denser than air should be. It pressed against his chest, made his breathing labored. Each inhalation was work. The ceiling beams sagged—not from age or poor construction, but from something less tangible: the sheer metaphysical weight of misery concentrated in one place.

If houses could commit suicide, this one was trying.

Dan's hand went to his chest, seeking the reassurance of the one clean thing he owned: the Zuna stone. It hung from a cord around his neck, a smooth river stone his mother had given him when he was small. She'd told him it came from the stars, that all things did, and that when life felt heavy, he should hold it and remember that his true home was not down here in the mud, but up there in the cold, clean fire of the heavens.

The stone was cool against his skin—perfectly, wonderfully cool—like touching snow or spring water. It was the only temperature in the house that felt honest.

He pressed forward.

Outside, the night was alive with sound. Crickets chirped their sharp, urgent song—the sound of small things announcing their existence to the dark. The wind dragged through the brittle grass with a dry whisper, like a thousand voices trying to say something just below the threshold of hearing. A dog barked in the distance, the sound carrying clear and lonely across the hills of Judah.

It's funny how noise works when you're trying to be silent. Every sound outside seemed designed to betray him. The smaller the sound, the more dangerous it felt. A pebble shifting under his foot might as well have been thunder. The scrape of his tunic against the wall could wake the dead.

Or worse—wake his father.

Dan reached the doorframe of Aviel's room and stopped.

He pressed himself against the wall and listened.

From inside came the low, ragged sound of snoring—but not the comfortable snoring of a man at peace. This was the frantic, uneven breathing of someone running a race in their sleep, fleeing something they couldn't escape. It was the sound of a man drowning on dry land.

Dan's throat tightened.

Three nights ago, they'd fought. Dan had tried—again—to convince his father to leave the house, to come outside, to walk in the sun, to remember what it felt like to be alive. And Aviel had responded with words that still burned like acid in Dan's memory:

"If you're still searching for your source, boy," his father had hissed, his voice thick with bitterness, "then pick up a stone and give it a name. Call it God if you want. Only a fool seeks answers from a power he cannot touch."

The words had hit Dan like a physical blow.

Because Dan *was* seeking. Desperately. He was searching for something his father had given up on—the belief that existence had a point, that the universe bent toward something better than decay, that there was a height worth climbing toward.

His mother had taught him that.

"We're from the stars," she used to say, her voice warm as firelight. "All of us. We're made of stardust, Danny. So when the world feels heavy, when the mud tries to claim you, remember: your home is *up*. Always up."

Dan believed her.

He had to.

Because if she was wrong—if his father was right, and existence was just a meaningless accumulation of matter slowly collapsing under its own weight—then what was the point of any of it?

No.

He refused that answer.

Tonight, he would prove his father wrong. Or at least, he would try.

He lifted his hand and, with a quick breath, pinched out the torch flame between his thumb and forefinger. The sting barely registered. What mattered was the darkness that rushed in to fill the space—thick, total, drowning.

But Dan didn't need light for what came next.

He knelt beside his father's door and closed his eyes.

The stone floor bit into his knees, so cold it burned. He slowed his breathing—four counts in, hold, six counts out—just as his mother had taught him. She'd been a dreamwalker too, one of those rare souls who could navigate the sleeping world with the same intention others brought to waking. Some people had feared her for it, muttering about witches and dark arts. But Zuna had only laughed.

"There's nothing dark about dreams," she'd told Dan. "Dreams are where we remember what we're made of. They're the closest we get to home while we're still breathing."

She'd taught him the TECHNIQUE: how to empty himself, how to shed the weight of waking thought, how to step *sideways* into the vast, limitless country of sleep.

Dan focused.

*Forget yesterday.*

He released his memory of his mother's face, let it dissolve like salt in water.

*Forget tomorrow.*

He released his fear of what might happen if he failed tonight.

*Forget now.*

This was the hardest part—letting go of the awareness of his body, the cold floor, the ache in his knees, the smell of rot, the distant crickets. All of it had to go. He had to become empty, a vessel with no content, a space where something else could enter.

His breathing slowed further.

The darkness behind his eyelids began to shift.

And then, with a sensation like falling sideways through a door that wasn't there—

He was in.

---

The Dreamscape opened around him like a vast, silent country.

It wasn't a place, exactly. Or rather, it was every place and no place. The fundamental substance of it—what Dan thought of as "dream-stuff"—had the quality of dense fog mixed with starlight. It shifted constantly, responding to thought and will, capable of becoming anything: stone or water, mountain or abyss.

Dan had been here many times before.

But he'd never entered someone else's dream uninvited.

He stood now at the edge of his father's sleeping mind, and what he saw made his heart clench.

The boundary was a wall—thick, dark, pulsing with a sick, bruised light. It was made of memory and regret, layer upon layer of it, compressed into something nearly solid. Dan could *feel* the contents: a child's scream cut short, a hand slipping from his grasp, the smell of blood and burning stone, the final moment of a city's destruction, the weight of failure so complete it had its own gravity.

This was his father's internal ledger—every mistake, every loss, every moment of powerlessness, all of it pressed together into a barrier designed to keep the world out.

Or perhaps to keep Aviel in.

Dan placed his hand against the wall.

It was warm. Feverish.

*Please,* he thought. *Let me through.*

For a long, terrible moment, nothing happened.

Then—

The wall convulsed. Sound exploded through Dan's awareness: screaming, weeping, the crash of stone, the crackle of fire. Images flashed: faces twisted in pain, hands reaching for help that wouldn't come, his mother's body being lowered into the ground, the first shovel of dirt hitting the shroud—

Dan gasped but held firm.

*I'm not here to judge you,* he thought toward the wall. *I'm here to help.*

The pressure intensified, became crushing, unbearable—

And then broke.

Dan stumbled forward, suddenly through, released into the interior space of his father's dream.

What he found was a wasteland.

Not a dramatic, apocalyptic wasteland—that at least would have had a certain grandeur. This was something worse: a flat, featureless plain of gray dust that stretched to the horizon in every direction. The sky was the same dull gray. There was no sun, no stars, no landmark of any kind. It was a place that had given up on the possibility of change.

And in the center of it, alone, stood Aviel.

Dan's father looked smaller here. Diminished. He stood with his shoulders hunched, his arms wrapped around himself, as if trying to hold his own shape together against some invisible wind.

He was waiting.

Waiting for his dreams to begin, perhaps. Or waiting for them to end.

Dan felt a surge of love so fierce it hurt—the kind of love that sees someone destroying themselves and can't look away, can't stop caring, can't give up even when giving up would be so much easier.

*Alright then,* Dan thought. *Let's see if I can make you understand.*

He lifted his hand and spoke a single word into the gray stillness:

"Rain."

The response was immediate.

The dust of the dreamscape—that fundamental substance from which all dreams are woven—began to swirl. It coiled upward like living smoke, darkening from gray to a deep, mineral brown. It thickened, gaining weight and substance, condensing from vapor to liquid to solid in the space of a heartbeat.

And then it began to fall.

At first, just pebbles—small, smooth stones that clicked and clattered as they hit the ground. Aviel looked up, confused. A stone struck his shoulder. Another hit his hand. He picked it up, and in the logic of dreams, it transformed in his grip: the dull stone became gold, heavy and pure, stamped with symbols of ancient kingdoms.

His eyes widened.

More stones fell, and each one that Aviel touched became treasure: copper coins, silver rings, gems that caught light that didn't exist. And because this was a dream, because the mind believes what it's shown, Aviel began to gather them.

"Mine," he whispered, his voice carrying across the gray plain.

He shoved the treasure into his pockets. When they filled, he stuffed coins into his belt, under his tunic, clutched handfuls against his chest. More and more fell, and he tried to catch them all, his movements becoming frantic, desperate.

"Mine. Mine. All mine."

The stones kept falling.

They piled around his feet, rose to his ankles, his knees. Still he grabbed at them, unable to stop, driven by a hunger that had no bottom. The pile grew higher. It reached his waist. His chest.

And still the stones fell.

Now Aviel was trapped, buried to his neck in treasure he couldn't release. His hands were frozen in the act of grasping, his fingers curled around coins that had fused to his palms. The weight pressed down, squeezing the air from his lungs. His face turned red, then purple.

His mouth opened in a silent scream.

Dan watched, his heart aching.

*Do you see?* he thought toward his father. *This is what you're doing to yourself.*

For just a moment, Aviel's eyes cleared. He looked up, looked past the stones, and saw the sky—that flat, gray nothing. And then, as Dan had planned, as he'd hoped with every fiber of his being, a single point of light appeared in that grayness.

A star.

Small. Distant. But undeniably there.

Aviel stared at it, and something in his expression cracked. Not broke—that would come later, or never—but cracked, just enough to let in a sliver of something like doubt.

*You have a choice,* Dan thought. *You always have a choice.*

And then, gently, because this was his father and he loved him despite everything, Dan released the dream.

The stones vanished. The gray plain dissolved. Aviel's consciousness drifted back into the comfortable darkness of dreamless sleep, where it would rest until morning, processing what it had been shown in the wordless, sideways way that dreams do their work.

Dan opened his eyes.

---

He was back in his body, kneeling on the cold stone floor outside his father's room. The transition was jarring—from the limitless possibility of the Dreamscape to the crushing, specific reality of the house of rot.

The smell hit him first. It always did. The thick reek of decay seemed worse after the clean emptiness of the dream country, as if his senses had been scrubbed raw and were now taking in the full horror of the place with fresh intensity.

He tried to stand, and his legs nearly gave out.

The cost of the dreamwork hit him all at once.

His muscles felt like they'd been replaced with wet sand—heavy, loose, unwilling to obey. A sharp pain lanced through his temples, the beginning of what he knew would be a skull-splitting headache. Worse, when he tried to swallow, his throat felt wrong—thick, swollen, as if something had grown there in the minutes he'd been gone.

He lifted a trembling hand to his mouth.

His lips felt numb. Swollen. The flesh had the texture of old leather.

He tried to whisper a single word: "Mother."

What came out was a garbled mess—a thick, clumsy sound that bore no resemblance to human speech.

Ice flooded his veins.

*No. No, not this. Anything but this.*

He tried again: "Moth—"

The word died in his throat, caught on something that shouldn't be there, transformed into a choked, guttural rasp.

His instrument was breaking.

The dream had cost him something after all. Perhaps it was the intensity of it—shaping another person's sleeping mind, forcing creation where only entropy had been allowed. Perhaps it was Dagon's price, the sea god's punishment for Dan's interference in his worshiper's sacred misery.

Or perhaps—and this was the thought that terrified him most—perhaps this was just the beginning.

Dan staggered to his feet.

He made his way down the dark hallway, one hand trailing along the wall for balance, moving by memory more than sight. He reached the main room where the hearth's last coals cast a dull red glow—the exhausted heart of a dying fire.

He stumbled to the narrow window slit and pressed his face against the opening.
