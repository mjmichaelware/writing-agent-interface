The Weight of the Sky - Full Manuscript Synopsis
(New Synopsis)

The story begins in Hebron in 1003 BCE, a time when King David ruled over Judah, but the Philistines, with their iron weapons, still held significant power. In this tumultuous era, we meet a young boy named Dan, whose home is a physical manifestation of his father Aviel's grief-fueled hoarding after the death of Dan's mother, Zuna. Unlike his father, Dan is a dreamwalker, a rare visionary who can consciously control his dreams. He sees the dreamscape not just as a subconscious world, but as a universal realm made of "dust," the smallest unit of creativity. The dreamscape is layered, from the lowest depths of Pride and Gluttony to the highest reaches of Truth and a mysterious Light. In the dreamscape, all things are possible because all things exist within it.

The novel establishes a crucial metaphysical conflict: some people believe the "low gods," such as Dagon, Baal, and El, hold up the Earth from beneath, making them the most powerful. Others believe the "high gods" who watch from above—Yahweh, Courage, and Hope—are the true divine authorities. Dan's journey is a physical manifestation of his spiritual quest to ascend and find the highest God.

One night, Dan enters the dreamscape to send a message to his sleeping father. He forms creative "dust" into rocks that fall around Aviel. As his father instinctively hoards them, Dan's message becomes clear: his father is being consumed by his earthly possessions. Only when the rocks threaten to suffocate him does Aviel reluctantly let go, and Dan ends the dream. Aviel, having brought the gods of Philistia with him, worships Dagon, the fish god of abundance. Angered by Dan’s interference, Dagon appears to Dan in an uncontrolled dream. Dan finds himself on a ship in an endless ocean, face-to-face with the deity. Dagon attempts to coerce him, but Dan remains steadfast, recalling Dagon’s past specific defeats: how his statue crumbled before the Ark of the Covenant and how his temple was destroyed by Samson. Dagon dismisses this, claiming the "realm of the living cannot touch the realm of the Dead." Dan calls Dagon a "deity of death," to which Dagon replies, "Look at the war within you and the wars around you; death is ever upon you. I provide you a raft for it to lose its sting." Dan denies him, enraging the god, and the dream ends.

The next morning, a heated conversation ensues. Aviel, realizing the dream was a message for his son—a rejection of Dagon—recalls a past, cynical conversation where he told Dan, "if you're looking for a God, then pick up a rock and make it so." In his anger, Aviel tells Dan to leave. Dan packs his meager possessions and begins his spiritual and physical quest, traveling north from Hebron to Mount Hermon, the closest point to the spirit realm. His journey is an effort to shed "earthly treasures" that tie him down.

Part 2: Trials and Transformations

As Dan travels, he encounters people and places that reflect the very gods he has rejected. In Bethlehem, he rejects the seductions of an Edomite woman, angering the fertility goddess Astarte. Astarte appears in the well's reflection, taking the form of his deepest desire, but Dan is saved by the inner wisdom of his mother, Zuna, who believed humanity was made of stardust. After leaving Bethel, where he observes people worshiping Yahweh but still focusing on earthly leaders, Dan has a lucid dream. Having shed some burdens, he ascends higher, past a smiling moon, and dances with the stars.

At one point in his journey, Dan, filled with overconfidence, dreams he is recklessly exploring the dreamscape. This, a consequence of the "blindfolds of euphoria" after reaching such great heights during his recent ascensions. Not paying attention, he stumbles upon a secret council of the 12 low gods. He listens as they discuss him. A furious Dagon blames Dan's interference for his troubles, and Lord El, the chief of the low gods, accuses Dagon of being consumed by the Lord of "Anger." The council spots Dan, and the dream ends.

His most difficult test comes in Megiddo. The city is a devoted stronghold of the very low gods he has angered, and they strike at him aggressively, making the very ground and water hostile. Just before this, Dan momentarily relapses, spending lavishly on food and lodging, which causes his dream ascent to falter. The ultimate assault comes in his sleep. The low gods force him to fall through the dreamscape's layers of mal-existence. Lust, Anguish, Anger, and Pain, where he sees the "blighted husks of died Hope." He then reaches the layer of Gluttony and Envy, manifesting as the Lord of the Flies, Beelzebub—an endless, nameless sea of flies made to consume everything, including others’ being. Below this, he senses the beckoning pull of something even worse seeking to devour him. This sheer encounter with malevolence shatters Dan's mind. He wakes in a coma, speaking in the confused tongues that fell over humanity after the Tower of Babel. Although he spoke the same language he had always spoken, no one could understand him.

Upon his recovery, he sheds his last "earthly treasure," his wineskin, symbolizing his complete liberation from even the body’s essential needs. He now carries only two smooth stones. The stones are symbolic of Hope and Faith, the only thing Dan finds worthy of carrying with him and the only thing that he believes can be taken with him through death. As he nears the Sea of Galilee, he encounters the Sea Peoples. Having no material possessions but a couple of "worthless" stones, they take what he values most: his right eye. This is his final, brutal sacrifice. He arrives in the city of Dan, and his confused tongue leads the people to hear "Daniel," a new identity that symbolizes his shift from judging others (even Gods), to desiring to be judged by the ultimate God.

At the base of Mount Hermon, he meets an Anakim giant with six fingers on each hand and six toes on each foot, a brother of Goliath and a survivor of the Flood. He had been living on the mountain in shame, attempting to ascend but always failing for he meant to undo the wrongs of his forefathers. The giant can understand Daniel's confused tongue because he lived through the fall of the Tower of Babel though its fall did not affect him. Rapha explains it such that he has an uncanny ability to understand other people's spirits but cannot understand his own. That's because he's broken and being pulled apart at the seams between the law of his body and the law of his mind. To the giant, Daniel offers the most valuable gift anyone can give: a new perspective. He teaches the giant that all things were made "perfectly imperfect" and that the weight of the sky is not his to carry. Dan gives the Giant, named Rapha, just one of his stones (the one his mother had given him) saying, "This one gives me hope, though you must understand if I hold on to that which gives me Faith at all costs." The giant had been unable to look forward with hope for he had been so burdened and concerned with the past. With hope, and this new understanding of all things being made perfectly imperfect, Rapha sheds his burdens. and cries tears of joy so much so that he starts a little flood of his own. Daniel begins his climb, now propelled by the vivid "fear of what lies beneath." At the summit, he is taken up, body and spirit to keep the goodness preserved, like only one had done before him, bypassing death and ascending into the dreamscape. There, he becomes a deity of Love, a complete negation of the negative emotions he experienced during his fall.

Part 3: The Descent and Ascension of a New Cycle

As Daniel ascends, he passes a spirit falling toward Earth, which he instinctively tries to catch, sensing its profound gravitational pull. This spirit is Isabel.

The narrative shifts to introduce Kasha, a witch who worships Beelzebub. Her cat, Sak, is accidentally hexed by her using Yahweh's name in a spell, transforming him into an "overly curious" cat. Sak is the narrator of the story, a deity of Curiosity who speaks from a high palace in the dreamscape. Beelzebub, the Lord of the Flies, has grown lazy because the Philistines’ self-destruction with iron weapons has made his work of chaos unnecessary.

Kasha finds him lounging, but Sak, seeing through his handsome disguise, flees. Kasha is angry, but Beelzebub, who is "second to Lucifer," offers her his "truth." He tells her to look into his eyes, and she sees every pain, betrayal, and fall she has ever experienced. He tells her, "the truth I see is, it would have been better to have never been born or things made." and she agrees, joining him on a descent to the bottom of the pit where his brother, Lucifer, is sure to be as soon as his work is done. From their unspeakable union, Isabel is born, an immortal being who embodies hell itself.

Isabel lives a life where "hell is everything is bad and getting worse." She tries to end her life, cutting herself into a million pieces until she is just shy of returning to dust. She experiences all the negative states of being, even reaching the nothingness of Pride, but her innate righteousness, a part of her from the breath of God, cannot be consumed by it. At the absolute bottom, Isabel makes a conscious choice to love existence instead of hating it, opposing her father and uncles. In a profound act of forgiveness, she forgives her father and detaches herself from the hell that created her. She finally dies, and her soul ascends in the dreamscape, becoming a deity of Forgiveness, the very level from which she fell.

Epilogue: The Reunion and Aviel's Peace

Isabel’s spirit meets Daniel just below the level of Love in the dreamscape. An unspoken love and longing born from their shared suffering explodes in a cosmic union, a flash of light brighter than a thousand quasars, and they become one. In this moment, they (or It) can see what makes up the neverending nothingness at the bottom: Pride—an existence existing within its own nothingness, a self-contained world of pure selfishness with nothing to consume but its own ego. As one realizes the ultimate truth above: the blinding light at the very top is caused by a mirror, reflecting everything beneath it—from the highest love to the lowest depths of pride. For a moment, It can see in its reflection everything that had ever existed and everything that ever could including its own full potential. And even at these new heights, all knowing what lies beneath, the gravity of seeing one's full potential in the light almost shatters the new spirit causing it to fall again.

What is born from their union is a loyal love, a grace so profound it can even love Pride and not from pity, but from understanding. They go on to multiply this love by creating children, ensuring it goes on forever.

The story concludes in Hebron, now a city in a unified Israel ruled by King David. Dan's father, Aviel, had waited patiently, yet his suffering continued, a silent burden he carried alone. Then one night, his peace was disturbed. He looked up to the heavens and saw a new star—one that had never been there before. It was impossibly bright and impossibly distant. A quiet laugh, one that started deep in his belly and shook him to his core, broke free and echoed through the silent night until he could laugh no more. In that moment, the scholar knew the truth: his son had made it and would not be returning home. Hope, reborn, surged through him. He immediately put his own house in order, a lifelong project he had avoided. He denounced Dagon and took a new wife.

But this was not the final peace. One night, Aviel was jolted awake, trembling as he lay next to his new wife. When she asked what disturbed him, he told her of what he had seen. The very star that was his son's loyal love had split, giving birth to two smaller stars. But one was duller, a resentful twin. As Aviel watched, the duller star, consumed by pride, began to circle its brighter brother. It grew closer, and closer still, until it ripped a fiery vortex into its twin, devouring it in a brutal act of stellar cannibalism. Aviel recognized the story of Cain and Abel playing out in the heavens, starting the cycle of sin all over again.
The final words of the book hang in the air, a whisper on the night's wind.

"Here we go again.”
