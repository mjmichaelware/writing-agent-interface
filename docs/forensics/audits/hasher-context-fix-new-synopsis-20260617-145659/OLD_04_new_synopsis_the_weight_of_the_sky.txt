Synopsis of "The Weight of the Sky"
Part 1: The Ascent of Daniel
The story begins in the city of Hebron in 1003 BCE, during the early reign of King David over Judah. We are introduced to Dan, a young boy living in a house consumed by the refuse and labyrinthine hoarding of his father, Aviel. This decay is a direct consequence of the death of Dan's mother, Zuna, years prior, a grief that has twisted Aviel into his current state. Dan is a dreamwalker, capable of lucidly controlling his own dreams.
The novel establishes a unique metaphysical concept: the dreamscape. This is not merely a realm of individual dreams but a universal land that is simultaneously the spirit realm and the repository of all layers of existence. These layers are arranged hierarchically, from the lowest depths of Pride (existing as pure nothingness), to Gluttony and Envy (manifesting as an endless sea of flies), through layers of suffering like Anger, Anguish, and Lust, all the way up to "good" layers like Truth and Curiosity, culminating in a mysterious Light at the very top. The dreamscape is born from "dust"—not earthly dust, but the smallest unit of creativity, capable of forming and shaping reality within dreams.
Chapter 1: A Message Sent, A God Angered One night, Dan enters the dreamscape with a crucial mission: to send a message to his sleeping father, Aviel. He needs to inform his father that he is leaving home and that Aviel must prepare his heart for this departure. Within the dream, Dan, wielding the creative "dust," causes rocks to form and fall around his father. Aviel, in his dream, instinctively grasps at them as if they are precious possessions, reflecting his waking hoarding. Only when the rocks threaten to suffocate him does Aviel realize his folly. Dan then ends the dream.
Aviel, having brought the gods of Philistia with him from the east, worships Dagon, the fish god of abundance. Angered by Dan's interference in his work within Aviel's heart, Dagon appears to Dan in an uncontrolled dream. Dan finds himself on a ship in a vast ocean, with a man's head at one end and a fish's tail at the other – Dagon's symbolic form. Dagon, accompanied by "friends," attempts to coerce Dan, but Dan remains steadfast. He recalls Dagon's past defeats, specifically how he fell before the Ark of the Covenant and how his temple was destroyed by Samson. Dagon dismisses this, claiming the "realm of the living cannot touch the realm of the Dead." Dan scoffs, calling Dagon a "deity of death, a gatekeeper," to which Dagon replies, "Look at the war within you and the wars around you; death is ever upon you. I provide you a raft for it to lose sting." Dan, however, denies him, further enraging the god, and the dream ends.
Chapter 2: The Departure and the Northern Quest The next morning, Aviel, still reeling from the dream-vision, calls for Dan. Though he initially misinterprets the dream as a sign for himself to return to his scholarly roots, he soon realizes it was a message for Dan – a rejection of Dagon, the god of the house. Aviel and Dan have a heated conversation, recalling a previous discussion where Aviel cynically suggested that "if you're looking for a God, then pick up a rock and make it so," implying that anything one worships or dedicates time and effort to can become a god. In his anger, Aviel tells Dan to leave immediately.
Dan gathers his meager belongings: a walking stick, a water jug, 20 shekels earned in the vineyards and marketplace, a blanket, and some dried figs, fruits, and grains. Aviel, surprisingly, gives him a wine bottle and five additional shekels for bribes, which Dan keeps separate. Dan leaves Hebron through the northern gates, observing a rich man with servants and music, wondering if he too is "aiming up." He bids farewell to his friends and departs his quiet, immaculate room, reflecting his belief that one must put their own house in order before attempting to straighten the world.
Dan's journey is a physical manifestation of his spiritual quest: he aims to go as high as he can go to find a true God. This means traveling straight North on the map, from Hebron to the city of Dan at the base of the Sea of Galilee, and ultimately to the summit of Mount Hermon, where the veil between the living and spirit realms is thinnest, a place closest to the dreamscape.
Part 2: Trials and Transformations
As Dan travels through the cities of ancient Israel, his experiences directly reflect the local deities. He observes people performing rituals in temples, witnessing the worship of Dagon, Baal (the storm god), and other Canaanite gods. He sees their idols and sacred asterisks. His ascent is marked by his ongoing effort to shed fleshly desires, materialism, immediate gratification, hedonism, and negative emotions like resentment and pride – "treasures of the Earth" that tie him down.
Every now and then, Dan goes to sleep and has a controlled, lucid dream, a reflection of his spiritual progress. As he sheds these burdens, he is able to ascend higher and higher in his dreams, jumping, flying, and bouncing. After leaving Bethel, for instance, he dreams of going past the moon, which smiles at him, and dancing with the stars. However, he cannot pass the stars; there seems to be no end to them.
* Bethlehem: Just north of Hebron, Dan meets an Edomite woman by a well who attempts to seduce him. He rejects her, angering her goddess, Astarte (the fertility goddess). Astarte appears to Dan in the well's reflection, taking the form of his greatest desire. Dan is almost completely ensnared, nearly slipping into the well, but he is saved by the inner voice and wisdom of his mother, Zuna, a fellow dreamwalker who believed humanity was made of stardust and destined to return to the heavens.
* Bethel: While the people here worship Yahweh and "aim up," Dan observes they don't look high enough, still focusing on earthly leaders rather than a higher divine authority.
* Saul's Birthplace: Dan finds the city still mourning King Saul, confused why they long for a "true king" when David already reigns, and why they look to Earth for a king when they have a higher God. He hears conflicting stories about Saul's righteousness, adding to his confusion.
* Joseph's Well: At a dried-up well, Dan feels a profound spirit of betrayal.
* A Strange Encounter: In an unnamed land, a man mistakes Dan for "young Jacob," another famous visionary, stating, "You've returned? Have you seen things?" This unnerves Dan, unaware the man is simply confusing him with another dream-seer.
The Uncontrolled Dream and the Low Gods' Council: At one point, Dan has an uncontrolled dream, like most people. He finds himself in a strange, overgrown land with giant trees, insects, and tall grass. He comes upon a clearing with a large, flickering red flame, like a burning house. Around it, a dozen figures sit in unusual, throne-like chairs. These are revealed to be the 12 "low gods" (or, in the book's inverted hierarchy, "low" in the sense they hold up the Earth from beneath, unlike the "high" gods who watch from above): Dagon, Baal, Beelzebub, Astarte, El, and others. They are discussing Dan, who has angered several of them, particularly Dagon, who Lord El accuses of being possessed by the deity of "Anger." Dan stumbles upon their council, they spot him, and the dream ends.
The Ordeal in Megiddo: Dan eventually reaches Megiddo, a lowland city built on the piled ruins of old cities, heavily devoted to the very gods Dan has angered. Here, the "low gods" strike him aggressively:
* The very ground beneath his feet tugs at him due to a particular god.
* He feels ill due to Mot, the god of sickness and plague.
* Baal makes his water taste bad.
* The people, even the children, give him dirty glances, charging exorbitant prices for food and lodging, offering no hospitality.
Just before this ordeal, Dan momentarily relapses, spending lavishly on food and lodging, adding to his "earthly treasures." This causes a temporary setback in his dream ascensions; he passes the moon, but it frowns, and he is unable to dance with the stars.
The true assault comes in his sleep in Megiddo. Lord El and Lady Astarte literally strike him down in his dreams, forcing him to fall through the dreamscape's layers of human suffering. He descends through Lust, Anguish, Anger, and Pain, witnessing the "blighted husks of died hope." He then reaches the layer of Gluttony and Envy, manifesting as the Lord of the Flies in his true form: an endless, nameless sea of flies, stretching from horizon to horizon like a boundless ocean. Below this, he senses a strong gravitational pull, hinting at the layer of Pride, which exists as pure "nothingness" in its own self-contained universe, though he cannot yet see it.
The sheer malevolence and proximity to these depths shatter Dan's mind. He wakes up speaking in tongues and falls into a coma for several days. Luckily, worshipers of Yahweh (the high God) from Bethel find him and take him into their home. During his recovery, having lost everything, Dan lets go of his anger and fear of the layers of human suffering. He works for the family to help repay their kindness, and upon leaving, he sheds his last "earthly treasure": his wineskin. He now carries only two smooth pebbles: one from his mother, a "still place" stone for calming himself and returning to a state of heightened consciousness, and another similar stone given to him by a woman he met on his journey.
Part 3: The Ultimate Ascension and the Birth of a New Cycle
The Sea Peoples and the Sacrifice of Sight: As Dan approaches the Sea of Galilee, he encounters the Sea Peoples, who are thieves. Having no material possessions, they take what they value most: his right eye. This brutal act is also partly a consequence of Dan, having lost his fear, mocking them with the only two words he can utter while speaking in tongues: "Sea Peoples."
The City of Dan and a New Identity: Arriving at the city of Dan, one-eyed and still speaking in tongues, the people try to learn his name. "Dan," meaning "to judge," had been fitting for his earlier journey of critiquing gods. However, he now desires not to judge the gods, but to "be judged by the ultimate God." The people, misunderstanding, hear his name as Daniel ("to be judged"), and this becomes his new identity, a symbolic rebirth akin to Jacob becoming Israel or Sarai becoming Sarah.
The Anakim Giant: At the base of Mount Hermon, Daniel meets an Anakim giant. This six-fingered, six-toed being has been alive since the Flood, surviving by climbing Mount Hermon. He is descended from the fallen angels who corrupted humanity and is the brother of Goliath, recently slain by King David. The giant is conflicted; a "good part" of him, inherited from his angelic ancestry, constantly "aims up." Daniel, having nothing material, offers the giant something far more valuable: hope, a listening ear, and friendship. In return, the giant gives Daniel a spiritually potent dagger (which is like a sword to Daniel due to its size), meant to help him "aim even higher." The giant, finally feeling relief, weeps tears of happiness.
The Final Ascent of Mount Hermon: Daniel begins his final climb. The "low gods" launch renewed attacks (sickness, loneliness from Astarte, thirst from Baal), but Daniel, now propelled by the vivid "fear of what lies beneath" (the trauma of the lower dreamscape layers), continues his ascent, unmoved by their assaults. He is now completely unburdened by earthly desires.
At the very top of Mount Hermon, Daniel sees the 200 holes left by the fallen angels, as mentioned in the Book of Enoch. In a miraculous event, like Enoch, Daniel is taken up, body and spirit, effectively surpassing death. He enters the spirit world, becoming a deity of Love within the dreamscape, representing the complete negation of the negative layers of emotion and suffering he experienced during his fall. He takes a throne in this realm.
Part 4: The Descent of Kasha and the Birth of Isabel
As Daniel ascends into the dreamscape, he passes a spirit falling down towards Earth, as if to be born. He attempts to grasp this spirit and prevent its birth, sensing its profound gravitational pull, but it slips through his fingers. This spirit is Isabel.
The narrative then shifts back in time to introduce Kasha, the Witch of Jezreel Valley. Kasha is a worshiper of Beelzebub, a "low god," a tradition passed down from her people rather than a sign of inherent malice. Earlier in Daniel's journey, as he passed through the Jezreel Valley, he met Kasha and her cat, Sak. Kasha was frantically calling out to Beelzebub, asking Daniel if he had seen her "Lord of the Flies." Daniel, misunderstanding her, excitedly confirmed he was looking for him, believing he was referring to a higher god. Kasha, who worships the low gods, was repulsed, believing he was going in the wrong direction. Sak, Kasha's cat, immediately took to Daniel, climbing onto his shoulders as if they had known each other forever. This cat is later revealed to be the narrator of the entire story, a deity of Curiosity (Sak'ranut in Hebrew), narrating from a high palace in the dreamscape at the level of Curiosity. Earlier, Kasha had accidentally struck Sak with a spell in Yahweh's name, transforming him from a curious but cautious cat into an "extremely curious and not at all cautious cat," frequently getting dangerously close to death.
Kasha, searching for her Lord of the Flies, experiences sleep paralysis. During this, six of the seven deadly Princes of Hell visit her, but the Lord of the Flies, the seventh, does not appear. She continues her journey south, mirroring Daniel's ascent by seeking her "low god." She eventually finds the Lord of the Flies in Ekron. While his true form is the abhorrent sea of flies, he appears to Kasha as a very handsome, smooth, cunning, and sly man, being the brother of Lucifer. Sak, however, sees through the illusion and immediately flees, never to be seen again in the physical world, but continuing his narration from his high dreamscape palace.
Kasha expresses anger at the Lord of the Flies for Sak's departure, despite having worshipped him since birth. To win her trust, he reveals what he calls his "truth": not himself, but a vision of Kasha's own accumulated pain, doubt, and betrayals throughout her life. Witnessing this trauma, Kasha agrees with his nihilistic philosophy that "it's better to never have been born."
Kasha mistakenly believes the Lord of the Flies is Lucifer. He corrects her, stating that Lucifer is his brother, residing beneath him in "the pit," endlessly working, and that to meet him, one must descend and await his work's completion. Kasha agrees to this journey. They go down together, both literally and figuratively, and from their unspeakable union, Isabel is born. The cat narrator explicitly refuses to describe the "disgusting" act of their conception. Kasha then agrees to be put into a deep sleep, to shorten her waiting time for Lucifer, effectively abandoning Isabel.
Part 5: Isabel's Redemption and the Grand Design
Isabel grows up without a mother, raised by her father, the Lord of the Flies, who is "second to Satan." She is forced to descend with him into the Lake of Fire and the Pit, born of sin and fire, an immortal being who embodies hell itself and cannot die, no matter how dire her circumstances. She desperately tries to end her life – by eating inedible fruits, starving herself, even cutting herself into a million pieces – but fails. Her father is utterly disgusted by her attempts, giving her no satisfactory answer as to why her "time is not numbered" like humans'.
Through a long and arduous series of events, Isabel finds within herself forgiveness for her father and for her very state of being. This is presented as the complete opposite of Jesus's cry of abandonment on the cross; Isabel chooses forgiveness for everything her father had done and would do, extending it even to her uncle, Lucifer. In this moment of profound forgiveness, Isabel loses her immortality and her tie to her father. She finally dies, and her soul ascends, becoming a deity of Forgiveness.
Isabel's spirit meets Daniel at the same level of Love in the dreamscape. They are reunited, transcending their earthly and spiritual suffering.
The Hierarchy and the Weight of the Sky: At this point, Daniel and Isabel realize that Sak, the cat narrator, is Curiosity, residing just above them in the dreamscape hierarchy. The complete hierarchy is understood:
* Love and Sacrifice (Daniel and Isabel)
* Curiosity (Sak, above Love, because "what is Love without curiosity, what is forgiveness without curiosity?")
* Truth (above Curiosity, because "you can't have love without truth, you can't have forgiveness without proof.")
* Above Truth, there is only a blinding Light. Daniel, now capable of seeing past the Sea of Flies to the nothingness of Pride, realizes that this Light at the very top is actually a mirror, reflecting everything beneath it – good and bad – so brightly that its true nature cannot be perceived.
The book's title, "The Weight of the Sky," is a complex play on words, questioning who truly holds up the sky (the high gods above or the low gods beneath), and exploring the paradox that "the higher that someone ascends, the stronger the downward pulling full forces are" as one becomes less grounded in familiar territory.
Epilogue: Aviel's Peace The story concludes in an epilogue, returning to Aviel in Hebron. King David now reigns over all of Israel, and the Philistines are in decline. Aviel sees a new star in the sky, a star that had not been there before, twinkling just like the star from the dream-vision his son had sent him years ago. In this moment, Aviel knows that his son has "made it" but will not return home. He finally finds it within himself to let go of the burdens that had consumed him, takes another wife, and finds peace, bringing a quiet, redemptive close to his own journey.
