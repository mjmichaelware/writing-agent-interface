Chapter 7: The Pit




The last rays of the sun, the color of old blood and bruised fruit, were staining the sky behind him as Dan approached the gates of Megiddo. The city rose before him, not a gentle hill like Bethlehem or a proud peak like Jebus, but a tell – a layered mound of history, built upon layers of forgotten ages. A place where countless lives had been lived, and countless more had ended, their dust mingling in the very soil of the place.


The encounter with the **woman seeking her Winged Lord**, had left a **chill** in the air that had nothing to do with the setting sun. Her frantic energy, her warped faith, had cast a long shadow that seemed to stretch and deepen as he neared the ancient stones of the city walls.


He tightened his grip on the strap of his waterskin, stood up straight with his shoulders back, and walked towards the gate, the heavy leather feeling oddly cold against his palm. The air itself seemed to grow heavy, thick with the scent of ancient earth and something else, something that whispered of things long buried and best left undisturbed, a faint, almost imperceptible hum that vibrated just beneath the threshold of hearing.


He passed through the thick stone archway, the transition feeling less like entering a known city and more like stepping into new territory. The air didn't change temperature, but it felt... old. A stagnant weight, like standing at the bottom of a deep well where the air hasn't moved in years, thick with the breath of things long buried.


The stones underfoot felt older here, worn smooth not just by the passage of feet, but by the relentless grind of Time itself, a force that felt coiled and watching, sensing his presence, a silent, ancient sentinel.


The people in the street seemed peculiar too. Not the easy smiles of Shechem or the open faces of Bethel or even the wary sorrow of Shiloh. Their eyes, as they looked at him, held something guarded, something... hard. A coldness that seemed to seep out from beneath their skin, a mirror reflecting a soul that had seen too much and liked too little. 


They didn't call out welcomes or offer lodging right away, as was the custom with his people. They simply stopped what they were doing – haggling over goods, mending nets, chatting by doorways – and watched him pass, their stares following him like invisible threads pulling at his clothes, his pack, his very being. It was the deep, unsettling silence of a crowd that chooses not to acknowledge you, but simply to observe with a silent, palpable disapproval.


A group of children playing with pebbles in a dusty square stopped their game abruptly as he drew near. Their laughter, a sound that should have been light and joyful, cut off like a snapped string. Their young faces, usually innocent and curious, turned towards him, and their gazes were sharp, judging, giving him looks that spoke of a deep-seated dislike he hadn't earned, a dislike that felt ancient, taught.


Even the children seemed to sense he didn’t belong, that something about him was fundamentally unwelcome here. One small boy, no older than five, threw a stone that bounced harmlessly near Dan's feet, his eyes wide with a hostility that felt too large for his small body. He was clearly not accustomed to the hospitality of his people. And the boy with messy black hair, and kind eyes which had seen too much picked his way through the refuse once more.


He walked deeper into the city, the streets winding upwards onto the tell, the incline steady and demanding on his legs. The ground under his feet felt strangely resistant, not just from the slope. Sometimes it gave a strange, soft give, like stepping onto thick moss, but it was only packed earth and stone. At other moments, it felt solid, yet seemed to vibrate with a low hum he felt more in his bones than heard with his ears, a subtle, unsettling thrumming that spoke of something vast and alive beneath the surface. This low, persistent vibration, a quiet buzzing, seemed to grow stronger with every step, a subtle warning that tightened the muscles in his back.


He passed a public well, its stone lip worn smooth by centuries of ropes and hands. As he neared it, meaning to check if its water looked clean, a ripple, violent and sudden, burst across the surface of the still water, not from wind, but from some unseen disturbance beneath, as if the water itself recoiled from him, spitting a fine spray of droplets onto the dusty ground.


A shiver traced a cold path down his spine. The earth under his feet felt sluggish, clinging to his sandals, making each step require more effort than it should. It wasn't just tiredness from the journey; it was a profound lethargy, a feeling of being pulled down, anchored to the heavy, layered, resistant soil of Megiddo.


The city itself seemed to sigh with a low, unhappy sound that wound through the narrow streets as he passed, a feeling that resonated with the ache that had settled in his bones, pulling him towards the ground. A city built on layers of battle and worship, dedicated to powers who valued roots and earth and the strength found in the low places, could become a very uncomfortable spot for someone whose very essence was aimed at the stars. Megiddo, perched between valley and sky, a place of crossroads and conflict, seemed determined to show Dan exactly which side it was on: the side that preferred things rooted, heavy, part of the long, undeniable history of the earth.


He needed food, water. His stomach rumbled, a hollow echo in the oppressive air, a basic need that felt somehow wrong in this place of spiritual rejection. His throat felt dry, parched by the dusty road and the strange, thick air of the city.


He saw a vendor selling dried fruit and nuts near what looked like a small marketplace, a cluster of stalls huddled together like wary sheep. He approached, holding out a few **Gerah**, hoping for a simple transaction, a brief moment of normalcy. The vendor, a man with a face like cracked leather and eyes that seemed to hold the coldness of old stones, eyed him up and down, his gaze lingering on Dan's simple traveler's clothes, on something unseen that marked him as an outsider, unwanted.


"What give for a handful of figs?" Dan asked, his voice feeling thin and dry in the heavy air.


The vendor scoffed, a dry, unpleasant sound that scraped like stone on stone. "For you, traveler? Double that of anyone else. Perhaps triple. My goods don't care for your... Ways." He gestured vaguely towards the sky, towards the space above Dan's head, where the stars would soon appear. He made it sound like an insult, an impurity, a stain upon his very presence.


Dan's hand, still outstretched with the Gerah, began to tremble slightly. The vendor's cold, dismissive gaze made the silver feel like a burden, a heavy, useless weight. He needed his remaining silver for lodging, for the long road ahead to places even further north. He turned away, hunger a sharp pang in his belly, a physical manifestation of the city's rejection.


He saw another vendor, this one with large clay jars of water, beads of condensation suggesting coolness within. His throat felt raw, parched by the dusty road and the strange, thick air of the city. He approached, holding out a shiny Gerah, the simple need for water overriding his growing unease.


"Water, please."


This vendor, a woman with tight lips and colder eyes than the first, eyes that seemed to hold the grayness of storm clouds, took the piece without a word. She poured water into a simple clay cup. Dan lifted it to his lips, eager for the cool, clear liquid, picturing the relief it would bring. The moment it touched his tongue, a wave of sickening disgust washed over him.


The water tasted foul, stagnant, like liquid decay mingled with ash and bitter herbs, the flavor of something that should have been pure, utterly corrupted. It burned his tongue, felt thick and wrong, like drinking mud and sorrow. He gagged, spitting it out onto the dusty ground near the stall, the taste lingering, vile and pervasive, coating his mouth with bitterness.


The vendor watched him, a thin, cruel smile touching her lips, a smile that didn't reach her cold eyes. "Doesn't suit your palate, traveler? Our waters are blessed by the Lords who hold up the Earth. Perhaps you prefer the thin, tasteless water of the high places, where nothing truly grows." She snatched the coin from the counter where he had placed it, pocketing it with a speed that belied her casual cruelty, the Gerah disappearing into her robes as if it had never been offered.


Dan wiped his mouth with the back of his hand, the rotten taste refusing to leave, a constant reminder of the city's rejection. A cold anger began to simmer within him, a slow burn against the injustice. The water, meant to sustain, had become a weapon, a physical manifestation of the city's spiritual hostility. It was Baal, he knew, the storm-claimers, the water-bringers who also controlled the depths of the earth. Their power flowed through the very water he needed to survive, and they had tainted it, turning it to poison.


They were angry. He felt their anger, a palpable pressure in the air, a silent roar against his very presence, against his daring to aim higher than their domain. He wouldn't be able to buy food or water here if this was the reception at every turn. His stomach cramped with hunger, his throat burned with thirst and the taste of corrupted water, a physical torment adding to the spiritual pressure.


The physical toll of the city's hostility was beginning to weigh heavily. The earth pulled at him with invisible ropes, the air felt thick and resistant, like trying to breathe underwater, and the people were cold, greedy, and unwelcoming. This was the battleground of his Internal War.


He pushed on, needing to find shelter before night fell completely, before his strength failed entirely. The feeling of lethargy grew stronger with every step, as if the very stones of the tell were trying to absorb his energy, to make him part of their ancient, layered history. He walked through the maze of streets, past homes made of sun-baked mud brick and stone, past silent, watchful children who stopped their play to stare with unsettling intensity, past adults whose eyes narrowed as he passed, some even turning their backs pointedly as he approached.


The ground beneath his sandals felt uneven, sometimes giving a strange, soft give, as if the earth itself were rippling, shifting in discomfort or anger at his presence, like skin crawling over bone. He felt a growing pressure, a crushing weight that seemed to push down from the very air above him, a silent, powerful opposition that mirrored the downward pull of the earth below. The subtle buzzing, which had begun as a faint hum, now felt like a low vibration in his bones, a constant, unsettling thrumming beneath the city's surface.


He needed lodging. Urgently. He couldn't sleep out in the open in a place that felt this hostile, this... alive with rejection. His body ached, his head throbbed, and the persistent, foul taste in his mouth was a torment.


He approached what looked like a larger home set slightly apart from the others, built of sturdy stone, the kind of place known for taking in travelers where they shared a common space. The owner, a large man with arms like tree trunks and a face carved from weathered rock, came to the door, his expression wary, assessing.


"Lodging?" Dan asked, his voice raspy from thirst and the strange taste, trying to keep the desperation out of it.


The man looked him over, his eyes sharp, missing nothing. He seemed to see something in Dan that others had reacted to, that mark of the outsider, the one who aimed too high for this low-lying place.


"For the night?" he rumbled, his voice low and gravelly. "We have space. A place to lay your mat in the common room." He paused, his eyes flicking down to Dan's pouch, a calculating gleam in them. "But it will cost you. More than usual. A visitor like yourself... attracts attention. Unwanted attention, by some. And attention comes at a price." He named a weight, easily twice what Dan had paid in Shechem or even Dothan for a simple corner.


It was a bribe, pure and simple. A shakedown. The price of passage through a place that wished him gone. It was his father's 5 heavy Shekels. The reserve meant for trouble, for just such moments when the world demanded more than it offered. It was earthly weight, tied to the world his father valued above all else, a world of possessions and palpable things.


A bitter irony that this money, meant perhaps to smooth a path downwards, into the accumulation his father understood, would now be used to secure a moment of respite on his journey upwards, a small victory against the pull of The Pit.


He reached into the hidden pouch, his fingers closing around a few Shekels from his “bribe stash,” feeling their solid, undeniable presence. He pulled them out and placed them in the man's outstretched hand. They felt cool and heavy, a tangible surrender to the demands of this place, a physical payment for spiritual passage.


The man's eyes widened slightly as he weighed the pieces in his hand, his thick fingers turning them over. A few Shekels was a significant sum, a payment well above the norm for a simple night's shelter. His expression softened marginally, the hardness easing into something like satisfied indifference, the look of someone who understood the true value of such a payment here.


"Aye," he grunted. "That will suffice. Come in, traveler. Find a space by the hearth. The meal is finished, but the fire is warm, and no one here will bother you tonight. Your payment has been noted."


Dan stepped inside the traveler's house, leaving the hostile streets of Megiddo behind. He felt the lingering pull of the earth, the phantom taste of poisoned water, the echoes of angry stares and whispering stones. But he had pushed through. He had endured the city's venom, paid the price demanded by the gatekeepers of this place.


Exhausted, hungry, and deeply unsettled, he found a space by the fading embers of the hearth in the common room, where other travelers were already settled, their forms quiet in the dim light. He unrolled his thin mat, placed his pack beneath his head, and lay down. The fire's warmth did little to chase the chill that the city had pressed into his bones.


He needed rest. Desperately. He needed to escape the oppressive weight of Megiddo, to find the upward path in the Dreamscape, to cleanse his spirit of the city's clinging hostility, to seek the clarity that lay far above this place of decay and demands—a clarity he associated with The Source of All. He closed his eyes, focusing his will, yearning for the familiar lift, the journey towards the stars. He needed to fly, to remind himself that the heights still existed, that the downward pull was not the only force in the world.


But this time, the familiar ascent was denied. The dream-currents, usually responsive to his will, felt thick and resistant, like trying to swim through mud. A cold, invisible hand seemed to press down on his chest, pinning him to the earth of his dream, denying him the sky. His spirit strained, pushed, but the upward path remained closed, sealed by an unseen force. The Dreamscape became real around him not as a field or a sea, but as a vast, empty place filled with a heavy, gray dread.


He felt their purpose before he saw them. A presence. Or rather, two. They took shape from the grayness, beings of immense power, though not in the twisted forms he’d seen before. Here, in the dream's sharp clarity, they looked like towering people, but filled with power that would shatter a normal mind. Lord El, ancient and terrible in his quiet strength, stood beside a Lady goddess, glowing with a beauty that promised both bliss and total destruction. El’s form was like a mountain of polished obsidian, his skin reflecting the dim gray light, his eyes like twin voids that seemed to absorb all light and warmth. The Lady, a shifting, shimmering form of captivating allure, her skin like moonlight on water, her hair a cascade of dark, living shadows, promised everything and gave nothing, a siren's call made manifest. Her eyes, when they met his, were like huge, dark storms, swirling with ancient fury and cold, calculating intent. It was the Lady from the god's meeting, whose beauty offered everything and gave nothing. El and the Lady, holding hands, their combined power a crushing weight in the gray space.


They didn't speak. They didn't need to. Their message hit him like a physical blow – cold, final, and filled with old, angry power. He had listened where he shouldn't have listened. This was the judgment of the Lord of Anger, a breaking of sacred rules they felt, a violation of holy boundaries.


Then, they struck. Not with a hand or a weapon, but with pure will, a command sent right into his spirit. It hit him in the chest, a shockwave that stole his breath and broke his focus. The feeling of wanting to go up vanished, completely wiped out. Instead, there was a sickening lurch, a terrifying flip. His dream, meant for climbing, was violated. His spirit's core was warped into a tool for his collapse.


He was falling. Falling down.


It started slow, a dizzying plunge, but sped up with terrifying speed. The grayness above him rushed away, replaced by a deeper and deeper blackness below. This wasn't a thrilling flight into the stars; this was a dive into something dark and huge and hungry. The air grew thick, heavy, pressing in, making it hard to breathe. It smelled of wet dirt, then of rot, then of something much worse – the sick, foul stench of decomposition, of things that had lain dead and breaking down for ages, a choking smell of filth and awful decay, thick with unclean promises. A low, constant buzzing, like distant swarms, began to fill the air, growing steadily louder. This was the battleground of his Internal War.


He tumbled head over heels, disoriented, the very concepts of Up and Down ceasing to exist, only the sickening feeling of falling remaining. The speed was impossible now, the blackness a blur. As he fell, the darkness itself seemed to show him things – not with light, but with a terrible, inner sight.


He plunged through the very layers of human failing, each a chilling revelation. He felt the cold grip of Lust, a sticky, clinging miasma that promised false warmth but delivered only emptiness, its tendrils brushing against his spirit. He plummeted past Gluttony, a bloated, grasping void, its hunger a palpable, consuming force that tried to pull him in. He fell through Anger, a fiery, explosive storm that raged and consumed, its heat scorching his very essence. He then descended through Resentment, a thorny thicket of old wounds and unforgiveness, its sharp edges tearing at his soul.
He plunged past the ragged edges of Despair, sharp as broken glass, the twisted look of broken Wills. He felt the heavy weight of old Sadness, heavy as buried stones, the piled-up Hurt of ages sinking. He witnessed the moments where Hope had dried up and died, leaving behind only dry, blighted husks. He saw the places where Courage had failed, leaving gaping voids, like wounds that wouldn't heal. It was a terrible show of broken spirits, flashing past him in the terrifying plunge, a brutal violation of his very soul, an awful falling into the gloom.


He felt strange shapes rush past him – twisted roots like grabbing hands, sharp stones that seemed to cut at his spirit, messed-up faces screaming silently, old buried things disturbed and falling with him into the deep hole. Whispers seemed to come from the blackness below, quiet at first, then getting louder and louder, a huge sound of hissing whispers, too fast to understand, but saying a chilling "welcome to the dark," a sound of contamination. This was everything the boy had fought against, the terrible twisting of the only true freedom he knew.


He tried to go up, to make his will push him upwards, to grab onto the light he knew was above. But it was useless. The power that hit him was total, impossible to fight. He was a stone dropped into an endless well, a body giving up to the pull of an unseen, monstrous gravity. He was falling into The Pit. Panic exploded, hot and sharp, quickly followed by a deep, cold Terror that froze him. This wasn't just falling; it was being taken by the awful deep, a fall into complete Helplessness. He was being claimed by The Pit.


The blackness got deeper, becoming something smothering that pushed against him. The sounds changed – the whispers stopped, replaced by a constant, growing buzzing. Quiet at first, like far-off swarms, it got louder and louder, a huge, many-legged hum that filled his ears, his head, shaking in his very bones. The smell of decay got stronger, becoming too much, a thick, sickening smell of spoiled things, of rot given shape, of the most terrible, gross, awful, rotting, foul filth. It was the smell of the Pit's deep corruption, a stench of spiritual sickness.


The buzzing was deafening now, a loud, smothering sound of swarms. The darkness was total except for faint, scattered sparkles of something dark and shiny below, like scattered jewels that had fallen into the awful slime. The air felt thick with tiny, fast movements, things brushing past him in the dark, wings beyond counting beating the air, a thick crowd of things.


He got ready for hitting bottom, for the crashing end of the fall into the absolute lowest point, the soiled, physical hole.


Then, with a sudden, shocking stop, the fall ended. One moment, he was plunging incredibly fast through the black, buzzing horror. The next, he was hanging there, perfectly still. The quiet after the loud, rushing fall was total, except for the constant, sickening buzzing all around him, a living sound of swarming, gross life, a vibrating presence.


He was in a huge, dark space, the blackness so deep it felt like a heavy blanket. And beneath his bare feet, stretching out into the terrible gloom as far as his inner eyes could see, was it. The Lord of the Flies. Envy, in its true form. Not a single figure, not a clear shape, but an endless sea. It was literally made of flies. Not billions, not trillions, but an endless, unnamed number, a living, moving carpet of shiny black bodies, buzzing wings, and eyes with eyes beyond counting. It was the ground itself, a vast, moving ocean of dark insects that reached to a horizon he couldn't see. Like looking down from high above the deepest ocean, where all is water, here all was fly. It moved with a low, inner power, a gross, sticky, awful, rotting tide that seemed to breathe with the life of the swarm, like madness made real, an abomination of creation itself.


And as his fall stopped, leaving him hanging just above this horrifying, living blanket, the flies, the innumerable, terrible flies that were The Lord of the Flies, turned their collective attention upwards. Disgusting tiny heads made for consumption, all turned at once. Every. Single. One. Of. Them. Looked. At. Him. He could feel their gaze, cold and old, the feeling of the whole huge swarm watching. It was the place of dust, decay, and death given form, a sickening, endless thing, a terrible twisting of what ground should be, a first awful sickness.


He had been thrown down into The Pit, stopped just above The Lord of the Flies himself, a sign of the power and wrath of El and the Lady, made stronger by the worship in the city above. He hung there, stuck in the stinking, buzzing dark, the eyes of The Lord of the Flies fixed on him, trapped between the layers of the spirit world, unable to go up, unable to fall further into the fly-god's awful hold. It was a crawling horror, a presence against everything clean and bright, a tainting of hope itself.


(And yes, dear reader, that was The Lord of the Flies in his true, horrifying form—the ultimate mirror of your own base envy—a sight he earned by refusing to heed the warning signs of his own descent into the dark.)


Beneath Envy's endless swarms, Dan sensed the profound, unseen tug of Entitlement, a swirling vortex of insatiable desire, demanding all. And at the very bottom, he felt the vast, unseen presence of Pride, the root of all, seeking to devour him even now too.


The image of the buzzing horror, the oppressive dark, and the myriad eyes burned in Dan’s mind. But he wasn't falling anymore. Or suspended. There was only… blackness. A deep, unresponsive void. The terrifying overload of feelings from The Pit faded, replaced by a profound, unending null.


He did not know how long he was there. Time didn't matter. The sun set and rose. And set again. And rose again.


Coming back to knowing where he was wasn't a gentle waking up, but a slow, painful pull back from very far away. His body hurt, stiff and weak, heavy as a sack of stones. His head ached dully, a persistent throb behind his eyes. He felt the rough feel of straw under him, pressing into his back. A fire burned in his throat, searing and unbearable – a thirst so deep it felt like his very bones were drying out.


He tried to move, his arms and legs heavy, unresponsive, bound by a profound weakness, by an exhaustion that went soul-deep. He tried to speak, to ask for water, but the sounds that came out weren't words. They were rough, mixed-up sounds, guttural noises that didn't mean anything in any language he knew. A stream of strange vowels and consonants, pouring from his lips uncontrollably, a foreign tongue spilling from his own mouth.


He was lying on a mat in a simple room, the walls made of mudbrick and stone. Close by, he saw a small altar made of rough, uncut stones piled together, a simple, unadorned structure.


Figures were nearby, their faces blurry at the edge of his vision, looking down at him with confused and scared looks. He saw hands reaching for him, heard voices raised in alarm, their words a jumble he couldn't decipher, lost in the mix of sounds erupting from his own mouth. He felt a cool hand touch his forehead, heard a gentle murmur he couldn't understand. These were not the hostile faces of Megiddo's streets. These were faces etched with concern, with something that felt like... kindness.


He saw, even through his blurry vision, simple symbols woven into their clothes, carved into a wooden beam overhead – symbols that spoke of The Source of All, of a faith that looked upwards, not down.


He was awake. But he wasn't okay. Water, was all he could think. His mind, which had soared among the stars and plumbed the depths of terrifying pits, was now trapped in a body that spoke a language no one, least of all himself, could understand.


The Pit had left its mark, a chilling echo that stayed not just in his soul, but on his very tongue, completing his Sacrifice of Judgment. He had gone down, and the fall had broken him.


What does a traveler do when his mind is clear, his destination fixed, but the voice that connects him to the world of men is forever shattered?
