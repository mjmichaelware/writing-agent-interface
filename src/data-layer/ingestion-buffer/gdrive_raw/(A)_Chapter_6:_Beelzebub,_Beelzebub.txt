Chapter 6: Beelzebub, Beelzebub

The stones of Shechem felt lighter beneath Dan’s feet as he stepped out of the House of Smoke and Oil into the soft morning light. A new day, a fresh start, or so they always promised, those bright, clean mornings after a night of necessary indulgence. He had woken before the sun, the scents of perfume and spiced lamb still clinging to his clothes, a ghost of the night's comfort, but his mind was clear, sharp, and eager to embark on the road ahead. He sought out a shepherd’s family, offering them a small piece of Gerah—a mere glint from the pouch, a tiny fragment of the earthly burden he was determined to shed. Their simple kindness was a balm after the chaos of Gibeah and the subtle, rooting atmosphere of Bethel. He’d traded a few Gerah for sturdy new sandals, the leather promising many miles, and a pouch of dried figs and hardtack—practical provisions for the path.
The people of Shechem, already bustling in the early dawn preparing for work, were happy to see him off. He spoke to a merchant packing donkeys by the city gate. “North, you say? Towards Dothan?” The merchant adjusted a strap, his face kind. “Aye, a good road, mostly. Follow the valley plain until it narrows and the hills press in again. You’ll reach Dothan by late evening, if you leave now and keep a steady pace.” He eyed Dan’s strong stride, a flicker of ancient knowledge in his gaze. “Though… if you’ve no pressing need there, many travelers prefer to skirt around Dothan. Better to press straight on towards Megiddo the next morning.”
Dan thanked him, clutching his new sandals. He’d heard the subtle warning, the unspoken unease surrounding Dothan. But the man couldn't know what Dan had seen in his dreams, the far greater, colder powers that held sway in the depths. Compared to the monstrous form of Dagon or the unsettling council of ancient gods, any earthly town felt like a shadow play. He thought of the stone altar in his dream, the vastness of the cosmos, the cold light he sought. What earthly fear could compare to the chill of the divine?
He lingered in Shechem until the sun climbed high, nearing its zenith, simply enjoying the unburdened quiet—the sounds of the marketplace, the easy flow of people, the way the light played on the mountainsides. It wasn't the sticky comfort of Bethel that had tried to root him, that subtle, insidious pull to forget his purpose; it was a simple, uncomplicated enjoyment, a moment of quiet peace before the road called again. It was nearing day’s half when he nodded to himself, checked his provisions, and stepped through the northern gate, leaving the city nestled between its sentinel mountains behind him.
The path to Dothan followed the broad valley for a time, a gentle, almost lazy start to the day's long march. The terrain rolled and dipped like a vast, rumpled blanket, easy on the legs and pleasant to the eye. The sky above was generous with clouds today, great, soft ships of white and gray drifting lazily, providing plenty of shade and keeping the glare at bay. It was a soft, veiled light that made the greens of the hillsides look richer, the browns of the path deeper. He walked with a good stride, enjoying the solitude, the steady rhythm of progress. Hour after hour, the miles melted away beneath his new sandals, a steady drumbeat of anticipation for the unknown that beckoned.
As afternoon wore on and the sun dipped towards the western horizon, the hills began to press in closer, the valley narrowing, just as the merchant had said. The air grew cooler, carrying the scent of damp earth and distant cooking fires. He kept his pace, pushing on as the light began to soften. He reached the outskirts of Dothan as twilight began to paint the sky in shades of rose and purple.
Dothan sat nestled in a small valley, surrounded by slopes dotted with trees. As he entered the town proper, the first thing he sought was water. His waterskin was light. He spotted a cistern—a circular opening in the ground, surrounded by a low stone lip, the kind of place where weary travelers came to draw life. He approached it, his hand already reaching for his waterskin.
But as he neared, a strange feeling settled over him, cold and profoundly unsettling. The air didn't feel different, but the place did. The cistern was a dry, circular opening in the ground, its darkness absolute—the physical memory of The Pit. And clinging to it was a lingering sense of betrayal and despair, a memory of abandonment and cruelty etched into the very stone, a history of being trapped, of being left to thirst in the pit.
As he stood there, feeling the eerie chill of the dry pit, a stooped shepherd—his beard like tangled wool, his back bent with decades of watching flocks—emerged from the grove nearby, his movements slow and deliberate. He eyed Dan with cloudy pupils that seemed to look through him rather than at him, and chuckled softly, a dry, rustling sound.
“Back again, are ye? You and your dreams... Always talkin’ of stars and dust and bowin’ sheaves.”
Dan froze. His heartbeat was a trapped bird frantic against his ribs, a pure, unadulterated terror that turned his blood to ice. How? How did this man know? Not just about dreams, but about his dreams, about stars, about... Dust? The words, so specific, so utterly personal, were impossible for a stranger to utter. A cold dread, sharp as a winter blade, pierced him. It wasn't just recognition; it was an invasion, a violation of his innermost self, as if the shepherd had pulled out his deepest, most private visions. The shepherd's eyes, cloudy and ancient, seemed to bore into the very core of his being, seeing the echo of another, a past now tied to his very name, a name whispered in this valley for generations. The air around him seemed to thicken, the evening chill turning into an icy prickle on his skin.
The shepherd tilted his head, the cloudy eyes holding a strange, knowing light. “Best keep your mouth shut, lad. Dreamers don’t last long around priests, not these days.” He chuckled again, a low, unsettling sound, like dry leaves skittering across barren ground, and shuffled away into the gathering gloom of the grove, leaving Dan alone with the chilling echo of his words. For this valley, you see, has a way of remembering, and the past, as any good storyteller knows, can be a tricky thing, reaching out to touch the present in unexpected ways, blurring the lines between what was and what is. (The elder knew his secret, yet Dan did not ask how. The refusal to engage in Curiosity about the Truth is always the first sign that the Internal War is in retreat.)
For the first time since leaving the familiar walls of Hebron, Dan ran. His new sandals slapped against the dusty path as he bolted away from the broken cistern and the impossible knowledge in the old man's eyes. He ran towards the center of the town, towards the noise and presence of other people, desperate to shake off the feeling of being seen, of being known in a way no stranger should know him.
The town center of Dothan was a maze of stone and mud brick houses, clustered tightly together as if for comfort and defense, huddled around the places where water could be found. People moved through the narrow pathways, the sounds of evening settling in—cooking fires being lit, voices calling out, the distant bleating of flocks. The people were outwardly hospitable, quick to offer a stranger a place by their fire or a corner in their home for the night. This was The Snare, he realized, the subtle, silken thread of comfort that could bind a man to a place, make him forget the journey, the purpose, the Ascent.
However, as Dan looked closer, he saw the symbols woven into their garments, carved into the lintels of their doors—the familiar marks of El, ancient and distant, but clearly revered here, alongside other symbols he recognized from Jebus, symbols that whispered of Baal and storm power. He remembered the advice from Shechem—don't stay in Dothan. And now, seeing the symbols of powers he instinctively feared, and feeling the unsettling presence by The Pit, he questioned his decision not to have simply skirted the town as advised.
He saw glimpses of activity that reinforced his unease—the glint of knives, the lowing of animals destined for sacrifice. The wrong sacrifices, Dan thought, a quiet certainty settling in his gut, a certainty that whispered of the dark, clinging forces he had encountered in his dreams. Despite the genuine kindness in the offers of hospitality, something in him resisted settling amongst these symbols and practices.
He politely refused several offers, muttering thanks and excuses about needing to find specific lodgings. But then he saw a family gathered outside a smaller, simpler home. They too offered a place to stay, and as he looked at their garments, their doorpost, he saw no symbols of El or Baal, only older, less common markings that spoke of harvest and the turning of the seasons, tied to the rhythm of the earth. Their faces were open, kind. Hesitantly, relief warring with caution, he said yes.
He offered the woman of the house a small Gerah. "For your kindness," he said, pressing it into her hand. She demurred, but her eyes showed her appreciation. She led him into their simple home, the air warm with the smell of their evening meal.
Their home was small but tidy, two rooms made of sturdy mudbrick and stone. In the corner of the main room, Dan saw a low altar made of rough, uncut stones piled together, simple and unadorned. It looked humble. His childlike Curiosity, always a strong current within him, prompted him to ask.
"That altar?" Dan inquired, gesturing towards the pile of stones. " Why is it made?"
The man of the house, a quiet, thoughtful sort with gentle eyes, nodded. "It is made for The Source of All. The One who is before all things, who dwells beyond the skies."
The woman, bustling with their meal, added, her voice soft but firm, "We seek Its favor, not the fleeting power of those who claim dominion here." She gestured vaguely downwards and outwards, implying the gods whose symbols were scattered throughout the town. "The Storm-Claimer, the Earth-Binder... their power is strong, yes, but tied to the dust. Not like The Source of All, whose kingdom is beyond the skies, beyond mortal reach."
Dan felt a flicker of recognition, a resonance with his own deepest yearnings. They, too, looked past the immediate, the tangible, towards something higher. They spoke of El and Baal with a quiet disapproval that mirrored his own instinctive recoil. They spoke of The Source of All with reverence, not naming It with words Dan had heard elsewhere, but speaking of Its nature in a way that felt True.
They talked as the sun dipped below the horizon, painting the sky in glorious, fading colors. They shared their meal—a simple stew, fresh bread, and fruit. And before they ate, the family did something Dan had never seen before. The father, mother, and even the small children looked up towards the darkening sky, their faces turned upwards, and whispered words of thanks. It was a peculiar practice, this quiet act of acknowledging the unseen, of speaking gratitude to the source before partaking of the bounty come from above. It settled warmly in his chest, a quiet, unexpected peace.
Finally, the fatigue of the day, coupled with the profound comfort of finding kindred spirits, claimed him. He was shown to a small, clean space, given a blanket, and bid goodnight. He lay down, expecting perhaps another dream-struggle, another encounter with the oppressive forces of the deep.
But sleep came instantly and completely. It was the deepest sleep he had ever known, a vast, black expanse of perfect rest that seemed to stretch on for an eternity. The hours melted away, leaving no trace, no memory of passage, just a profound, unwavering stillness. He sank into it, deeper and deeper, until the very concept of time ceased to exist. There were no dreams, no struggles, no looming figures from the deep. Just profound stillness and replenishment, a true balm for a weary soul. He woke what felt like an age later, fully rested, his body and mind singing with unexpected vitality, as if he had been reborn. Yet, a small part of him felt a pang of disappointment. He had not had the opportunity to attempt his Ascent, to test his newfound strength in this place of quiet faith.
He made sure to wake well before the first hint of sunrise, the sky still a deep, inky blue. He quietly slipped out of the shelter, leaving a small Gerah on the blanket, a silent thank you to the sleeping house. He had a long journey to Megiddo, and the day needed to start early.
The path from Dothan to Megiddo stretched ahead, a promise of a new country. The land here was fertile, dipping into valleys, rising onto gentle slopes. The air was cool and fresh, carrying the scent of dew-kissed earth and distant woodsmoke. As the sun began its slow Ascent, the clouds from the day before had vanished, leaving a vast, clear expanse of blue overhead. The light grew steady and bright, warming his back as he walked.
He passed through small, unassuming villages. It was in one such village, as he walked along the edge, that he saw a sight that made his stomach clench.
A child, small and thin, its wrists bound with rope, being led away by two older men conversing about a “Lord Moloch.” The child’s face was streaked with dirt and tears, its eyes wide with fear. Something primal twisted in Dan’s gut, a fierce instinct to intervene, to shout, to fight. But a cold, sharp ache settled in the marrow of his chest, a physical rending that pinned him against the mudbrick wall. He was a stranger, passing through. He couldn't possibly understand the situation. What a terrible thing to assume, he thought, his own quick judgment pricking at him. He didn't know the Truth, only the raw, unsettling image. The moment passed, the small, bound figure disappearing down a path, leaving Dan with a heavy stone in his chest and the bitter taste of helplessness.
He continued his journey, the image lingering in his mind like a persistent burr. He reached the northern edge of Dothan’s influence, finding a small marketplace set up where travelers often stopped. He traded a small amount of Gerah for more provisions. He refilled his waterskin at a well here, the water cool and clean, blessedly present.
He walked for many more hours, the landscape gradually changing as he left the hills behind and approached the vast, open expanse of an enormous fertile valley. The terrain softened, the fields stretching out towards distant horizons, a gape beneath the endless sky. He knew Megiddo lay ahead, overlooking the great valley, a place of countless battles and history etched into the earth.
The city rose ahead, built upon a tell, a mound of countless layers of history, each one a tombstone for a forgotten past. The moment his foot crossed some unseen boundary into the city's sphere of influence, he heard an absurdity. A woman's voice, raw with anguish, crying out.
"Wherefore art [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE]?!"
He looked up, scanning the dusty path that wound into the city proper. He saw her then, a figure walking rapidly towards him, but screaming at the ground, her voice a desperate, heartbroken wail.
"Baal-Zebub, Baal-Zebub, O’ My Baal-Zebub. Wherefore art [PRIMORDIAL_SEVERE_MANDATE]?!"
She was beautiful in her grey headedness. A splendor etched with time and sorrow, her face, though lined, held a striking grace, her eyes, wide and searching, seemed to hold the wisdom of ages and the pain of a raw, fresh wound. Her hair was unbound, flowing around her shoulders like a silver-streaked waterfall. Her movements were quick, almost erratic, driven by a desperate, frantic energy. She was dressed in simple, dark robes, yet she wore them with an air that spoke of a different time, a forgotten grandeur. And at her feet, a tiny, sleek black cat, no bigger than his hand, darted and weaved, its tail twitching with an almost frantic excitement. As Dan drew closer, the little creature, with eyes like emeralds, spotted him. It let out a tiny, delighted chirp, then launched itself forward, a black blur, weaving between his legs, rubbing against his ankles with an almost desperate affection. Before Dan could even react, it sprang, nimble as a shadow, up his leg, then his arm, settling on his shoulder with a soft purr, nudging its small head against his cheek, as if it had known him forever and was utterly thrilled to be reunited.
Their paths converged, and they met in the middle of the dusty road, the woman's frantic energy swirling around Dan like a sudden, unsettling wind. Her eyes, fixed on the ground a moment before, snapped up to his, wide and desperate.
"Have you seen him, boy?" she gasped, her voice raw, her gaze sweeping the empty air above them, her hands trembling as they gestured frantically upward, a desperate plea to the vast, indifferent sky. "My Lord! My Baal-Zebub! He has forsaken me! Kasha of Jazreel! My work... it is unmade. Tell me, you who walk this road, have you seen The Lord of the Flies?"
Dan's eyes widened, a spark of pure, unadulterated excitement igniting within him. A Lord that flies? He pictured something soaring, something unseen, a Truth-teller, a master of secrets. A surge of exhilarating energy coursed through him, a wild, joyful recognition. The kitten on his shoulder, feeling the tremor of his excitement, purred a low, rumbling song, its tiny claws kneading gently into his tunic, a small, warm anchor.
"Yes!" Dan exclaimed, his voice rough with excitement, tumbling out faster than he could control. "I seek him too! The Winged Lord! The one who holds the hidden Truth! I am going up, as high as I can go, to find him! Is that where he is? High up, beyond the clouds?" He gestured wildly upwards, his heart hammering with a desperate, hopeful joy. The small black cat on his shoulder, sensing his excitement, purred louder, its tiny claws kneading gently into his tunic, its emerald eyes fixed on his face.
The woman, Kasha, stared at him, her frantic energy replaced by a sudden, chilling stillness. Her wide, searching eyes narrowed, a flicker of profound disgust and confusion replacing the desperation. Her lips thinned into a line of cold disapproval. She recoiled, stepping back from him as if he carried a contagion. Her gaze, which had been fixed on the heights, now dropped to the dusty road, then swept southward, towards the distant, hazy hills that descended into the lowlands. The kitten twitched an ear, a tiny ripple of unease passing through its fur, sensing the sudden coldness from the woman.
"Up?" she rasped, her voice losing its raw edge, hardening into a chilling, almost pitying contempt. "Ascent, you say? Foolish boy! My Lord of the Flies does not concern himself with barren heights! He commands the depths! He is the Lord of the Flies, yes, but he rules the teeming, fertile darkness below! He is the one who brings the bounty of the earth, the one who roots us firmly in the soil! And he has gone south, child! South! Away from this valley! He is gone to the deepest, most fertile places, where his power can truly flourish, far from the meddling of those who seek to pluck souls from the good, rich earth!" Her voice dropped to a low, bitter murmur, almost to herself. "His work... undone. He left me to this barrenness." The kitten, usually so attuned to the woman's moods, seemed to ignore her now, pressing its small head harder against Dan's cheek, purring a soft, reassuring rumble.
Dan stared at her, the initial surge of excitement draining away, replaced by a cold, prickling unease. Her words, her disgust, her southward gaze—he felt a chasm open between them, a silent, widening gulf. The Lord of the Flies, not a soaring deity of light, but a power of the depths, of the very earth he sought to transcend. He saw the Truth in her eyes, a profound, unwavering conviction in her chosen god, a faith rooted in the rich, dark soil she spoke of. And in that moment, even through his confusion and the chill of her disapproval, he saw a strange, quiet kindness in her gaze, a weary understanding, as if she pitied his misguided striving for the cold, distant stars. She was a woman of the earth, bound to its cycles, her faith a deep, unyielding root in the soil.
"Farewell, then, boy," Kasha said, her voice softer now, tinged with a deep, ancient sorrow. She nodded slowly, her gaze sweeping over him, taking in his simple clothes, his determined stance, his single, searching eye. "May your path lead you where you believe it should, though it leads away from all that is warm and True." She turned, her dark robes and silver streaks swirling around her, and began to walk briskly southward, her tiny black cat, after one last affectionate head-nudge to Dan, leaping from his shoulder to follow her, a small, dark shadow melting into the fading light.
Dan watched her go, the dust settling behind her, leaving him alone on the road. The setting sun cast long, lonely shadows across the Jezreel Valley, the air growing heavy with the scent of dry earth and the lingering echo of Kasha's words. (Dan felt the judgment of his Ascent for the first time—that he sought the highest Truth but might miss the necessary Love of the earth. The reader, safe in their chair, is left to wonder which high-minded pursuit they cling to that keeps them from the simple, terrifying act of Love.) He stood there for a long moment, the warmth of the kitten's brief presence still a phantom comfort on his shoulder, the weight of his walking stick and water container familiar in his hands. He had faced another, and had been judged, again. But he had not broken. He had not turned back. The path ahead, though unseen, still called. And the gates of Megiddo, dark and silent, seemed to watch him, waiting.
