Chapter 1: Stardust to Stardust

The torch light, clenched in dan's hand, trembled, casting suspicious shadows across the narrow hallway. The frail flame scattered light like cheap, copper coins. It was a nervous thing. It shivered not from wind, but from the erratic tempo of the boy’s muffled heart, a frantic drummer in a condemned dwelling.

Each barefoot step on the cold, sun-baked stone was a profound, private wager, where the slightest misstep risked the betrayal of a clank, a dry scrape, or any sound that might announce his small, forbidden errand. This was no mere silence; it was a coiled, ancient emptiness that smelled of sulfur and expectant dust. It was the patient, waiting dread of the stone in the field.

This was not a dwelling, but the heavy, unmoving lair of a man devoted to the earth’s sheer, magnetic force. Therefore, Dan’s every calculated movement toward the exit was an act of quiet, necessary heresy.

The four-room house, which had once been a space warmed by mother’s songs, was no longer a simple home. this house had been built in the heart of king david’s reign, around 1010 bce, but the man, Dan’s father, aviel, had brought his own gods with him from the Western plains.

The boy, dan, who was sixteen years old, understood this ruin not as accident, but as design. It had grown with a terrible symmetry, not merely into a ruin, but a physical testament to father aviel’s catastrophic unraveling since her disappearance. It was a vile, intricate shrine, a temple built of forgotten things, dedicated by a scholar who chose to worship the memory of his own disaster, which is to say: a life willingly surrendered to the earth’s inexorable pull.

Navigating its turns was not simple walking; one was forced to pick a path through the tangle of matter, feeling its terrible, rooting force with every breath, as if the air itself possessed a heavy, unpayable score. The entire structure felt like a pressure applied inward, seeking to crush its inhabitants back into the formless clay from which all flesh begins. The few who possessed the rare talent of mind-over-matter, those who could command elements or wish things into existence, often found their power bound by a patient observer, a presence possessing a long memory and a tale longer than the threads in a ball of yarn.

The house was cold, but with the specific, internal chill of a place where the sun has never reached.

Piles of broken pottery shards and failed mechanisms—things that had betrayed their original purpose—loomed like small, silent monuments to futures that died stillborn. Rotting textiles, mother’s sacred threads left mid-shuttle, had unraveled into mildew and waste, a promise broken not by time, but by overwhelming grief. The decaying fabrics sloughed off the looms, draping over discarded, brittle animal bones, making a horrifyingly dry rustle—the sound of the inner self surrendering to the material.

The bones were everywhere: small, sharp ribs and smooth, bleached vertebrae scattered beneath the refuse. Withered sandals and rusted bronze tools lay scattered like the cast-off implements of a defeated messenger. Clay tablets, once stacked neatly with father aviel’s luminous texts on the sky and the Word, were now tumbled and cracked, their wisdom turning to the most meaningless kind of grit.

Desiccated fruits, once heavy with the promise of life, now clung to their seeds like mummified burdens, a stark testament to a harvest withheld. The water skins were brittle and cracked, smelling of stale leather and profound thirst, mocking the very concept of living water. Thirst was the final state of everything here, a physical manifestation of an acute inner craving. This paragraph of refuse was a full statement on the abandonment of life's daily duties, a turning away from the light of the sun and the work under it.

The air was a thick, sugary bruise on the tongue, the kind of sickeningly-sweet stench that breeds in closed places and calls itself comfort. It was the heavy, cloying breathing of a place that devours its own air. The boy with kind eyes, Dan, whose eyes had witnessed too much raw, exposed suffering, picked his way through the refuse. A bone-deep chill settled on his spine, born not just of the filth, but from the corrupt heart of a man who saw treasure in rot and comfort in pity. father aviel had been a scholar whose mind was sharp as flint. He had traded that bright, clean currency for the slow, sticky joy of letting go.

He had ceased the daily rites of cleaning and maintenance, the small, repetitive acts that hold the chaos of the world at bay. This deliberate cessation of order, this willful embrace of collapse, was the true horror. It was the choice to stop fighting gravity, a choice that makes the earth cling to you a little too much. The profound silence of the house was marked only by the low, organic sounds of decay: the drip of moisture, the scuttle of insects, the inevitable, soft collapse of neglected structures. Silence was the final word.

Dan had to leave this place of reckoning before the material decay claimed him, too. Turning back was not an option—it was a history of failure written in the first book of all beginnings. He knew this house was a place consumed, a silent monument to what was lost in the fires of Sodom.

He paused by the hearth, where the glowing coals pulsed like a small, exhausted red heart in the surrounding blackness.

He crept to aviel’s doorframe and listened. A low, ragged snoring, the sound of a man trying to outrun his own breath, issued from within. The boy with messy hair held his breath, and with a quiet snuff, deliberately extinguished the torch. He plunged the area into a deep, choking darkness, relying only on the sharp memory of light and the faint moonlight filtering through a narrow window slit in the hallway wall. The pungent smell of burnt wick lingered, a bitter tang of parting.

His father was deep asleep. This sleeper was something else—a being who had chosen to stop striving. The ache in Dan's chest was a cold, dull throb—the physical cost for the upward path he was about to take.

He remembered his father’s final, damning dismissal:

"If you are still searching for a god, boy," aviel had hissed, "then pick up a stone and make it so! Seek your answers from a power you can touch."

The words were a bitter acid, a justification for his father's descent into matter-worship, where only tangible things held power.

If power could be forged by the crushing force of misguided will, then where should an unburdened heart turn? Dan’s gaze instinctively sought the open sky, searching the vast, starry tapestry above, which he could see now through the window slit. He knew its unseen source was the truth. He was already lifting his focus, preparing for the upward work. Dreams had always taken him there—not fleeting images, but profound, deliberate exercises of will, where he could walk up infinite stairs or soar above the rooftops in a single jump. He looked up now, and the stars gazed back, ancient and knowing, their silent light a beacon of promise. That was where he would turn.

The rare ability to enter and command the dreamscape was a perilous gift he shared with his mother, zuna. It was a control over his own mind, a power to enter and manipulate the dreams of others without using the word lucid.

Dan knelt by his father's cot. The cold stone of the floor immediately seized his bare knees, the sensation sharp and immediate. He slowed his breath, silencing the frantic voices in his inner self as his mother, zuna, had taught him.

He remembered her hands—rough, reddened from the lye, scrubbing their pots until they gleamed. Her knuckles would bleed sometimes, the skin raw from the ash and sand.

"The last scrubbing is the one that matters, Dan," she would say, holding a pot up to the sun. "Do not fear the emptiness. Make the vessel empty before you try to use it."

He forced his mind empty, chasing the last fragments of earthly attachment away. He let go of the phantom scent of zuna’s memory and the comforting shadow of aviel’s history. The pressure of leaving the familiar was a sharp agony, a deep shedding.

The stone beneath his knees dissolved, not instantly, but with a horrifying, slow certainty, as if the floor itself was sinking into quicksand. He waited through a full breath before the sensation of matter completely vanished. In that terrifying, perfect stillness—that necessary, terrible emptiness—he stepped sideways and into the Dreamscape.

The immediate change was the silence. The Dreamscape was a place of endless, ethereal gray, a formless void. It was as things must have been before time, when the first words of truth were spoken.

The journey began not with a lift, but an upward commitment.

He stood at the base of a rope ladder, forged of solidified starlight, its perfect, cold geometry stretching into the black overhead. The line was blood-warm, slick with something between sweat and oil, and it vibrated with the predatory hum of the emptiness it pierced. Each grip burned with tiny splinters that felt alive, burrowing into his palms. He climbed until the air around him was thin and cold, ringing with the silence of the unmanifest.

He passed the highest rung, a boundary between realms, and unstitched himself from the climb, shedding the concept of time itself.

He fell, a terrifying, absolute surrender, plunging into a stagnant, empty field illuminated by an ethereal gray light. He had landed, with the soundless impact of something dropped into velvet, deep within his father's sleeping awareness.

aviel stood alone in the gray. The raw, indistinguishable material of creation lay scattered around them. Dan drew a breath of pure intent, and a primal surge of focus flowed through him. His will and word, the quiet command of his heart, began to stir the refuse.

The dust coiled into a vortex. Dan exhaled, a soundless act of command:

"Let it rain."

The creation was instantaneous and corrupt. First, glittering motes of sand rained down, then knit together, hardening, forming smooth pebbles and then heavy stones that clicked softly as they accumulated. It was the unnatural, accelerated genesis of a devastating accumulation.

The father saw gold. His hands moved through the stones, but his eyes saw talents, oil jars, linen—everything he’d ever wanted. He wept as he gathered them, his fingers frantic, the noise of his hidden greed a soundless scream in the dream. He shoved them into his pockets and then beneath his clothing.

"Mine," he whispered, hoarding his own delusion, collecting the ultimate, silent pillar of permanent regret.

He gathered the ever-burgeoning accumulation until his very form was distended, crushed. He became a lonesome pillar of stone in a barren field, the accumulation squeezing the breath from his lungs. The father's eyes glazed over, his mouth open in a soundless gasp. The suffocating mass of his possessions silenced him, permanently locking his hands in the posture of hoarding.

Then, with a final, deliberate breath that was both a sigh and a judgment, Dan summoned a wind, a quiet, purifying current. The shaping left him hollow, leaving a sharp, internal ache behind his ribs. The wind swept across the field, gathering the grit and its crushing creations, carrying everything back to the nothingness.

In that brief moment of relief, the father looked up and saw a single star pierce the gray, impossibly bright. But even there, in the star's radiant core, a colder light struggled to be born, a subtle resentment prefiguring the strife of brother against brother. a chilling foreshadowing of the first murder.

Dan poured his final, potent intent into that fleeting vision: the boy must depart from the father, and the father must prepare his heart. He held the image of the star, imprinting it upon his father's mind. Then, gently, he released his father's awareness.

The stench hit him first—rot, waste, surrender's sweet perfume—before the cold floor registered beneath his feet. The coldness was a physical ache that settled in his bones. Drained but with renewed sense of purpose, Dan left his father's room. The darkness of the hallway was a sudden, heavy, oppressive presence. He felt smaller now, his confidence spent.

He felt the now freezing floor beneath his bare feet, a shocking return to matter. The shadows seemed to watch as he carefully traversed the labyrinth of refuse.

He knelt by the glowing coals in the hearth. He extended a steady hand, coaxing a flame from the embers with a small piece of kindling. The flame caught with a quiet hiss. He lit his torch, its light a familiar, hopeful presence in the gloom, and with new, resolute purpose, left the main room.

Back in his room, the door closed behind him. Dan's small room was a meticulously tidy corner of perfect order—the hard-won proof that he had put his own house in order before daring to criticize the world. This was his sanctuary, his final act of self-possession.

He stood for a moment, letting the silence of the room press out the house's decay.

He sought oblivion on the cot. He needed to be calm, like the man of wisdom who knows when to speak and when to keep silent. Yet, even as the darkness claimed him, a chilling dread coiled around his fading awareness. Sleep was not rest but summons.

He found himself standing on a shore of glittering, black sand. The water was unnaturally calm, a sheet of polished glass. He saw a small boat tethered nearby, but with a proud, youthful gesture of will, he dispersed it with his mind. He stepped out onto the water, and it held him, savoring the impossible ease of his stride.
