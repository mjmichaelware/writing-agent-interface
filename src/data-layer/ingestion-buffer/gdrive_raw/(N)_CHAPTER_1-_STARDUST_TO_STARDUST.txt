CHAPTER 1: STARDUST TO STARDUST

Let me tell you about a boy named Dan who could walk through dreams. On the night he used this gift to break into his father's sleeping mind, he learned exactly why some doors should stay locked.

Dan was sixteen, with kind eyes that had seen too much sorrow and messy dark hair that refused to stay flat no matter how often he pressed it down. He lived in a limestone house in Hebron with a father who no longer remembered how to be a father, and he possessed an inheritance from his dead mother that most would call a blessing but which felt, increasingly, like a burden he hadn't asked to carry.

The gift was this: Dan could step sideways into the dreams of sleeping men.

His mother had called it dreamwalking, though she'd also called it dangerous, forbidden, and in the last months before the fever took her, absolutely necessary for what was coming. She'd taught him the basics: how to quiet his own mind until it became still as deep water, how to find the thread connecting him to another sleeper, how to follow that thread into their dreaming. What she hadn't taught him, because there'd been no time, was how to survive what he'd find there.

Three nights ago, Dan and his father had fought. Not the usual careful dance they'd perfected since Mother died (Dan asking gentle questions, Father responding with silence or single words), but a real fight, with raised voices and accusations neither of them meant. Or rather, accusations they'd both meant desperately but hadn't known how to say any other way.

The fight had ended with Father turning away, returning to his treasure chamber as he always did, and Dan left alone in the cold hearth-room wondering if there was any man left inside the shell his father had become.

Tonight, Dan decided to find out.

Not to save him. You cannot save someone who refuses saving, and Dan had learned that particular truth the hard way. No, tonight was about something else entirely. Tonight was about testing the limits of his power, about proving to himself that he could do this thing his mother had taught him, about stepping into the deep end and seeing if he'd drown.

Young people do this sort of thing. They think they're cleverer than they actually are.

It went spectacularly wrong.

§

The house, if you could still call it that, had been decaying for two years now. Ever since Mother died, Father had let everything go to rot. Not from neglect exactly. More from a kind of active forgetting, as though if he stopped maintaining the house where she'd lived, he could somehow unmake the time they'd spent together.

The smell hit you first. Sharp urine from chamber pots left unemptied. Sweet-sick stench of old food. And underneath, a deeper corruption: the smell of a house abandoning its purpose, of walls that no longer bothered to keep the world out.

Dan moved through the corridor barefoot, torch held low. Every step was negotiation with disaster. The floor was mined with broken pottery, gnawed bones, and Mother's things. Her spindle whorls. Her loom weights. Her carved combs. All scattered like relics of a civilization that had simply stopped.

He had to reach Father's room. Father would be asleep by now, lost in whatever dreams the grief-mad dream.

The door stood half-open. Dan paused at the threshold, torch casting uncertain light across a room he barely recognized.

Two years ago, this had been a bedchamber. Now it was a tomb for beautiful things.

Father had filled it floor to ceiling with treasure. Not gold or silver (Father had never cared for such things), but objects. The remains of Mother's weaving: thread and cloth in every color you could name. Crimson bright as pomegranate seeds. Gold soft as late-afternoon light. Green deep as new leaves. Amber, violet, ochre, rose. Every color except three: never blue, never purple, never scarlet. Those colors belonged to priests, and Mother had honored old laws even when she'd broken older ones.

All of it rotted now. The threads had mildewed. The cloth had frayed. Moths had made kingdoms in the wool.

And there, in the center of it all, Father slept.

Aviel ben Zerah. Once the finest star-reader in Hebron, a man whose mind had been sharp as fresh-knapped flint, who could predict the movements of heaven and advise kings. Now he lay curled on his side among the ruins of his wife's work, one hand clutching a rotten tapestry to his chest, breathing in shallow gasps like a man drowning on dry land.

Dan's throat tightened. He wanted to shake his father awake, force him to see what he'd become. 

But that wasn't why he'd come.

He set the torch in a sconce and sat down on the cold floor beside his father's sleeping form.

Time to prove he could do this.

Dan closed his eyes and began.

§

The METHOD Mother had taught him was simple in theory: quiet your mind, find the thread, follow it across. In practice, it was like trying to thread a needle in the dark while someone screamed in your ear.

Dan breathed. Slow. Measured. Let the sounds of the house fade. Let the smell fade. Let the weight of his body fade until he felt light as ash, as though the downward pull itself had loosened its grip.

And there. A thread. Thin and fragile, stretching from somewhere in his chest to somewhere in his father's chest, visible only when Dan looked at it sideways, the way you see stars better when you don't look directly at them.

He reached for it.

The moment his awareness touched the thread, something seized him and pulled.

The room vanished.

§

The dreamscape was formless dark, a void that waited for will to give it shape. Dan felt his father's dream coalescing around him. Not building but remembering itself into existence, the way a word you've forgotten suddenly comes back.

The dark condensed into space. Walls appeared, limestone and shadow. A floor solidified beneath Dan's feet.

He was standing in the treasure chamber again. But this was the chamber as Father dreamed it. Perfect, orderly, with no rot or moths or ruin. The threads gleamed. The cloth lay folded in neat stacks. Everything was arranged with the kind of precision that came from deep sorrow trying very hard to look like contentment.

And there was Father.

Father stood in the center of the room, running his hands over the treasures with a small, satisfied smile. He looked younger here. Unburdened. His face had lost the gray, hollow cast it wore in waking life.

Dan opened his mouth to call out and stopped.

Because he'd just seen what his father was hoarding.

It wasn't just Mother's things.

Scattered among the cloth and thread were objects that had no business being there. A bronze fish-scale amulet. A clay tablet inscribed with words in no language Dan recognized. And in the corner, half-hidden by folded tapestries, a small shrine.

The shrine was simple: a stone altar, a bowl of water that somehow stayed liquid in this frozen dream, and behind it, carved into the limestone wall itself, the image of a fish. Not a real fish. Something older. Something with too many teeth and eyes that seemed to follow Dan even though they were only scratched stone.

Dagon.

The name came to Dan unbidden, rising from some deep place in his memory. One of the old gods. The Philistine deity of abundance, of the fertile depths, of the easy path that led down and down.

"Father," Dan said.

Aviel turned. For a moment his face was blank, as though he couldn't quite place who Dan was. Then recognition dawned, followed immediately by something else. Fear. Shame. Anger.

"You shouldn't be here," Father said.

"You're worshiping Dagon." Dan's voice came out flat. "In Mother's memory, you're building a shrine to a Philistine god."

"You don't understand."

"Then help me understand."

Father's hands moved to the treasures, touching them as though he could draw strength from their presence. "Your mother spent her life reaching for the heights. Reading stars. Teaching you to walk in dreams. Always ascending, always climbing. Do you know what that cost her?"

"The fever killed her."

"The reaching killed her." Father's voice was quiet, almost gentle. "The constant striving, the refusal to rest, the belief that we must always be climbing toward something higher. It wore her down. It burned her up. And for what? So she could teach you the same impossible dream?"

Dan felt something cold settle in his chest. "So you chose the opposite. You chose to sink."

"I chose peace." Father turned back to his treasures. "I chose abundance over want. Rest over struggle. The god of the depths offers what the god of the heights never could: an end to striving."

"This isn't peace, Father. This is drowning."

"No." The word was soft but absolute. "This is finally letting go."

And that's when Dan felt it. The presence. Something vast and patient, watching this conversation with mild interest. Something that had been here all along, waiting.

The walls of the dream began to ripple like water.

"Father," Dan said, "we need to leave. Now."

But Father just smiled. "He's here. Can you feel him? Can you feel how much he offers?"

The floor beneath Dan's feet started to soften. The limestone was turning to wet clay, then to mud, then to something that sucked at his ankles like hungry mouths.

"Father!"

"Let me show you," Father said, and reached for Dan's hand.

Dan jerked back. "No!"

The walls were melting now, stone flowing like water, and through the gaps Dan could see something else. A vastness. A depth without bottom. And rising from that depth, a shape.

The thing that rose from the depths was not fish and not man and not god as Dan understood gods. It was all three and none of them. Fish-scaled skin that looked like bronze in some lights and like wet clay in others. Eyes. So many eyes. Set in a face that kept shifting, as though Dagon wore his form the way other beings wore clothes, shrugging into it and out of it according to mood and need.

When he spoke, the dream itself shuddered.

"Always have you called, Aviel ben Zerah. In your grief, in your abandonment of the heights, in your descent into this place of treasures and dust. Always have you called, and now I answer."

The voice was not loud. It didn't need to be. It was the sound of stone grinding against stone in the depths. It was the sound of the sea's weight pressing down on drowned cities. It was patient and inexorable and utterly without mercy or cruelty, because mercy and cruelty both required caring, and this thing did not care about anything but its own nature.

Dan's father fell to his knees. "My Lord. My Lord of the depths."

"Rise," Dagon said. The word was not a command. It was simply a statement of fact, and Aviel rose because the dream itself lifted him. "You have served well. You have built your shrine in the ruins of false heights. You have chosen abundance over want. And now you shall have your reward."

Dagon turned one of his many eyes toward Dan.

"But who is this? Who is this young dreamer who walks where he was not invited?"

Dan couldn't move. Couldn't speak. The weight of Dagon's attention was like the weight of the ocean pressing down.

"My son," Father said quietly. "He doesn't understand. His mother filled his head with dreams of heights and stars. She taught him to reach upward when the only peace lies in descending."

"Ah." Dagon's form shifted, became almost gentle. Almost fatherly. "The son of Zuna. The child of the star-reader and the dreamwalker. Such gifts you possess, boy. Such potential wasted on climbing."

Dan found his voice. It came out smaller than he wanted. "I'm not interested in your abundance."

"No?" The eyes blinked, not all at once but in sequence, like ripples across water. "What does your mother's teaching offer you? Pain. Struggle. The endless exhausting work of reaching for heights that care nothing for you. I offer rest. I offer peace. I offer the sluggish, sweet warmth of a life no longer troubled by the cruelties of ascent."

"You offer drowning."

"I offer depth." Dagon moved closer, and the dream compressed around Dan. "Your father understands. He has tasted the bitterness of heights. He has reached and reached and watched the one he loved burn herself out on the altar of transcendence. Now he rests in my embrace. Now he accumulates treasure and dreams of abundance. Is this not better than the suffering your mother chose?"

Dan looked at his father. Aviel stood with his head bowed, hands still touching the rotted treasures, and on his face was an expression Dan had never seen before. Not quite peace. More like surrender. Like a man who has stopped fighting the current and let it carry him down and down.

"Come," Dagon said. "I shall show you what I offer."

The dream shifted. The walls dissolved. Dan found himself standing on the shore of a dark sea. The water was calm, mirror-smooth, reflecting nothing. And in that water, Dan saw images.

He saw himself older. Comfortable. Living in a house full of treasures. He had a wife whose hands weren't reddened by lye, whose back wasn't bent by labor. He had sons who would never know their grandfather's shame. He had wealth and ease and a life unmarked by the terrible cost of reaching for the heights.

"This is what awaits in the depths," Dagon said. "Not suffering. Not the burn of endless transcendence. Simply this: a good life. A peaceful life. A life of abundance."

Dan stared at the vision. And for a moment, just a moment, he felt the pull of it. How easy it would be to say yes. To let the current take him. To sink into the comfortable dark and dream of treasures.

Then he remembered his mother.

She had died reaching for the heights. She had burned herself out teaching him dreamwalking, preparing him for some future she'd seen in the stars. She had given everything to climb.

And she had been happy.

Not comfortable. Not at ease. But alive in a way his father had never been, even before grief broke him.

"No," Dan said.

Dagon's eyes fixed on him. All of them. At once.

"No?"

"No." Dan's voice was stronger now. "I don't want your depth. I don't want your abundance. I don't want the easy path."

The dream began to darken. The calm sea began to churn.

"You refuse me?" Dagon's voice lost its gentle quality. "You, a child, refuse what I offer? Let me show you the alternative. Let me show you what awaits those who cling to heights."

The vision changed. Dan saw himself older, climbing. Always climbing. He saw exhaustion. He saw failure. He saw the terrible loneliness of reaching for something that might not even exist. He saw himself burning out, just like his mother, giving everything for transcendence and dying with the work unfinished.

"This is your mother's path," Dagon said. "This is the cost of heights. Why choose suffering when abundance awaits?"

Dan's hands were shaking. Because Dagon wasn't lying. That was the worst part. The heights did demand everything. They did burn you up. They did kill you.

But.

"Better to burn than drown," Dan said.

Silence.

Then Dagon laughed. It was not a pleasant sound. It was the grinding of continental plates, the collapse of stone beneath impossible weight, the slow patient destruction of everything that reached too high.

"Then burn you shall," Dagon said. "But not yet. First, let me offer you prophecy. Let me tell you what your reaching will cost."

The darkness pressed in from all sides. Dan tried to move, tried to run, but the dream held him fast.

"The silence of the cosmos awaits you," Dagon said. Each word fell like a stone. "The star you become shall burn alone. When you speak, no man shall understand. Your instrument shall be shattered. Your tongue shall be made a heavy thing, speaking a foretold truth that is only heard as noise, the worthless babble of the judged. You will have no witness."

The words sank into Dan like hooks. Like brands. Like prophecy that tasted of salt and deep water and the patient crushing weight of depths.

"That is the cost of refusing me," Dagon said. "That is the price of heights. You will reach. You will climb. You will burn as your mother burned. And in the end, no one will hear you. No one will understand. You will burn alone, and your light will change nothing."

Dan wanted to scream. Wanted to deny it. Wanted to unhear the prophecy.

But he couldn't.

Because deep in his bones, in some part of himself that remembered his mother's dying words, he knew it was true.

"Now go," Dagon said. "Return to your waking world. Carry my words with you. And when the heights have taken everything and you are alone in the terrible cold of transcendence, remember: I offered you abundance. I offered you peace. And you chose suffering."

The dream exploded.

Dan did the only thing he could. He ran.

§

He ran through the melting corridors of his father's dream, past rooms that dissolved into wet shadow, past doorways that opened onto nothing. Behind him he could hear it. Not footsteps. Something heavier. The sound of accumulated weight shifting, the patient grinding of depths pursuing heights.

But Dagon wasn't chasing him. Dagon didn't need to. The prophecy was already inside Dan, working its way into his bones like cold water.

Dan knew how to wake himself. Mother had taught him that too. You grabbed something real, something that existed in both the dream and the waking world, and you pulled yourself along it like climbing a rope.

But there was nothing real here. Everything in his father's dream was fantasy, delusion, false memory.

Except.

Except his own body. Dan was real. And his body was sitting on the floor beside his father's sleeping form in the waking world.

He stopped running. Closed his eyes in the dream. Concentrated. Felt for the weight of his real body, the cold of the real floor, the smoke from the real torch.

Found it.

And pulled.

The dream shattered like glass hitting stone.

§

Dan gasped, eyes snapping open. He was back in the waking world, sitting on the cold floor of his father's room, torch still burning in its sconce, casting the same uncertain light.

But something was wrong.

His hands were shaking. His whole body was shaking. And when he tried to stand, his legs barely held him.

The exhaustion wasn't normal tiredness. It was deeper than that. It felt like something essential had been carved out of him and nothing but shell remained. Like he'd spent not minutes but years in that dream, burning himself up to fuel the crossing.

He looked at his father. Aviel still slept, face peaceful, one hand clutching the rotten tapestry. Dreaming his impossible dreams of abundance and rest.

Dan wanted to wake him. Wanted to drag him out of that dream and shake him until he understood what he was doing, what he was worshiping, what he'd become.

But he'd learned tonight what he'd really come to learn: his father was too far gone. Whatever Dagon offered, Aviel had already accepted it. The father Dan remembered, the star-reader with the sharp mind and the capacity for joy, was gone. What remained was just a man sinking into comfortable depths, choosing drowning over the pain of swimming.

You cannot save someone who refuses saving.

The thought came with a weight Dan hadn't expected. It wasn't angry or bitter. Just true. Heavy and true and final.

Dan stood on legs that trembled. The room spun. He caught himself against the doorframe, breathing hard.

He needed to leave this house. Needed to get away before Dagon's presence followed him out of sleep and into waking. Needed to...

He stopped.

Because he felt it again.

A grinding. Deep beneath the house. Deep beneath the city. Deep beneath the bedrock itself. As though something vast had shifted its weight, as though some ancient sleeper had rolled over in the depths and sighed with satisfaction.

And from his father's sleeping form, Dan heard a voice. Not his father's voice. Something older. Something that tasted of salt water and drowned things and the patient hunger of depths.

"Go then," the voice said. It was quiet. Almost gentle. "Climb your heights. Burn yourself out as your mother did. And when the silence takes you, when no one hears and no one understands, remember: I offered you peace. I offered you abundance. And you chose suffering."
Dan ran.
He ran through the corridor, past the scattered treasures, past the smell of rot and abandonment, out of the house and into the cold night air of Hebron. The stars were out, hard and bright and impossibly distant. The same stars his mother had loved, the same stars she'd died reaching for.
Dan didn't stop running until he reached his own small room at the edge of the compound.
Only then did he collapse against the wall, gasping, the night air burning in his lungs.
What had he done?
He'd broken into his father's dream. He'd confronted a god. He'd refused Dagon's offer. And in return, he'd been given a prophecy. A curse. A future that tasted of isolation and incomprehensible burning and voices that spoke truth but were heard only as noise.
No witness.
The words echoed in his head. You will have no witness.
Dan slid down the wall until he was sitting on the cold floor of his room. The exhaustion was getting worse. Not better. It spread through his limbs like cold water, like the weight of depths pulling him down.
This was the cost his mother had warned him about. Dreamwalking always demanded payment. Every time you stepped into someone else's sleep, you burned a little of yourself to fuel the crossing. The stronger the dreamer, the more protected the dream, the more you had to burn.
And Dagon's presence in his father's dream had made it strong. Protected. Hostile to intrusion.
Tonight Dan had burned too much.
He dragged himself to his sleeping mat. His body felt light, hollow, as though the core of him had been scooped out and only the shell remained. His vision swam. His hands when he looked at them seemed distant, disconnected, like they belonged to someone else.
Dan closed his eyes.
Sleep came fast, dragging him down like deep water.
But even as unconsciousness took him, he heard it. Two sounds, distinct and impossible.
From below, from somewhere deep beneath the house, beneath the city, beneath the bedrock of the world: grinding. Patient. Ancient. The sound of accumulated depths shifting their weight. Dagon, settling back into his eternal waiting, satisfied with the seeds he'd planted in a boy's heart.
And from outside Dan's door: a different sound. Soft. Not quite tapping, not quite rapping. Almost like purring. As though something small waited there, entreating entrance at his chamber lattice. Something that had watched. Something that had followed. Something that wasn't Dagon.
Dan's eyes fluttered closed. He tried to open them again, tried to see what waited at his door, but exhaustion pulled him down.
The darkness behind his eyelids wasn't complete. It held a quality of waiting. Of presence. Of things not yet begun but inevitable. The grinding from below. The purring from his door. Two forces. Two futures. Two paths diverging.
And Dan, caught between depths and heights, burning and drowning, prophecy and promise.
He closed his kind eyes and let the exhaustion take him.
Whatever came next, whether grinding or purring, depths or heights, isolation or witness, could wait until morning.
If morning came.
