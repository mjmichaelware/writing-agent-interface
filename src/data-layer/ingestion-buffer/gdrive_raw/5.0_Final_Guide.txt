The Exhaustive Universal Style and Constraint Prompt

I. Prose and Diction Constraints (The Language of Friction)

Negated Language: Define phenomena not by what they are, but by what they are not (e.g., "not mere light," but "the absence of all discernible shadow"). This emphasizes the spiritual violation or profound nature of the element being described.

The Law of Physical Substitution: Eliminate all direct, single-word expressions of emotion or spiritual state (e.g., "sad,"

"holy," "evil"). Substitute these abstract states entirely with tangible, physical phenomena that act as their consequence (e.g., Shame as "viscous drag," Resolve as a "cold, fixed pressure").

Diction of Weight and Resistance: Favor vocabulary that conveys friction, pressure, viscosity, weight, and coldness when describing internal states, psychological obstacles, or the protagonist's chosen path.

The Exclusion of Platitude: Strictly eliminate all forms of cliché, simple motivational language, and phrases that suggest an easy resolution or simple comfort. The prose must be severe, unsentimental, and philosophically dense.

The Unnamed Diction: All newly introduced objects or settings within the chapter must be used to organically derive a self-contained, unique lexicon of metaphors for that chapter, linking the physical environment to the central spiritual conflict without borrowing specific symbols from previous chapters.

II. Sentence and Structural Mandates (The Architecture of Thought)

Rhythmic Complexity: The standard sentence structure must be complex and multi-clausal, designed to be read rhythmically. Sentences should feel long, disciplined, and purposeful.

Punctuation as Logic: Utilize colons, semicolons, and em dashes as logical operators—not mere separators—to directly fuse sensory detail (the observed effect) with thematic abstraction (the philosophical cause) within the same continuous thought.

Pacing by Internal Density: The forward momentum of the plot must be slowed and weighted by the exhaustive rhetorical unpacking of the protagonist's internal conflict and philosophical processing. Physical action should be minimal but profound.

The Four-Fold Clause: Prioritize the rhetorical structure that connects at least four distinct conceptual components (e.g., Action → Consequence → Philosophical Cause → Allegorical Symbol) within a single extended sentence.

III. Thematic and Allegorical Mandates (The Law of Duality)

The Allegory of Value: Establish a clear, non-theological system of value where all concepts of inward-turning, comfort, and cessation are linguistically rendered as diminishing, toxic, or collapsing. Conversely, all concepts of outward-turning, discipline, and difficulty must be rendered as purifying, expanding, or true.

Theological Lens Mandate: Treat every object, setting, and action as having a direct, active moral or spiritual commentary. Descriptions must not be passive; they must convey the moral pressure of the environment.

The Rule of Universal Stakes: The conflict must always be framed as a struggle of Human Will against Existential Inertia or Personal Clarity against Universal Chaos. This ensures the stakes are immediately relatable across all belief systems.

The Allegory of Process: Every major event in the chapter must function primarily as irrefutable, physical proof of a theological or philosophical principle at work.

The Cost of Simplicity: Any moment of directness, simplicity, or resolution by the protagonist must be immediately followed by a descriptive passage detailing the immense, painful energetic or spiritual expenditure required to maintain that simplicity.

IV. Narrative and Character Constraints (The Voice of Authority)

Antagonist’s Paradox: Any force of opposition or temptation must be defined by a sensory contradiction (e.g., silence louder than chaos, beautiful decay) and must offer the seductive promise of ultimate, final rest.

Rhetorical Space: Use carefully placed unanswerable philosophical questions (often italicized or parenthetical) within the narrative flow to emphasize the protagonist’s intellectual isolation and the narrative's vast metaphysical scale.

Protagonist's Voice: The protagonist’s internal and external voice must maintain an unflinching, disciplined, and emotionally contained register, serving as the immovable, simple point of clarity against the surrounding narrative complexity.

The Authority of Voice: The narration must maintain an absolute, cold, and unwavering tone of authority regarding the reality and stakes of the events, never apologizing or diminishing the fantastical nature of the conflict.

The Final Action Mandate: The chapter's climax must culminate in an action of definitive, unburdened abandonment of a previous state of stasis or false comfort.

The Philosophical Coda: The final sentence must be a single, severe, universal statement delivered from the high, detached voice of the narrator, ensuring the chapter closes on an note of exalted, profound difficulty.

The Unresolved Nexus: The opening of the chapter must function as a high-pressure synthesis of the inherited shock and immediate internal crisis from the preceding chapter's final second, allowing no temporal or descriptive pause.

Negation of Character Summary: Avoid summarizing the protagonist's emotional journey. Instead, detail their state through the physical consequence of their internal struggle (e.g., trembling hand, fixed gaze, numb tongue).

The Rule of Threefold Symbolism: Ensure any prominent symbol derived from the chapter can be interpreted simultaneously on the physical, psychological, and existential levels, guaranteeing broad, diverse resonance.

Voice Modulation: The narrative must clearly and quickly modulate its complexity when shifting from the complex internal world of the protagonist to the simple, direct dialogue or action.

The Ultimate Rhetorical Template (Micro-Level Mandates)
Apply the following ten rhetorical and compositional mandates to the provided chapter. This template governs the micro-level construction of your prose to ensure absolute consistency in tone, rhythm, and conceptual delivery.

I. Mandates on Conceptual Delivery

The Rule of Delayed Abstraction: When introducing a complex philosophical concept, always present the physical sensation first, and reserve the abstract label for the end of the sentence or the following sentence. (e.g., The hand trembled, not from the cold, but from the realization of a colossal, self-imposed lie.)

Rhetoric of Diminishment: Any description of temptation, comfort, or stasis must utilize language that actively diminishes its value even while describing its appeal (e.g., "The soft light was a poison," or "The deep silence was merely the absence of a required question").

The Unsettling Conjunction: Use conjunctions (and, but, for) to link ideas that are semantically opposed, creating a constant tension (e.g., "He felt the quiet and the terrible cost of that quiet," or "The air grew heavy, for the simple truth was a weight he had forgotten").

II. Mandates on Voice and Focus

The Mandatory Gaze Shift: Every major paragraph must contain a mandatory Gaze Shift where the narrative perspective moves from a close-up on the protagonist's internal state to a wide shot of the metaphysical environment (e.g., from the cold skin on his arm to the infinite, indifferent sky).

The Narration of Immediacy: The narration must constantly reference the immediacy and physical location of the ongoing thought, anchoring the most abstract reflection to the current physical scene (e.g., "The answer was a cold knot in his throat," or "He processed the revelation with the small, dry mechanics of his exhausted mind").

The Exclusion of Historical Context (Asides): Strictly prohibit the narrator from stepping out of the immediate scene to provide historical, cultural, or character background. All necessary context must be embedded as present-tense pressure or unresolved memory.

III. Mandates on Structural Cohesion

The Triple-Aphorism Rule: Ensure every major scene (e.g., confrontation, breakthrough, refusal) is internally summarized by at least three distinct philosophical statements or aphorisms that organically arise from the action, rather than being placed externally by the narrator.

The Rule of Terminal Tension: The peak of conflict must not result in relief. It must result in a state of terminal tension—a new, more demanding problem or realization that makes the previous struggle seem simple by comparison.

The Repetition of Weight: A specific, non-symbolic word that denotes weight, pressure, or mass must be rhythmically repeated across the chapter's key paragraphs (e.g., "mass,"

"volume," "density") to create a recurring, subconscious sense of material opposition.

The Unresolved Question: The chapter must close with a final, definite statement that, upon immediate reflection, contains a final, vast, and unresolved question about the nature of the reality the protagonist has just chosen.
