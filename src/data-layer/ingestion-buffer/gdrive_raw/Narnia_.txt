Chapter 1: Stardust to Stardust

The flame was a frail, frightened thing, dancing with uncertainty, and its light cast suspicious shadows across the narrow hall. The tremor in the boy’s heart matched the light's flicker. Every quiet step he took, with his bare feet on the cold, sun-baked stone, was a great risk. (you see, it is often in the quietest moments that the greatest evil is done.) He was trying to be quieter than silence itself, because the slightest sound would certainly betray his secret mission. This silence was not peace; it was a coiled emptiness, the patient dread of something ancient and watchful. This house, we must understand, was no longer a home at all. It was the lair of a man who had freely chosen to become heavy—a soul devoted to the sheer, stubborn weight of the earth. For Dan, merely to walk inside it was an act of ascent.

---

The four-room house, which had once been a cozy haven warmed by his mother Zuna, had become a tragic illustration of his father’s great moral collapse. It was, quite simply, a place where things lost their meaning, like a toy you no longer care for. It was a life willingly surrendered to the mere drag of staying put. (when a man chooses to stop caring, as you or i might neglect to mend a torn curtain, the decay inside his heart quickly shows itself on the outside.) Walking its paths was a tedious business, forcing one to wrestle with the snare of matter, feeling its terrible gravity with every breath, as if the air had been turned into a thick, clinging syrup meant to tire out your soul.

---

The house held a clammy, internal chill, the sort one might find at the bottom of a well that has been dry for a generation. Piles of broken pottery stood like ancient cairns, their stained edges whispering not of forgotten meals, but of the sharp, cutting burden of history. Rotting textiles—sacred threads Zuna had left mid-shuttle—had unraveled into mildew and waste. It was the clear spectacle of a promise broken, not by accident, but by neglect. it was like seeing a perfectly good suit of armor deliberately tossed into the muck until all the metal had rusted away.

---

The air itself was a torment—a thick, cloying stench of dried herbs, stagnant decay, and, worst of all, human waste. It was sickeningly sweet, the heavy breathing of a place that devours its own air. Dan, whose eyes had seen too much of his father’s mental nakedness, picked his way through the refuse. A deep chill settled on his spine, born not just of the filth, but from the wrongness of a man who saw treasure in rot and found perverse comfort in pity. this, friends, is the true moral crime of our story: Aviel had traded brilliant insight for mere squalor. His sharp intellect, once a bright flame, was now choked by debris, as if someone had deliberately buried a perfectly good lamp under a pile of filthy rags.

---

Dan knew he had to leave this place of moral judgment before he, too, was consumed. The essential truth, the only lesson worth remembering, was simply this: turning back was not an option.

---

He tiptoed past the hearth. His anchor against the house's chaos was the zuna stone. It was a river stone his mother had polished smooth, a touch of cool certainty that anchored his heart to reality.

---

As he reached his father's doorframe, he held his breath and, with a quiet snuff, deliberately extinguished the torch. He plunged the area into a deep, choking darkness. The pungent smell of burnt wick lingered, a bitter tang of parting.

---

His father was deep asleep. He lay there not as a man resting from honest labor, but as a being who had chosen to stop climbing. The ache in Dan's chest was the cold, dull throb of the physical cost for the path he was about to take.

---

He recalled his father’s final, damaging dismissal: "My son, if you are still looking for a god, then pick up a stone and make it so! belief is attention, nothing more!" It was a harsh, final statement, yet it defined the battle: if power was simply focused will, Dan needed a subject worthier of his absolute devotion than a mere stone.

---

Dan knelt by his father's cot. The cold stone of the floor immediately seized his bare knees, grounding him painfully. He slowed his breath, silencing the frantic voices in his mind as his mother, Zuna, had taught him.

"The last scrubbing is the one that matters," she would say. (and what sensible person would argue with that?) "You must empty it entirely, or the light will only show what you tried to hide. He forced his mind empty. In that stillness, he slipped from the confines of flesh and entered the dreamscape. The journey began not with a lift, but a climb. He found himself at the base of a helix of solidified starlight, winding into the void. The helix warped into a single, thick rope, heavy and vibrating with the energy of the abyss. He climbed until the air around him was thin and cold, ringing with the silence of the unmanifest.

now, we come to a difficult point: The ultimate price of ascent was this—to rely on any external lifeline was to rely on matter. To reach the star, he had to trust only his own will. He ripped his palms free, a searing sensation of loss. The rope did not snap from weakness; it vanished, annihilated by his own terrifying, absolute choice to climb unbound.

---

He fell, a soul-wrenching drop through the shattered dreamscape, plunging into a stagnant, empty field. He had landed, deep within his father's sleeping mind.

---

Aviel stood alone. dust began to swirl around him. Dan drew a breath of pure intent. He exhaled, a soundless act of command: "Let it rain." The creation of Aviel's wish was instantaneous and corrupt. Sand rained down, forming heavy stones. it was the creation of the first burden. Aviel saw gold. He wept as he gathered them. He became a pillar of stone.

---

Then, Dan summoned a wind, a quiet, purifying current. The wind swept across the field, carrying the dust and its crushing, stone creations back to the nothingness. Aviel looked up and saw a single star pierce the gray, impossibly bright. But in the star's radiant core, a colder light struggled to be born, a subtle resentment prefiguring the ultimate judgment.

---

Dan poured his final, potent intent into that vision: the son must depart, and the father must prepare his heart.

---

The stench hit him first—rot, waste, surrender's sweet perfume—before the cold stone of the floor registered beneath his feet. He made his way back through the refuse, lit his torch, and with new, resolute purpose, left the main room.
